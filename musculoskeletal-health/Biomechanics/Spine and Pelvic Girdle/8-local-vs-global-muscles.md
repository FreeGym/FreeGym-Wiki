# Local vs Global Muscles: Bergmark’s Framework, 37 Years Later

For clarity, consider a simplified clinical version of the local-versus-global muscle model: deep “local stabilizers” such as transversus abdominis and multifidus hold the spine together, while superficial “global movers” such as rectus abdominis, obliques, and erector spinae create movement. In this caricature, pain appears when the local system “switches off,” after which the global muscles compensate by becoming tight or overactive.

That is not what Bergmark’s original framework said. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

Published in 1989, Bergmark’s work is now approximately 37 years old. It was a mechanical-engineering analysis of lumbar stability and load sharing, not a clinical screening system, a pain theory, or a mandatory local-muscle-first exercise program. Its quantitative calculations focused mainly on simplified, static, symmetric upright-loading conditions, including upper-body weight and shoulder-carried loads, and represented selected structures and force paths rather than every anatomical detail. Bergmark grouped muscles according to their principal mechanical relationship to the lumbar spine and external loads. He did not divide the body into good stabilizers and bad movers, and he did not present transversus abdominis as the single muscle responsible for spinal safety. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

A useful first rule is: **local and global describe a component’s principal load path and the directness of its action on the lumbar spine, not its importance.** Local muscles influence lumbar segments and spinal curvature more directly. Global muscles balance larger external moments and transfer force between the thorax and pelvis. Both systems can move the spine. Both can contribute to stiffness and compression. Neither system functions alone. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

The modern update is not “throw Bergmark away.” It is:

> **Keep the mechanical insight. Drop the clinical hierarchy.**

That correction preserves task-specific differences in leverage and mechanical efficiency while avoiding a clinical hierarchy that Bergmark did not test. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

## What Bergmark was trying to solve

Bergmark’s central problem was mechanical: how can the lumbar spine remain controlled when external loads, body weight, and muscle forces produce compression, shear, bending, and rotational moments? Because the lumbar spine contains multiple motion segments, the body must do more than prevent the entire trunk from falling forward. It must also manage the shape and behavior of the lumbar column itself. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

That distinction led Bergmark to separate two scales of muscular action. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

The **global system** was responsible primarily for balancing external loads and controlling the position of the thorax relative to the pelvis. The **local system** acted more directly on lumbar vertebrae and helped control spinal curvature, intersegmental relationships, and lumbar stiffness after the global system had balanced the larger external moment. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

This was a modeling decision. Bergmark explicitly acknowledged that the classification was not perfectly clean and that muscles could have mixed mechanical roles. He assigned structures according to their main function in the model, not merely by how deep they were, what they looked like in an anatomy illustration, or whether a therapist considered them “postural.” ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

That is the first major correction: **Bergmark’s framework was a model of force distribution, not a taxonomy of healthy and dysfunctional muscles.** ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

## The original local system

In Bergmark’s formulation, local muscles generally had direct attachments to lumbar vertebrae and could therefore influence the shape, position, and stiffness of the lumbar column more directly. The system included structures such as multifidus, interspinales, intertransversarii, lumbar portions of the erector-spinae system, and mechanically local portions of quadratus lumborum. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

“Local” did not mean that these muscles only stabilized. Bergmark described multifidus as capable of transferring force, producing movement, and controlling lumbar lordosis. The short interspinales and intertransversarii were considered mechanically relevant partly because their short fiber lengths and vertebra-to-vertebra attachments could influence segmental stiffness. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

The local lumbar portions of the erector-spinae system could also extend the lumbar spine while contributing to its control. Calling a muscle “local” therefore did not remove its ability to create torque, compression, or movement. It identified the principal mechanical role associated with its attachments. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

Using **local stabilizer** to mean a muscle that holds still but does not move is anatomically and mechanically misleading. Muscles produce force. Depending on posture, moment arm, activation level, and the activity of other muscles, that force can create movement, resist movement, increase stiffness, alter compression, or do several of those things at once. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

## The original global system

Bergmark’s global system included muscles and force mechanisms that acted over a larger distance between the thoracic cage and pelvis. These structures were positioned to balance external loads and control the gross orientation of the trunk rather than directly controlling each lumbar motion segment. Examples included thoracic portions of erector spinae, rectus abdominis, internal and external oblique, mechanically global portions of quadratus lumborum, and the global effects of intra-abdominal-pressure-related forces. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

Bergmark classified psoas as global according to its principal mechanical role, despite its direct lumbar attachments. He also discussed possible indirect contributions from latissimus dorsi but did not include every plausible structure in the final stability calculations. These exceptions show why “local means attached to the vertebrae” was never an absolute anatomical law. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

The global system was not mechanically unimportant to stability. A global extensor can resist a large external flexion moment. The obliques can resist trunk rotation or lateral flexion. Rectus abdominis can contribute to sagittal-plane stiffness. Quadratus lumborum can help balance frontal-plane loading. These are stabilizing actions even though the muscles are classified as global. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

The better distinction is therefore:

- **Local:** more direct control of lumbar vertebral relationships and regional curvature.

- **Global:** larger-scale control of trunk position and external moments.

That is a difference in principal mechanical role and load path, not a difference between stabilizers and movers. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

## The split was never anatomically clean

Some muscles contain portions that behave differently. Bergmark separated lumbar and thoracic portions of the erector-spinae system because their attachments and mechanical effects were not identical. Quadratus lumborum could also be divided into portions with more local or more global effects. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

The abdominal wall further blurred the distinction. Abdominal muscles can create large global trunk moments, alter intra-abdominal pressure, tension fascial structures, and contribute to spinal stiffness. Bergmark acknowledged that pressure-related forces could have both local and global effects rather than fitting neatly into one compartment. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

This means a muscle name alone may not tell you its mechanical category. Fiber direction, attachment, region, posture, loading direction, and co-activation all matter. A muscle can create a global trunk moment in one task while contributing to regional stiffness in another. The nervous system recruits motor units and muscle regions for tasks; it does not read simplified rehabilitation diagrams. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

The practical correction is:

> **Local and global are overlapping mechanical descriptions, not two sealed anatomical teams.**

## Local does not mean deep

Redrawing the local-versus-global framework as deep versus superficial creates several problems. ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/); [11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/))

Multifidus does contain deep fibers with short vertebral attachments, but it also contains more superficial fibers with longer lever arms and different activation behavior. In an experimental arm-movement study, deep and superficial multifidus fibers showed different activation patterns, supporting the idea that “multifidus” is not one homogeneous switch. ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

Lumbar multifidus morphology is also described inconsistently across the anatomical and imaging literature. A 2020 scoping review found substantial inconsistency in how researchers defined multifidus fascicles, layers, attachment patterns, and imaging regions. This makes broad statements such as “the multifidus is atrophied” less precise than they appear unless the measurement method, spinal level, muscle region, and outcome are specified. ([11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/))

Conversely, some structures commonly called superficial or global make important contributions to spinal stiffness. Large erector-spinae and abdominal forces can oppose external moments and raise trunk stiffness substantially. Depth does not determine whether a muscle can stabilize, and superficial location does not make a muscle a mere mover. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019))

A better anatomical rule is: **depth changes leverage and attachment, but function emerges from the whole task.** ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

## Global does not mean dysfunctional compensation

Treating the global system as overactive, dominant, or compensatory by default creates a similar problem. Global-muscle activity must be interpreted in relation to the task, load, symptoms, and the rest of the motor strategy. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

There is no general physiological rule that makes global-muscle activity pathological. When external load increases, larger trunk muscles must generally produce more force to oppose the resulting moments. During a heavy hinge, carry, squat, sprint, or throw, substantial global-muscle activity is expected rather than evidence of dysfunction. Modern modeling shows that the relative stabilizing contribution of each trunk muscle changes with the direction and magnitude of the task. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

Increased activation can sometimes be a protective response to pain or perceived threat. It can also increase compression, fatigue, energy cost, and movement rigidity if maintained beyond what the task requires. The problem is therefore not “global muscles are active.” The relevant question is whether the total strategy is appropriately scaled, adaptable, and tolerable. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

## Where transversus abdominis entered the story

Later rehabilitation language often elevated transversus abdominis, usually abbreviated **TrA**, to the signature “local stabilizer.” Bergmark did not present TrA as a sovereign local muscle. His analysis discussed the abdominal wall and its relationship to pressure and lumbar mechanics, but the familiar pairing of “TrA plus multifidus equals the local system” is a later clinical simplification rather than a literal reproduction of the 1989 framework. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

TrA is anatomically and mechanically relevant. Its abdominal-wall attachments allow it to alter abdominal-wall tension and contribute to pressure-related mechanics, while experimental studies show anticipatory activity during some limb movements. But relevance does not establish exclusive control. TrA acts within broader abdominal and limb-related synergies, and its recruitment varies with the task. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

A 2013 study enrolled seven healthy participants, with complete electromyography data available for six, and concluded that TrA participated in a broader global or diagonal muscle synergy rather than behaving as an isolated local unit. A 2015 study of 12 people with chronic low back pain and 13 controls, using combined TrA/internal-oblique surface recordings, likewise found that anticipatory activation was task-specific: the pattern changed according to the movement and side tested. ([8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

The modern interpretation is not that TrA is unimportant. It is that **TrA is a participant, not a commander.** ([8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

## Stability is not the absence of motion

Bergmark’s framework is easier to understand when “stability” is separated from “stillness.” In his engineering model, mechanical stability meant that a loaded structure maintained static equilibrium under small perturbations rather than buckling. That model-specific construct is not synonymous with a perfectly fixed spinal angle, segmental stiffness, pain, or clinical function. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/))

Panjabi’s later framework divided spinal stability into passive, active, and neural-control subsystems. Bones, discs, facets, ligaments, and capsules contribute passive restraint. Muscles generate active forces. The nervous system coordinates how much force is produced, when it appears, and how it changes as the task changes. ([2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/))

Bergmark and Panjabi were addressing related but different mechanical questions. Bergmark examined how muscle systems distribute load and influence lumbar stability. Panjabi described interacting subsystems that preserve control. Neither model demonstrates that pain results from one inactive muscle, and neither is a stand-alone diagnostic test or treatment algorithm. Stability, stiffness, motor control, and clinical function are related but not interchangeable. A modeled increase in stability does not by itself demonstrate greater measured stiffness, better motor control, less pain, or lower injury risk. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7680716/))

## There is no universal master stabilizer

One of the clearest modern corrections to local-muscle folklore comes from Cholewicki and VanVliet’s 2002 trunk-stability analysis. Eight healthy men performed isometric exertions while investigators combined muscle measurements with a lumbar stability model. Within those tested and modeled isometric conditions, no single group reduced modeled stability by more than approximately 30%, and the contribution of each muscle changed according to the load’s direction and magnitude. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

That was a small mechanistic study, not a clinical trial. It cannot tell us which exercise treats back pain, and it does not rule out a muscle being disproportionately important in a specific task, posture, pathology, or individual. It does, however, directly challenge the idea that the spine depends on one universally indispensable muscle. Modeled stability emerged from the combined activity of the trunk musculature, with different muscles becoming more or less important under different loading conditions. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

McGill and colleagues reached a similar systems-level conclusion in their narrative review of lumbar muscle coordination: no single recruitment pattern guarantees stability under every condition. The nervous system selects among many possible activation combinations while balancing sufficient stiffness against the compression, fatigue, and movement restriction created by muscle force. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

This is the modern replacement for the master-stabilizer model:

> **Stability is an emergent property of coordinated force sharing.**

## “Not firing” is usually the wrong phrase

In the studies discussed here, “not firing” generally misdescribes delayed onset, reduced amplitude, asymmetry, or altered regional recruitment. Complete electrical silence was not the reported finding. Researchers may also observe increased co-contraction or reduced movement variability. Those findings are not interchangeable. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

A muscle can be active but late. It can be strong in a maximal test but poorly coordinated in a rapid task. It can show reduced activation during one movement and increased activation during another. It can be inhibited by pain, guarded because of pain, or appropriately quiet because another strategy is more efficient. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

The phrase “not firing” compresses all of those possibilities into an unsupported diagnosis. A more precise description is usually available: delayed, reduced, asymmetric, poorly coordinated, low-force, low-endurance, painful, or not sufficiently recruited for the tested task. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

## The TrA timing finding and what it did not prove

In 1996, Hodges and Richardson reported delayed TrA activation in people with low back pain during rapid arm movement. The finding was important because it suggested that low-back-pain populations could show altered anticipatory trunk-muscle behavior before a predictable limb perturbation. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/))

The study did not prove that delayed TrA activation caused the participants’ pain. It did not establish that every person with back pain has the same delay. It did not show that the muscle was weak, inactive during all tasks, or responsible for mechanical instability. It examined a specific timing variable during a specific experimental task. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/))

Later work made the picture less uniform. TrA timing and laterality depend on movement direction, limb, preparation, load, and the muscle synergy required by the task. Research on arm movement has shown both global-synergy behavior and task-specific bilateral activation rather than one invariant feedforward pattern. ([8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of experimentally induced lumbar pain further complicates causal interpretation. Across 26 studies, pain produced task-dependent neuromuscular adaptations. The pooled evidence included delayed TrA onset during perturbation tasks and increased erector-spinae activity in full flexion, but several findings were based on low-quality evidence and varied across tasks. Pain can therefore alter motor behavior; altered motor behavior cannot automatically be assumed to have created the pain. ([13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

The responsible interpretation is: **timing matters in some tasks, but timing is not a universal diagnosis.** ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006); [13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

## Multifidus is a region, not a switch

Treating multifidus as the posterior counterpart to TrA, as one deep muscle that should activate locally and independently, obscures its regional anatomy and task-dependent behavior. ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/); [11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/))

Deep and superficial multifidus fibers differ in attachment, leverage, and activity. Experimental evidence indicates that they can be recruited differently during the same limb movement. Calling the entire muscle either active or inactive can therefore erase important regional behavior. ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

Imaging studies also measure different outcomes: total cross-sectional area, functional contractile area, fat infiltration, thickness, asymmetry, and ultrasound echo intensity. These variables are not equivalent. The literature’s inconsistent anatomical descriptions and imaging boundaries make direct study-to-study comparisons difficult. ([11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/))

A 2023 state-of-the-art narrative review concluded that chronic low back pain is associated with heterogeneous back-muscle changes rather than one universal dysfunction. Some people show atrophy, fat infiltration, altered activation, reduced endurance, or changed motor control; others do not show the same pattern. Deep and superficial regions may also behave differently. ([14](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm12175510))

A 2025-published systematic review and meta-analysis, appearing in the 2026 volume of the _European Spine Journal_, found that multifidus showed the most consistent group-level differences in chronic low back pain, including reduced total or functional cross-sectional area and greater fat infiltration at some lower lumbar levels. However, the review also found high heterogeneity, and most underlying evidence was observational. These findings cannot determine whether muscle change preceded pain, followed pain, reflected deconditioning, or varied with age and activity. ([15](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00586-025-09454-z))

Multifidus morphology can therefore contribute to a clinical hypothesis. It is not an imaging verdict and does not prove that a person lacks spinal stability. ([11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/); [14](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm12175510); [15](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00586-025-09454-z))

## What athlete-specific evidence shows

Evidence from general chronic-low-back-pain populations does not automatically transfer to trained athletes. Athlete studies involve different activity profiles, sport-specific loading, possible asymmetry, and heterogeneous relationships among morphology and symptoms. ([16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340))

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis examined lumbar multifidus morphology and composition in athletes with and without chronic nonspecific low back pain. Eighteen studies were included, but the article reports conflicting participant totals: 1,142 in the abstract and 1,065 in the main study-characteristics section. The pooled analyses did not detect statistically significant group differences in multifidus cross-sectional area, thickness, or echo intensity. The GRADE certainty was low to very low, and studies varied in sport, pain definition, imaging method, and spinal level. ([16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340))

This does not show that multifidus is irrelevant to athletes. It shows that a simple morphology story, in which smaller or poorer-quality multifidus equals chronic back pain, was not supported consistently across the available athlete data. It does not establish that the groups were morphologically equivalent, because null findings may reflect genuine similarity, heterogeneity, measurement limitations, or imprecision. ([16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340))

The athlete-specific gap is important: the evidence cited here does not establish that lifters or other athletes with back pain must complete isolated local-muscle retraining before global strengthening or return-to-sport loading. That is an absence of support for a universal prerequisite, not proof that sequencing never matters for an individual athlete. ([16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## Hollowing and bracing are different strategies

**Abdominal hollowing** usually means drawing the lower abdominal wall inward with relatively low superficial-muscle activity, often with the aim of selectively recruiting TrA and deeper abdominal structures. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

**Abdominal bracing** means increasing abdominal-wall and trunk-muscle co-contraction without deliberately drawing the abdomen inward. The aim is generally greater whole-trunk stiffness rather than selective activation. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

These are not competing moral philosophies. They are different motor strategies with different mechanical effects. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

Grenier and McGill compared hollowing and bracing in eight healthy men using electromyography and a lumbar stability model. In an idealized model simulation embedded in the study, a brace increased the model’s stability index by approximately 32% and compression by approximately 15% relative to the modeled comparison condition. Selective TrA activation produced a much smaller modeled stability effect than broad co-contraction. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

That study supports a mechanism claim: under its assumptions, broad co-contraction produced a larger modeled stability effect than isolated hollowing, at the cost of greater modeled compression. It does not prove that bracing treats pain better, that everyone should brace maximally, or that hollowing has no instructional value. The sample was small, healthy, and male, and the outcome was modeled mechanical stability rather than pain, function, injury risk, or lifting performance. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

Hollowing can be used as a low-load motor-learning task within some motor-control programs, including for awareness and selective recruitment. Its clinical value should be judged by the person’s goals and response rather than inferred from the modeled mechanism alone. Bracing is mechanically more relevant when a task requires greater trunk stiffness, but the appropriate level should be scaled to the task. Neither strategy should be treated as a universal posture. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## More stiffness is not always better

Co-contraction can increase spinal stiffness because opposing muscles resist displacement in multiple directions. Gardner-Morse and Stokes demonstrated this mechanically in a lumbar model, and later work has repeatedly shown that trunk-muscle coordination can increase stability. ([5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

But muscle force also produces compression and consumes energy. Greater co-contraction can raise modeled spinal loading and muscular demand; if sustained, it may accelerate fatigue and restrict useful movement. The optimal strategy is not maximum stiffness; it is **sufficient stiffness for the task**. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

Higher-demand tasks can require greater trunk stiffness for a brief period, whereas low-demand activity generally requires less. A capable system should be able to increase stiffness, direct it according to the load, and reduce it when the demand passes. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

From a clinical perspective, the ability to **relax** is therefore part of trunk control. Persistent bracing may reflect a tight-control strategy in some people, but that interpretation should be tested against the task, symptoms, and function rather than assumed from bracing alone. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

## Tight control and loose control

Low-back-pain research does not reveal one universal motor-control pattern. Some people move less, co-contract more, breathe rigidly, and increase trunk stiffness. Others show reduced control, delayed responses, excessive or poorly distributed movement, or difficulty generating sufficient force. Many people change strategy according to the task. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

Van Dieën and colleagues described these possibilities as a conceptual spectrum between **tight control** and **loose control**. Tight-control strategies emphasize stiffness, co-contraction, and reduced movement. Loose-control strategies involve less stiffness or less precise control. Either can be useful in a particular situation, and either can become costly when poorly matched to the task. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

The authors also emphasized that motor-control findings in low back pain are divergent and that clinically valid methods for assigning people to these proposed phenotypes remain limited. The tight-versus-loose model is therefore a reasoning aid, not a validated diagnosis. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

This moves the discussion beyond “local muscles weak, global muscles tight.” Assessment may identify a need for greater trunk-force capacity, less persistent bracing, different movement options, or graded exposure to a feared task. These are hypotheses to test against function and response, not diagnoses inferred from one activation pattern. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## Pain changes motor control

Pain can change muscle recruitment even when no pre-existing motor deficit has been established. Experimental pain research shows changes in timing, amplitude, co-contraction, and task strategy, but the direction of those changes is variable rather than uniform. ([13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

That variability makes biological sense. A nervous system responding to pain may stiffen a region, redistribute force, avoid a direction, slow movement, or recruit alternative muscles. These strategies can be protective in the short term. If a protective strategy persists beyond the demand that elicited it, it may increase fatigue, tissue loading, or movement restriction, but that progression cannot be inferred from one activation test. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

Modern pain science is the necessary guardrail. Pain is a sensory and emotional experience associated with, or resembling that associated with, actual or potential tissue damage. Pain and nociception are not identical, and pain is influenced by biological, psychological, and social factors. An altered muscle strategy can be relevant without being the complete explanation for a person’s symptoms. ([21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7680716/))

## Motor-control exercise: what the clinical evidence shows

**Motor-control exercise**, or MCE, usually involves assessing control during specific movements, teaching lower-load activation or coordination, and progressively integrating that control into functional tasks. Some programs emphasize TrA and multifidus; others use broader movement-control approaches. The category is therefore more varied than the phrase “local muscle training” suggests. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

A 2016 Cochrane review included 29 trials with 2,431 participants who had chronic nonspecific low back pain. Motor-control exercise was more effective than minimal intervention for several pain and disability outcomes, but there was little or no clinically important difference when it was compared with other forms of exercise or manual therapy. The authors concluded that exercise choice could reasonably reflect patient preference, goals, therapist expertise, cost, and safety rather than presumed superiority of MCE. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis included 18 studies involving 1,356 participants, with 13 studies entering the meta-analysis. Compared with inactive control, placebo, or minimal intervention, motor-control exercise reduced pain immediately after intervention. Compared with other exercise, it produced small benefits for some postintervention outcomes, although the authors advised caution in interpreting the reductions. Variation in programs, comparators, and participants still limits claims that one specific muscle-recruitment method is uniquely effective. ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37432388/))

The evidence-supported conclusion is not that motor-control exercise fails. Compared with minimal intervention, it can improve some pain and functional outcomes; compared with other exercise or manual therapy, average differences are generally small or absent. It is one reasonable exercise approach among several, not proof that the local system must be repaired before other training can begin. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37432388/))

## General exercise remains important

The broader exercise literature makes the same point. A 2021 Cochrane review included 249 trials and 24,486 participants with chronic low back pain. Compared with no treatment, usual care, or placebo, exercise improved pain by roughly 15 points on a 0-100 scale and disability by roughly 7 points. The pain point estimate of 15.2 points just met the review’s prespecified 15-point threshold for clinical importance, although the confidence interval included effects below that threshold; the disability difference did not meet the prespecified 10-point threshold. Compared with other conservative treatments, average differences were generally small and often not clinically important. The evidence extracted for the review was current to April 2018. ([18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2))

That review included many exercise categories rather than one privileged modality. Strengthening, endurance work, aerobic exercise, movement-control exercise, Pilates-type approaches, and mixed programs can all be reasonable depending on the person and context. No single exercise form has demonstrated universal superiority across chronic nonspecific low-back-pain populations. ([18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2))

For lifters and athletes, this is especially important. The practical goal should extend beyond reproducing a low-load abdominal contraction on a treatment table toward progressively tolerating the force, speed, range, fatigue, and coordination demands of the actual sport. ([18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## What the clinical guideline actually recommends

The 2021 JOSPT clinical practice guideline recommends specific trunk activation and movement-control exercise for people with chronic low back pain **when movement-control impairment is part of the presentation**. That subgroup wording matters. The guideline does not recommend the same local-muscle intervention for every person with back pain. ([19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

The same guideline also recommends or supports several other exercise categories for chronic low back pain, including trunk strengthening and endurance exercise, multimodal exercise, aerobic exercise, aquatic exercise, and general exercise training. Specific activation is one option within a broader evidence-based treatment landscape. ([19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

The guideline therefore supports a targeted principle rather than a local-first ideology. The recommendation does not prove a causal local-muscle deficit or validate a perfectly reliable diagnostic subgroup:

> **Use movement-control exercise when the assessment identifies a relevant movement-control problem.** ([19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## What a local-muscle exercise can and cannot prove

An exercise that increases TrA or multifidus activity does not prove that those muscles caused the pain. It demonstrates that the exercise recruited the measured tissue under the tested condition. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

An exercise that reduces symptoms immediately does not prove that it “realigned” the spine or restored segmental stability. An immediate response does not identify a unique mechanism. Altered loading, motor behavior, and contextual or pain-related processes are possible explanations, but each remains a hypothesis unless independently tested. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7680716/))

An exercise that feels difficult does not necessarily identify weakness in one named muscle. Difficulty is not specific to one cause and may reflect coordination demands, breath holding, effort, pain, fear, leverage, or fatigue. Those possibilities should be tested rather than assumed. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

The clinical value of an exercise lies in what it helps the person do next, not in how convincingly it supports a story about one muscle.

## The assessment implication

A modern local-versus-global assessment should not begin by asking which muscle is “off.” It should begin by defining the task. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

During what movement does the person lose tolerance? At what load, range, speed, or fatigue level? Is the strategy excessively rigid, insufficiently controlled, or simply unfamiliar? Does changing breathing, stance, range, tempo, or load change the symptoms? Those questions are more actionable than attempting to assign a static hierarchy to individual muscles. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

Research on anticipatory TrA activity used electromyography and carefully timed perturbation tasks. A clinician cannot infer a millisecond-scale onset delay from standing posture, abdominal shape, or a verbal statement that the core “looks inactive.” The measured phenomenon and the clinical observation are not the same thing. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

Likewise, a multifidus image or ultrasound measure should be interpreted according to spinal level, region, measurement definition, sport, age, symptoms, and training history. Group-level morphology findings do not identify an individual pain generator or prescribe one specific exercise. ([11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7236939/); [15](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00586-025-09454-z); [16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340))

A practical, system-level assessment can ask whether the person can:

- Produce low-level trunk tension without unnecessary breath holding.

- Increase stiffness when load or speed requires it.

- Maintain breathing and pressure regulation under submaximal demand.

- Move the hips and thorax while controlling the lumbar region appropriately for the task.

- Tolerate spinal motion when motion is required.

- Relax after the demand has passed.

These are system-level capabilities rather than tests of one master muscle. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

## The programming implication

### Selective activation is a tool, not an entrance exam

Low-load trunk-control drills, which may include TrA or multifidus activation and coordinated breathing, may be selected for awareness or coordination practice, or as a low-load way to reintroduce trunk loading. Compared with minimal intervention, motor-control exercise can improve some outcomes in chronic nonspecific low back pain; compared with other exercise or manual therapy, average outcomes are generally similar. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304); [20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37432388/))

The evidence reviewed here does not establish successful isolation as a universal prerequisite for every squat, hinge, carry, run, or return-to-sport progression. It does not establish that everyone must master selective TrA contraction before beginning global strengthening. Other exercise approaches produce similar average outcomes in many chronic-low-back-pain comparisons. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2))

Use selective activation when it solves a real problem. Do not turn it into a ritual.

### Build whole-trunk capacity

The trunk must produce and resist flexion, extension, lateral flexion, and rotation. It must also transfer force between the thorax, pelvis, arms, and legs. That requires local muscles, global muscles, pressure regulation, passive tissues, and neural coordination working together. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

In practice, programming may therefore progress beyond low-load activation toward endurance, force production, loaded carries, hinges, squats, pushes, pulls, rotation or anti-rotation tasks, and the movements required by the person’s sport or occupation. The exact progression depends on symptoms, goals, training age, and tissue tolerance rather than on a universal local-to-global sequence. ([18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

### Scale the brace to the task

A higher brace may be useful for some brief, high-demand efforts. The same strategy may be inefficient for low-demand activity and may reinforce an unnecessarily rigid pattern in someone already using tight control. Mechanistic studies show that co-contraction can increase stiffness while also increasing compression and muscular demand, while the tight-control framework describes how a high-stiffness strategy may become poorly matched to some tasks. ([5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

The training goal is not one permanent abdominal contraction. It is the ability to choose from a range:

- Low tension for quiet movement.

- Moderate tension for controlled submaximal work.

- High tension for heavy or rapid force transfer.

- Timely relaxation once the demand ends.

That is motor adaptability. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

### Train movement as well as resistance to movement

A broadly capable program may include anti-extension, anti-rotation, and anti-lateral-flexion exercises. It may also include controlled flexion, extension, rotation, and side-bending when those motions are appropriate and progressively tolerated. Stability does not require eliminating spinal movement; it requires controlling the amount, timing, and load of movement. ([2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

A program that teaches only rigidity may improve resistance to motion without fully preparing the person for tasks that require controlled movement through variable ranges. Programming should therefore reflect the actual demands of daily life, work, or sport. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## What the framework is not

The local-versus-global framework is **not** deep versus superficial. Multifidus itself has regions with different mechanical and activation behavior, while large superficial muscles contribute substantially to stiffness. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/))

It is **not** stabilizers versus movers. The same muscle force can create movement in one context and resist movement in another. Local muscles can produce torque; global muscles can stabilize. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

It is **not** healthy versus dysfunctional. Global-muscle activity is not inherently compensation, and local-muscle selectivity is not inherently superior. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

It is **not** a pain diagnosis. Muscle activation, timing, morphology, strength, and pain can be related, but pain cannot be reduced to the status of one muscle system. ([13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819); [21](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7680716/))

It is **not** a mandatory exercise order. Compared with minimal intervention, motor-control exercise can improve some outcomes, but it generally produces similar average outcomes to other exercise or manual therapy. Current reviews and guidelines support multiple exercise approaches. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [18](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009790.pub2); [19](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2021.0304))

## Working vocabulary for Tier 2

The **local muscle system** is Bergmark’s mechanical category for muscles or muscle portions acting relatively directly on lumbar vertebrae and regional spinal curvature. It is not synonymous with deep, small, tonic, or healthy. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

The **global muscle system** is the category for muscles and force mechanisms acting over a larger trunk-pelvis distance to balance external loads and control thorax-pelvis orientation. It is not synonymous with superficial, phasic, compensatory, or dysfunctional. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

**Segmental control** means managing motion and force relationships among individual spinal motion segments rather than controlling only the trunk as one rigid block. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/))

**Stiffness** is resistance to deformation or displacement under load. Muscle co-contraction can increase stiffness, but stiffness is not automatically equivalent to good clinical function. ([5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

**Stability** must be defined within the model being used. In Bergmark’s engineering model, it referred to maintaining equilibrium under small perturbations; in broader clinical reasoning, controlled behavior also depends on passive tissues, active muscle forces, and neural control rather than one muscle. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))

**Co-contraction** means simultaneous activation of muscles with opposing or differently directed actions. It can increase multidirectional stiffness while also increasing compression and energy cost. ([5](https://doi.org/10.1097/00007632-199801010-00019))

**Abdominal hollowing** is a low-load drawing-in strategy commonly used to bias deeper abdominal recruitment. It is a motor-learning strategy, not proof that the spine was previously unstable. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

**Abdominal bracing** is broader abdominal and trunk co-contraction used to increase whole-trunk stiffness. In the cited model, it produced a larger stability-index effect than isolated hollowing but also raised muscular effort and compression. ([6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014))

An **anticipatory postural adjustment** is muscle activity occurring before a predictable disturbance, such as rapid arm movement. Its timing depends on the task and should not be treated as a universal measure of spinal health. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [9](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gaitpost.2014.11.006))

**Motor-control exercise** is exercise designed to improve the timing, coordination, or control of trunk and movement strategies, often progressing from lower-load tasks to functional activity. Compared with minimal intervention, it can improve some outcomes, but it is not consistently superior to other exercise or manual therapy. ([17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004); [20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37432388/))

**Tight control** and **loose control** are conceptual descriptions of relatively rigid/high-stiffness and relatively low-stiffness or less constrained strategies. They are not yet validated diagnostic subtypes. ([12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917))

## The 37-year verdict

Bergmark’s framework remains useful because it highlights two real mechanical problems: the body must balance large external moments, and it must manage the shape and behavior of the lumbar column within those moments. Global force transfer and local segmental control are both useful concepts. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

What has not aged well is the clinical hierarchy built around the model. Modern evidence does not support the idea that deep muscles are the only true stabilizers, that superficial muscles are mere compensators, that TrA or multifidus acts as a master switch, or that isolated local activation must precede all meaningful loading. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [8](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humov.2012.12.011); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

The modern view is distributed and task-specific. Trunk control reflects muscles, pressure-related forces, passive tissues, sensory feedback, and motor planning working together. Different tasks require different mixtures of stiffness and movement. Pain can alter those strategies, but no single altered muscle pattern explains every case. ([4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9); [12](https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2019.7917); [13](https://doi.org/10.1097/j.pain.0000000000002819))

The practical rule is:

> **Train coordination, capacity, and adaptability, not a muscle hierarchy.**

## The takeaway

Bergmark’s local-versus-global framework was a mechanical model, not a rehabilitation commandment. Local muscles act more directly on lumbar vertebrae and regional curvature. Global muscles balance larger external moments and transfer force between the thorax and pelvis. The boundary is not perfectly clean, and many muscles contribute to both movement and stability. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1050-6411%2803%2900043-9))

Transversus abdominis and multifidus matter, but neither is a universal master stabilizer. Delayed activation in one task does not mean a muscle has switched off. Morphological differences do not automatically explain pain. In the cited model, bracing produced a larger stability-index effect than hollowing, but it also increased modeled compression. Compared with minimal intervention, motor-control exercise can improve some outcomes in chronic nonspecific low back pain, but average differences are generally small or absent when it is compared with other exercise or manual therapy. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/); [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2006.10.014); [7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8961451/); [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11805677/); [15](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00586-025-09454-z); [16](https://doi.org/10.26603/001c.162340); [17](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012004))

Thirty-seven years later, Bergmark’s most durable insight is not that some muscles are more important than others. It is that the spine must solve force problems at more than one scale. ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636))

**Global muscles manage the larger load. Local muscles help manage the segments inside it. The nervous system coordinates both.** ([1](https://actaorthop.org/actao/article/view/22636); [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1490034/); [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832259/))
