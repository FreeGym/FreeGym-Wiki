# How Do Hysterectomy and Ovary Removal (Surgical Menopause) Affect Muscle Gain and Recovery?

For training-relevant endocrine questions, the main distinction is usually not "hysterectomy" versus "no hysterectomy." It is whether the ovaries are removed. The surgery itself can still matter for training through healing time, pain, activity restriction, pelvic-floor rehabilitation needs, deconditioning, and the underlying reason for surgery. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know); [NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/))

A hysterectomy removes the uterus; depending on the operation, the cervix, fallopian tubes, and ovaries may or may not also be removed. If both ovaries are left in place, ovarian hormone production can continue, and surgical menopause does not necessarily occur at the time of hysterectomy. However, ovary-conserving hysterectomy has been associated with earlier ovarian failure in some prospective studies and meta-analyses. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [Moorman et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22067716/); [Huang et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36759829/))

Bilateral oophorectomy removes both ovaries. In a premenopausal woman, this causes surgical menopause: an abrupt loss of ovarian estrogen and progesterone production and a reduction in ovarian androgen contribution. This is biologically different from natural menopause, where ovarian function declines over years rather than overnight. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [Kotsopoulos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25523946/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/))

## What happens hormonally

Removing the uterus alone does not directly remove ovarian hormone production if the ovaries are conserved. In a prospective cohort of women aged 30 to 47, hysterectomy with ovarian preservation was associated with nearly double the risk of ovarian failure compared with women with intact uteri, but this was a risk over time rather than immediate menopause. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [Moorman et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22067716/))

Removing both ovaries before natural menopause causes an abrupt estrogen drop. This matters for bone, vasomotor symptoms, sleep symptoms, genitourinary symptoms, and possibly muscle/connective tissue biology, but the strength of evidence differs by outcome. ([The North American Menopause Society, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35797481/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/))

Bilateral oophorectomy also lowers testosterone. In the Rancho Bernardo Study, postmenopausal women with hysterectomy plus bilateral oophorectomy had total and bioavailable testosterone levels more than 40 percent lower than women with intact ovaries after adjustment for age and BMI. In the Nurses' Health Study, bilateral oophorectomy was associated with about 25 percent lower testosterone than natural menopause. ([Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [Kotsopoulos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25523946/))

## Direct evidence on lean mass after oophorectomy

The direct muscle evidence after bilateral oophorectomy is limited.

A cross-sectional NHANES analysis of 4,209 women found that hysterectomy with oophorectomy was associated with higher fat mass and lower lean mass compared with no surgery, and the association was stronger when surgery occurred at younger ages. This supports concern, but because it was observational and cross-sectional, it cannot cleanly separate surgery, hormones, indication for surgery, age, activity, body weight, medication use, and deconditioning. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/))

A more directly relevant prospective WHAM study followed premenopausal women undergoing risk-reducing bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy and compared them with women who retained their ovaries. At 24 months, there was no difference in body weight and no broad body-composition difference, although the surgery group gained slightly more abdominal visceral adipose tissue. In this observational analysis, hormone therapy use was not associated with different weight or body-composition outcomes, but hormone therapy use was not randomized. ([Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/))

Practical interpretation: surgical menopause may worsen body-composition trajectory in some women, especially when it occurs young, but the available evidence is mixed. The available studies do not show that bilateral oophorectomy reliably causes large lean-mass loss or makes hypertrophy impossible. A more cautious conclusion is that surgically menopausal women are under-studied for strength, hypertrophy, and recovery endpoints. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/))

## Surgical menopause versus natural menopause

Natural menopause is associated with unfavorable body-composition changes, especially around the final menstrual period. In SWAN, the rate of fat gain increased and lean mass declined during the menopause transition, with changes continuing until roughly two years after the final menstrual period. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6483504/))

Surgical menopause differs because the ovarian hormone change is abrupt. That abruptness can make symptoms sudden and can coincide with a post-operative interruption in training, especially when oophorectomy occurs before the usual age of menopause. However, the claim that surgical menopause causes a larger direct muscle-gain deficit than natural menopause is not established by surgical-menopause-specific resistance-training trials or muscle-protein-synthesis studies. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know); [Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/); [Javed et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31461147/))

A prospective BMI analysis did find greater BMI increases after hysterectomy with bilateral oophorectomy than after natural menopause, but BMI is not a muscle-specific endpoint and does not distinguish fat from lean mass. ([Gibson et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23007036/))

## Muscle gain with resistance training

Resistance training remains effective after menopause. A meta-analysis of resistance-training studies in postmenopausal and older women found significant increases in lean body mass, although the evidence quality was graded as moderate and the average intervention was relatively short. ([Thomas et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595144/))

A larger 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis found that exercise training improves body composition in postmenopausal women, with resistance or combined training having stronger effects on muscle-related outcomes than aerobic training alone. ([Khalafi et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10306117/))

There is a major evidence gap. Most resistance-training evidence is in naturally postmenopausal women, older women, or mixed menopause-status samples, and there are not enough surgical-menopause-specific RCTs measuring hypertrophy, strength, muscle-protein synthesis, soreness, tendon adaptation, recovery markers, and training progression after bilateral oophorectomy. ([Thomas et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595144/); [Khalafi et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10306117/); [Dam et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33542694/))

## Recovery and training tolerance

Training problems after oophorectomy may be indirect rather than direct muscle effects: surgical downtime and activity restriction, pain, pelvic-floor rehabilitation needs, vasomotor symptoms, sleep disturbance, symptom-related fatigue, reduced training volume, and deconditioning. These are practical pathways rather than proof of impaired muscle adaptation. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know); [NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/))

In WHAM, hot flushes increased from 5.3 percent before risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy to 56.2 percent at three months, and night sweats increased from 20.2 percent to 47.2 percent. Hormone therapy reduced but did not eliminate symptoms; 40 percent of hormone-therapy users and 80 percent of non-users reported vasomotor symptoms after surgery. ([Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/))

A related WHAM sleep analysis found that overall sleep quality was not affected by risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy, but new-onset sleep disturbance was common, particularly in women with severe vasomotor symptoms. ([Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/))

That matters for training because symptoms, sleep disturbance, and activity restriction can reduce training quality even if muscle tissue can still adapt. The direct evidence that surgical menopause independently impairs "recovery biology" in trained women is weak; the stronger evidence is that symptoms after abrupt estrogen withdrawal and post-operative recovery needs can make it harder to train consistently and recover subjectively. ([Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

## Connective tissue and tendons

Estrogen plausibly affects tendon and connective tissue through collagen turnover, tendon structure, and tissue stiffness, but the human evidence is not clean enough to say that surgical menopause predictably causes tendon injury or poor tendon adaptation. ([Ganderton et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27282453/))

A systematic review of female sex hormone supplementation and tendon outcomes concluded that the evidence was low-level, contradictory, and inconsistent. It did not establish a clear positive or negative effect of estrogen supplementation on molecular, mechanical, or morphological tendon outcomes. ([Ganderton et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27282453/))

Practical interpretation: after abrupt menopause, it is reasonable to be conservative with tendon loading progression, especially when returning after surgical downtime and activity restriction. This is a cautious training inference, not a proven oophorectomy-specific tendon outcome. The claim that ovary removal directly "wrecks tendons" is stronger than the available evidence. ([Ganderton et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27282453/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

## Estrogen therapy and muscle

Estrogen therapy should not be framed primarily as a muscle-building drug.

A JAMA Network Open systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 randomized clinical trials including 4,474 postmenopausal women found no statistically significant beneficial or detrimental association of hormone therapy with lean body mass. The pooled difference was small: hormone-therapy users lost 0.06 kg less lean body mass than controls, with low certainty evidence. ([Javed et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31461147/))

A separate randomized trial in early postmenopausal women found that transdermal estrogen enhanced skeletal muscle mass gains during 12 weeks of resistance training, but strength and functional outcomes improved with training without clear between-group superiority across all performance measures. This is interesting, but it is small and not specific to surgical menopause. ([Dam et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33542694/))

For women with premature or early menopause, including many women who undergo bilateral oophorectomy before the usual age of menopause, the 2022 North American Menopause Society statement frames hormone therapy around symptom control, individualized risk assessment, and long-term health risks such as bone loss rather than around hypertrophy optimization. The statement identifies hormone therapy as the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and genitourinary syndrome of menopause and as a therapy that prevents bone loss and fracture. ([The North American Menopause Society, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35797481/))

## Testosterone and muscle

The androgen story is biologically plausible but clinically unsettled. ([Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [Kotsopoulos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25523946/); [Davis et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31474158/))

Ovaries continue to contribute to circulating androgens after menopause, and bilateral oophorectomy is associated with lower testosterone than natural menopause or ovarian conservation. This may be clinically relevant in carefully assessed sexual-function complaints, especially hypoactive sexual desire disorder, but low testosterone numbers do not automatically prove that testosterone treatment will improve muscle gain or recovery. Evidence is much weaker for using testosterone levels or testosterone therapy to explain or treat fatigue, mood, body composition, muscle gain, or recovery. ([Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [Kotsopoulos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25523946/); [Davis et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31474158/))

The Global Consensus Position Statement on testosterone therapy for women states that the only evidence-based indication for testosterone therapy in women is hypoactive sexual desire disorder, and that physiologic-dose testosterone has not shown statistically significant effects on lean body mass, total body fat, or muscle strength. ([Davis et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31474158/))

So testosterone may be clinically relevant for carefully selected sexual-function indications, but the evidence does not support using it as a general muscle-gain or recovery intervention in women after oophorectomy.

## Common misattributions

Uterus-only hysterectomy, bilateral oophorectomy, natural menopause, and post-operative deconditioning are different mechanisms.

A woman who had a hysterectomy with ovaries retained may struggle because of surgical recovery, pain, pelvic-floor rehabilitation needs, loss of conditioning, or sleep disruption, but she did not necessarily enter surgical menopause at the time of surgery. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know); [NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Moorman et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22067716/); [Huang et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36759829/))

A woman who had both ovaries removed before natural menopause may face abrupt vasomotor symptoms, sleep disturbance, estrogen loss, androgen reduction, and a sudden interruption in training because of surgery and recovery restrictions. Those are not the same as ordinary aging or a temporary post-op layoff. ([NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

A woman who gains fat or loses strength after surgery may be experiencing reduced training volume, lower protein intake, poorer sleep, pain-limited movement, reduced daily activity, or body-composition changes associated with surgery and menopause status. Those explanations should be checked before assuming the ovaries directly caused permanent muscle resistance. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/); [Nunes et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35187864/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

## Practical interpretation

Bilateral oophorectomy before natural menopause can make training harder, mainly because the hormonal change is abrupt and symptoms, sleep disturbance, recovery restrictions, and deconditioning can interfere with energy, consistency, and subjective recovery. ([NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [The North American Menopause Society, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35797481/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

The direct evidence that surgical menopause causes a large, unavoidable impairment in muscle gain is weak. One cross-sectional study suggests lower lean mass after hysterectomy with oophorectomy, especially at younger ages, but a prospective 24-month RRSO study did not find broad lean-mass or body-composition deterioration beyond a small increase in visceral adipose tissue. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/))

Resistance training still works after menopause. The most defensible training strategy is progressive resistance training, enough total protein, sleep protection, gradual reloading after surgery, pelvic-floor-aware exercise progression, and individualized medical management of menopausal symptoms. ([Thomas et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595144/); [Khalafi et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10306117/); [Nunes et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35187864/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know); [NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [The North American Menopause Society, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35797481/))

Hormone therapy may be very important for symptom control, bone health, and quality of life in appropriate candidates, especially after early surgical menopause, but it should not be sold as a guaranteed muscle-building solution. ([Javed et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31461147/); [The North American Menopause Society, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35797481/))

## What evidence is still missing

The biggest gap is not whether estrogen, testosterone, and menopause biology matter. They do. The gap is surgical-menopause-specific training evidence. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/); [Thomas et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595144/); [Khalafi et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10306117/))

The most useful studies would be resistance-training RCTs in surgically menopausal women, with comparison groups for natural menopause, ovary-conserving hysterectomy, and premenopausal controls. Outcomes should include lean mass, appendicular lean mass, muscle cross-sectional area, strength, power, muscle-protein synthesis, soreness, tendon pain, collagen-turnover markers, sleep, vasomotor symptoms, pelvic-floor symptoms, protein intake, energy intake, training volume, hormone-therapy use, testosterone exposure, age at surgery, and time since surgery.

## Bottom line

Ovary-conserving hysterectomy does not equal surgical menopause, although ovarian function may decline earlier in some women. ([American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/hysterectomy); [Moorman et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22067716/); [Huang et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36759829/))

Bilateral oophorectomy before natural menopause creates an abrupt endocrine change that can make training and recovery feel harder, especially through symptoms, sleep disturbance, fatigue, pain, interrupted training, and post-surgical deconditioning. ([NHS, Hysterectomy recovery](https://www.nhs.uk/tests-and-treatments/hysterectomy/recovery/); [Hickey et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312002/); [Hickey et al., 2021 sleep](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34116835/); [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Recovery After Hysterectomy](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/experts-and-stories/the-latest/recovery-after-hysterectomy-what-you-need-to-know))

The evidence does not show that muscle gain becomes impossible or that the direct muscle effect is usually large. Resistance training remains effective, and the most evidence-based approach is to rebuild progressively while treating the modifiable barriers: symptoms, sleep, under-fueling, pain, pelvic-floor issues, and post-surgical deconditioning. ([Karia et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33879451/); [Price et al., 2023/2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37410931/); [Thomas et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8595144/); [Khalafi et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10306117/); [Nunes et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35187864/))

Testosterone loss is real, but testosterone therapy is not well supported as a muscle or recovery treatment in women; the strongest evidence-based indication remains hypoactive sexual desire disorder. ([Laughlin et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10690870/); [Kotsopoulos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25523946/); [Davis et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31474158/))
