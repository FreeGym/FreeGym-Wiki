# Should Women Train Differently During Perimenopause?

Current evidence does not support replacing standard training principles with a completely different training system during perimenopause. The better-supported practical answer is that the basics become less negotiable: progressive resistance training, adequate protein intake, aerobic conditioning, sleep protection, and flexible recovery management. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [González-Gálvez et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38353251/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/); [Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/); [Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf))

The direct perimenopause-specific trial evidence is still limited. A 2025 systematic review of randomized trials on lifestyle interventions for perimenopausal symptoms found that exercise and health education may help, but the studies were heterogeneous and methodologically inconsistent enough that firm exercise-prescription rules remain difficult. The same review found no diet-only trials in perimenopausal women. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/))

## The strongest practical answer

Women in perimenopause probably do not need "menopause workouts" that replace normal training principles. Current evidence more strongly supports applying the same principles with more attention to muscle, bone-related outcomes, power, sleep, symptom variability, and cardiometabolic health. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [Greendale et al., 2019](https://insight.jci.org/articles/view/124865); [Shieh et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36542065/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/); [Choudhary & Bansal, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40694785/); [Reid & Fielding, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22016147/))

A useful summary is:

Resistance training becomes more important, but not because strength training suddenly works differently. It becomes more important because the menopause transition is a period when lean mass, fat distribution, bone-related outcomes, and the sleep/recovery context can shift. Observational longitudinal SWAN data found that fat gain accelerated and lean mass began declining around the menopause transition, while overall weight gain did not show the same menopause-specific acceleration. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://insight.jci.org/articles/view/124865); [Shieh et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36542065/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/))

## Body composition: scale weight is not the whole story

The common claim that "menopause causes weight gain" is partly oversimplified. Longitudinal evidence suggests that midlife weight gain is strongly related to chronological aging and lifestyle, while menopause-related hormonal changes appear more clearly linked to body composition and fat distribution. A review of midlife body-size evidence concluded that weight change is more attributable to aging, whereas body composition and fat distribution are more attributable to ovarian aging. ([Karvonen-Gutierrez & Kim, 2016](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9032/4/3/42))

In SWAN, fat gain doubled and lean mass declined starting around the final menstrual period window, but weight itself rose more linearly before menopause and flattened after the transition. This means a woman can experience a meaningful body-composition shift even when the scale does not tell the full story. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://insight.jci.org/articles/view/124865))

This matters for training because the goal is not only "weight control." A more evidence-aligned goal is preserving muscle, maintaining bone-loading capacity, limiting central or visceral-fat gain, and protecting function. SWAN follow-up also found that menopause-transition lean-mass loss and fat-mass gain were associated with later bone and fracture outcomes, even though fat gain and lean loss had different relationships with BMD. ([Shieh et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36542065/); [Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf))

## Resistance training: more emphasis, not a new rulebook

The case for resistance training is strong, but most evidence is from postmenopausal or mixed midlife samples rather than perimenopause-only trials. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials in healthy postmenopausal women found that resistance training improved lower- and upper-extremity strength and cardiorespiratory fitness outcomes, while effects on BMD and anthropometric outcomes were more debated. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [González-Gálvez et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38353251/))

A 2025 randomized trial is especially relevant because it included pre-, peri-, and postmenopausal women. Seventy healthy, moderately active women were randomized to usual activity or a 12-week supervised low-impact resistance program performed four days per week. The program improved hip strength, balance, flexibility, vastus intermedius muscle thickness, and lean mass, and the response did not differ by menopause status. ([Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

That finding argues against the idea that perimenopausal women need a fundamentally different training stimulus to adapt, at least in the context of a short-term, supervised, low-impact resistance-training program. It supports the simpler idea that progressive, well-designed resistance training still works across the menopause transition. ([Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

## Training volume: enough stimulus, but recoverable

Higher-volume resistance training may produce somewhat greater hypertrophy in postmenopausal and older women, but the evidence does not mean "more is always better." A 2024 secondary analysis of randomized trials found that higher-volume resistance training produced greater whole-body lean-mass gains than lower-volume resistance training in postmenopausal and older women. The authors reported no specific grant funding and no known competing financial interests. ([Nunes et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S016749432400150X))

The practical interpretation, cautiously extrapolated to perimenopause, is that women should not underdose strength training, but the dose has to be recoverable. For many women, that means a structured weekly pattern, commonly two to four weekly lifting sessions, with progression over time, rather than sporadic light exercise or constant high-intensity work. Adult physical-activity guidance supports muscle-strengthening activity on at least two days per week, and the mixed-menopause-status trial used four supervised resistance sessions per week. ([Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

## Power and balance: useful, but evidence is more indirect

Power training is reasonable as an option during perimenopause, but the direct perimenopause-specific evidence is not strong enough to say that every woman needs a radically different "power-first" program.

The rationale is partly indirect: muscle power declines earlier and more steeply than strength with aging and is linked with physical functioning in older adults. In the 2025 pre-, peri-, and postmenopause resistance-training trial, balance and strength improved across menopause groups. ([Reid & Fielding, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22016147/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

A practical version is to add small doses of power work after a strength base is in place: controlled jumps or hops if joints tolerate them, fast-but-safe concentric lifting, step-ups, medicine-ball throws, kettlebell deadlifts or swings when appropriate, loaded carries, and balance-challenging strength work. This should be individualized for joint pain, pelvic-floor symptoms, injury history, and training age rather than treated as a universal perimenopause rule. ([Reid & Fielding, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22016147/); [Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf))

## HIIT: useful tool, not mandatory medicine

High-intensity interval training does not become automatically "bad" in perimenopause. But it may become harder to recover from when sleep is poor, bleeding is heavy, vasomotor symptoms are disruptive, or life stress is high, so the decision to use it should be based on recovery and symptoms rather than menopause status alone. ([Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/); [Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/))

In obese postmenopausal women, a small randomized trial found that 12 weeks of HIIT reduced visceral adipose tissue more than combined moderate walking plus resistance training, with both approaches improving several body-composition and inflammatory outcomes. ([Nunes et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30277921/))

However, a 2020 meta-analysis of HIIT in women found that body-composition effects were more evident before menopause than after menopause, and the authors concluded that more studies are needed in postmenopausal women. ([Dupuit et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32613697/))

So the evidence does not support forcing HIIT as a menopause-specific requirement. A better practical rule is to use it when it is recoverable and useful: one to two sessions per week may suit some women, while less or none may be appropriate during poor-sleep or high-symptom periods. This is a programming interpretation, not a perimenopause-specific trial rule. ([Nunes et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30277921/); [Dupuit et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32613697/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full))

## Protein: important, but not magic without training

Protein matters, especially when the goal is preserving or gaining lean mass. The available evidence should not be simplified into "higher protein does not help." A more careful interpretation is that protein intake, training stimulus, baseline intake, and the size of the protein difference being tested all matter. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Silva & Spritzer, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27922938/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/))

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis found that whey protein combined with resistance training improved some strength and lean-mass outcomes in postmenopausal women, while whey protein without resistance training did not show significant benefits for strength or lean mass. The authors reported no conflicts of interest. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/))

Cross-sectional evidence also links higher protein intake and physical activity with skeletal muscle mass in postmenopausal women, but this type of study cannot prove causality. ([Silva & Spritzer, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27922938/))

Randomized evidence is mixed on whether pushing protein modestly above RDA-level intake adds extra lean-mass gains in postmenopausal women who are already resistance training. One small 10-week trial found that higher protein intake did not improve lean-mass gain compared with an RDA-level intake during a resistance-training protocol, but both groups were training and the achieved protein difference was modest, about 1.18 g/kg/day versus 0.87 g/kg/day. That trial does not show that higher protein never helps, and it does not test whether protein can compensate for undertraining. ([Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/))

The practical interpretation is not "protein does nothing." It is: hit adequate protein consistently, consider whether intake is high enough for the person's goals and training load, and pair protein with progressive training. Small trials using modest protein-dose differences should not be overread as proof that higher-protein approaches are useless. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Silva & Spritzer, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27922938/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/))

## Sleep and recovery: this is where programming may need to change

Perimenopause often changes the training environment more than the training rules. Sleep disruption, hot flashes, mood symptoms, joint aches, heavier bleeding, and life stress can reduce the margin for aggressive programming when those symptoms are present. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [Choudhary & Bansal, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40694785/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/); [Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/))

A 2025 systematic review in menopausal women found that exercise interventions, including aerobic exercise, yoga, Pilates, resistance training, stretching, and relaxation-based approaches, were associated with improvements in sleep quality and psychological well-being. The authors reported no conflicts of interest. ([Choudhary & Bansal, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40694785/))

But exercise is not a guaranteed sleep treatment. Prior reviews and trials have found mixed results depending on the intervention, population, and whether sleep was measured subjectively or objectively. This is why recovery-aware programming is sensible: use RPE or reps-in-reserve, adjust volume during bad-sleep weeks, and avoid stacking heavy lifting, HIIT, and poor sleep as if recovery capacity were unchanged. ([Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/))

## Hot flashes and menopausal symptoms

Exercise is good for health, but it is not a consistently proven treatment for vasomotor symptoms. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis found that exercise may reduce vasomotor-symptom severity, but certainty was low and effects weakened when higher-risk-of-bias studies were removed; frequency reductions were not clearly significant. ([Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/))

That matters because women should not be told that hot flashes, night sweats, or poor sleep are happening because they are not exercising hard enough. Training can help some symptoms and overall health, but symptom management may require broader medical, behavioral, and environmental strategies. ([Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/); [The 2023 nonhormone therapy position statement of The North American Menopause Society](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37252752/))

## What should actually change?

The program does not need to become completely different. The priorities should become clearer.

Progressive resistance training should be central. The evidence supports strength and fitness gains in postmenopausal women, with more mixed evidence for BMD and anthropometric outcomes, and a direct mixed-menopause-status trial shows women can adapt across pre-, peri-, and postmenopausal stages. ([González-Gálvez et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38353251/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

Power and balance should be included in small, appropriate doses. The evidence is more indirect, but the functional rationale is strong, and balance improved in a mixed menopause-status resistance-training trial. ([Reid & Fielding, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22016147/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

Aerobic training should stay in the plan. Zone-2-style work, brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or similar steady aerobic training fits adult physical-activity guidance and can often be easier to place around lifting than frequent HIIT during high-symptom or poor-sleep weeks. ([Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/))

HIIT should be optional and flexible. It can be useful, especially for visceral-fat and fitness goals, but the evidence does not support making it compulsory for all perimenopausal women. ([Nunes et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30277921/); [Dupuit et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32613697/))

Protein should be adequate, consistent, and not minimized. The evidence supports protein as part of a resistance-training plan, and small trials using modest protein-dose differences should not be interpreted as proof that higher protein is irrelevant. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Silva & Spritzer, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27922938/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/))

Recovery should be programmed, not wished into existence. During weeks with poor sleep, heavy bleeding, hot flashes, high stress, or joint pain, it is reasonable to reduce volume, avoid maximal efforts, or shift HIIT to lower-intensity aerobic work. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/); [Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/))

## Practical interpretation

Perimenopausal women should not be told that everything they knew about training stops applying. Progressive overload, adequate nutrition, consistency, and recovery still fit the available evidence. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [González-Gálvez et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38353251/); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/); [Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf))

But perimenopause can make poor programming less forgiving when symptoms, sleep disruption, or recovery constraints are present. A plan built around inadequate protein or inconsistent nutrition, no strength training, frequent high-intensity work despite poor sleep, and no recovery flexibility is less aligned with the available evidence. A plan built around progressive lifting, enough protein, aerobic conditioning, symptom-aware flexibility, and recovery is better aligned with the evidence. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/); [Choudhary & Bansal, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40694785/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/))

The best answer is: not completely different, but more deliberate.

## Quick reference

Resistance training: prioritize progressive full-body strength training two to four times per week, using the lower end as the evidence-based minimum and the higher end when it is recoverable. ([Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf); [Svensen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39480197/))

Power: add small doses of safe, joint-appropriate explosive or fast-concentric work. ([Reid & Fielding, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22016147/))

Cardio: keep regular aerobic work; use HIIT only when recovery supports it. ([Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2018](https://odphp.health.gov/sites/default/files/2019-09/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf); [Nunes et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30277921/); [Dupuit et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32613697/))

Protein: ensure adequate daily intake, do not overinterpret small dose-comparison trials as proof that higher protein is useless, and pair protein with resistance training. ([Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Silva & Spritzer, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27922938/); [Rossato et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28895933/))

Recovery: adjust training load when sleep, bleeding, vasomotor symptoms, joint pain, or stress are high. ([McNulty et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40992413/); [Qian et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2023.1092294/full); [Buchanan et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5181601/); [Liu et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35904028/))

Weight gain: do not focus only on scale weight; menopause-related changes may show up more clearly as fat distribution and lean-mass changes. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://insight.jci.org/articles/view/124865); [Karvonen-Gutierrez & Kim, 2016](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9032/4/3/42))
