# Training & Recovery

The most debated questions about how women should train, recover, fuel, and adapt across life stages.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Is Creatine Actually Useful for Women?

Creatine is no longer just a bodybuilding supplement. It is now marketed to women for strength, recovery, brain fog, perimenopause, mood, and healthy aging. But how much of that is evidence, and how much is supplement hype?

**Competing claims:**

1. Creatine improves strength, power, lean mass, and high-intensity performance in women who train
2. The benefits are real but smaller or less consistent in women because most creatine research has been done in men
3. Creatine may be especially useful during perimenopause and postmenopause because of changes in muscle, bone, and brain energy metabolism
4. Claims about cognition, PMS, mood, and hormonal symptoms are promising but not strong enough for broad recommendations

**Evidence needed:** Female-specific creatine trials, systematic reviews on active women, studies in peri/postmenopausal women, cognition and mood trials, safety data on long-term supplementation.

## Do Women Recover Differently From Hard Workouts?

Some women feel wrecked for days after hard training. Others are told women recover faster than men because they use lower absolute loads or have different muscle physiology. Is recovery actually sex-specific, or mostly about training status, sleep, food, and load management?

**Competing claims:**

1. Women recover faster than men from some types of exercise because they produce less absolute force and may have lower muscle damage
2. Women recover slower when sleep, low energy availability, iron deficiency, menstrual symptoms, or life stress are not accounted for
3. Hormonal fluctuations across the menstrual cycle affect soreness, inflammation, fatigue, and perceived recovery
4. Training history, nutrition, workload, and sleep explain more recovery variation than biological sex alone

**Evidence needed:** Studies comparing male and female recovery after matched exercise, muscle damage and soreness research, menstrual-cycle recovery studies, sleep and nutrition interaction data, athlete monitoring studies.

## Is Cycle Syncing Your Workouts Real Science or Wellness Marketing?

Cycle syncing says women should train differently across the menstrual cycle: push hard around ovulation, go lighter before or during the period, and match workouts to hormones. It sounds intuitive, but does the evidence support a universal training plan?

**Competing claims:**

1. Strength, endurance, coordination, and injury risk shift enough across the cycle to justify phase-based training
2. Cycle effects exist, but they are small, inconsistent, and highly individual
3. Symptoms matter more than cycle phase: cramps, sleep disruption, heavy bleeding, migraine, and fatigue should guide training adjustments
4. Cycle syncing is useful as self-awareness, but rigid hormone-based programming is mostly wellness marketing

**Evidence needed:** Meta-analyses on menstrual-cycle phase and performance, studies using confirmed ovulation and hormone measures, injury-risk research, trials comparing cycle-based programming with standard progressive training.

## Should Women Train Differently During Perimenopause?

Perimenopause can bring sleep disruption, heavier or irregular bleeding, hot flashes, mood changes, joint aches, belly-fat gain, and slower recovery. But does it require a different training strategy, or just better execution of the basics?

**Competing claims:**

1. Perimenopausal women need more emphasis on resistance training, power, protein, and recovery to counter muscle and bone loss
2. Training does not need to be completely different; progressive strength training and adequate nutrition work at every age
3. Hormonal changes make high-intensity training, sleep, and recovery harder to manage, so programming should become more flexible
4. Weight gain in perimenopause is mostly about aging and lifestyle, but estrogen loss changes fat distribution and cardiometabolic risk

**Evidence needed:** Exercise trials in perimenopausal women, strength and power training studies, protein and body composition research, sleep and recovery studies, longitudinal data separating aging from menopause effects.

## Do Women Need Different Protein Intake Than Men?

Protein recommendations are usually given as grams per kilogram of body weight, with no sex adjustment. But women have different muscle protein synthesis kinetics, different leucine thresholds in some studies, and very different intake patterns in real life — many women under-consume protein chronically.

**Competing claims:**

1. Per kg of body weight, women need the same protein as men for muscle, bone, and recovery; the issue is that women under-eat it, not that the target should change
2. Women may benefit from slightly higher protein per meal because of differences in muscle protein synthesis response and leucine sensitivity
3. Older women, especially postmenopausal, need substantially more protein than current guidelines recommend to preserve muscle and bone
4. Protein timing across the day matters more for women because of typically lower per-meal intake, not because of a fundamental sex difference

**Evidence needed:** Sex-stratified protein dose-response studies, muscle protein synthesis data in women across the cycle and lifespan, intake epidemiology in active women, RCTs in postmenopausal women on protein and lean mass outcomes.

## Is Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) Underdiagnosed in Women?

RED-S — chronic low energy availability with downstream effects on hormones, bone, mood, immunity, and performance — is well-documented in elite female athletes. But the framing assumes a clinical extreme, while many recreationally active women may sit in a milder version of the same physiology and never get it named.

**Competing claims:**

1. RED-S is a clinical syndrome largely confined to elite or aesthetic-sport athletes; broad screening of recreationally active women would over-diagnose normal variation
2. Subclinical low energy availability is widespread among active women and explains a large share of menstrual irregularity, stress fractures, fatigue, and stalled progress that get attributed to other causes
3. The diagnostic criteria are fuzzy: how low is "too low," over what duration, and how many downstream markers must shift before it counts as RED-S
4. Treatment is conceptually simple — eat more — but practically hard because of disordered-eating overlap, body-image pressure, and time-cost of preparing more food

**Evidence needed:** Prevalence studies in non-elite active women, validation of low-energy-availability screening tools, longitudinal data on subclinical RED-S and bone or metabolic outcomes, treatment-response studies, overlap with disordered eating and the relative apolitical disordered eating in athletes (RED-S vs. eating disorder discrimination).

## Do Hormonal Contraceptives Affect Strength and Endurance Training Adaptations?

Hormonal contraception is used by tens of millions of women, including many who lift seriously, run, cycle, or compete. Yet the training literature has historically excluded contraceptive users or treated them as a noisy subgroup. The few studies that exist are mixed and underpowered.

**Competing claims:**

1. Hormonal contraceptives blunt training adaptations slightly (small reductions in strength, lean mass gains, or VO₂ max improvements) compared with naturally cycling women
2. The effect, if any, is too small to matter for non-elite training; consistency, sleep, nutrition, and progressive overload dominate
3. Different formulations (ethinyl estradiol dose, progestin type, monophasic vs. multiphasic, oral vs. IUD vs. implant) produce different effects on strength, endurance, and recovery — collapsing them into one category obscures real signals
4. The bigger issue is that contraceptive use changes baseline hormone profiles enough to confound any cycle-phase research, and the field has not adequately addressed this

**Evidence needed:** Training intervention RCTs stratified by contraceptive type, meta-analyses on hormonal contraception and strength/endurance adaptations, longitudinal data on contraceptive switching and performance, mechanistic studies on synthetic vs. endogenous hormone effects on muscle and tendon.

## Is Heavy Lifting Safe and Beneficial During Pregnancy?

Pregnancy guidelines have been moving toward "keep doing what you were doing," but heavy lifting specifically remains contested. Some obstetricians still advise women to drop weights significantly; others — and most strength coaches — point to evidence that experienced lifters can train through pregnancy with adjustments.

**Competing claims:**

1. Experienced lifters can continue heavy resistance training through pregnancy with minimal risk if they monitor RPE, breath-holding, and pelvic floor symptoms
2. Heavy compound lifts increase intra-abdominal pressure to levels that may worsen diastasis recti, prolapse risk, or pelvic floor function postpartum
3. The default advice should be modality continuation, not modality restriction — telling experienced lifters to "just walk" during pregnancy harms strength, mood, and postpartum recovery
4. Risk-benefit varies by pregnancy stage, prior training history, and individual symptoms; one-size-fits-all advice in either direction is wrong

**Evidence needed:** Prospective studies of resistance training in experienced lifters during pregnancy, pelvic floor and diastasis outcome data by training intensity, postpartum return-to-training comparisons, guidelines synthesis across countries and specialty groups.

## Do Women Need Different Volume or Intensity Than Men for the Same Adaptation?

Most strength and endurance programming is built on research done predominantly in men. The defaults — sets, reps, intensities, progression rates — get applied to women without adjustment. Whether that's a bug or a non-issue is genuinely contested.

**Competing claims:**

1. The same relative loads, sets, and reps produce equivalent adaptations in women and men; programming differences are unjustified
2. Women may tolerate higher volumes and shorter rest periods than men because of fiber-type distribution and faster between-set recovery
3. Women respond differently to high-intensity vs. high-volume training, and current guidelines under-prescribe intensity or over-prescribe volume depending on the goal
4. Individual variation within sexes is larger than between sexes, making sex-specific programming a distraction from individualization

**Evidence needed:** Sex-stratified RCTs on volume and intensity, fiber-type and recovery research, hypertrophy and strength meta-analyses with sex as a moderator, longitudinal female-athlete training studies.

## Are Women More Prone to Certain Injuries Because of Anatomy, Hormones, or Training History?

Female athletes tear ACLs at 2-8 times the rate of male athletes in matched sports. They also report more stress fractures, hip pain, and certain tendinopathies. The "why" gets contested fiercely — anatomy, hormones, training, or all of the above.

**Competing claims:**

1. Anatomical factors (Q-angle, narrower notch, pelvic width) make certain injuries mechanically more likely in women
2. Hormonal cycling (ligament laxity around ovulation, estrogen effects on tendon) increases injury risk during specific cycle phases
3. Training history and movement quality — historically less exposure to plyometric, deceleration, and strength training in girls' youth sports — explain most of the gap
4. Low energy availability and undiagnosed RED-S drive a large share of stress fractures and overuse injuries that get blamed on "female anatomy"

**Evidence needed:** Injury epidemiology stratified by sport and training exposure, neuromuscular training intervention trials, hormone-injury correlation studies with confirmed cycle phase, RED-S and stress fracture data, comparisons of injury rates as girls' sports training quality has improved over time.
