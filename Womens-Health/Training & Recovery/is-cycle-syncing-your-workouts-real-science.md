# Is Cycle Syncing Your Workouts Real Science

Cycle syncing is best understood as a **self-awareness tool**, not a validated universal training system. Hormones do fluctuate across the menstrual cycle, and some studies find phase-related changes in performance, symptoms, recovery, or injury-risk markers. But the best evidence does **not** support a rigid rule like "train hard at ovulation, deload before your period" for everyone. Group-level effects are usually small, inconsistent, and strongly affected by study quality, phase-confirmation methods, and individual symptoms. (McNulty et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023; Schlie et al., 2025) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40695607/ "Effects of menstrual cycle phases on athletic performance and related physiological outcomes: a systematic review of studies using high methodological standards - PubMed"))

## What cycle syncing is and is not

In this article, cycle syncing means changing training based on menstrual-cycle phase: harder training around the late follicular/ovulatory window, lighter training in the late luteal phase or during menstruation, and nutrition/recovery choices matched to presumed hormone levels.

That is different from **cycle-aware coaching**, which means tracking cycle status, symptoms, sleep, bleeding, pain, perceived exertion, readiness, and performance, then adjusting training when the individual data justify it. The second approach is much better supported than fixed phase templates. (McNulty et al., 2020; Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"))

Natural-cycle phase templates also should not be assumed to apply cleanly to people using hormonal contraception or to people whose cycles are irregular, anovulatory, or absent. Many studies exclude these groups or use small samples of eumenorrheic participants, so clinically significant symptoms or reproductive-health conditions should be interpreted individually rather than forced into a natural-cycle phase template. (Elliott-Sale et al., 2021; McNulty et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053180/ "Methodological Considerations for Studies in Sport and Exercise Science with Women as Participants - PMC"); [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

## Performance across the cycle

A large systematic review and meta-analysis of exercise performance found a possible **trivial reduction** in performance during the early follicular phase, the phase that starts with menstruation, compared with other phases. The authors judged the evidence as low quality and concluded that general phase-based training guidelines were not warranted. (McNulty et al., 2020) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

That review included 78 studies, but many had small sample sizes, inconsistent phase definitions, and limited hormone verification. In the pairwise meta-analysis, the average effect for early follicular versus all other phases was trivial, and in sensitivity analyses restricted to moderate- and high-quality studies, the effect was essentially null. (McNulty et al., 2020) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

A newer systematic review restricted to studies using stronger methodological standards, including measurement of estradiol, progesterone, and luteinizing hormone, still found mixed results. It included 19 studies and 279 participants; 58 percent reported a significant phase effect on at least one performance-related outcome, but the direction and magnitude varied, and maximal or explosive strength was largely unaffected. (Schlie et al., 2025) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40695607/ "Effects of menstrual cycle phases on athletic performance and related physiological outcomes: a systematic review of studies using high methodological standards - PubMed"))

The practical implication is that menstrual phase may matter for some athletes and some outcomes, but the evidence does not support a universal "best phase" for performance. (McNulty et al., 2020; Schlie et al., 2025) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40695607/ "Effects of menstrual cycle phases on athletic performance and related physiological outcomes: a systematic review of studies using high methodological standards - PubMed"))

## Strength, power, and resistance training

A systematic review and meta-analysis focused on strength-related outcomes found non-significant trivial-to-small effects across early follicular, ovulatory, and mid-luteal phases. The authors concluded that strength-related measures are minimally altered across the cycle, though they also warned that study quality limits certainty. (Blagrove et al., 2020) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S144024401930814X "Variations in strength-related measures during the menstrual cycle in eumenorrheic women: A systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"))

A 2023 review of reviews reached a similar practical conclusion: current evidence does not show a clear influence of menstrual-cycle phase on acute strength performance or resistance-training adaptations. It also noted that many claims about phase-based resistance training rely on low-quality or indirect evidence. (Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

Some newer strength-focused reviews have suggested possible phase patterns, such as less favorable strength performance in the early follicular phase and better performance around late follicular or ovulatory phases. But those findings sit inside a broader literature with high heterogeneity, small samples, and inconsistent phase confirmation, so they are not enough to justify a universal strength-training template. (Niering et al., 2024; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4663/12/1/31 "The Influence of Menstrual Cycle Phases on Maximal Strength Performance in Healthy Female Adults: A Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

## Confirmed ovulation and hormone measurement matter

One major problem in this field is that many studies estimate cycle phase using calendar counting alone. That can be misleading because ovulation timing varies, luteal length varies, and apparently regular bleeding does not guarantee that a cycle was ovulatory. (McNulty et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023; Burden et al., 2024) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"); [BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/10/2/e002095 "Measure do not guess: a call to action to end assumed and estimated menstrual cycle phases in research"))

Reviews that emphasize better methods recommend confirming ovulation and hormone status with tools such as luteinizing hormone testing and estradiol/progesterone measurement rather than assuming phase from day count. Without that, it is difficult to know whether a measured performance change was actually caused by the hormonal environment assigned to that phase. (Elliott-Sale et al., 2021; Burden et al., 2024; Schlie et al., 2025) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053180/ "Methodological Considerations for Studies in Sport and Exercise Science with Women as Participants - PMC"); [BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/10/2/e002095 "Measure do not guess: a call to action to end assumed and estimated menstrual cycle phases in research"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40695607/ "Effects of menstrual cycle phases on athletic performance and related physiological outcomes: a systematic review of studies using high methodological standards - PubMed"))

This is one reason fixed cycle-syncing templates can sound cleaner than the science: simple phase blocks do not always match the variability of real cycles. (McNulty et al., 2020; Burden et al., 2024; Schlie et al., 2025) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/10/2/e002095 "Measure do not guess: a call to action to end assumed and estimated menstrual cycle phases in research"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40695607/ "Effects of menstrual cycle phases on athletic performance and related physiological outcomes: a systematic review of studies using high methodological standards - PubMed"))

## Trials of cycle-based programming

Direct trials comparing cycle-based programming with standard training are small and preliminary. (Sakamaki-Sunaga et al., 2016; Vargas-Molina et al., 2022; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26554551/ "Effects of Menstrual Phase-Dependent Resistance Training Frequency on Muscular Hypertrophy and Strength - PubMed"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0765159722000569 "Menstrual cycle-based undulating periodized program effects on body composition and strength in trained women: a pilot study - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

One 12-week resistance-training study in 14 eumenorrheic women trained each arm with different phase-dependent frequencies and found no major differences in hypertrophy or strength between protocols. The participants were physically active but not regularly resistance-trained, and the authors suggested that hormonal variation did not meaningfully drive the training adaptations observed. (Sakamaki-Sunaga et al., 2016) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26554551/ "Effects of Menstrual Phase-Dependent Resistance Training Frequency on Muscular Hypertrophy and Strength - PubMed"))

A small pilot trial randomized 10 resistance-trained women to either menstrual cycle-based undulating periodization or traditional undulating periodization for eight weeks. Strength increased in both groups; one bench-press comparison favored the cycle-based group, but results across outcomes were mixed, and the sample was far too small to establish that cycle-based programming is superior. (Vargas-Molina et al., 2022) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0765159722000569 "Menstrual cycle-based undulating periodized program effects on body composition and strength in trained women: a pilot study - ScienceDirect"))

So far, there is no strong trial evidence that a fixed menstrual-cycle-based program beats well-designed progressive training that already manages volume, intensity, recovery, and symptoms. (Sakamaki-Sunaga et al., 2016; Vargas-Molina et al., 2022; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26554551/ "Effects of Menstrual Phase-Dependent Resistance Training Frequency on Muscular Hypertrophy and Strength - PubMed"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0765159722000569 "Menstrual cycle-based undulating periodized program effects on body composition and strength in trained women: a pilot study - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

## Symptoms may matter more than phase

A scoping review found wide variability in how athletes perceive the menstrual cycle's effect on performance, with reported negative effects ranging from very low to nearly universal across studies. The most common explanation for reduced performance was not abstract cycle phase, but menstrual symptoms. (Oester et al., 2024) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"))

A longitudinal study in elite female basketball players found that menstrual-cycle phase effects were limited and inconsistent, while symptom burden/frequency was more consistently associated with poorer sleep, reduced recovery, and higher stress. (Kullik et al., 2025) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"))

A study of professional footballers using repeated tracking over multiple cycles found some phase-related differences in match-running outputs, but also large intra- and inter-individual variability. The authors concluded that individualized monitoring may be more useful than broad phase rules. (Brown and Duffield, 2024) ([Opus at UTS](https://opus.lib.uts.edu.au/rest/bitstreams/cf628fdb-9cc8-4af2-8dc4-7ddf73702381/retrieve "Influence of Menstrual Phase and Symptoms on Match Running in Professional Footballers"))

This supports a more practical rule: consider adjusting training when symptoms, poor sleep, fatigue, unusually high perceived exertion, or low recovery show up in the athlete's own tracking, not simply because a cycle-tracking app says someone is in a particular phase. (Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025; Brown and Duffield, 2024) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"); [Opus at UTS](https://opus.lib.uts.edu.au/rest/bitstreams/cf628fdb-9cc8-4af2-8dc4-7ddf73702381/retrieve "Influence of Menstrual Phase and Symptoms on Match Running in Professional Footballers"))

## Injury risk

The injury-risk evidence is not settled. (Dos'Santos et al., 2023; MacMillan et al., 2024) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0280800 "Effects of the menstrual cycle phase on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury risk surrogates in eumenorrheic and naturally menstruating women: A systematic review | PLOS One"); [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02074-5 "The Association Between Menstrual Cycle Phase, Menstrual Irregularities, Contraceptive Use and Musculoskeletal Injury Among Female Athletes: A Scoping Review | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

A systematic review on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury-risk surrogates found only seven eligible studies using biochemical phase verification and/or ovulation testing. Findings were mixed, study quality was low to very low, and the authors concluded that evidence was inconclusive and should not be used to make firm phase-based training recommendations. (Dos'Santos et al., 2023) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0280800 "Effects of the menstrual cycle phase on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury risk surrogates in eumenorrheic and naturally menstruating women: A systematic review | PLOS One"))

A broader scoping review of menstrual-cycle phase, menstrual irregularities, hormonal contraception, and musculoskeletal injury found possible phase-related signals, especially around the late follicular phase, but emphasized inconsistent data, differing methods, and no established direct causal relationship. (MacMillan et al., 2024) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02074-5 "The Association Between Menstrual Cycle Phase, Menstrual Irregularities, Contraceptive Use and Musculoskeletal Injury Among Female Athletes: A Scoping Review | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

A systematic review and meta-analysis on actual muscle injuries found no statistically significant association between luteal versus follicular phase and muscle injury risk, with very low certainty evidence. The authors stated that no practical or clinical recommendations could be made from the available evidence. (Guthardt et al., 2026) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-36763-0 "The influence of the menstrual cycle on muscle injuries - a systematic review and meta-analysis | Scientific Reports"))

So injury-risk research does not currently justify a universal rule like "avoid hard training during ovulation" or "avoid intense work before menstruation." It supports better monitoring, better study design, and individualized risk management. (Dos'Santos et al., 2023; MacMillan et al., 2024; Guthardt et al., 2026) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0280800 "Effects of the menstrual cycle phase on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury risk surrogates in eumenorrheic and naturally menstruating women: A systematic review | PLOS One"); [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02074-5 "The Association Between Menstrual Cycle Phase, Menstrual Irregularities, Contraceptive Use and Musculoskeletal Injury Among Female Athletes: A Scoping Review | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-36763-0 "The influence of the menstrual cycle on muscle injuries - a systematic review and meta-analysis | Scientific Reports"))

## Quick reference

Universal "train hard at ovulation, go light before/during the period" plan: **not supported** by current evidence. (McNulty et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

Performance: early follicular performance may be slightly lower on average, but the effect is trivial and low-certainty. (McNulty et al., 2020) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

Strength: most meta-analytic evidence shows minimal or inconsistent phase effects. (Blagrove et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S144024401930814X "Variations in strength-related measures during the menstrual cycle in eumenorrheic women: A systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

Training adaptations: there is no robust evidence that cycle-based programming beats standard progressive training. (Sakamaki-Sunaga et al., 2016; Vargas-Molina et al., 2022; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26554551/ "Effects of Menstrual Phase-Dependent Resistance Training Frequency on Muscular Hypertrophy and Strength - PubMed"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0765159722000569 "Menstrual cycle-based undulating periodized program effects on body composition and strength in trained women: a pilot study - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

Injury risk: possible signals exist, but evidence is inconsistent and not strong enough for phase-specific injury-prevention rules. (Dos'Santos et al., 2023; MacMillan et al., 2024; Guthardt et al., 2026) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0280800 "Effects of the menstrual cycle phase on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury risk surrogates in eumenorrheic and naturally menstruating women: A systematic review | PLOS One"); [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02074-5 "The Association Between Menstrual Cycle Phase, Menstrual Irregularities, Contraceptive Use and Musculoskeletal Injury Among Female Athletes: A Scoping Review | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-36763-0 "The influence of the menstrual cycle on muscle injuries - a systematic review and meta-analysis | Scientific Reports"))

Symptoms: this is the strongest practical reason to adjust training. (Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"))

## Practical interpretation

Cycle syncing is useful when it means **tracking your own cycle, symptoms, sleep, recovery, and performance** and using that information to make flexible training decisions. (McNulty et al., 2020; Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025; Brown and Duffield, 2024) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"); [Opus at UTS](https://opus.lib.uts.edu.au/rest/bitstreams/cf628fdb-9cc8-4af2-8dc4-7ddf73702381/retrieve "Influence of Menstrual Phase and Symptoms on Match Running in Professional Footballers"))

Cycle syncing overreaches when it claims that all women should follow the same hormone-based template, or that certain phases are automatically good or bad for strength, endurance, coordination, or injury risk. (McNulty et al., 2020; Blagrove et al., 2020; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023; Dos'Santos et al., 2023; MacMillan et al., 2024) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S144024401930814X "Variations in strength-related measures during the menstrual cycle in eumenorrheic women: A systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"); [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0280800 "Effects of the menstrual cycle phase on anterior cruciate ligament neuromuscular and biomechanical injury risk surrogates in eumenorrheic and naturally menstruating women: A systematic review | PLOS One"); [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02074-5 "The Association Between Menstrual Cycle Phase, Menstrual Irregularities, Contraceptive Use and Musculoskeletal Injury Among Female Athletes: A Scoping Review | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

A reasonable approach is to keep standard progressive training as the base, track symptoms and performance for several cycles, and adjust sessions when the individual pattern is clear. (McNulty et al., 2020; Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025; Brown and Duffield, 2024) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"); [Opus at UTS](https://opus.lib.uts.edu.au/rest/bitstreams/cf628fdb-9cc8-4af2-8dc4-7ddf73702381/retrieve "Influence of Menstrual Phase and Symptoms on Match Running in Professional Footballers"))

Someone who reliably has severe menstrual symptoms, poor sleep, or marked fatigue may choose to move the hardest session, lower volume, extend warm-ups, or emphasize recovery on those days. Someone who feels normal during menstruation does not need to reduce training just because a template says so. (McNulty et al., 2020; Oester et al., 2024; Kullik et al., 2025; Colenso-Semple et al., 2023) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01319-3 "The Effects of Menstrual Cycle Phase on Exercise Performance in Eumenorrheic Women: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis | Sports Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S144024402400077X "Inconsistencies in the perceived impact of the menstrual cycle on sport performance and in the prevalence of menstrual cycle symptoms: A scoping review of the literature - ScienceDirect"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1663657/full "Frontiers | The impact of menstrual cycle phase and symptoms on sleep, recovery, and stress in elite female basketball athletes: a longitudinal study"); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full "Frontiers | Current evidence shows no influence of women's menstrual cycle phase on acute strength performance or adaptations to resistance exercise training"))

The best current answer is: **cycle effects are real for some people, but universal cycle-syncing workout plans are ahead of the evidence.
