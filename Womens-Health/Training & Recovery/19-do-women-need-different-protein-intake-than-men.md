# Do Women Need Different Protein Intake Than Men?

Among nonpregnant, nonlactating adults, women do **not** currently have a clearly established separate protein requirement from men per kilogram of body weight. This discussion excludes pregnancy and lactation, where separate recommendations apply. The stronger conclusion is that protein targets should be adjusted for **training status, age, energy deficit, menopausal or older-adult context, frailty/sarcopenia risk, and total meal pattern**, rather than for sex alone. ([Institute of Medicine, 2005](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10490); [Rand et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12499330/); [Morton et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/); [Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352); [Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/); [Nasimi et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831323003162))

The practical issue is still very real: some women may end up with lower absolute protein per meal because of smaller meals, dieting, low energy availability, or lower total energy intake. That can make the same per-kilogram target harder to reach, especially at breakfast, during fat loss, and after menopause. ([Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/); [Fortis et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3773); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352); [Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/))

## What changes and what does not

The adult protein RDA is usually expressed as **0.8 g/kg/day**, and the per-kilogram value is not meaningfully different for men and women; sex-specific gram-per-day values mostly reflect different reference body weights, not a different biological requirement per kilogram. The RDA is also an adequacy target intended to meet the needs of nearly all apparently healthy adults, not an optimal target for muscle gain, athletic recovery, menopause, or aging. ([Institute of Medicine, 2005](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10490); [Rand et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12499330/); [Morton et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/); [Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/))

For active people, the evidence base generally supports higher intakes than the RDA, but not a different women-only per-kilogram target. A meta-analysis of resistance-training trials found that gains in fat-free mass plateaued around **1.6 g/kg/day**, with an upper confidence interval near **2.2 g/kg/day**, and those data were not used to create separate targets for women and men. ([Morton et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/))

A small indicator amino acid oxidation study in endurance-trained athletes directly compared males and females after a 20 km run. The estimated breakpoints were almost identical: **1.60 g/kg/day in males** and **1.61 g/kg/day in females**, with recommended intakes of **1.81 and 1.89 g/kg/day**, respectively. The authors concluded that post-exercise protein needs were increased in endurance-trained athletes but similar between sexes. ([Williamson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37710376/))

Female-only athlete studies can still find high requirements. For example, an IAAO study in female athletes after variable-intensity intermittent exercise estimated a requirement around **1.41 g/kg/day** and a recommended intake around **1.71 g/kg/day**. That supports higher protein for active women, but because it was not a direct male-versus-female comparison, it does not prove that women need more protein per kilogram than men. ([Wooding et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28692631/))

## Muscle protein synthesis in women

The acute muscle protein synthesis data in women cited here do **not** support a simple claim that women always need a higher leucine threshold than men. ([Mallinson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37787091/); [Apicella et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39880386/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

In resistance-trained young women, one trial compared **15 g, 30 g, and 60 g whey protein** after repeated bouts of whole-body resistance exercise. The 15 g dose did not stimulate myofibrillar protein synthesis to the same extent, while 30 g and 60 g increased myofibrillar protein synthesis, with no clear advantage of 60 g over 30 g. ([Mallinson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37787091/))

In another acute study in young women, post-exercise myofibrillar protein synthesis did not differ after **1.5 g essential amino acids**, **15 g whey**, or **20 g whey**, even though those doses provided different leucine amounts. That suggests that a universally higher leucine threshold in young women is not established. ([Apicella et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39880386/))

In overweight postmenopausal women undergoing short-term energy restriction, **35 g whey protein** was sufficient to maximize acute myofibrillar protein synthesis, with or without resistance exercise. That finding is useful for per-meal planning during weight loss, but it is still an acute MPS study rather than a long-term lean-mass outcome trial. ([Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

## Menstrual cycle effects

Current stable-isotope studies cited here do not justify changing protein targets across the menstrual cycle for muscle protein synthesis. ([Apicella et al., 2026](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050624725000142); [Apicella et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40679399/))

A randomized crossover study in healthy eumenorrheic women compared early follicular and mid-luteal phases and found that whole-body and muscle protein synthesis responses did not differ meaningfully by phase. ([Apicella et al., 2026](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050624725000142))

A related study comparing low-estradiol and high-estradiol phases found that myofibrillar protein synthesis rates did not differ with higher estradiol concentrations. The authors concluded that estradiol did not appear to be an important acute regulator of muscle protein synthesis in eumenorrheic women. ([Apicella et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40679399/))

This section should be read narrowly: it addresses acute protein-synthesis outcomes, not training, appetite, symptoms, or energy availability. It means that the current small acute MPS evidence does not support a protein rule like "eat more protein in the luteal phase" for muscle protein synthesis. ([Apicella et al., 2026](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050624725000142); [Apicella et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40679399/))

## Older and postmenopausal women

Within this review, older and postmenopausal women are the group where higher protein targets are most defensible, but the reason is mostly **aging physiology**, not female sex alone. ([Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Elstgeest et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522007729); [Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Ioannidou et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1279770724004366); [Nasimi et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831323003162))

The [PROT-AGE position paper](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/) recommends roughly **1.0 to 1.2 g/kg/day** for many adults over 65, with higher intakes for active older adults or those with acute/chronic disease, while noting that severe kidney disease may require protein restriction under medical supervision.

In the [Health ABC cohort](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522007729), higher protein intake in older women was associated with less appendicular lean mass loss over three years, while the same appendicular lean-mass association was not seen in men. Higher protein was also associated with lower mobility limitation risk in both men and women. This suggests possible sex-specific associations in older adults, but as an observational study, it cannot prove that women biologically require a higher dose.

A [meta-analysis focused on postmenopausal women](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/) found that whey protein supplementation was most useful when combined with resistance training; whey alone did not consistently improve lean mass or strength. The paper reported no external funding and no conflicts of interest.

A [randomized trial in healthy postmenopausal women](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1279770724004366) found that resistance training improved skeletal muscle mass and strength, but a higher-protein diet did not clearly add to the effect of training over 12 weeks. That weakens the claim that all healthy postmenopausal women need "substantially" more protein regardless of training, baseline intake, or nutritional status.

A [broader meta-analysis in older adults](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831323003162) found that whey protein alone did not consistently improve lean mass or strength overall, but it improved physical function and appeared more useful in sarcopenic or frail subgroups. Whey plus vitamin D improved lean mass, strength, and function; the authors proposed that this may relate to correction of vitamin D insufficiency or deficiency.

## Protein timing and meal distribution

Protein timing appears to matter less than total daily intake and resistance training in the timing trial cited here, but protein **distribution** can matter practically. ([de Branco et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S026156141930010X); [Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/))

A [trial in postmenopausal women](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S026156141930010X) compared whey protein timing around resistance training and found no major difference between consuming protein immediately around training versus later when total intake was controlled. This supports the view that immediate timing is not the main issue when daily protein and training are adequate.

The more common problem is uneven intake. In [well-trained Dutch athletes](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/), average daily protein intake exceeded 1.2 g/kg/day in both men and women, but protein distribution was skewed toward dinner. Breakfast was below 20 g protein in 58 percent of athletes, lunch in 36 percent, and dinner in only 8 percent.

So the practical recommendation for many women is not "women need different timing biology." It is: **make sure each meal actually contains enough high-quality protein for the goal and context**, especially breakfast and lunch. ([Mallinson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37787091/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352); [Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/))

## Real-world intake patterns in active women

The claim that many women under-consume protein is context-dependent. Some active women do under-consume or underfuel, especially during low-energy availability, dieting, or restrictive eating patterns. But some athlete cohorts meet or exceed protein targets while under-consuming carbohydrate, vitamin D, calcium, or total energy. ([Barney et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2475299124024090); [Fortis et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3773))

In [NCAA Division I cross-country runners from a single university](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2475299124024090), both women and men met protein recommendations, with women averaging about **2.09 g/kg/day** and men about **1.92 g/kg/day**, while more common inadequacies involved carbohydrate, vitamin D, and, for some athletes, calcium.

In [female endurance athletes](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3773), qualitative and dietary-record data show that underfueling and carbohydrate deficits can occur even when protein intake is prioritized and average protein intake is within sport recommendations.

This matters because protein intake should not be interpreted separately from total energy availability. A woman eating 1.6 g/kg/day protein but chronically under-eating calories may still be underfueled, so protein intake alone would not solve the larger nutrition problem. ([Fortis et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3773))

## Bone outcomes

Protein is relevant to musculoskeletal health, but current evidence does not support a clean female-specific protein target for bone. ([Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Wolf et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00198-025-07393-5))

A [2025 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00198-025-07393-5) of combined protein and exercise interventions in middle-aged and older adults found no robust conclusion that added protein meaningfully improved bone outcomes compared with exercise alone. Sex subgroup analyses did not show a clear pattern that women responded differently from men.

For postmenopausal bone health, protein should be considered one part of a broader exercise-and-nutrition package, not a stand-alone female-specific protein target. ([Wolf et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00198-025-07393-5))

## Quick reference values

**Healthy sedentary adults:**  
The RDA remains **0.8 g/kg/day**, but this is an adequacy target, not an optimization target for training, fat loss, aging, or menopause. ([Institute of Medicine, 2005](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10490); [Rand et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12499330/); [Morton et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/); [Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/))

**Active women and resistance-trained women:**  
Within the evidence reviewed here, a useful anchor for resistance training is **~1.6 g/kg/day**, with an upper confidence interval near **2.2 g/kg/day** in the Morton et al. meta-analysis. This is not clearly different from men. ([Morton et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28698222/))

**Endurance-trained women after demanding training:**  
IAAO data suggest requirements can be around **1.8 to 1.9 g/kg/day** during recovery, but the available direct comparison found similar values in women and men. ([Williamson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37710376/))

**Older/postmenopausal women:**  
A reasonable floor is often **1.0 to 1.2 g/kg/day**, with higher intakes such as **1.2 to 1.6 g/kg/day** more defensible during resistance training, energy restriction, frailty, sarcopenia risk, or poor baseline intake. ([Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Nasimi et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831323003162); [Ioannidou et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1279770724004366); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

**Per meal:**  
The clearest women-specific per-meal data in this article are narrower than a universal rule: young trained women responded to around **30 g whey** after demanding resistance exercise, while overweight postmenopausal women in energy restriction responded maximally to around **35 g whey**. Meal planning should still account for body size, age, training, protein quality, and energy status. ([Mallinson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37787091/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

## Practical interpretation

The strongest claim is: **nonpregnant, nonlactating women generally do not need a separate per-kilogram protein target from men.** ([Institute of Medicine, 2005](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10490); [Rand et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12499330/); [Williamson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37710376/))

The better practical claim is: **many women need more deliberate protein planning** because smaller meals, dieting, low energy availability, aging, and menopause can make the same target harder to hit. ([Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/); [Fortis et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/23/3773); [Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

The "higher per meal" claim is partly plausible, especially in older or postmenopausal women and during energy restriction, but it is not proven as a universal female-specific leucine rule. ([Mallinson et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37787091/); [Apicella et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39880386/); [Larsen et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623725352))

The "older postmenopausal women need more than the RDA" claim is well supported, but mainly because the RDA is low for aging-related muscle preservation goals, not because all postmenopausal women need a uniquely female target. ([Bauer et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23867520/); [Kuo et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36235862/); [Nasimi et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831323003162); [Ioannidou et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1279770724004366))

The "timing matters more for women" claim should be reframed: timing does not appear fundamentally more important because of sex, but **meal distribution** often matters because breakfast and lunch are commonly too low in protein. ([de Branco et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S026156141930010X); [Gillen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27710150/))
