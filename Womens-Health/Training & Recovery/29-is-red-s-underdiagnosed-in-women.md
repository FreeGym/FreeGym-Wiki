# Is Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) Underdiagnosed in Women?

Questionnaire-defined RED-S risk indicators occur among some recreationally active women, but current evidence does not quantify how often clinically diagnosed RED-S is missed in this population. Three selected recreational studies reported that 35.1 to 50.0 percent of participants met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff, but these were cross-sectional, nonrepresentative studies using questionnaires rather than clinician-confirmed diagnoses. The evidence supports clinical evaluation of symptomatic or otherwise high-risk women and careful follow-up of positive screens, not diagnosing large numbers of active women from a screening score alone. The cited evidence does not compare universal questionnaire screening with targeted case-finding on health outcomes in the general recreationally active female population. ([Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245); [Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x); [Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

## What RED-S is and is not

Energy availability is the dietary energy remaining for normal physiological functions after subtracting exercise energy expenditure. It is commonly expressed relative to fat-free mass. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418))

**Energy availability = (energy intake - exercise energy expenditure) / kg fat-free mass** ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418))

The 2023 International Olympic Committee consensus defines RED-S as a syndrome of impaired physiological or psychological functioning caused by exposure to problematic, generally prolonged or severe, low energy availability. Possible consequences include altered reproductive and metabolic function, impaired bone health, reduced immunity, hematological changes, psychological effects, and impaired performance. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994))

The IOC framework is widely used, but the breadth and construct validity of the RED-S syndrome remain scientifically debated. Critics argue that the evidence is uneven across the proposed multisystem outcomes, while IOC consensus authors defend the construct. This debate coexists with evidence linking sustained energy deficiency with reproductive, endocrine, and skeletal effects, while arguing against attributing every nonspecific symptom to a single syndrome. ([Jeukendrup et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02108-y); [Mountjoy et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02219-0); [Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418); [Barrack et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546513520295))

Not every calorie deficit or brief period of low energy availability is RED-S. The current framework distinguishes potentially adaptable low energy availability from problematic low energy availability that produces clinically relevant impairment. Elite status is not part of the definition: the central exposure is an energy mismatch relative to exercise demands, which can occur in professional athletes, recreational athletes, and highly active exercisers. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994))

The 2023 IOC framework does not define “subclinical RED-S” as a diagnostic category. It is clearer to distinguish among suspected low energy availability, individual indicators such as menstrual disturbance or metabolic suppression, and a clinician-assessed RED-S syndrome. Otherwise, prevalence estimates can inadvertently count a risk factor, a symptom, and a diagnosis as though they were interchangeable. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914))

## Why underdiagnosis is difficult to measure

There is no single diagnostic test for RED-S or low energy availability. The IOC RED-S Clinical Assessment Tool Version 2 uses a three-stage process: initial screening, assessment of objective primary and secondary indicators with severity stratification, and clinician-led diagnosis and treatment. It requires consideration of alternative explanations for the findings and is not intended to be used as a stand-alone questionnaire. ([Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914))

This means there is no stable reference standard against which to count missed cases. A prevalence study using calculated energy availability, another using the LEAF-Q questionnaire, and another using menstrual or hormonal markers may be measuring different constructs. A 2025 systematic review found that 2,737 of 6,118 athletes across 46 studies, or 44.7 percent, were classified as having low energy availability, while 460 of 730 athletes across eight studies, or 63.0 percent, were classified as being at risk for RED-S. The authors also documented substantial variation in methods and populations, so these pooled figures should not be interpreted as the prevalence of clinically diagnosed RED-S in recreational women. ([Gallant et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02130-0))

Menstrual function is an especially useful female-specific indicator, but it has limitations. Hormonal contraception can obscure spontaneous menstrual function; in a 2025 application of the IOC tool in elite athletes, menstrual indicators could not be assessed in about 48 percent of female participants who used hormonal contraception. This does not show how many had low-energy-related reproductive suppression or how many cases were missed, but it shows that an important diagnostic indicator may be unavailable or difficult to interpret. ([Heikura et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-108121))

## How low is “too low”?

An energy availability value below approximately 30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day has often been treated as a threshold for physiological disruption in women. That number arose largely from short-duration laboratory studies and should be interpreted as a region of increased risk rather than a diagnostic boundary. ([Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418))

A 2024 critical review concluded that menstrual disturbances occur both above and below 30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day. Controlled prospective data similarly show a graded increase in menstrual disruption as energy availability declines rather than a sharp biological cutoff. ([Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418); [Lieberman et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000001451))

There is also no validated duration cutoff, such as a fixed number of days or weeks, after which low energy availability becomes RED-S. The severity, duration, individual susceptibility, timing of energy intake, baseline health, and body system being examined all matter. Nor is there a fixed number of affected systems required for diagnosis; the IOC tool instead gives different weight to primary and secondary indicators and integrates them with clinical judgment. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914))

## What studies show in recreationally active women

In a cross-sectional study of 109 women recruited from New Zealand gyms and fitness centers, 45.0 percent met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff. The 95 percent confidence interval was 35.4 to 54.8 percent. This establishes that positive screens are not confined to elite sport, but it does not establish that 45 percent had RED-S or independently measured low energy availability. ([Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245))

In a Chinese study of aesthetic-sport participants, 35.1 percent of 114 recreational athletes met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff, compared with 55.8 percent of 52 elite athletes. This supports a higher burden of questionnaire-defined risk in the elite group while also showing that relevant indicators occur at the recreational level. ([Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x))

A 2024 study of only 24 recreational female athletes found that 50 percent met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff and 37.5 percent reported menstrual dysfunction. However, positive screening was not associated with lower bone mineral density or poorer postural stability in this small cross-sectional sample. The result illustrates why a positive questionnaire cannot be assumed to represent established multisystem disease. ([Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533))

Together, these studies show that questionnaire-defined risk indicators are not exclusive to elite athletes. Because the studies used selected samples and screening approaches rather than clinician-adjudicated diagnosis, they do not provide a representative prevalence estimate for the broader population of women who run, lift weights, attend fitness classes, or train recreationally. ([Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245); [Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x); [Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

## How reliable are the screening tools?

The LEAF-Q is a widely used female screening questionnaire. It contains 25 questions covering menstrual function, gastrointestinal symptoms, and injuries. The original study enrolled 84 women aged 18 to 39 who trained at least five times per week. Reliability was evaluated in 37 dancers and endurance athletes, while diagnostic-performance estimates were derived in a separate validity sample of 45 endurance athletes. Against a composite of current energy availability and/or reproductive and bone-health findings, not clinician-confirmed RED-S, it had 78 percent sensitivity and 90 percent specificity. The authors described it as a complement to disordered-eating screening, not a diagnostic test. ([Melin et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2013-093240); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

Its performance does not necessarily transfer to every sport or population. In 60 professional female footballers, 32 percent screened positive, but LEAF-Q classifications did not distinguish athletes with relevant clinical markers. Most components had poor accuracy and predictive value, partly because contact and impact injuries can inflate the injury score independently of energy availability. ([Dasa et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00605-4))

A 2026 methodological review evaluated 13 highly cited questionnaires used in athletic populations for RED-S or related risk screening. Psychometric evidence scores were generally low; many tools lacked predictive validation, responsiveness testing, or adequately reported sensitivity and specificity. The validation samples were frequently concentrated in young women, particular sports, and limited geographic settings. The review concluded that inappropriate reliance on these instruments can produce both misclassification and overestimation or underestimation of risk. ([Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

The newer Female Energy Deficiency Questionnaire was developed from pooled data on 202 competitively and recreationally active young women. A model-development set of 152 participants was used to select predictors, and the final model was tested in an internally held-out 50-person set drawn from the same pooled dataset. It identified low fasting total triiodothyronine with 84.2 percent sensitivity, 80.6 percent specificity, and 82 percent accuracy. This is promising, but it predicts one metabolic marker rather than clinically confirmed RED-S and still requires independent external validation in larger and more diverse populations. ([Salamunes et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003742))

## Which health outcomes are best supported?

Female-specific evidence links lower energy availability and related Triad risk patterns with reproductive disruption and bone-health outcomes. Menstrual disturbances become more frequent as energy availability declines, although individual responses vary and there is no reliable threshold that predicts the response of every woman. ([Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418); [Lieberman et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000001451); [Barrack et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546513520295))

Prospective evidence also supports cumulative risk. In a multisite cohort of 259 exercising girls and women, individual Female Athlete Triad risk factors were associated with an approximately 15 to 20 percent incidence of bone stress injury, while combinations of risk factors were associated with incidences of approximately 30 to 50 percent. This does not prove that every injury was caused by low energy availability, but the increasing risk with clustered menstrual, bone, and nutritional indicators is clinically important. ([Barrack et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546513520295))

The 2025 systematic review found that low-energy classifications were associated with poorer running, endurance, training response, coordination, concentration, explosive power, and agility in the included studies. Evidence for injuries in general was mixed, while impaired bone health and bone stress injury showed more consistent associations. ([Gallant et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02130-0))

Fatigue, low mood, recurrent illness, impaired recovery, and stalled performance can occur with RED-S, but all are nonspecific. They can also arise from iron deficiency, sleep loss, infection, thyroid disease, psychological stress, excessive training, medication effects, and other conditions. Because these symptoms are nonspecific, current clinical guidance supports considering low energy availability within a differential assessment rather than attributing the symptoms to RED-S alone. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914))

## What treatment evidence shows

The REFUEL randomized controlled trial enrolled 76 exercising women with oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea. Women assigned to increase intake by 20 to 40 percent achieved an average increase of approximately 330 kcal/day, or 18 percent above baseline. This modest increase improved the likelihood of menstrual recovery compared with maintaining usual intake and was accompanied by an average body-weight increase of approximately 4.9 percent. ([De Souza et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/deab149))

Despite improved energy status, body weight, and menstrual frequency, the trial detected no significant between-group improvement in total-body or lumbar-spine bone mineral density over 12 months. Bone outcomes may therefore require longer recovery, more sustained ovulatory cycles, or additional intervention. The return of bleeding alone does not establish that ovulation or ovarian steroid concentrations have normalized; several consecutive normal-length cycles are more informative. ([De Souza et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac044); [Williams et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02332-0))

A psychological analysis of REFUEL found that supported increases in dietary intake and body fat did not worsen disordered-eating attitudes, perceived stress, or depressive symptoms. Importantly, this was not merely an instruction to “eat more”: the intervention included guidance from a registered dietitian and psychologist. ([Strock et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2023.106079))

Treatment can involve increasing intake, reducing exercise expenditure, or both. The practical problem is identifying why the mismatch arose. Restriction motivated by weight or shape concerns requires a different response from inadvertent under-fuelling caused by rising training load, low appetite, limited nutrition knowledge, financial constraints, food access, or insufficient time to obtain and prepare food. These practical contributors are recognized in reviews, but the cited literature does not establish their relative contribution to treatment response. ([Chesters et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-025-00010-w); [Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994))

## RED-S and eating disorders overlap but are not the same diagnosis

RED-S describes physiological and psychological impairment arising from problematic low energy availability. An eating disorder is a psychiatric diagnosis defined by characteristic eating behaviors, cognitions, distress, and impairment. An eating disorder can cause RED-S, but low energy availability can also develop without an eating disorder, for example, when training volume rises faster than intake or when appetite and food access do not match expenditure. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Chesters et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-025-00010-w))

In a cross-sectional study of 202 female endurance athletes, 65 percent met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff, 21 percent screened positive for disordered eating, and 23 percent for exercise addiction. Only 26.5 percent of those with a positive LEAF-Q result also screened positive for disordered eating. Because all measures were self-reported and the sample comprised competitive endurance athletes, these are not diagnostic or recreational-population prevalence figures. The study demonstrates incomplete overlap between the questionnaire-defined constructs, but it cannot determine causal order or show that women without a positive disordered-eating screen were under-fuelling inadvertently. ([Fahrenholtz et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2022.869594))

The converse problem also matters: subclinical disordered eating or an eating disorder may be present before measurable RED-S indicators appear. A physiological questionnaire may miss the underlying eating pathology, while a conventional eating-disorder questionnaire may miss inadvertent under-fuelling. Current reviews therefore support assessing both physiological consequences and eating or exercise behaviors rather than treating either screen as a substitute for the other. ([Chesters et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-025-00010-w); [Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994))

## Broad screening or targeted case-finding?

Universal questionnaire screening could identify women who would otherwise be missed, but it could also classify normal or unrelated menstrual, gastrointestinal, and injury experiences as evidence of low energy availability. Tool performance varies by sport, and no self-report questionnaire covers all RED-S outcomes with adequate validation. ([Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2); [Dasa et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00605-4))

The available cited evidence does not compare universal questionnaire screening with targeted case-finding on health outcomes in the general recreationally active female population. In organized sport and sports-medicine settings, including preparticipation examinations, the 2025 Female Athlete Triad consensus recommends screening and monitoring exercising girls and women for relevant risk factors. In any setting, evaluation is especially reasonable when an active woman develops otherwise unexplained menstrual disruption, recurrent bone stress injury, persistent decline in recovery or performance, significant dietary restriction, compulsive exercise, or several less-specific indicators occurring together. A questionnaire can initiate that assessment, but it should be followed by nutritional, menstrual, training, psychological, and medical evaluation rather than being treated as the diagnosis. ([Williams et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02332-0); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

This approach is intended to reduce two clinically relevant errors: missing sustained under-fuelling associated with reproductive or skeletal consequences, and attributing unrelated findings to RED-S without adequate differential diagnosis. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

## Limits and evidence still needed

The major evidence gaps are:

- Representative prevalence studies of non-elite active women using clinician-adjudicated RED-S criteria rather than questionnaire scores alone. ([Gallant et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02130-0); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

- External validation of screening tools across sports, ages, ethnic groups, training levels, and hormonal-contraceptive status. ([Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2); [Dasa et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00605-4); [Heikura et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-108121))

- Longitudinal studies showing whether isolated or mild low-energy indicators predict later bone stress injury, loss of bone density, metabolic suppression, or persistent reproductive dysfunction. ([Gallant et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02130-0); [Barrack et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546513520295); [Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418))

- Randomized trials comparing increased intake, reduced training, combined interventions, and different levels of dietetic or psychological support. ([De Souza et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/deab149); [De Souza et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac044); [Strock et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2023.106079); [Williams et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02332-0))

- Studies that directly distinguish inadvertent low energy availability, intentional weight-control behavior, subclinical disordered eating, and diagnosable eating disorders. ([Chesters et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-025-00010-w); [Fahrenholtz et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2022.869594))


## Quick reference

- Three selected recreational cohorts: 35.1 to 50.0 percent met the LEAF-Q risk cutoff, but these are not estimates of diagnosed RED-S. ([Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245); [Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x); [Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533))

- LEAF-Q original validation: 78 percent sensitivity and 90 percent specificity in a 45-person endurance-athlete validity sample, against a composite criterion rather than a RED-S diagnosis. ([Melin et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2013-093240); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

- Traditional low-energy reference point: approximately 30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day, but not a universal diagnostic threshold. ([Salamunes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1139/apnm-2023-0418); [Lieberman et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000001451))

- REFUEL intervention: approximately 330 additional kcal/day improved menstrual recovery in exercising women with oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea. ([De Souza et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/deab149))

- Bone density: no significant between-group improvement in total-body or lumbar-spine bone mineral density after 12 months despite better energy status and menstrual frequency. ([De Souza et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac044))


## Practical interpretation

- RED-S is not confined to elite or aesthetic-sport athletes, but its prevalence among the general population of recreationally active women is unknown. ([Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245); [Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x); [Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533))

- “At risk for low energy availability” should not be translated into “has RED-S.” ([Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))

- Menstrual disruption and recurrent bone stress injury provide stronger clinical signals than fatigue or stalled performance alone. ([Barrack et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546513520295); [Gallant et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02130-0); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914))

- Screening should assess both low-energy physiology and eating-disorder or compulsive-exercise risk. ([Chesters et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-025-00010-w); [Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106994); [Williams et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02332-0))

- Treatment evidence supports structured refuelling, but bone recovery may lag behind menstrual and metabolic improvement. ([De Souza et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/deab149); [De Souza et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac044))

- The most accurate conclusion is that questionnaire-defined risk indicators are present in some active women, while current evidence neither quantifies clinical underdiagnosis nor supports treating positive screening scores as diagnosed RED-S. ([Slater et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsnem.2015-0245); [Meng et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-020-00344-x); [Gimunová et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17533); [Stellingwerff et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106914); [Lodge et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s44410-026-00020-2))
