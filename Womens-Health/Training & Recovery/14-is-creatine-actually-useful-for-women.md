# Is Creatine Actually Useful for Women?

Creatine is useful for some women, but the strongest evidence cited here is still for training-related outcomes: strength, power, high-intensity exercise, and possibly lean body mass when paired with resistance training. The cited evidence is weaker or more indirect for broad claims about “brain fog,” PMS or PMDD symptom treatment, mood in healthy women, perimenopause symptoms, or healthy aging as a stand-alone supplement. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"); [Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"); [Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"); [Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"); [Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"); [Korovljev et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40854087/ "The Effects of 8-Week Creatine Hydrochloride and Creatine Ethyl Ester Supplementation on Cognition, Clinical Outcomes, and Brain Creatine Levels in Perimenopausal and Menopausal Women"); [Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [Citherlet, 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1716285/full "Commentary: The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [EFSA, 2024](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.9100 "Creatine and improvement in cognitive function"); [Lyoo et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22864465/ "A randomized, double-blind placebo-controlled trial of oral creatine monohydrate augmentation for enhanced response to a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor in women with major depressive disorder"))

## What the evidence is strongest for

The most directly relevant recent review for active women is a 2025 systematic review of creatine supplementation in physically active females, which searched five databases through July 2024 and included 27 studies. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

That review found mixed results: compared with placebo, 3 of 11 studies improved strength or power outcomes, 4 of 17 improved anaerobic outcomes, and 1 of 5 improved aerobic outcomes. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

The authors concluded that participant heterogeneity, varied performance tests, different dosing protocols, poor handling of female-specific physiology, and a small evidence base limit confidence in female-specific performance conclusions. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

So the most defensible claim is not “creatine works for all women,” but “creatine may help some active women, especially for strength, power, or repeated high-intensity exercise, but the female-specific evidence is inconsistent.” ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

## Strength, power, and lean body mass

An early randomized trial in young women found that creatine plus resistance training improved muscle phosphocreatine, maximal strength, intermittent exercise capacity, and fat-free mass more than placebo over 10 weeks. ([Vandenberghe et al., 1997](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9390981/ "Long-term creatine intake is beneficial to muscle performance during resistance training"))

That trial supports biological plausibility and shows creatine can work in women, but it was small and older, so it should not outweigh newer systematic reviews showing more mixed results across active female populations. ([Vandenberghe et al., 1997](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9390981/ "Long-term creatine intake is beneficial to muscle performance during resistance training"); [Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of 35 randomized trials found that creatine increased lean body mass overall by 0.68 kg and by 1.10 kg when combined with resistance training. ([Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

However, the same meta-analysis found a significant lean-body-mass increase in males but a non-significant increase in females: 1.46 kg in males versus 0.29 kg in females. ([Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

This does not prove creatine is ineffective in women; it means the average lean-body-mass effect appears smaller, less consistently detected, underpowered, more heterogeneous, or some combination of these possibilities in female subgroups. ([Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

A 2025 randomized trial found an acute lean-body-mass increase after a 7-day creatine “wash-in,” including in females, but found no additional lean-mass benefit after 12 weeks of resistance training compared with control; the authors noted that early lean-mass changes may partly reflect water-related effects rather than new contractile tissue. ([Desai et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/6/1081 "The Effect of Creatine Supplementation on Lean Body Mass with and Without Resistance Training"))

That trial had no placebo control and no a priori power analysis for the lean-mass question, so it should be interpreted cautiously. ([Desai et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/6/1081 "The Effect of Creatine Supplementation on Lean Body Mass with and Without Resistance Training"))

## Why female results may look less consistent

Creatine’s performance benefit has historically been demonstrated largely in male populations, which makes female-specific conclusions less certain. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

Female studies vary by training status, sport, menstrual status, contraceptive use, dosing protocol, and outcome measure, which makes it difficult to give one clean answer for all women. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

A menstrual-cycle randomized trial found that short-term creatine loading changed total body water, extracellular fluid, and intracellular fluid during the luteal phase without a clear body-weight change, which matters because “lean mass” measures can be influenced by fluid shifts. ([Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"))

That trial reported no external funding; the creatine monohydrate was donated by AlzChem, and one author disclosed serving as a scientific advisor for AlzChem, so the findings should be interpreted with that product-donation and conflict-of-interest context in mind. ([Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"))

## Perimenopause and postmenopause

Creatine may be more relevant during and after menopause when paired with resistance training, but the strongest evidence cited here is still for muscle-related outcomes, not for hormone symptoms. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"); [Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"))

A systematic review and meta-analysis in older females found that creatine combined with resistance training improved muscle strength, especially when resistance training lasted at least 24 weeks. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"))

However, that review found no overall muscle-mass benefit and rated the certainty of evidence as low, so it should not be read as strong evidence that creatine reliably increases muscle mass in older women. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"))

That review received no external funding, but two authors disclosed creatine-industry relationships, including industry-sponsored creatine research, creatine donations, travel support, and an AlzChem scientific advisory role; the findings should be interpreted with that in mind. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"))

For bone health, the evidence is more cautious. A 2-year randomized controlled trial in postmenopausal women found that creatine plus exercise had no effect on femoral neck, total hip, or lumbar spine bone mineral density, although it improved some measures of proximal femur geometry and 80-meter walking time. ([Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"))

That newer 2-year trial weakens the idea that creatine should be described broadly as a proven bone-density supplement for postmenopausal women. ([Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"))

A small randomized trial published online in 2025 in 36 peri- or menopausal women tested creatine hydrochloride and creatine ethyl ester for 8 weeks and reported improvements in some reaction-time, brain creatine, lipid, and symptom-related outcomes, but the trial was short, small, four-arm, and formulation-specific. ([Korovljev et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40854087/ "The Effects of 8-Week Creatine Hydrochloride and Creatine Ethyl Ester Supplementation on Cognition, Clinical Outcomes, and Brain Creatine Levels in Perimenopausal and Menopausal Women"))

That perimenopause trial is interesting, but it is not enough to support broad claims that creatine treats menopause symptoms, mood swings, or brain fog in the general population. ([Korovljev et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40854087/ "The Effects of 8-Week Creatine Hydrochloride and Creatine Ethyl Ester Supplementation on Cognition, Clinical Outcomes, and Brain Creatine Levels in Perimenopausal and Menopausal Women"))

## Cognition, brain fog, and mood

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of adult cognitive trials found small benefits for memory, attention time, and processing-speed time, but no significant improvement in overall cognitive function or executive function. ([Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"))

The same review rated the certainty of evidence as moderate for memory but low for processing speed, overall cognition, executive function, and attention, so cognition claims should be framed as promising but not settled. ([Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"))

The subgroup analysis suggested greater benefit in females, but that does not make the evidence female-specific enough to recommend creatine broadly for “brain fog.” ([Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"))

A 2026 commentary also raised a unit-of-analysis concern about that meta-analysis, arguing that several correlated cognitive outcomes from the same participants were treated as independent effect sizes, which can inflate precision and statistical power. ([Citherlet, 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1716285/full "Commentary: The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"))

The European Food Safety Authority also concluded that a cause-and-effect relationship had not been established between creatine supplementation and improvement in cognitive function in one or more domains. ([EFSA, 2024](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.9100 "Creatine and improvement in cognitive function"))

So cognition claims should be framed as exploratory and domain-specific, not as evidence that creatine broadly treats “brain fog.” ([Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [Citherlet, 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1716285/full "Commentary: The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [EFSA, 2024](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.9100 "Creatine and improvement in cognitive function"))

For mood, one randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in women with major depressive disorder found that 5 g/day creatine monohydrate added to SSRI treatment produced a faster and greater antidepressant response than SSRI plus placebo over 8 weeks. ([Lyoo et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22864465/ "A randomized, double-blind placebo-controlled trial of oral creatine monohydrate augmentation for enhanced response to a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor in women with major depressive disorder"))

That depression trial supports creatine as a possible adjunctive treatment in a specific clinical context, not as a general mood supplement for healthy women. ([Lyoo et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22864465/ "A randomized, double-blind placebo-controlled trial of oral creatine monohydrate augmentation for enhanced response to a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor in women with major depressive disorder"))

The menstrual-cycle trials cited here address fluid distribution or exercise recovery rather than PMS or PMDD symptom treatment, so they should not be used as direct evidence that creatine treats PMS or PMDD. ([Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"); [Gordon et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/16/3567 "The Effects of Creatine Monohydrate Loading on Exercise Recovery in Active Women throughout the Menstrual Cycle"))

## Safety in women

A female-specific systematic review and meta-analysis found that only 58 of 656 creatine studies were female-only studies, and only 29 female studies monitored adverse outcomes. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"))

Across 951 female participants in studies that monitored adverse outcomes, the review reported no deaths or serious adverse events and no significant differences in total adverse events, gastrointestinal events, weight gain, or renal or hepatic function. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"))

That safety review received no external funding, but the authors emphasized that future female studies should monitor and report adverse outcomes more comprehensively. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"))

The safety evidence is reassuring for generally healthy women studied in trials, but “no significant difference” does not prove absence of risk, and it does not automatically establish safety for every medical situation, such as kidney disease, pregnancy, complex medication use, or clinical populations excluded from performance trials. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"))

## Conflict-of-interest and funding bias notes

The 2025 active-female systematic review reported no external funding and no conflicts of interest, making it one of the cleaner evidence summaries for performance in active women. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

The 2022 lean-mass meta-analysis reported author conflicts: Darren Candow disclosed industry-sponsored creatine research, creatine donations, travel support, and an AlzChem advisory role; Scott Forbes disclosed prior advisory work for a company selling creatine products. ([Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

The 2021 older-female meta-analysis reported no external funding, but it included similar author conflicts involving creatine-industry research, donations, travel support, and advisory roles. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"))

The menstrual-cycle fluid trial reported no external funding; AlzChem donated the creatine used in the study, and one author disclosed serving as a scientific advisor for AlzChem. ([Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"))

The 2025 lean-mass trial reported no external funding, but the article disclosed that sponsored creatine from True Protein was used in the creatine arm. ([Desai et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/6/1081 "The Effect of Creatine Supplementation on Lean Body Mass with and Without Resistance Training"))

The perimenopause trial was small and product-specific; the publisher page reports no potential author conflict of interest, but the study was partially funded by the Provincial Secretariat for Higher Education and Scientific Research and Vireo Systems, so it should not be used as a standalone basis for menopause-related claims. ([Korovljev et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40854087/ "The Effects of 8-Week Creatine Hydrochloride and Creatine Ethyl Ester Supplementation on Cognition, Clinical Outcomes, and Brain Creatine Levels in Perimenopausal and Menopausal Women"))

## Quick reference values

Active females: 27 studies; 3/11 strength or power outcomes, 4/17 anaerobic outcomes, and 1/5 aerobic outcomes improved versus placebo. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))

Lean body mass meta-analysis: overall +0.68 kg; creatine plus resistance training +1.10 kg; female subgroup +0.29 kg and not statistically significant. ([Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

Older females: creatine plus resistance training improved strength, especially with resistance training lasting at least 24 weeks, but did not show an overall muscle-mass benefit. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"))

Postmenopausal bone: 2 years of creatine plus exercise did not improve bone mineral density, although some bone geometry and walking outcomes improved. ([Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"))

Cognition: Xu et al. reported small benefits for memory, attention time, and processing speed; no significant overall cognition or executive-function benefit; mostly low-certainty evidence outside memory; and later statistical/regulatory critiques weaken broad cognition claims. ([Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [Citherlet, 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1716285/full "Commentary: The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [EFSA, 2024](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.9100 "Creatine and improvement in cognitive function"))

Female safety: no deaths or serious adverse events reported in monitored female trials; no significant differences in total adverse events, gastrointestinal events, weight gain, or renal/hepatic measures. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"))

## Practical interpretation

Creatine is most evidence-based for women who resistance train or do repeated high-intensity exercise, but it should be framed as a possible training aid rather than a guaranteed strength or lean-mass enhancer. ([Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"); [Delpino et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900722002040 "Influence of age, sex, and type of exercise on the efficacy of creatine supplementation on lean body mass"))

For postmenopausal women, creatine may be reasonable to study or consider alongside resistance training for strength and function, but it should not be marketed as a proven bone-density supplement. ([Dos Santos et al., 2021](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3757 "Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation Combined with Resistance Training on Muscle Strength and Muscle Mass in Older Females"); [Chilibeck et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37144634/ "A 2-yr Randomized Controlled Trial on Creatine Supplementation during Exercise for Postmenopausal Bone Health"))

For cognition, mood, PMS, and menopause symptoms, the evidence is early, mixed, too specific, or statistically uncertain enough that it does not justify broad recommendations. ([Moore et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/2/429 "A Randomized Controlled Trial of Changes in Fluid Distribution across Menstrual Phases with Creatine Supplementation"); [Korovljev et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40854087/ "The Effects of 8-Week Creatine Hydrochloride and Creatine Ethyl Ester Supplementation on Cognition, Clinical Outcomes, and Brain Creatine Levels in Perimenopausal and Menopausal Women"); [Xu et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1424972/full "The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [Gordon et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/16/3567 "The Effects of Creatine Monohydrate Loading on Exercise Recovery in Active Women throughout the Menstrual Cycle"); [Citherlet, 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1716285/full "Commentary: The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults"); [EFSA, 2024](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.9100 "Creatine and improvement in cognitive function"); [Lyoo et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22864465/ "A randomized, double-blind placebo-controlled trial of oral creatine monohydrate augmentation for enhanced response to a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor in women with major depressive disorder"))

The safest summary is: creatine monohydrate appears reasonably safe in studied female populations and may help some women with training outcomes, but many broad women-specific claims are ahead of the evidence. ([de Guingand et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1780 "Risk of Adverse Outcomes in Females Taking Oral Creatine Monohydrate"); [Tam et al., 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/2/238 "Does Creatine Supplementation Enhance Performance in Active Females? A Systematic Review"))
