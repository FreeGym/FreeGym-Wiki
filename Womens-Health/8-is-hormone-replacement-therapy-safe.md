# Is Hormone Replacement Therapy Safe?

Systemic menopausal hormone therapy is not a single exposure. The first major split is between estrogen alone and estrogen plus a progestogen. In women with a uterus, unopposed estrogen probably increases endometrial hyperplasia risk, which is why progestogen-containing regimens are used when the endometrium must be protected. (Kim et al., 2025) ([PubMed][1])

So the evidence does not support either extreme slogan - “HRT is dangerous and should be avoided” or “HRT is safe and beneficial for everyone.” The balance changes with the regimen, route, duration, baseline risk, and time since menopause. For women younger than 60 years or within 10 years of menopause onset and without contraindications, the benefit-risk ratio is generally more favorable for symptom treatment and prevention of bone loss. (Rossouw et al., 2002; Anderson et al., 2004; Boardman et al., 2015; Goldštajn et al., 2023; The 2022 Hormone Therapy Position Statement Advisory Panel, 2022) ([PubMed][4]; [PubMed][8]; [PubMed][9]; [PubMed][11]; [PubMed][2])

## What changed after the WHI

The major shift in menopausal hormone therapy use after 2002 followed the Women’s Health Initiative (WHI) study results, but WHI tested specific oral regimens for chronic disease prevention in older postmenopausal women, not a generic category called “HRT.” Across the two hormone trials, the baseline mean age was 63.4 years. The interventions were oral conjugated equine estrogen (CEE) plus medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA) for women with a uterus, or oral CEE alone for women after hysterectomy. (Crawford et al., 2018; Manson et al., 2017) ([PubMed][5]; [PubMed][3])

In the combined CEE+MPA trial, absolute excesses per 10,000 person-years were 7 more coronary events, 8 more strokes, 8 more pulmonary emboli, and 8 more invasive breast cancers, with 5 fewer hip fractures and 6 fewer colorectal cancers. Overall health risks exceeded benefits for primary prevention. In the estrogen-alone CEE trial, stroke increased and hip fracture decreased, with no overall benefit for chronic disease prevention. (Rossouw et al., 2002; Anderson et al., 2004) ([PubMed][4]; [PubMed][8])

One reason the conversation later shifted is that post-WHI declines in use were seen even among younger symptomatic women for whom therapy is often considered, suggesting that the original risk message was widely generalized beyond the women actually studied. (Crawford et al., 2018) ([PubMed][5])

## Breast cancer risk depends strongly on the regimen

Randomized evidence does not support treating estrogen-only and combined therapy as the same thing. In long-term WHI follow-up, prior randomized CEE plus MPA increased breast cancer incidence (HR 1.28), whereas prior randomized CEE alone in women with prior hysterectomy lowered breast cancer incidence (HR 0.78) and breast-cancer mortality (HR 0.60). A 2024 meta-analysis of randomized estrogen-alone trials reached a similar conclusion across the included trials. But the 2025 Cochrane long-term review still rates the overall long-term estrogen-only effect on breast-cancer risk as probably little to no difference, so the most rigorous summary is that estrogen-only looks more favorable than combined therapy, not that it is proven broadly protective. (Chlebowski et al., 2020; Chlebowski et al., 2024; Bofill Rodriguez et al., 2025) ([PubMed][6]; [PubMed][19]; [PubMed][15])

Observational evidence is less reassuring for long-duration systemic use, especially for combined therapy. The 2019 Collaborative Group pooled analysis found excess breast-cancer risk with every systemic menopausal hormone therapy type except vaginal estrogens, with higher risk for estrogen-progestagen than estrogen-only regimens and a clear duration-response pattern. During years 1 to 4 of current use, the relative risk was 1.60 for estrogen-progestagen and 1.17 for estrogen-only; during years 5 to 14 it was 2.08 and 1.33, respectively. Some excess risk persisted for more than 10 years after stopping. This analysis was funded by Cancer Research UK and the Medical Research Council. (Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019) ([PubMed][7])

The cleanest reading is not that “estrogen protects against breast cancer” or that “all HRT causes breast cancer.” It is that breast-cancer risk is highly regimen-specific. Combined estrogen-progestogen has the clearest evidence for increased incidence. Estrogen-only looks materially different from combined therapy in randomized trials and generally more favorable on breast-cancer outcomes, though not risk-free overall and not proven broadly protective in all settings. (Chlebowski et al., 2020; Chlebowski et al., 2024; Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019; Bofill Rodriguez et al., 2025) ([PubMed][6]; [PubMed][19]; [PubMed][7]; [PubMed][15])

## Cardiovascular risk was not imaginary, but it was overgeneralized

Systemic hormone therapy should not be used to prevent chronic disease or cardiovascular disease. In the WHI estrogen-alone trial, CEE did not increase coronary heart disease but did increase stroke (HR 1.39) and reduced hip fracture (HR 0.61). The 2022 updated evidence review for the USPSTF similarly found that estrogen-only lowers fractures but increases stroke and venous thromboembolism, while estrogen-plus-progestin lowers fractures but increases invasive breast cancer, stroke, and venous thromboembolism. (Anderson et al., 2004; Gartlehner et al., 2022) ([PubMed][8]; [PubMed][16])

The cardiovascular risks, then, were real. But the original WHI findings were often generalized beyond the women actually studied, which is not what the long-term randomized data show. Over 18 years of WHI follow-up, hormone therapy was not associated with higher all-cause, cardiovascular, or cancer mortality overall. (Crawford et al., 2018; Manson et al., 2017) ([PubMed][5]; [PubMed][3])

## Timing matters, but mostly for coronary outcomes

The timing hypothesis has real support, but it should not be overstated. In a Cochrane review of cardiovascular outcomes, women who started hormone therapy less than 10 years after menopause had lower mortality (RR 0.70) and lower coronary heart disease risk (RR 0.52) than controls, but still had higher venous thromboembolism risk (RR 1.74). When therapy was started more than 10 years after menopause, there was no clear coronary benefit and both stroke (RR 1.21) and venous thromboembolism (RR 1.96) increased. The review reported no potential conflicts of interest. (Boardman et al., 2015) ([PubMed][9])

A mechanistic randomized trial points the same way. In ELITE, oral estradiol slowed progression of carotid intima-media thickness when started within 6 years after menopause, but not when started 10 or more years after menopause. Among WHI participants aged 50 to 59, estrogen alone also produced a more favorable global risk profile than placebo over long-term follow-up, whereas the combined CEE+MPA regimen did not. (Hodis et al., 2016; Prentice et al., 2021) ([PubMed][10]; [PubMed][20])

Timing does not seem to be a universal safety shield. In the large pooled breast-cancer analysis, relative risks for a given preparation were similar for women who started MHT at ages 40-44, 45-49, 50-54, and 55-59, with risk mainly tracking regimen and duration. So “start early” is mostly a coronary hypothesis, not a free pass on breast-cancer risk. (Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019) ([PubMed][7])

## Route matters

The best-supported route difference is venous thromboembolism. A 2023 systematic review found that evidence comparing oral and transdermal estrogen is limited and generally low quality, but identified VTE as the clearest clinical difference, supporting transdermal estrogen as safer than oral therapy on that outcome. A 2025 systematic review focused on women with VTE risk factors likewise found no increased VTE risk with transdermal estrogen in the included studies. (Goldštajn et al., 2023; Hicks et al., 2025) ([PubMed][11]; [PubMed][17])

That does not mean transdermal therapy is proven safer for every major endpoint. The strongest evidence supports a lower clotting-risk signal; evidence for other long-term outcomes remains thinner. (Goldštajn et al., 2023) ([PubMed][11])

## “Bioidentical” is not one evidence category

Claims that “bioidentical hormones are safer than synthetic hormones” run ahead of the evidence. For progesterone versus synthetic progestins, the main meta-analysis found lower breast-cancer risk with progesterone-containing regimens (RR 0.67), but this result came from only two cohort studies and one population-based case-control study, with moderate risk of bias, and there were no cardiovascular outcome data. That is suggestive observational evidence, not proof of superior overall clinical safety. (Asi et al., 2016) ([PubMed][12])

For compounded “bioidentical” hormone therapy, the evidence is weaker still. The main systematic review of randomized trials concluded that there were insufficient RCTs to assess breast-cancer, endometrial-cancer, or cardiovascular risk. This review also had a clear financial conflict: several authors were employees of Professional Compounding Centers of America (PCCA), a compounding-pharmacy supplier, and another author received a consulting fee from PCCA. Even this conflicted review did not show long-term safety; it showed that long-term safety is largely unstudied. ACOG’s 2023 Clinical Consensus likewise states that evidence to support claims of superior safety or effectiveness is lacking and that compounded bioidentical menopausal hormone therapy should not be prescribed routinely when FDA-approved formulations exist. (Liu et al., 2022; ACOG Clinical Consensus No. 6, 2023) ([PubMed][13]; [PubMed][18])

So the evidence-based answer is narrower than the marketing claim. Regimens using progesterone rather than synthetic progestins may differ on some outcomes, but broad claims of superior safety are not yet proven by randomized long-term clinical endpoints, and compounded products are especially under-evidenced. (Asi et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2022; ACOG Clinical Consensus No. 6, 2023) ([PubMed][12]; [PubMed][13]; [PubMed][18])

## Benefits that are real

Hormone therapy’s major proven benefit is symptom control. A 2025 network meta-analysis of 41 randomized trials found several estrogen-containing regimens among the most effective options for moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms. These trials had at least 12 weeks of follow-up, so they are more informative about symptom efficacy than about long-term cancer or cardiovascular safety. The review reported no known competing financial interests. (Amador et al., 2025) ([PubMed][14])

Bone protection is also real. In the long-term Cochrane review, fracture reduction remains the clearest long-term clinical benefit from hormone therapy. The 2022 USPSTF evidence report likewise found fewer fractures with both estrogen-only and estrogen-plus-progestin. A 2025 meta-analysis of 24 randomized trials also reported better menopause-specific quality-of-life scores and higher lumbar and hip bone density with HRT, though this review leaned on shorter-term trials and symptom scales rather than the major long-term safety endpoints; it reported no conflicts of interest. (Bofill Rodriguez et al., 2025; Gartlehner et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2025) ([PubMed][15]; [PubMed][16]; [PubMed][21])

## On funding and conflicts of interest

The conflict picture here is mixed, not absent. The large 2019 pooled breast-cancer analysis was funded by Cancer Research UK and the Medical Research Council, and the Cochrane cardiovascular review reported no potential conflicts of interest. The clearest conflict in the evidence used here is the compounded-bioidentical review described above. Some other papers in this list also report author-level disclosures; for example, the 2025 endometrial Cochrane update reports travel-related support to one author, and the 2020 WHI breast-cancer follow-up includes author financial disclosures. (Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019; Boardman et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2025; Chlebowski et al., 2020) ([PubMed][7]; [PubMed][9]; [PubMed][13]; [PubMed][1]; [PubMed][6])

## Quick reference values

* Combined oral CEE + MPA in WHI: 7 more coronary events, 8 more strokes, 8 more pulmonary emboli, and 8 more invasive breast cancers per 10,000 person-years; 5 fewer hip fractures. (Rossouw et al., 2002) ([PubMed][4])
* Estrogen-alone CEE in WHI: CHD HR 0.91; breast cancer HR 0.77 (95% CI 0.59-1.01); stroke HR 1.39; hip fracture HR 0.61 over average 6.8 years. (Anderson et al., 2004) ([PubMed][8])
* WHI long-term breast cancer follow-up: CEE alone HR 0.78 for incidence and 0.60 for breast-cancer mortality; CEE+MPA HR 1.28 for incidence. (Chlebowski et al., 2020) ([PubMed][6])
* Timing subgroup meta-analysis: started less than 10 years after menopause, RR 0.70 for mortality, RR 0.52 for CHD, RR 1.74 for VTE; started more than 10 years after menopause, RR 1.21 for stroke and RR 1.96 for VTE. (Boardman et al., 2015) ([PubMed][9])
* Observational pooled breast-cancer risk in current users: years 1 to 4, RR 1.60 for estrogen-progestagen and 1.17 for estrogen-only; years 5 to 14, RR 2.08 and 1.33. (Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019) ([PubMed][7])
* Compounded bioidentical hormone therapy: insufficient randomized evidence to assess breast-cancer, endometrial-cancer, or cardiovascular risk. (Liu et al., 2022) ([PubMed][13])

## Practical interpretation

* HRT is not one exposure. Estrogen-alone after hysterectomy and estrogen-plus-progestogen with a uterus have different risk profiles. (Kim et al., 2025; Anderson et al., 2004; Rossouw et al., 2002) ([PubMed][1]; [PubMed][8]; [PubMed][4])
* For healthy symptomatic women close to menopause, the balance is often more favorable than broad generalizations of the original WHI results implied, but systemic therapy still should not be sold as a way to prevent cardiovascular disease or chronic disease. (Crawford et al., 2018; The 2022 Hormone Therapy Position Statement Advisory Panel, 2022; Gartlehner et al., 2022) ([PubMed][5]; [PubMed][2]; [PubMed][16])
* Combined therapy carries the clearest breast-cancer signal. Estrogen-only looks different and generally more favorable for breast-cancer outcomes in randomized evidence, though not risk-free overall. (Chlebowski et al., 2020; Bofill Rodriguez et al., 2025; Collaborative Group on Hormonal Factors in Breast Cancer, 2019) ([PubMed][6]; [PubMed][15]; [PubMed][7])
* Timing and route matter, especially for cardiovascular and clotting risk: starting earlier is more favorable for coronary outcomes, and transdermal therapy likely lowers VTE risk compared with oral therapy. (Boardman et al., 2015; Hodis et al., 2016; Goldštajn et al., 2023; Hicks et al., 2025) ([PubMed][9]; [PubMed][10]; [PubMed][11]; [PubMed][17])
* “Bioidentical” is not a synonym for proven safer. Evidence suggesting progesterone may be better than synthetic progestins is observational, and compounded bioidentical products lack long-term clinical-endpoint data. (Asi et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2022; ACOG Clinical Consensus No. 6, 2023) ([PubMed][12]; [PubMed][13]; [PubMed][18])
