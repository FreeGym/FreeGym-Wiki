# Heavy Menstrual Bleeding

“Heavy periods” can be normal variation, a treatable symptom of a non-uterine issue (like ovulatory dysfunction), or a sign of an underlying disorder (including uterine pathology or a bleeding disorder). The hard part is that “heavy” is *both* subjective (how it affects your life) and, in older research, defined by an objective cutoff that doesn’t map neatly onto symptoms.

## What “heavy” means in research vs clinical care

Historically, “menorrhagia” was defined statistically as menstrual blood loss above about **80 mL per cycle**, based on population measurements that treated ~60–80 mL as the upper end of “normal.” ([Hallberg et al., 1966](https://doi.org/10.3109/00016346609158455)) ([OBGYN Journal][1])

Modern clinical terminology generally uses **heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB)** to mean bleeding that is **excessive for the individual and interferes with physical, social, emotional, and/or material quality of life**, whether or not the volume exceeds a specific mL cutoff. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][2])

Because objective measurement of mL blood loss is rarely done in routine care, many studies and clinics rely on symptom-based definitions or structured tools rather than laboratory quantification. ([Warner et al., 2004a](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.015); [Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([Enlighten Publications][3])

## Why hourly soak-through and clots can be alarming (and why they’re not a diagnosis)

Symptom-based HMB definitions used in research often include experiences like **“gushing/flooding”** or **bleeding through a pad/tampon within ≤2 hours**, alongside longer duration of menses. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656)) ([Haematologica][4])

Even so, symptom severity does not perfectly map onto the older “>80 mL” definition. In a cohort of women referred for “heavy periods,” **only ~34%** had measured blood loss >80 mL, illustrating how subjective “heavy” and objective “>80 mL” often diverge. ([Warner et al., 2004a](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.015)) ([Enlighten Publications][3])

This is why “hourly soak-through” (or clots) can be a strong signal that something needs attention *clinically* (because it disrupts life and can drive iron loss), but it doesn’t automatically identify the cause (fibroids vs ovulatory dysfunction vs bleeding disorder vs other). ([Munro et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][6])

## How common is HMB (and why estimates vary so much)

Across reviews, reported prevalence varies widely because studies use different definitions (objective mL thresholds vs self-report vs QoL-based definitions vs symptom checklists). A recent scoping review that synthesized systematic reviews reported prevalence estimates ranging from **~5% to ~58%** across settings and measurement approaches. ([Ammerdorffer et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12905-025-04157-5)) ([Springer][7])

A more recent systematic review of population prevalence studies (2007–2025) likewise reported wide variation, with examples ranging from **~15%** in population-based estimates using restrictive definitions up to **~65%** in surveys using symptom self-report. ([Maganti et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/S3050-5038%2825%2900167-0)) ([The Lancet][8])

## Do heavy periods always mean fibroids, endometriosis, or a clotting disorder?

No. HMB is a symptom with a broad differential diagnosis.

FIGO’s PALM–COEIN system classifies causes of abnormal uterine bleeding into **structural** causes (PALM: polyp, adenomyosis, leiomyoma, malignancy/hyperplasia) and **non-structural** causes (COEIN: coagulopathy, ovulatory dysfunction, endometrial, iatrogenic, not otherwise classified). ([Munro et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][6])

### Bleeding (clotting) disorders: important, but not “always”

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (53 studies; >41,000 patients) estimated an overall **~30% prevalence** of bleeding disorders among premenopausal patients evaluated for HMB (with wide uncertainty), with subgroup estimates around **~16% in adults** and **~39% in adolescents**. ([Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([ScienceDirect][9])

In that same meta-analysis, estimated pooled prevalences included **von Willebrand disease ~8%** and **platelet function defects ~9%** among patients evaluated for HMB. ([Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([ScienceDirect][9])

Older, narrower systematic review data focusing on von Willebrand disease among women with menorrhagia found a pooled prevalence around **~13%** (95% CI roughly 11–16%). ([Shankar et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1471-0528.2004.00176.x)) ([PubMed][10])

In a prospective multicenter US cohort of adolescents referred for HMB, **33%** were diagnosed with a bleeding disorder (including low vWF levels 16%, vWD 11%, and qualitative platelet dysfunction 4.5%), using a structured hematologic evaluation. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656)) ([Haematologica][4])

### Structural causes (like fibroids) are common—but not the only story

In a large cross-sectional study of **chronic abnormal uterine bleeding** (not limited to HMB alone) in reproductive-aged women, **non-structural causes predominated** (about **~65% COEIN vs ~35% PALM**), with ovulatory dysfunction the single most common classification. ([Sun et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000011457)) ([PMC][11])

That same study illustrates a key practical point: even when bleeding is abnormal, **many patients fall into non-structural categories** (especially ovulatory dysfunction), so “it must be fibroids” is not supported as a universal rule. ([Sun et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000011457)) ([PMC][11])

### What about endometriosis?

In FIGO’s widely used AUB etiologic classification (PALM–COEIN), **endometriosis is not a primary etiologic category for abnormal uterine bleeding**, which reflects that HMB is not considered specific to endometriosis in the same way structural uterine causes and hemostatic/ovulatory causes are categorized. ([Munro et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][6])

That doesn’t mean endometriosis cannot coexist with HMB in an individual—it means that when clinicians and researchers classify *causes of bleeding*, they typically work through PALM–COEIN categories rather than treating “endometriosis” as the default explanation for heavy flow. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][2])

## Do heavy periods always cause iron deficiency?

Heavy bleeding increases *risk* of iron deficiency, but it is not iron deficiency by definition—and not everyone with heavy bleeding becomes iron deficient.

Measured menstrual blood loss is strongly related to iron lost in menstrual fluid. In an observational study quantifying iron loss, women with menorrhagia had substantially higher median iron losses per cycle than women with “normal” blood loss, and iron loss correlated strongly with measured blood loss. ([Napolitano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00277-013-1901-3)) ([PubMed][12])

In a 2024 study of premenopausal blood donors, menstrual blood loss was an **independent determinant** of both hemoglobin and ferritin; HMB defined by PBAC ≥150 had a prevalence of **~13%** in that donor cohort and was associated with anemia (OR ~3.6). ([Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890)) ([University of Helsinki][13])

However, the classic “>80 mL” cutoff does *not* cleanly separate people with and without iron problems. In the “Menorrhagia II” study, the authors concluded the 80 mL criterion had limited clinical usefulness because it was not prognostic for problems **including iron status** in referred women. ([Warner et al., 2004b](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.016)) ([PubMed][14])

Taken together: heavier bleeding pushes ferritin and hemoglobin down on average, but individual outcomes depend on baseline iron stores, dietary intake/absorption, and other iron losses (e.g., blood donation). ([Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890); [Napolitano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00277-013-1901-3)) ([University of Helsinki][13])

## If you’ve “always been heavy” and labs are normal, is it just your baseline?

Sometimes heavy flow can be a longstanding personal baseline (especially under the QoL-based definition). But “I’ve always been this way” does **not** reliably rule out an underlying condition.

Bleeding disorders are often present from adolescence, and meta-analytic and prospective cohort data show a non-trivial prevalence among people evaluated for HMB—especially adolescents. ([Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014); [Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656)) ([ScienceDirect][9])

In the adolescent prospective cohort, time from first bleed to bleeding disorder diagnosis was measured in years (median 4 years overall), highlighting that longstanding symptoms can still reflect an undiagnosed disorder rather than “normal for me.” ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656)) ([Haematologica][4])

And because iron effects are variable, “normal hemoglobin” does not necessarily imply “no impact,” since iron deficiency can occur before anemia and ferritin is a separate marker of iron status used in research definitions. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656); [Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890)) ([Haematologica][4])

## Competing claims: what the evidence supports (and what it doesn’t)

**“Heavy periods always indicate fibroids, endometriosis, or clotting disorders.”**
HMB has multiple etiologies under PALM–COEIN, and evidence shows substantial proportions without those specific diagnoses; bleeding disorders are common enough to consider, but not universal (e.g., ~16% adult pooled estimate in one meta-analysis). ([Munro et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014); [Sun et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000011457)) ([OBGYN Journal][6])

**“Flow varies widely among healthy women, and ‘heavy’ is often normal.”**
Measured menstrual blood loss varies widely in populations, and “heavy” symptoms often don’t match the >80 mL threshold (only ~34% exceeded it in one referral cohort), which is why current definitions emphasize QoL impact rather than an mL cutoff. ([Hallberg et al., 1966](https://doi.org/10.3109/00016346609158455); [Warner et al., 2004a](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.015); [Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][1])

**“Heavy periods always cause iron deficiency and need treatment.”**
Iron loss increases with blood loss and HMB is associated with lower ferritin/hemoglobin on average, but the 80 mL criterion alone does not reliably predict iron status and not everyone with heavy symptoms becomes iron deficient. ([Napolitano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00277-013-1901-3); [Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890); [Warner et al., 2004b](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.016)) ([PubMed][12])

**“If you’ve always had heavy periods and labs are normal, it’s just your baseline.”**
Longstanding symptoms can be baseline variation *or* reflect a persistent, undiagnosed disorder; adolescents with HMB had a 33% bleeding-disorder diagnosis rate in a prospective cohort, and meta-analytic data suggest bleeding-disorder prevalence is meaningfully higher in adolescents than adults. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656); [Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([Haematologica][4])

## Quick reference: evidence-based anchors

* Older “statistical abnormality” definition: **~>80 mL/cycle** as the historical menorrhagia cutoff. ([Hallberg et al., 1966](https://doi.org/10.3109/00016346609158455); [Warner et al., 2004b](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.016)) ([OBGYN Journal][1])
* Modern clinical definition: **bleeding that interferes with QoL**, not strictly an mL cutoff. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][2])
* “My periods are heavy” ≠ “>80 mL”: **~34%** exceeded 80 mL in one referred cohort. ([Warner et al., 2004a](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.015)) ([Enlighten Publications][3])
* Bleeding disorders among people evaluated for HMB: **pooled ~30% overall**, with **~16% adults** and **~39% adolescents** in one 2025 meta-analysis. ([Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([ScienceDirect][9])
* Adolescents referred for HMB: **33%** diagnosed with a bleeding disorder in a prospective cohort. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656)) ([Haematologica][4])
* Iron: menstrual blood loss is an **independent determinant** of ferritin/hemoglobin; HMB (PBAC≥150) associated with anemia in one donor cohort. ([Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890)) ([University of Helsinki][13])

## Practical interpretation

* If “heavy” means “it’s disrupting my life,” that aligns with the modern clinical definition of HMB even if an objective mL cutoff isn’t known. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1002/ijgo.12666)) ([OBGYN Journal][2])
* HMB is a **symptom**, not a diagnosis—classification frameworks and meta-analyses show multiple possible etiologies (structural, ovulatory/endocrine, hemostatic, and sometimes no clear cause). ([Munro et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Hall et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12905-024-02921-7); [Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([OBGYN Journal][6])
* Iron problems are common enough to take seriously, but they’re not inevitable; measured volume cutoffs alone don’t perfectly predict iron status. ([Ekroos et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/aogs.14890); [Warner et al., 2004b](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajog.2003.11.016)) ([University of Helsinki][13])
* Longstanding heavy bleeding can still merit evaluation—especially if it started around menarche—because bleeding disorders are relatively prevalent in adolescent HMB cohorts. ([Zia et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3324/haematol.2019.225656); [Comishen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtha.2024.11.014)) ([Haematologica][4])
