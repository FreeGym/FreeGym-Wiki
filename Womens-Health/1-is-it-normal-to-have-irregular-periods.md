# Is it normal to have irregular periods?

"Normal" menstrual cycles don't run like clockwork, and there's no single universal cutoff for "irregular." Researchers typically define irregularity using statistical thresholds for cycle frequency (days from one period to the next) and regularity (how much that frequency varies within a person), with different expectations for different ages. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

One thing worth understanding: ovulatory function exists on a spectrum from a single off cycle to a recurring chronic pattern, so one irregular cycle isn't the same as persistent irregularity. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

## What "regular" means in clinical definitions

The International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics (FIGO) defines a menstrual cycle as the interval from the start of bleeding in one cycle to the start of bleeding in the next. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

FIGO uses population percentiles to define "normal" cycle frequency in adults as 24-38 days. Cycles under 24 days are considered "frequent" and cycles over 38 days are "infrequent." (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

FIGO defines regularity by the difference between a person's shortest and longest cycle lengths over time, with age-specific cutoffs: ≤9 days difference for ages 18-25 and 42-45, and ≤7 days difference for ages 26-41. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

Under this framework, people with infrequent or irregular bleeding should be considered to have an ovulatory disorder phenotype until proven otherwise, because irregular bleeding patterns often reflect ovulatory dysfunction. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

## Why "21-35 days is the only healthy range" is too strict

The commonly repeated "21-35 days" window isn't the only research-backed framing.

In a large U.S. digital cohort (Apple Women's Health Study), participants without reported PCOS or fibroids had a median cycle length of 28 days (IQR 26-30) and 5th-95th percentiles of 22-38 days. A non-trivial fraction of apparently healthy cycles fall outside 21-35 days depending on how "healthy" is defined and which percentile cutoffs are used. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

That study compared its observed distribution to FIGO's "normal" range (24-38 days) and found a similar upper tail (95th percentile) and a somewhat shorter lower tail (5th percentile). (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

## How much cycle-to-cycle variation is typical?

Different studies define "variability" differently (standard deviation across cycles, adjacent-cycle differences, or shortest-to-longest range), so "normal" variation depends on the metric used. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

### Population-level cycle variability

In the Apple Women's Health Study, average within-individual cycle variability was about 4-6 days across most age groups. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

In a global Flo app cohort (over 1 million users), 69% of women had less than 6 days of cycle-length variation, and 25% had 0-1.5 days of variation. Larger variation (6+ days) was more common in younger women. (Grieger & Norman, 2020). ([JMIR][4])

### Variability in prescreened healthy women

In a prospective 1-year study of healthy women prescreened for normal-length and ovulatory cycles, the median within-woman variance in cycle length was 3.1 days (range 0.25-27.25). Even in a carefully selected "healthy/ovulatory" sample, some individuals show substantial variability. (Henry et al., 2024). ([OUP Academic][5])

### How "7-9 days" fits into this

The "7-9 days" concept shows up in two different ways:

FIGO's definition of "regular" cycle frequency uses a shortest-to-longest difference of ≤7 or ≤9 days depending on age group. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

Some research definitions (including the Apple Women's Health Study) define irregularity using adjacent-cycle changes (e.g., mean difference between adjacent cycles ≥7 days), which is a different metric than FIGO's shortest-to-longest range. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

So "7-9 days is normal" is a threshold/terminology statement about how regularity is defined, not proof that any specific individual with that much variation has no underlying issue. (Munro et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

## Life stages where irregularity is common

### Adolescence

FIGO notes that in people under 18, infrequent and irregular cycles are common. "Normal" cycle length may not be established until about the sixth year after menarche. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

In the first year after menarche, ovulatory dysfunction may be present in roughly 50% of adolescents, with typical cycle length often 21-45 days, though cycles can be as short as 20 or over 60 days. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

### Menopausal transition

FIGO describes the years beyond ~45 as a transition with more infrequent and irregular cycles and endocrine variability, consistent with increased cycle instability approaching menopause. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

The Apple Women's Health Study found cycle variability lowest at ages 35-39, higher in younger and older groups (under 20, 45-49), and markedly higher over 50. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

## "Irregular periods always signal hormonal problems": what the evidence supports

Irregular or infrequent bleeding patterns are treated as an ovulatory disorder phenotype in FIGO terminology. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

But FIGO also notes that ovulatory dysfunction exists on a spectrum from singular episodes to recurring chronic disorders, which is why episodic irregularity can occur without implying a permanent condition. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

And "regular cycles" don't guarantee ovulation: FIGO reports that single-cycle anovulation can occur even with otherwise regular bleeding patterns, with reported frequencies ranging from 3.7% to 26.7% across studies. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

## What degree of irregularity predicts health problems?

The strongest evidence linking cycle irregularity to later health outcomes comes from large prospective cohorts and meta-analyses, which treat menstrual cycle characteristics as risk markers.

### Type 2 diabetes

In the Nurses' Health Study II (1993-2017; 75,546 premenopausal women), irregular and long cycles across the reproductive lifespan were associated with higher risk of incident type 2 diabetes. Reporting always-irregular cycles or no periods at ages 29-46 was associated with 66% higher risk compared with very regular cycles, with similar elevated risks for earlier age windows. Cycle length ≥40 days was also associated with increased risk compared with 26-31 days. (Wang et al., 2020). ([JAMA Network][6])

The associations were stronger in the context of overweight/obesity and other lifestyle risk factors like diet quality and physical activity. (Wang et al., 2020). ([JAMA Network][6])

### Cardiovascular disease

In a Nurses' Health Study II analysis of 80,630 women followed over 24 years, irregular cycles or no periods and long cycles were associated with increased CVD risk. Irregular/no periods at ages 29-46 had a hazard ratio of 1.40, and cycle length ≥40 days at ages 18-22 had a hazard ratio of 1.44 vs 26-31 days, with similar associations in other age windows. (Wang et al., 2022). ([JAMA Network][7])

### Meta-analysis of cardiometabolic outcomes

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis found that irregular menstrual cycles were associated with higher risks of multiple cardiometabolic outcomes: overall cardiovascular disease (RR 1.43) and diabetes (RR 1.75) in pooled observational data. (Afzal et al., 2026). ([ScienceDirect][8])

These data support a practical distinction: persistent irregularity and/or very long cycles (e.g., ≥40 days or too irregular to estimate) show clearer links to later cardiometabolic risk than small day-to-day variation around a stable average. (Wang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Afzal et al., 2026). ([JAMA Network][6])

## Stress, travel, and lifestyle changes

Direct experimental evidence is limited (for obvious ethical/logistical reasons), but the evidence base does support stress as a correlate of menstrual disruption.

A 2024 systematic review of 41 studies concluded that most studies report an association between psychological stress and menstrual dysfunction, with common disruptions including irregular menstruation and abnormal menstrual flow. That review found no consistent relation between stress and abnormal cycle length. (Poitras et al., 2024). ([ScienceDirect][9])

Large observational datasets also report associations between menstrual cycle characteristics and lifestyle/health factors, though these aren't designed to establish causality. The Apple Women's Health Study reported associations between higher BMI and longer cycles and greater variability. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

## Quick reference thresholds

These are definitions/thresholds used in practice and research. They're helpful for deciding when variation is "within expected bounds" vs "irregular" by terminology, but not a guarantee of health or disease.

Cycle frequency (adults): "Normal" is 24-38 days; frequent is under 24; infrequent is over 38. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

Regularity (cycle-to-cycle range): shortest-to-longest difference ≤7 days (ages 26-41) or ≤9 days (ages 18-25 and 42-45). (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

Typical variability (large cohorts): many individuals show variability in the "few days" range, e.g., average within-individual variability ~4-6 days in Apple Women's Health Study. (Li et al., 2023). ([Nature][2])

Common in app data: 69% with less than 6-day variation in one large global app cohort. (Grieger & Norman, 2020). ([JMIR][4])

Amenorrhea definition (secondary): cessation of bleeding for ≥180 days after previous menses in a previously menstruating person. (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

## Practical interpretation

"Regular cycles (21-35 days) are the only healthy pattern." Evidence-based definitions commonly use a broader "normal" window (e.g., FIGO 24-38 days), and large cohorts show meaningful tails beyond 35 days even when excluding some diagnosed gynecologic conditions. (Munro et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

"Variation of 7-9 days cycle-to-cycle is normal and not concerning." A 7-9 day threshold is best understood as a regularity definition (shortest-to-longest difference) and a commonly used research cutoff, while typical variability in large cohorts is often smaller. (Munro et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Grieger & Norman, 2020). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

"Irregular periods always signal hormonal problems (PCOS, thyroid, stress)." Irregular/infrequent bleeding patterns are treated as an ovulatory-disorder phenotype, but ovulatory dysfunction can be episodic (not necessarily chronic) and irregularity is also common at specific life stages (post-menarche years; menopausal transition). (Munro et al., 2022). ([orbi.uliege.be][1])

"Stress, travel, and lifestyle changes cause temporary irregularity that self-corrects." Higher-quality evidence supports an association between psychological stress and menstrual dysfunction, and FIGO's framework allows for singular episodes of ovulatory disruption, consistent with the idea that some irregularity can be transient. (Poitras et al., 2024; Munro et al., 2022). ([ScienceDirect][9])
