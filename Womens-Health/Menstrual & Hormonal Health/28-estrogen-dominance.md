# Estrogen Dominance

"Estrogen dominance" is best understood as a loose wellness shorthand for several different physiologic patterns, not as a validated clinical diagnosis. The phrase can point toward something real - estrogenic activity that is relatively insufficiently opposed by progesterone in a specific context - but it does not have a standardized estrogen-to-progesterone cutoff, a validated symptom profile, or a single treatment pathway. Clinical and research literature generally discusses pieces of these situations under more precise categories: ovulatory dysfunction and abnormal uterine bleeding, luteal-phase deficiency, perimenopause, fibroids, endometriosis and progesterone resistance, PMS/PMDD, or exogenous hormone exposure. ([ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction); [Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009); [Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682); [Ali et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/12/8/1117); [Schmidt et al., 2017](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/appi.ajp.2017.16101113); [Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/))

## What the term is and is not

"Estrogen dominance" is not a formal disease entity in the way that polycystic ovary syndrome, endometriosis, uterine fibroids, abnormal uterine bleeding due to ovulatory dysfunction, or premature ovarian insufficiency are clinical entities. It is not diagnosed by a single serum estradiol value, a saliva hormone panel, or a universal estrogen/progesterone ratio. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/))

The phrase is most defensible when it means "estrogenic signaling is relatively unopposed by progesterone in this person, at this time, in this tissue." That can happen during anovulatory cycles, some perimenopausal cycles, some luteal-phase patterns, and in tissue-specific conditions such as endometriosis or fibroids. ([ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009); [Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682))

The phrase becomes misleading when it implies that PMS, breast tenderness, weight gain, fibroids, mood symptoms, or heavy bleeding all come from the same hormone imbalance and should all be treated by "raising progesterone," using progesterone cream, detoxing estrogen, or taking hormone-balancing supplements. Trials and reviews do not support that broad treatment logic. ([Ford et al., 2012](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome); [Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/); [Donnez, 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/9/12/3948))

## Why the biology sounds plausible

Estradiol and progesterone change dramatically across a normal menstrual cycle. Estradiol rises before ovulation and also contributes during the luteal phase, while progesterone is low before ovulation and rises after ovulation because the corpus luteum produces it. In one study of healthy women with daily cycle monitoring, median serum progesterone was very low in the follicular phase and much higher in the luteal phase, while estradiol peaked around ovulation. ([Anckaert et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352551721000111))

That means the estrogen/progesterone "ratio" is naturally high before ovulation, changes around ovulation, and changes again after ovulation. A ratio without cycle phase, ovulation timing, assay method, and clinical context is therefore difficult to interpret. Estradiol measurement itself is also assay-sensitive, especially at lower concentrations, which is one reason phase-specific interpretation matters. ([Verdonk et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0009898119318121); [Anckaert et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352551721000111))

The biologic idea of "unopposed estrogen" is real in a narrower sense. In anovulatory cycles, ovulation does not occur, the corpus luteum does not form normally, and progesterone exposure may be inadequate to organize and stabilize the endometrium after estrogen-driven proliferation. This is why abnormal uterine bleeding due to ovulatory dysfunction is a recognized clinical category. ([ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction); [Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011))

## Where the concept maps onto real clinical patterns

### Anovulation and abnormal uterine bleeding

The strongest clinical version of the idea is anovulatory bleeding. If estrogen stimulates the endometrium without the usual post-ovulatory progesterone pattern, bleeding can become irregular, prolonged, or heavy. Clinically, this is better described as abnormal uterine bleeding due to ovulatory dysfunction, not as a general diagnosis of "estrogen dominance." ([ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction); [Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011))

This distinction matters because the workup is not simply "check estrogen." The clinician needs to ask whether ovulation is occurring, whether bleeding is structurally driven by polyps or fibroids, whether there are coagulopathy or medication contributors, whether thyroid/prolactin/PCOS patterns are present, and whether endometrial evaluation is needed based on age, risk factors, and bleeding pattern. ([Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction))

### Perimenopause

Perimenopause is another setting where the "relative estrogen excess" framing can be partly true. The menopause transition is characterized by irregular bleeding patterns and wide hormone fluctuations. Early in the transition, estradiol is often preserved and may be intermittently high, while ovulation and luteal progesterone exposure can become less consistent. Later in the transition, estrogen becomes more variable and generally declines. ([Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

This is why some perimenopausal people can have breast tenderness, heavier or less predictable bleeding, migraines, mood changes, and sleep disruption during a period that is not simply "low estrogen." But perimenopause is not one stable hormone state; it is a fluctuating transition. "Estrogen dominance" may describe one slice of that transition, but it does not describe the whole transition. ([Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

### Luteal-phase deficiency

Luteal-phase deficiency is the clinical term often closest to what wellness content means by "low progesterone." The [American Society for Reproductive Medicine](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/) describes clinically detected luteal-phase deficiency classically as a short luteal phase, often 10 days or less, while emphasizing that the condition remains difficult to diagnose reliably. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

A single progesterone measurement above 3 ng/mL supports that ovulation occurred, but ASRM states that no minimum serum progesterone concentration reliably defines a normal fertile luteal phase. Progesterone is secreted in pulses and can fluctuate substantially over short periods, which makes one-off testing especially limited. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

So, "low progesterone" can be real, but it is not always easy to prove, and it should not be inferred from symptoms alone. A more precise question is: Was this cycle ovulatory, how long was the luteal phase, when was progesterone measured relative to ovulation, and what clinical outcome is being evaluated? ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

## Where the concept breaks down

The concept breaks down when it assumes that symptoms are caused by absolute estrogen excess or a fixed estrogen/progesterone ratio. PMS and PMDD are the clearest examples. Experimental work suggests that PMDD symptoms can reflect abnormal sensitivity to normal ovarian hormone changes, rather than consistently abnormal hormone levels. In classic ovarian-suppression and hormone add-back studies, symptoms improved when ovarian cycling was suppressed and returned when estradiol/progesterone exposure was reintroduced in susceptible participants. ([Schmidt et al., 1998](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199801223380401); [Schmidt et al., 2017](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/appi.ajp.2017.16101113))

Progesterone treatment also does not reliably support the simplified "low progesterone causes PMS" model. A [Cochrane review of progesterone for premenstrual syndrome](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome) did not establish progesterone as an effective PMS treatment and did not identify a subgroup that clearly benefited; the evidence was insufficient rather than definitively negative. ([Ford et al., 2012](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome))

A newer study in women consulting for infertility or recurrent miscarriage found that some PMS symptoms were associated with lower progesterone and a lower progesterone/estradiol ratio, but the ratio did not discriminate PMS well, and it was not stronger than progesterone alone. This supports a possible biologic signal in some subgroups, not a validated diagnostic ratio for the general population. ([Turner et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43032-023-01375-w))

## Symptom-by-symptom interpretation

### PMS and mood symptoms

PMS and PMDD are not best explained as simply "too much estrogen." The stronger model is hormone sensitivity: normal cyclic changes in estradiol, progesterone, and progesterone metabolites can trigger symptoms in susceptible people. ([Schmidt et al., 1998](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199801223380401); [Schmidt et al., 2017](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/appi.ajp.2017.16101113))

That does not mean hormones are irrelevant. It means the problem may be the brain's response to hormone change, rather than a consistently abnormal hormone level. This is one reason prospective symptom tracking across at least two cycles is more informative than a single hormone panel ordered without cycle timing. ([Schmidt et al., 2017](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/appi.ajp.2017.16101113); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

### Breast tenderness

Cyclic breast tenderness is plausibly hormone-related because it often tracks the luteal phase and improves after menstruation, but the evidence does not establish a universal estrogen/progesterone ratio that diagnoses it. Reviews describe several proposed mechanisms, including estrogen, progesterone, prolactin, and breast-tissue sensitivity, rather than one confirmed endocrine cause. Studies have not consistently shown abnormal circulating estrogen, progesterone, or prolactin levels in people with mastalgia, so tissue sensitivity and cyclic responsiveness may matter as much as serum levels. ([Kataria et al., 2014](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4141056/))

### Heavy bleeding

Heavy or irregular bleeding can occur with anovulation and relative lack of progesterone effect on the endometrium, but it can also come from fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis, coagulopathies, medications, endometrial disorders, malignancy/hyperplasia, or causes not otherwise classified. This is why the PALM-COEIN framework separates structural from non-structural causes of abnormal uterine bleeding. ([Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction))

### Fibroids

Fibroids are hormone-responsive, but the simple "estrogen grows fibroids, progesterone fixes estrogen dominance" story is incomplete. Progesterone signaling can promote fibroid-cell proliferation, extracellular matrix accumulation, and growth-related pathways, although some progestin-based therapies may reduce bleeding in selected patients through endometrial effects. Reviews of progestogen treatment for fibroids have found limited or low-quality evidence for benefit, and some authors argue that routine progestogen use for fibroids has historically exceeded the evidence base. ([Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682); [Ali et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/12/8/1117); [Donnez, 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/9/12/3948); [Sangkomkamhang et al., 2020](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD008994_progestogens-or-progestogen-releasing-intrauterine-systems-uterine-fibroids-other-preoperative))

This is a key place where the wellness framing can be actively misleading. If someone has fibroids and heavy bleeding, the appropriate question is not "how do we add progesterone to balance estrogen?" It is "what is the size, location, bleeding impact, anemia status, fertility goal, and best evidence-based treatment option?" ([Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Sangkomkamhang et al., 2020](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD008994_progestogens-or-progestogen-releasing-intrauterine-systems-uterine-fibroids-other-preoperative))

### Endometriosis

Endometriosis is often estrogen-dependent, but the more precise concept is local estrogen production/signaling, inflammation, and progesterone resistance, not necessarily high systemic estrogen. Endometriosis tissue can behave as though it is inadequately restrained by progesterone even when blood hormone levels do not look dramatically abnormal. ([Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682))

## Testing: what is useful and what is not

Hormone testing can be useful when it is tied to a specific clinical question. Examples include confirming ovulation, evaluating amenorrhea or irregular cycles, assessing suspected PCOS or thyroid/prolactin contributors, investigating infertility, or contextualizing perimenopausal symptoms. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

Progesterone testing needs ovulation-based timing. A "day 21 progesterone" only makes sense for someone who ovulates around day 14 of a 28-day cycle. For longer, shorter, or irregular cycles, testing should be timed to the estimated mid-luteal window after ovulation, and even then a single value mainly confirms ovulation rather than proving an optimal luteal phase. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

Estradiol also needs phase-specific interpretation. A value that is normal before ovulation may mean something different in the early follicular phase, midcycle, luteal phase, perimenopause, or while using exogenous hormones. ([Anckaert et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352551721000111); [Verdonk et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0009898119318121); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

Saliva or spot hormone ratios used to sell compounded hormones or progesterone creams should be treated cautiously. A review of "bioidentical" hormone marketing found that progesterone creams were promoted with broad claims around "estrogen dominance," while reliable evidence for many of those claims was lacking. More recent guidance from [ACOG](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/clinical-consensus/articles/2023/11/compounded-bioidentical-menopausal-hormone-therapy), [NAMS](https://journals.lww.com/menopausejournal/fulltext/2022/07000/the_2022_hormone_therapy_position_statement_of_the.4.aspx), and the [Endocrine Society](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/101/4/1318/2804494) also cautions against using salivary or spot hormone testing to individualize compounded hormone dosing. ([Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/); [ACOG, 2023](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/clinical-consensus/articles/2023/11/compounded-bioidentical-menopausal-hormone-therapy); [NAMS, 2022](https://journals.lww.com/menopausejournal/fulltext/2022/07000/the_2022_hormone_therapy_position_statement_of_the.4.aspx); [Santoro et al., 2016](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/101/4/1318/2804494))

## Treatment implications

The practical response to someone who identifies with "estrogen dominance" should not be dismissal. The symptoms may be very real. The right move is to translate the label into testable clinical questions.

If the main issue is irregular or heavy bleeding, evaluate for ovulatory dysfunction and structural causes such as fibroids, polyps, or adenomyosis. ([Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction))

If the issue is cyclic mood symptoms, distinguish PMS from PMDD with prospective symptom tracking and consider evidence-based PMS/PMDD approaches rather than assuming progesterone deficiency. ([Schmidt et al., 1998](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199801223380401); [Ford et al., 2012](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome))

If the issue is suspected luteal-phase dysfunction, confirm ovulation timing, luteal length, and clinical context; do not diagnose it from one poorly timed progesterone value. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

If the issue is perimenopause, recognize that estradiol can surge or fluctuate, progesterone exposure can fall in some cycles, cycles can become irregular, and symptoms can fluctuate. Management should be based on the symptom pattern, bleeding pattern, risk profile, and treatment goals rather than on the assumption of one stable hormone imbalance. ([Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

If the issue is fibroids or endometriosis, tissue-specific biology matters. Progesterone can be therapeutic in some gynecologic contexts, but it is not universally anti-estrogenic in fibroids, and endometriosis is often better described through estrogen dependence plus progesterone resistance. ([Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682); [Ali et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/12/8/1117))

## Marketing and funding-bias note

The "estrogen dominance" framework has been used to market progesterone creams, compounded "bioidentical" hormones, saliva testing, supplements, detox protocols, and hormone-balancing programs. A review by [Fugh-Berman and Bythrow](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/) specifically describes progesterone cream marketing that linked broad symptoms and conditions to "estrogen dominance," while noting that reliable evidence for many claims was lacking. ([Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/))

Conflict-of-interest note: that review was not a randomized trial; it was a critical review. The paper disclosed partial support from an Attorney General Prescriber and Consumer Education Grant, plaintiff-side expert testimony by one author related to hormone therapy litigation, and pharmaceutical-sales employment by the other author. Those disclosures do not invalidate the paper, but they should be considered when weighing it. ([Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/))

## Evidence gaps

There is no well-validated estrogen/progesterone ratio cutoff that diagnoses "estrogen dominance" across cycle phases, ages, assays, and clinical outcomes. Normal ratios vary across the cycle, estradiol assays vary, and progesterone secretion is pulsatile. ([Anckaert et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352551721000111); [Verdonk et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0009898119318121); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

There is no strong RCT evidence base for treating a self-identified "estrogen dominance" population as one group. The closest evidence comes from narrower literatures: progesterone for PMS, progesterone/progestogens in abnormal bleeding contexts, menopausal hormone therapy, fibroid treatment, and fertility-related luteal support. These cannot be collapsed into one generalized protocol. ([Ford et al., 2012](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Sangkomkamhang et al., 2020](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD008994_progestogens-or-progestogen-releasing-intrauterine-systems-uterine-fibroids-other-preoperative))

The most useful future research would be prospective and cycle-timed: daily or repeated hormone measures, ovulation confirmation, symptom diaries, bleeding quantification, imaging where relevant, and subgroup analyses by age, cycle regularity, perimenopause stage, fibroids, endometriosis, PCOS, and medication exposure. ([Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Turner et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43032-023-01375-w))

Qualitative research would also help, because the popularity of "estrogen dominance" signals a language gap. Patients are often describing cyclic suffering, heavy bleeding, breast tenderness, mood shifts, or perimenopausal volatility; clinicians may hear an imprecise wellness label. The better clinical move is to translate the label into physiology, not to dismiss the person using it. ([Fugh-Berman & Bythrow, 2007](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2219716/); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))

## Practical interpretation

"Estrogen dominance" captures a real intuition: estrogen and progesterone balance changes across the cycle, and some symptoms can occur when progesterone exposure is absent, reduced, mistimed, or ineffective at the tissue level. That intuition is strongest for anovulatory bleeding, some perimenopausal cycles, and some luteal-phase questions. ([ACOG, 2013](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2013/07/management-of-abnormal-uterine-bleeding-associated-with-ovulatory-dysfunction); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009); [ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/))

But as a diagnosis, the term is too broad. PMS, PMDD, fibroids, endometriosis, breast tenderness, weight change, heavy bleeding, and perimenopause do not share one proven estrogen/progesterone-ratio abnormality or one treatment. ([Schmidt et al., 1998](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199801223380401); [Ford et al., 2012](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003415_progesterone-premenstrual-syndrome); [Kim et al., 2013](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/34/1/130/2354682); [Sangkomkamhang et al., 2020](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD008994_progestogens-or-progestogen-releasing-intrauterine-systems-uterine-fibroids-other-preoperative))

The most evidence-based use of the phrase is as a starting point for investigation: Are cycles ovulatory? Is bleeding abnormal? Is progesterone being measured at the right time? Is the person in perimenopause? Are fibroids, endometriosis, PCOS, thyroid disease, hyperprolactinemia, medication effects, or pregnancy relevant? Once that is clarified, the treatment should target the actual condition rather than the label. ([ASRM, 2021](https://www.asrm.org/practice-guidance/practice-committee-documents/diagnosis-and-treatment-of-luteal-phase-deciency-a-committee-opinion-2021/); [Munro et al., 2011](https://obgyn.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1016/j.ijgo.2010.11.011); [Santoro et al., 2021](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/106/1/1/5937009))
