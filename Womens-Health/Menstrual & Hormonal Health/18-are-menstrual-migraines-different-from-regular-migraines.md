# Menstrual Migraine

Menstrual migraine is best understood as a reproducible migraine pattern linked to the menstrual window, not simply any headache that happens during a period. The strongest evidence supports a real hormone-linked pattern in some women, but diagnosis is imperfect, and “period headache” should not automatically be treated as menstrual migraine. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Vetvik and MacGregor, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1474442220304828))

## What menstrual migraine is and is not

The ICHD-3 Appendix defines pure menstrual migraine without aura as migraine attacks that occur only on day 1 ± 2 of menstruation, meaning days -2 to +3 if day 1 is the first day of bleeding, in at least two out of three menstrual cycles. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/))

Menstrually related migraine uses the same menstrual window, but attacks also occur at other times of the cycle. This is the more common pattern: many women have ordinary non-menstrual migraine attacks plus a higher-risk menstrual window. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Chalmer et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2804871))

The menstrual window includes both natural menstruation and endometrial bleeding after withdrawal of exogenous progestogens, such as bleeding during the hormone-free interval of combined oral contraceptives or cyclic hormone replacement therapy. That matters because hormone-withdrawal intervals can themselves be a predictable setting for attacks in some combined hormonal contraceptive users. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Merki-Feld et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7315546/))

A headache during a period is not enough for diagnosis. The attack still has to meet migraine criteria, and the timing has to repeat across cycles. A prospective diary is recommended for research and is often clinically useful because self-report does not reliably distinguish menstrual migraine from migraine that happens to coincide with bleeding. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

## Why diagnosis is harder than it looks

The “two out of three cycles” rule is practical, but it can misclassify people, especially those with frequent migraine. If someone has many migraine days per month, some attacks will fall in the menstrual window by chance. A statistical-method study concluded that the current criteria are useful for screening but can be too nonspecific for precise diagnosis. ([Barra et al., 2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-019-1035-7))

A large population-based study also found diagnostic problems with the standard criteria. In women with high-frequency episodic migraine or chronic migraine, the usual menstrually related migraine criteria misclassified many cases; the authors proposed stricter criteria requiring at least half of all attacks to begin in the menstrual window. ([Chalmer et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2804871))

A diary-validation study in 607 premenopausal women found that pure menstrual migraine was very rare, under 1 percent, while menstrually related migraine was more common. Self-reported menstrual migraine showed extremely poor accuracy, and agreement between statistical and diary-based ICHD-3 diagnoses was only minimal, which supports using cycle-and-headache tracking before making confident conclusions. ([Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

## Are menstrual migraine attacks different from non-menstrual migraine attacks?

Overall, yes, but the distinction is probabilistic rather than absolute. A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies found that menstrual migraine was associated with a worse clinical profile than non-menstrual migraine, including more attacks per month, more headache days, more aggravation with physical activity, and more accompanying symptoms; however, the certainty of evidence was low or very low, so the authors cautioned against overconfident conclusions. ([Wang et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36374351/))

The best diary-based evidence also supports the idea that menstrual-window attacks are harder. In the 2022 validation study, perimenstrual attacks were 37 to 50 percent longer than non-perimenstrual attacks and were associated with increased triptan intake. ([Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

The large 2023 population study found that menstrual migraine was associated with more accompanying symptoms, higher attack frequency, higher severity, and different treatment-response patterns compared with non-menstrual migraine. That supports menstrual migraine as a clinically meaningful phenotype, but not as a completely separate disease with a single diagnostic biomarker. ([Chalmer et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2804871))

## Estrogen withdrawal: plausible, but not the whole story

The classic explanation is estrogen withdrawal: estrogen falls in the late luteal phase before bleeding, and that fall may lower the migraine threshold. This theory is supported by older interventional work showing that estradiol could delay menstrual migraine in small samples and by prospective hormone-cycle studies showing migraine incidence rising around falling estrogen and bleeding. ([Somerville, 1972](https://doi.org/10.1212/WNL.22.4.355); [MacGregor et al., 2006a](https://doi.org/10.1212/01.wnl.0000233888.18228.19))

But the evidence is not as clean as the simple slogan suggests. A 2023 critical review concluded that the estrogen-withdrawal hypothesis remains biologically plausible, but the direct evidence is limited by small samples, inconsistent definitions, and mixed results. The same review notes that estrogen fluctuations are linked to menstrual migraine, but the exact mechanism remains uncertain. ([Raffaelli et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01664-4))

One reason the mechanism is probably broader than estrogen alone is that menstruation also involves prostaglandin release and other inflammatory or pain-sensitizing changes. A 2021 Lancet Neurology review described two main mechanistic hypotheses: estrogen withdrawal and prostaglandin release. ([Vetvik and MacGregor, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1474442220304828))

Estrogen supplementation trials also show why the story is complicated. In the largest estradiol-gel crossover trial summarized in the 2023 review, estradiol reduced migraine days during treatment but the primary result was not statistically significant, and migraine frequency increased after treatment stopped, consistent with a possible delayed withdrawal effect. ([MacGregor et al., 2006b](https://doi.org/10.1212/01.wnl.0000249114.52802.55); [Raffaelli et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01664-4))

## Treatment evidence

For acute treatment, menstrual migraine is generally treated as migraine, but trials often evaluate menstrual-specific outcomes because attacks can be longer, more severe, less responsive, and more likely to recur. A 2024 meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found evidence supporting sumatriptan and lasmiditan for acute menstrual migraine treatment, with frovatriptan showing advantages for recurrence-related outcomes. ([Khoo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-024-01848-6))

For women with predictable attacks, short-term perimenstrual triptan prevention has randomized-trial and meta-analysis support. A 2013 systematic review and meta-analysis of six double-blind placebo-controlled RCTs found that triptans were effective for short-term prevention, with frovatriptan 2.5 mg twice daily and zolmitriptan 2.5 mg three times daily among the preferred regimens in that analysis. ([Hu et al., 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1129-2377-14-7))

A 2023 network meta-analysis similarly found that frovatriptan 2.5 mg twice daily appeared suitable for prevention and sumatriptan 100 mg appeared suitable for acute treatment, but it also warned that sensitivity analyses and publication-bias concerns limited confidence. ([Zhang et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01625-x))

The 2024 meta-analysis included 26 randomized studies and concluded that frovatriptan was the most effective triptan option for prevention among the drugs studied. It also reported that CGRP monoclonal antibodies reduced headache days, but some of that evidence comes from broader migraine-prevention trials or post-hoc menstrual-window analyses rather than trials designed only for strictly diagnosed menstrual migraine. ([Khoo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-024-01848-6))

## Hormonal contraception: can help, can worsen, and aura changes the risk calculation

Hormonal contraception can improve menstrual migraine in some women, and shortened hormone-free intervals have been studied as one way to reduce withdrawal-associated attacks. In a study of women with menstrual migraine without aura, a 24/4 combined oral contraceptive regimen was superior to a 21/7 regimen for headache intensity and duration. ([De Leo et al., 2011](https://www.fertstert.org/article/S0015-0282%2811%2902186-8/fulltext))

Hormone-withdrawal intervals can also be associated with migraine attacks. Diary research in combined hormonal contraceptive users found migraine onset clustering around bleeding and the hormone-free interval, with attacks described as severe and long. ([Merki-Feld et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7315546/))

Migraine aura is the major safety issue with combined hormonal contraception. A systematic review found limited but concerning evidence that combined hormonal contraceptive use in women with migraine was associated with roughly two- to four-fold higher ischemic stroke risk in the included case-control studies, with the clearest concern in migraine with aura. ([Tepper et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010782416300518))

A large U.S. claims-based case-control study found that the joint effect of migraine with aura plus combined hormonal contraceptive use was associated with about a six-fold increased ischemic stroke risk compared with women who had neither risk factor. The same analysis did not find that combined hormonal contraception substantially further increased risk among women with migraine without aura. ([Champaloux et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002937817300275))

Current U.S. Medical Eligibility Criteria classify combined hormonal contraceptives as category 4 for migraine with aura, meaning an unacceptable health risk, and category 2 for migraine without aura, including menstrual migraine, when no other stroke risk factors are present. This means contraception is not a simple “good” or “bad” intervention for menstrual migraine. Shortened hormone-free regimens may help some women with migraine without aura, while combined hormonal contraception is generally avoided in migraine with aura under current U.S. guidance. ([Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/contraception/hcp/usmec/combined-hormonal-contraceptives.html); [Tepper et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010782416300518); [De Leo et al., 2011](https://www.fertstert.org/article/S0015-0282%2811%2902186-8/fulltext))

## Industry funding and conflict-of-interest notes

Some of the favorable triptan-prevention evidence involves manufacturer-funded trials. For example, a frovatriptan prevention trial found more headache-free perimenstrual periods with frovatriptan than placebo, but the study was supported by Vernalis Development and Endo Pharmaceuticals, and some authors were employees of or had relationships with pharmaceutical companies. The result should not be ignored, but it should be interpreted with that conflict in mind. ([Brandes et al., 2009](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1468-2982.2009.01840.x))

The 2013 triptan meta-analysis was supported by Chinese academic/public research funding and reported no conflicts of interest. The 2023 network meta-analysis reported no funding and no competing interests. The 2024 acute/preventive meta-analysis reported Taipei Medical University funding and no competing interests. These reviews are less directly conflicted than individual manufacturer-funded RCTs, but their pooled evidence still includes some industry-funded drug trials. ([Hu et al., 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1129-2377-14-7); [Zhang et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01625-x); [Khoo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-024-01848-6))

The 2023 estrogen-withdrawal review was funded by academic/foundation grants, including a Lundbeck Foundation Professor Grant, and several authors disclosed personal fees or relationships with migraine-drug companies outside the submitted work. That is not the same as a product-funded estradiol trial, but it is still worth noting when interpreting expert synthesis in a field with active pharmaceutical interest. ([Raffaelli et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01664-4))

## Quick reference values

Diagnostic window: day -2 to +3 of menstruation, where day 1 is the first day of bleeding. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/))

Minimum timing criterion: attacks in that window in at least two out of three menstrual cycles. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/))

Pure menstrual migraine: attacks occur only in the menstrual window; diary-validation data suggest this is rare. ([Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

Menstrually related migraine: attacks occur in the menstrual window and at other times; this is the more common clinically relevant pattern. ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Chalmer et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2804871))

Attack burden: perimenstrual attacks were 37 to 50 percent longer than non-perimenstrual attacks in a diary-validation study. ([Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

Most supported short-term prevention: perimenstrual triptans, especially frovatriptan regimens in meta-analyses, while remembering that some individual RCTs were industry-funded. ([Hu et al., 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1129-2377-14-7); [Zhang et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01625-x); [Khoo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-024-01848-6); [Brandes et al., 2009](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1468-2982.2009.01840.x))

## Practical interpretation

Menstrual migraine is different from regular migraine in timing, and often in severity, duration, recurrence, and treatment difficulty. The difference is real enough to affect diagnosis and treatment planning, but it is not a separate migraine disease with a single biomarker. ([Vetvik and MacGregor, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1474442220304828); [Wang et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36374351/); [Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

Estrogen withdrawal is the leading explanation, but the evidence supports it as a plausible and partly demonstrated mechanism rather than a complete explanation for every menstrual-window attack. Prostaglandins and other menstrual-cycle changes may also contribute. ([Raffaelli et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-023-01664-4); [Vetvik and MacGregor, 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1474442220304828))

Many “period headaches” are not automatically menstrual migraines. The better distinction is: does the headache meet migraine criteria, does it recur in the defined menstrual window, and does diary tracking show more than chance clustering? ([Headache Classification Committee of the International Headache Society, 2018](https://ichd-3.org/appendix/a1-migraine/a1-1-migraine-without-aura/a1-1-1-pure-menstrual-migraine-without-aura/); [Barra et al., 2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s10194-019-1035-7); [Verhagen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35514214/))

Hormonal contraception can help in some women, including when the hormone-free interval is shortened, but hormone-withdrawal intervals can also be associated with migraine attacks. In women with aura, combined hormonal contraception is generally avoided because the stroke-risk evidence and current eligibility guidance are strongest against combined hormonal contraception in migraine with aura. ([Tepper et al., 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010782416300518); [Champaloux et al., 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002937817300275); [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/contraception/hcp/usmec/combined-hormonal-contraceptives.html); [De Leo et al., 2011](https://www.fertstert.org/article/S0015-0282%2811%2902186-8/fulltext))
