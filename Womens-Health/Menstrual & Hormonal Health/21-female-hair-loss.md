# What's Actually Causing Hair Loss in Women - Stress, Iron, Thyroid, or Androgens?

Hair loss in women is best approached as a **pattern-and-trigger problem**, not a single-cause problem. The first split is usually between **shedding** and **miniaturization**: telogen effluvium is increased shedding after a trigger, while female pattern hair loss is progressive follicle miniaturization with lower density, often over the central scalp. These can coexist, which is why “stress,” “low ferritin,” “thyroid,” and “androgens” should be tested against the timeline and exam rather than treated as mutually exclusive explanations. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938); [Malkud, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4576621/))

## What the main diagnoses are and are not

**Telogen effluvium** is a shedding disorder. It usually presents as diffuse shedding rather than a widening part alone, and the classic history is shedding that begins about 2 to 3 months after a trigger such as illness, fever, childbirth, major weight loss, surgery, medication change, or severe psychological stress. Acute telogen effluvium is generally defined as lasting under 6 months; chronic telogen effluvium lasts longer. ([Grover and Khurana, 2013](https://ijdvl.com/telogen-effluvium/); [Asghar et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7320655/))

**Female pattern hair loss** is a miniaturization disorder. It may feel like shedding, but the core process is that terminal hairs become thinner and shorter over time, usually causing central scalp thinning or a widening part with relative preservation of the frontal hairline. Importantly, many women with female pattern hair loss have **normal circulating androgens** and no obvious signs of androgen excess. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

**Iron deficiency and thyroid disease** can contribute to shedding in some patients, but they are not the whole differential. They are best treated as potentially reversible contributors that should be looked for in the right clinical context, especially in diffuse shedding, heavy menstrual bleeding, restrictive diets, symptoms of thyroid disease, or chronic unexplained hair loss. Observational studies on ferritin and hair loss are mixed but support checking for iron deficiency in the right context; the exact ferritin threshold for hair growth is not settled. ([Kantor et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14708596/); [Olsen et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20947203/); [Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/); [Kakpovbia et al., 2021](https://jddonline.com/articles/impact-of-laboratory-work-up-and-supplementation-on-alopecia-patients-a-single-center-retrospective-S1545961621P0807X); [Freinkel and Freinkel, 1972](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/532819))

## Why “stress” is usually a trigger, not a diagnosis

Stress can be real, but “it’s stress” is often an incomplete answer. Telogen effluvium requires a plausible timeline: the trigger usually precedes shedding by weeks to months, because follicles shift into telogen before the hair is released. When shedding has continued for years, or when the part is widening, or when there is visible miniaturization, the diagnosis should be revisited rather than repeatedly attributed to stress. ([Grover and Khurana, 2013](https://ijdvl.com/telogen-effluvium/); [Asghar et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7320655/))

Post-pill shedding is biologically plausible and is described as a telogen-effluvium-type phenomenon after stopping estrogen-containing contraception. It should be treated as a timeline clue, not as proof that no other process is present. ([Grover and Khurana, 2013](https://ijdvl.com/telogen-effluvium/))

## What a systematic workup can and cannot find

A basic evaluation should separate **active shedding**, **pattern thinning**, and **scalp disease**. That usually means a timeline, medication and diet review, menstrual and hyperandrogenism history, scalp exam, part-width or standardized photographs, pull test or wash test when useful, and targeted labs. In a hospital-based study of 180 women with diffuse hair loss, telogen effluvium was the most common diagnosis, but chronic telogen effluvium, female pattern hair loss, alopecia areata, and mixed etiologies were also found. Specifically, 116 women had telogen effluvium, 28 had chronic telogen effluvium, 21 had female pattern hair loss, and 14 had more than one etiologic diagnosis. ([Malkud, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4576621/))

The yield of broad lab testing is real but limited. In a retrospective review of 131 alopecia patients seen by a hair specialist, 15.3 percent had an abnormal lab result; low ferritin was the most common abnormality at 6.5 percent. Supplementation did not significantly improve hair density or diameter in that short retrospective analysis, which argues against treating every borderline lab value as the primary diagnosis. ([Kakpovbia et al., 2021](https://jddonline.com/articles/impact-of-laboratory-work-up-and-supplementation-on-alopecia-patients-a-single-center-retrospective-S1545961621P0807X))

Scalp biopsy is not needed for every woman with shedding, but it can be useful when chronic diffuse hair loss is difficult to classify, especially when female pattern hair loss and chronic telogen effluvium are hard to distinguish. In a study using horizontally sectioned scalp biopsies in women with chronic diffuse hair loss, biopsy was used specifically to distinguish chronic telogen hair loss from female pattern hair loss, showing that clinical appearance alone can be insufficient in difficult cases. ([Sinclair et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15280836/))

## Ferritin: stricter than “normal,” but not settled

Ferritin is one of the most debated labs in women’s hair loss. A low ferritin clearly supports iron deficiency, but the hair-specific threshold is not established by high-quality randomized trials with objective hair outcomes. Some observational studies have found lower ferritin in women with telogen effluvium or other alopecia diagnoses, while other datasets are less supportive; taken together, they have not proven that raising ferritin to 50 to 70 ng/mL reliably regrows hair in otherwise non-deficient women. ([Kantor et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14708596/); [Olsen et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20947203/); [Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/))

One cross-sectional comparative study included 193 telogen effluvium patients, 104 female androgenetic alopecia patients, and 183 healthy controls. The telogen effluvium group had lower mean ferritin than controls and the female androgenetic alopecia group, but the study’s estimated diagnostic cutoff was about 24.45 ng/mL, not 50 to 70 ng/mL. That supports low ferritin as a contributor in some shedding patients, but it does not validate the common higher target as an evidence-based hair-growth threshold. ([Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/))

A 2024 observational study of women with telogen effluvium reported that iron supplementation was associated with patient satisfaction, but the outcome was subjective satisfaction rather than standardized hair counts, and the study design cannot establish causality. The finding is useful as real-world evidence, but it is not a substitute for a ferritin-threshold randomized trial. ([Soutou et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11149806/))

The practical interpretation is that ferritin below roughly the mid-20s is more clearly suspicious in some telogen effluvium studies, while the common 50 to 70 ng/mL target is a plausible clinical optimization target rather than a proven diagnostic cutoff. Iron deficiency should be corrected when present, and these studies do not prove that otherwise non-deficient patients should be treated to a universal higher ferritin target. ([Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/); [Kakpovbia et al., 2021](https://jddonline.com/articles/impact-of-laboratory-work-up-and-supplementation-on-alopecia-patients-a-single-center-retrospective-S1545961621P0807X); [Soutou et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11149806/))

## Thyroid disease

Thyroid dysfunction can cause diffuse shedding. Older clinical research found that hypothyroidism was associated with an increased proportion of telogen hairs, supporting the idea that thyroid deficiency can shift the hair cycle toward shedding. The evidence is not modern RCT evidence, but it is biologically and clinically consistent with thyroid-related telogen effluvium. ([Freinkel and Freinkel, 1972](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/532819))

That does not mean every woman with female pattern hair loss has thyroid disease. In practice, TSH is a reasonable targeted screen in diffuse shedding, chronic unexplained hair loss, symptoms of thyroid disease, postpartum contexts, menstrual changes, or other clinical clues. In low-risk women with classic female pattern hair loss and no symptoms, thyroid testing may have lower yield, but it is still commonly included because it is relatively simple and because missing thyroid disease changes management. ([Kakpovbia et al., 2021](https://jddonline.com/articles/impact-of-laboratory-work-up-and-supplementation-on-alopecia-patients-a-single-center-retrospective-S1545961621P0807X); [Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

## Androgens and female pattern hair loss

Female pattern hair loss is often called “androgenetic,” but that does not mean the patient must have high testosterone. The [AE-PCOS Society task force](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938) noted that most affected women with frontal-central female pattern hair loss have normal circulating androgen levels and no other androgen-excess signs. The better framing is that androgens can be involved through androgen sensitivity, local follicle biology, genetics, or systemic androgen excess in a subset. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

All women with suspected female pattern hair loss should at least be assessed clinically for signs of androgen excess. A biochemical androgen evaluation is especially important when hair loss is accompanied by hirsutism, acne, irregular periods, infertility, polycystic ovary syndrome features, rapid progression, or virilization; some expert guidance recommends circulating androgen evaluation in all female pattern hair loss. In otherwise typical female pattern hair loss, a normal androgen panel does not rule out the diagnosis, and an abnormal androgen panel should redirect attention toward causes of hyperandrogenism rather than only treating the scalp. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

## Minoxidil: useful, but not a diagnostic shortcut

Topical minoxidil has the strongest treatment evidence for female pattern hair loss. A [Cochrane review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/) concluded that 2 percent and 5 percent topical minoxidil improve female pattern hair loss, although the certainty was low to moderate and some comparisons between 2 percent and 5 percent did not show a clear difference. A 48-week randomized trial in 381 women found that 5 percent and 2 percent topical minoxidil improved outcomes compared with placebo, with 5 percent performing better on several prespecified endpoints. ([van Zuuren et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/); [Lucky et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15034503/))

A pragmatic minoxidil trial can make sense when the exam suggests female pattern hair loss, because delaying treatment for months while repeatedly searching for one perfect cause can allow miniaturization to progress. But minoxidil is not a substitute for correcting overt iron deficiency, thyroid disease, medication triggers, crash dieting, inflammatory scalp disease, or postpartum/telogen triggers. ([van Zuuren et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/); [Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

## Oral minoxidil

Low-dose oral minoxidil is discussed in the literature as an off-label option for female pattern hair loss, but the direct randomized evidence in women is still small. One randomized clinical trial compared oral minoxidil 1 mg daily with topical 5 percent minoxidil solution in female pattern hair loss over 24 weeks; another randomized trial compared 0.25 mg with 1 mg oral minoxidil and found greater efficacy with the 1 mg dose over 24 weeks. These trials support biological activity, but they are much smaller than the topical minoxidil evidence base. ([Rodrigues-Barata et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7325226/); [Ramos et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31473295/); [Nascimento e Silva et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35077777/))

The safety signal is mostly from observational data. In a multicenter retrospective study of 1,404 patients treated with low-dose oral minoxidil for hair loss, 943 were women, and only 1.7 percent discontinued because of adverse effects. Hypertrichosis, fluid retention, tachycardia, and lightheadedness are the adverse effects clinicians usually watch for; the low discontinuation rate is reassuring but does not replace randomized long-term safety data. ([Vano-Galvan et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33639244/); [Rodrigues-Barata et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7325226/))

## Anti-androgens: promising, uneven evidence

Spironolactone is used as an anti-androgen option in female pattern hair loss, especially when androgen-excess features are present, but direct randomized evidence remains limited. A 2025 double-blind pilot RCT randomized 48 women to spironolactone 100 mg daily or placebo for 24 weeks, with all participants also using topical minoxidil 3 percent. Terminal hair count improvement approached but did not reach conventional statistical significance, while moderate-to-marked photographic improvement was more common with spironolactone; menstrual irregularity was the main adverse effect and occurred more often in the spironolactone group. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938); [Werachattawatchai et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40978669/))

Finasteride is more complicated in women. A randomized placebo-controlled trial of finasteride 1 mg daily in postmenopausal women did not show efficacy. Higher-dose finasteride and dutasteride have supportive uncontrolled, dose-specific, or mostly male evidence, but the RCT base in women remains limited, and reproductive-age safety planning is essential because these drugs are not treated like routine cosmetic medications. Counseling should also include sexual-function and mood adverse effects; in 2025, the [European Medicines Agency](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/news/measures-minimise-risk-suicidal-thoughts-finasteride-dutasteride-medicines) confirmed suicidal ideation as a side effect of finasteride 1 mg and 5 mg tablets, with frequency unknown, and did not establish a direct link for dutasteride. ([Price et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11050579/); [van Zuuren et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/); [Almudimeegh et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11694415/); [European Medicines Agency, 2025](https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/news/measures-minimise-risk-suicidal-thoughts-finasteride-dutasteride-medicines))

The practical takeaway is that anti-androgens are not “wrong” for women, but they should be offered with honest uncertainty: spironolactone has emerging randomized evidence and a plausible role, while finasteride and dutasteride in women rely more heavily on dose-specific and lower-certainty evidence than topical minoxidil. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938); [van Zuuren et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/); [Werachattawatchai et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40978669/))

## Platelet-rich plasma

Platelet-rich plasma is another option for female pattern hair loss, but protocols vary substantially. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in women found that PRP improved hair density and thickness across different forms of female hair loss with a generally favorable safety profile. However, at least one randomized trial found that adding PRP to topical minoxidil was effective but not clearly superior to topical minoxidil alone, so PRP should be presented as an adjunct or alternative with cost and protocol uncertainty, not as a guaranteed upgrade. ([Yuan et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11342464/); [Agarwal and Mendiratta, 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10162769/))

## Quick reference values and clues

**Telogen effluvium:** diffuse shedding, often 2 to 3 months after a trigger; acute if under 6 months, chronic if longer. ([Grover and Khurana, 2013](https://ijdvl.com/telogen-effluvium/); [Asghar et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7320655/))

**Female pattern hair loss:** widening part, central scalp thinning, miniaturization, gradual progression; serum androgens may be normal. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

**Ferritin:** low ferritin is associated with some telogen effluvium cohorts in observational studies, but the literature is mixed and the best-supported diagnostic thresholds are not the same as the commonly cited 50 to 70 ng/mL target. A 2021 cross-sectional study estimated an optimal telogen effluvium cutoff around 24.45 ng/mL; higher targets remain clinically plausible but not RCT-proven. ([Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/); [Olsen et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20947203/))

**TSH:** useful when shedding is diffuse, chronic, postpartum, symptomatic, or unexplained; thyroid disease can shift follicles toward telogen. ([Freinkel and Freinkel, 1972](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/532819); [Kakpovbia et al., 2021](https://jddonline.com/articles/impact-of-laboratory-work-up-and-supplementation-on-alopecia-patients-a-single-center-retrospective-S1545961621P0807X))

**Androgen panel:** all women with suspected female pattern hair loss should be assessed clinically for androgen excess; biochemical testing is most important when hair loss is accompanied by acne, hirsutism, irregular periods, infertility, rapid progression, or other signs of androgen excess. Normal androgens do not exclude female pattern hair loss. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938))

**Biopsy:** consider when chronic diffuse hair loss is difficult to classify, especially when the practical question is chronic telogen hair loss vs female pattern hair loss. ([Sinclair et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15280836/))

## Practical interpretation

The most evidence-consistent approach is a hybrid: do a structured evaluation, correct clear reversible contributors, and start proven treatment early when the pattern suggests female pattern hair loss. Women should not be left for years with “it’s stress” when the part is widening, density is declining, or miniaturization is visible. At the same time, minoxidil should not be used to avoid diagnosing iron deficiency, thyroid disease, medication-triggered telogen effluvium, inflammatory scalp disease, or clinically important hyperandrogenism. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938); [Malkud, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4576621/); [van Zuuren et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6457957/))

For many women, the answer is not **stress vs iron vs thyroid vs androgens**. It is often **telogen shedding plus pattern susceptibility**, or **female pattern hair loss plus a reversible shedding trigger**. The goal is to identify what is active now: recent trigger, ferritin/iron status, thyroid status, androgen-excess signs, miniaturization, and scalp inflammation. ([Carmina et al., 2019](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/104/7/2875/5342938); [Cheng et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7882421/); [Sinclair et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15280836/))
