## Menstrual Synchrony

The idea that roommates or close friends “sync up” is usually called menstrual synchrony. The most defensible reading of the evidence is that period overlap is common, memorable, and real as an experience, but strong biological synchronization has not been convincingly demonstrated. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z); [Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z); [Ziomkiewicz, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0)) ([UCSB Psychology Labs](https://labs.psych.ucsb.edu/roney/james/other%20pdf%20readings/Wison%25201992%2520Critical%2520Reveiw%2520of%2520Menstrual%2520Synchrony.pdf "PII: 0306-4530(92)90016-Z"))

## What synchrony is and is not

Menstrual synchrony is not the same thing as two women having bleeding days that overlap in a given month. True synchrony would mean that menstrual onset dates move closer together over time, or remain unusually close, relative to an appropriate chance model because of living together, social contact, or a biological signal. ([McClintock, 1971](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0); [Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z))

That distinction matters because independent cycles can overlap often, especially when bleeding lasts several days and cycle lengths vary from month to month. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

Questionnaire evidence suggests many women are aware of the idea and report personal experiences of synchrony, but subjective reports cannot distinguish true biological synchronization from chance overlap, cycle variability, and selective attention. ([Arden et al., 1999](https://doi.org/10.1080/02646839908404593); [Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

## The original [McClintock study](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0)

The modern claim began with [McClintock’s 1971 Nature paper](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0) on women living in a college dormitory. The paper reported that menstrual onset dates among close friends and dormitory groups became closer together over time, and it suggested that social interaction could strongly affect the menstrual cycle. ([McClintock, 1971](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0)) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/229244a0 "Menstrual Synchrony and Suppression | Nature"))

This study is the basis for the popular claim that women who live together can sync their periods. ([McClintock, 1971](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0); [Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z))

Later critiques argued that the original approach was vulnerable to statistical artifacts, including how onset differences were calculated, how missing or irregular cycles were handled, and how subjects or cycles were excluded. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z))

## Why overlap can happen by chance

If two people have independent 28-day cycles, the maximum possible difference between their onset dates is about 14 days, and the average difference is about 7 days. Since menstruation often lasts several days, overlap between friends can happen frequently even if no syncing mechanism exists. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

Cycle variability makes this even more intuitive. If one person’s cycle is 27 days one month and 31 days another month, while another person’s cycle also shifts naturally, their onset dates can move closer together, coincide, and then drift apart again without pheromones or social entrainment. ([Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

This is why the confirmation-bias explanation is plausible: women may remember the striking months when periods overlap and forget the months when they are offset, even though both patterns can occur by chance. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

## Replication and statistical reanalysis

A critical review by [Wilson](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z) concluded that the menstrual synchrony literature then available contained major methodological problems. Wilson identified errors that could increase the likelihood of finding synchrony, including assumptions about random onset differences, incorrect handling of initial onset differences, and subject or onset exclusions; after correction, significant synchrony was not demonstrated. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z)) ([UCSB Psychology Labs](https://labs.psych.ucsb.edu/roney/james/other%20pdf%20readings/Wison%25201992%2520Critical%2520Reveiw%2520of%2520Menstrual%2520Synchrony.pdf "PII: 0306-4530(92)90016-Z"))

A study of 29 cohabiting lesbian couples by [Trevathan et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(93\)90017-F) found no evidence for menstrual synchrony. This was relevant because cohabiting couples provided a setting with close and repeated contact, which should have been favorable if social closeness reliably synchronized cycles. ([Trevathan et al., 1993](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(93\)90017-F))

[Strassmann](https://doi.org/10.1086/204592) studied menstrual hut attendance in a natural-fertility Dogon population and found that menstrual onset was not synchronized by village, lineage, household/economic unit, co-wife status, friendship, or lunar phase. This study was important because it avoided some problems of short college-dorm studies and used daily prospective observations over 736 consecutive days. ([Strassmann, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1086/204592); [Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

[Yang and Schank](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z) followed 186 Chinese women living in dormitories for more than one year and found that women living in groups did not synchronize their cycles. They also reanalyzed the [first McClintock study](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0) and argued that the apparent group synchrony was at the level expected by chance. ([Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z "Women do not synchronize their menstrual cycles | Human Nature | Springer Nature Link"))

[Ziomkiewicz](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0) studied Polish college women living in dormitories over five months, including 18 pairs and 21 triples, and found no menstrual synchrony. Social interactions were also not related to onset differences, while factors like cycle irregularity and body mass influenced apparent initial onset differences. ([Ziomkiewicz, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0 "Menstrual synchrony: Fact or artifact? | Human Nature | Springer Nature Link"))

A newer prospective study by [Siddiqui et al.](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23) of 62 female medical students sharing twin accommodation for 13 months reported synchrony in about 55 percent of 31 roommate pairs. The authors also noted important limits: the sample was small and specific, degree of friendship was not assessed, and larger datasets with stronger mathematical methods were needed. ([Siddiqui et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23))

## The pheromone claim

The pheromone version of the claim comes mainly from [Stern and McClintock’s 1998 Nature experiment](https://doi.org/10.1038/32408). In that study, axillary compounds collected from women at different cycle phases were applied under the noses of recipient women; compounds from the late follicular phase were reported to shorten cycles, while compounds from the ovulatory phase were reported to lengthen cycles. ([Stern & McClintock, 1998](https://doi.org/10.1038/32408))

That study is mechanistically interesting, but it is not the same as showing that roommates or close friends synchronize their menstrual cycles in everyday life. It tested exposure to collected underarm compounds under experimental conditions, not long-term social syncing in natural groups. ([Stern & McClintock, 1998](https://doi.org/10.1038/32408); [Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

[Schank’s review](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1006-y) of menstrual-cycle pheromone research concluded that the pheromone explanation was weakened by the broader failure to demonstrate menstrual synchrony and by serious problems in the pheromone studies themselves. ([Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1006-y))

[Strassmann](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579) also argued that skepticism was warranted until a cycle-altering human pheromone was chemically isolated and shown to produce the claimed effect reliably. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

The clearest bias concerns documented by the cited critiques are methodological and statistical. The [2023 prospective study](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23) reported no financial support and no conflicts of interest, [Wilson’s review](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z) acknowledged research support, and [Strassmann’s Dogon work](https://doi.org/10.1086/204592) acknowledged National Science Foundation and University of Michigan support. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z); [Strassmann, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1086/204592); [Siddiqui et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23))

Rather than treating absent conflict-of-interest statements in older papers as proof that no conflicts existed, the safer interpretation is to focus on the better-documented issue: different statistical choices can turn normal cycle variability into apparent synchrony. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

## Quick reference points

Original claim: [McClintock’s dormitory study](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0) reported menstrual onset convergence and suggested that social interaction could affect menstrual cycles. ([McClintock, 1971](https://doi.org/10.1038/229244a0))

Main statistical critique: [Wilson](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z) concluded that methodological errors in the literature inflated apparent synchrony and that corrected analyses did not demonstrate the effect. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z))

Chance-overlap explanation: independent cycles can appear close because two 28-day cycles are, on average, only about 7 days apart at onset, and bleeding lasts several days. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579))

Large dormitory replication: [Yang and Schank](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z) followed 186 dormitory residents for more than a year and found no synchrony. ([Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

Other null evidence: studies in cohabiting lesbian couples, Polish dormitory residents, and a Dogon natural-fertility population did not find convincing synchrony. ([Trevathan et al., 1993](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(93\)90017-F); [Strassmann, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1086/204592); [Ziomkiewicz, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0))

Newer positive study: [Siddiqui et al.](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23) reported synchrony in about 55 percent of 31 roommate pairs, but the study was small, observational, and limited by lack of data on degree of friendship. ([Siddiqui et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23))

## Practical interpretation

Your period can genuinely overlap with a roommate’s or friend’s period without requiring biological synchronization. Normal cycle variability, multi-day bleeding, and chance timing can produce overlap surprisingly often. ([Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z))

The strongest evidence does not support menstrual synchrony as a universal or strong effect. The better-supported explanation is mathematical coincidence plus selective attention to memorable overlaps. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z); [Strassmann, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1093/humrep/14.3.579); [Yang & Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1005-z); [Ziomkiewicz, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1004-0))

The most cautious conclusion is not that syncing is impossible in every pair, but that the classic claim that women who live or spend time together reliably synchronize their menstrual cycles through pheromones has not been convincingly shown. ([Wilson, 1992](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4530\(92\)90016-Z); [Schank, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-006-1006-y); [Siddiqui et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_382_23))
