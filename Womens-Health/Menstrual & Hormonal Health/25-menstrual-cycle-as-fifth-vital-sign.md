# Menstrual Cycle as a Fifth Vital Sign

The menstrual cycle is reasonable to treat as a structured health signal, but not as a stand-alone diagnostic test. [ACOG’s adolescent guidance](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2015/12/menstruation-in-girls-and-adolescents-using-the-menstrual-cycle-as-a-vital-sign), endorsed by the [American Academy of Pediatrics](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/137/3/e20154480/81407/Menstruation-in-Girls-and-Adolescents-Using-the), framed menstrual history as an additional vital sign, and newer reviews argue that the same logic can extend from menarche through menopause. The strongest case is for routinely asking, documenting, and interpreting cycle patterns; the weaker claim is that universal cycle-tracking in routine care has already been proven to improve diagnostic yield, cost, or outcomes in unselected primary-care settings. ([ACOG, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26595586/); [AAP, 2016](https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/137/3/e20154480/81407/Menstruation-in-Girls-and-Adolescents-Using-the); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

## What the “fifth vital sign” claim means

Calling the menstrual cycle a vital sign means that cycle pattern can function as a repeated physiologic marker: it can reveal endocrine, gynecologic, metabolic, nutritional, stress-related, and life-stage changes. It does not mean that one unusual cycle is automatically a disease, or that every routine visit should trigger a lab workup. ([ACOG, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26595586/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

The concept was first pushed most clearly in adolescents because early menstrual patterns can identify conditions that might otherwise be normalized or missed. A chart-review study of annual well visits found that complete menstrual history-taking was inconsistent across pediatrics, adolescent medicine, and family medicine, even though the menstrual cycle was already being described as a vital sign for adolescent and young female patients. ([Diaz et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17079600/); [McShane et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30081084/))

## What should be recorded

A practical menstrual “vital sign” should not be just “last menstrual period.” It should include cycle frequency or length, regularity or variation, bleeding duration, bleeding volume or heaviness, and symptoms such as pain or functional impairment. [FIGO’s abnormal uterine bleeding framework](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/) uses frequency, regularity, duration, and volume as core descriptors, and the [2025 comprehensive review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/) likewise treats these domains as central to cycle assessment. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

A practical routine-care intake can combine the [FIGO symptom domains](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/) with basic context for common cycle disruptions: date of last menstrual period, usual cycle interval, how much it varies, number of bleeding days, whether bleeding is heavy, whether pain limits activities, whether there is intermenstrual or unscheduled bleeding, whether there has been a recent change, and whether pregnancy, hormonal contraception, intense exercise, weight change, eating disorder symptoms, major stress, or life-stage transition could explain the pattern. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [ACOG, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26595586/); [Gordon et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/); [Harlow et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/))

## What counts as abnormal

[FIGO-based adult reference values](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/) commonly define normal cycle frequency as about 24 to 38 days, bleeding duration as 8 days or fewer, and cycle regularity as variation within roughly 7 to 9 days, depending on age. Abnormal uterine bleeding includes alterations in frequency, regularity, duration, or volume, as well as intermenstrual or unscheduled bleeding; chronic abnormal uterine bleeding is generally described as symptoms present for most of the previous 6 months. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

Those cutoffs need context. Adolescents often have physiologic irregularity after menarche, and PCOS/PMOS guidelines use age-since-menarche-specific definitions to reduce overdiagnosis. Perimenopause also changes cycle variability, with [STRAW+10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/) using persistent cycle-length differences of at least 7 days to mark the early menopausal transition and 60 or more days of amenorrhea to mark the late transition; [newer menopause literature](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S221385872500138X) notes that cycle-based staging can be less helpful in people who already have irregular cycles, hormonal contraception, ablation, or hysterectomy. ([Harlow et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/); [Teede et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37580314/); [Islam et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S221385872500138X))

## Why cycle pattern can be clinically useful

Irregular or infrequent cycles are one diagnostic clue for PCOS, for which polyendocrine metabolic ovarian syndrome, or PMOS, was announced as the new name in 2026 through a global consensus process, with a planned transition period. In adolescents, PCOS/PMOS diagnosis requires more caution because irregular cycles alone are not specific; guidelines emphasize hyperandrogenism plus persistent cycle irregularity rather than ovarian morphology alone. Diagnostic delay is common: in one 2023 study, menstrual irregularity was the first symptom identified by most participants, and the average time from first symptom awareness to diagnosis was 4.3 years. ([Joham et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35934017/); [Teede et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37580314/); [Teede et al., 2026](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736%2826%2900717-8/fulltext); [Endocrine Society, 2026](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2026/pcos-name-change); [Sydora et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10625259/))

Painful periods are also clinically important. Endometriosis is not diagnosed by cycle tracking alone, but persistent clinically significant dysmenorrhea or chronic pelvic pain despite empiric treatment should prompt evaluation for secondary causes, including endometriosis. Pain that interferes with school, work, or daily function, and pain accompanied by bowel or bladder symptoms, can strengthen the case for earlier evaluation. Recent reviews continue to find substantial diagnostic delay in endometriosis, with one scoping review reporting an average delay of 6.6 years across included studies and other reviews reporting delays of up to 10 years. ([ACOG, 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30461694/); [Fryer et al., 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07399332.2024.2413056); [De Corte et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39373298/))

Thyroid disease can alter menstrual bleeding and cycle pattern. Reviews describe menstrual disturbances in both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism, although the frequency and pattern vary by thyroid condition, severity, and study population. Cycle irregularity is therefore a prompt to consider thyroid disease when symptoms or clinical context fit, not proof of thyroid disease by itself. ([Krassas, 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11119728/); [Krassas et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20573783/); [Brown et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37372963/))

Amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea can also signal low energy availability, functional hypothalamic amenorrhea, eating disorders, or relative energy deficiency in sport. The [Endocrine Society guideline](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/) describes functional hypothalamic amenorrhea as chronic anovulation not due to organic causes, often associated with stress, weight loss, excessive exercise, or combinations of these factors. The [2023 IOC REDs consensus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37752011/) describes relative energy deficiency in sport as a syndrome related to low energy availability, and an [IOC prevention paper](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/17/1119) notes that self-reported menstrual dysfunction is strongly associated with clinically verified functional hypothalamic amenorrhea in female endurance athletes. ([Gordon et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/); [Mountjoy et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37752011/); [Torstveit et al., 2023](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/17/1119))

Stress is a plausible contributor to cycle disruption, but the evidence is heterogeneous. A 2024 systematic review found that most included studies reported an association between psychological stress and menstrual dysfunction, especially irregular menstruation and abnormal flow, while also noting variation in how studies defined both stress and menstrual outcomes. ([Poitras et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38950686/))

Heavy menstrual bleeding is a high-yield symptom because it can indicate anemia, iron deficiency, anovulatory bleeding, structural causes, or bleeding disorders. [ACOG guidance for adolescents with heavy menstrual bleeding](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31441825/) recommends evaluation for anemia, including ferritin, assessment for endocrine causes of anovulation, and evaluation for bleeding disorders when indicated. ([ACOG, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31441825/); [Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Munro et al., 2023](https://hsrc.himmelfarb.gwu.edu/gwhpubs/2243/))

Cycle patterns may also carry longer-term risk information. Large cohort studies have associated irregular or long cycles with later cardiometabolic outcomes, including cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes, although these studies show association rather than proof that changing the cycle pattern prevents those outcomes. ([Wang et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36282498/); [Wang et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33346844/); [Okoth et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36941638/))

## What the evidence does not yet prove

The evidence supports menstrual history as a clinically useful signal, but it does not yet prove that adding universal menstrual-cycle tracking to every primary-care intake catches a quantified “meaningful share” of delayed diagnoses. The evidence identified here includes targeted quality-improvement evidence, not broad primary-care outcome trials. In a pediatric sports medicine clinic, an electronic health record alert plus questionnaires and education increased appropriate diagnosis of menstrual dysfunction in athletes older than 12 from 2.1 percent to 30 percent over 10 months, but that was a targeted clinic population, not an unselected primary-care screening trial. ([Daniel et al., 2022](https://digitalcommons.wustl.edu/open_access_pubs/11346/))

The “almost no added cost” claim is also not established. Asking a few menstrual-history questions is low in material cost, but implementation still requires clinician training, interpretable thresholds, EHR fields, workflows, and strategies to avoid alert fatigue. The sports medicine quality-improvement project explicitly used staff education, patient education, alert optimization, and attention to alert fatigue, which means the intervention was more than simply adding one question to intake. ([Daniel et al., 2022](https://digitalcommons.wustl.edu/open_access_pubs/11346/))

## Measurement limitations

Patient-reported cycle metrics are useful, but they are not perfect. In a validation study comparing self-reported usual cycle length with prospective menstrual data, 43 percent of women reported a usual cycle length more than 2 days different from their observed mean, and 21 percent were misclassified when cycle length was grouped into broad categories. That does not make self-report useless; it means clinicians should interpret it as an estimate and prioritize trends, persistent changes, and symptoms. ([Small et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16882471/))

Heavy bleeding is especially difficult to quantify. A systematic review found wide sensitivity and specificity ranges across menstrual blood-loss measurement methods, including pictorial blood-loss assessment charts, menstrual pictograms, questionnaires, and patient complaint of heavy bleeding. The review concluded that every method has limitations and that pictorial methods can balance practicality with validation, but the funding matters: the review was funded by Bayer AG, and disclosed conflicts included Bayer employment, Bayer stock ownership, and Bayer-related payments. ([Magnay et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12905-018-0627-8))

In adolescents, the measurement problem is even sharper. A 2024 systematic review of instruments for adolescent dysmenorrhea and heavy menstrual bleeding identified 19 instruments, but only 8 had validation evidence; the authors concluded that only PIPPA and the adolescent Menstrual Bleeding Questionnaire were both validated and feasible, while adolescent PBAC cutoffs still need clearer clinical validation. ([Özcan et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38104798/))

## The overmedicalization risk

The main risk of the vital-sign framing is that it can turn normal variation into a medical problem if clinicians apply adult cutoffs too rigidly, ignore life stage, or lack time to interpret the result. Adolescence, perimenopause, hormonal contraception, stress, weight change, and athletic training can all affect cycle interpretation. The right response is not automatic testing after one unusual cycle; it is structured follow-up when the pattern is persistent, severe, new, symptomatic, or clinically discordant. ([Harlow et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/); [Gordon et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/); [Poitras et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38950686/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

This also means that “routine cycle assessment” should be framed as patient-centered history-taking, not mandatory commercial app tracking. A patient’s calendar, memory, paper diary, or app data may all be useful, but the clinical question is the same: has the pattern changed, is it outside expected ranges for this life stage, and is it associated with pain, heavy bleeding, anemia symptoms, hyperandrogenism, pregnancy risk, weight or exercise changes, or functional impairment? ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Small et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16882471/); [Özcan et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38104798/))

## Practical interpretation for routine care

The menstrual cycle should be asked about routinely in patients who menstruate, especially adolescents and patients presenting with pelvic pain, heavy bleeding, fatigue or anemia symptoms, infertility, acne or hirsutism, weight change, eating disorder symptoms, intense exercise, mood or stress concerns, or perimenopausal symptoms. This is consistent with the vital-sign framing, but the cycle pattern should be interpreted as a clinical clue rather than a diagnostic endpoint. ([ACOG, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26595586/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/); [ACOG, 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30461694/); [ACOG, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31441825/); [Teede et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37580314/); [Gordon et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/); [Harlow et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/); [Poitras et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38950686/))

A reasonable routine-care model is to record a short menstrual history at baseline and update it when there is a change. The key fields are last menstrual period, usual cycle length, cycle variability, bleeding duration, bleeding heaviness, pain or functional impact, intermenstrual or unscheduled bleeding, hormonal contraception or pregnancy context, and major lifestyle or medical changes. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [McShane et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30081084/); [ACOG, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26595586/))

Follow-up is most justified when there is persistent cycle length outside expected ranges, absent periods, sudden change from baseline, heavy bleeding, symptoms of anemia, pain that limits normal activities, bleeding between periods, signs of hyperandrogenism, infertility concerns, suspected pregnancy, or clinical signs of low energy availability, thyroid disease, eating disorder, or perimenopause. ([ACOG, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31441825/); [ACOG, 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30461694/); [Gordon et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28368518/); [Teede et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37580314/); [Harlow et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22343510/))

The strongest evidence-based position is therefore: yes, the menstrual cycle can be treated as a fifth vital sign in the sense of a routinely documented health indicator; no, the field has not yet proven that universal cycle tracking in routine primary care improves outcomes or reduces diagnostic delay at population scale. ([Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/); [Daniel et al., 2022](https://digitalcommons.wustl.edu/open_access_pubs/11346/))

## Evidence still needed

The field still needs diagnostic-yield studies in unselected primary-care populations, especially studies that measure how often routine menstrual assessment changes diagnosis or management. It also needs validation studies for brief patient-reported cycle metrics that can fit into routine intake, rather than relying only on detailed research diaries or specialty questionnaires. ([Small et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16882471/); [Özcan et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38104798/))

Implementation studies should measure clinician time, patient burden, training needs, EHR design, false positives, downstream testing, and alert fatigue. The existing sports medicine quality-improvement study is useful proof that structured workflows can change diagnosis rates in a targeted setting, but it does not settle the broader routine-care question. ([Daniel et al., 2022](https://digitalcommons.wustl.edu/open_access_pubs/11346/))

The highest-value evidence would be pragmatic cluster trials or stepped-wedge studies comparing usual care with structured menstrual-cycle assessment, measuring delayed diagnoses, patient-reported outcomes, unnecessary testing, referrals, cost, and equity across age groups and clinical settings. Until then, the “fifth vital sign” framing is best viewed as a clinically sensible documentation standard, not a fully validated screening program. ([Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

## Quick reference values and flags

Adult cycle frequency: about 24 to 38 days is commonly treated as normal in [FIGO-based definitions](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/). ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

Bleeding duration: 8 days or fewer is commonly treated as normal. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

Regularity: adult cycle-to-cycle variation is generally expected to stay within about 7 to 9 days, depending on age. ([Munro et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30198563/); [Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))

Heavy bleeding: clinically important when it is excessive by patient experience, causes flooding or frequent product changes, leads to anemia or iron deficiency, or impairs quality of life. ([ACOG, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31441825/); [Magnay et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12905-018-0627-8); [Munro et al., 2023](https://hsrc.himmelfarb.gwu.edu/gwhpubs/2243/))

Pain: clinically important when it is severe, progressive, persistent, or limits school, work, exercise, sex, or daily function. ([ACOG, 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30461694/); [Fryer et al., 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07399332.2024.2413056); [De Corte et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39373298/))

## Bottom line

The menstrual cycle deserves a place in routine clinical history because it can reveal patterns that connect to PCOS/PMOS, endometriosis, thyroid disease, heavy menstrual bleeding, anemia, bleeding disorders, functional hypothalamic amenorrhea, REDs, eating disorders, stress, perimenopause, and cardiometabolic risk. But the strongest evidence supports structured assessment and thoughtful interpretation, not indiscriminate medicalization or a claim that universal tracking has already been proven to improve outcomes. ([Vollmar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39906529/))
