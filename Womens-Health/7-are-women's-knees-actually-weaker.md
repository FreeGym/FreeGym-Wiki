# **Are Women’s Knees Actually Weaker?**

Female athletes do have higher ACL injury rates than male athletes in many sports, especially sports with frequent cutting, landing, and pivoting. But the best current literature does not support reducing this to a claim that female athletes have uniformly “weaker knees.” The evidence instead points to a multifactorial ACL risk profile, and prevention trials/meta-analyses show that neuromuscular training can reduce ACL injury risk. ([Childers et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38692337/); [Chia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622227/); [Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/)).

### What is actually higher

Systematic reviews show that the female-male gap in ACL injury is real, but it is not identical across all sports. A 2025 meta-analysis of adolescent athletes found that females had 1.56 times the ACL injury risk of males overall, with female soccer players showing the largest relative risk. A 2019 meta-analysis likewise found that females were not at uniformly higher risk in every sport category, but they were at higher risk in contact sports and in fixed-object, high-impact rotational landing sports. ([Childers et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38692337/); [Montalvo et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31009238/)).

A team-ball-sport meta-analysis found that non-contact injuries made up 55% of ACL injuries overall, 63% in females, and 50% in males. Female non-contact ACL incidence was higher than male incidence, and competition rates were far higher than training rates. ([Chia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622227/)).

### What “weaker knees” is and is not

The literature supports higher ACL injury rates in many female athlete populations, not a blanket conclusion that female knees are globally weaker in a simple strength sense. The more defensible interpretation is that female athletes often face a higher ACL injury risk profile, especially for non-contact mechanisms, and that this risk is shaped by several interacting factors rather than one built-in defect. ([Chia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622227/); [Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/)).

### Anatomy: pelvis, Q-angle, and knee geometry

The popular wider-pelvis/Q-angle explanation is probably too simple. In an older case-control study, females had larger quadriceps angles than males, but quadriceps angle was not associated with ACL injury history. In a prospective multivariate study, quadriceps angle did not emerge as the female risk signal; in females, the predictive combination included parent history of ACL injury, greater anterior-posterior knee laxity, and higher body mass index. ([Hertel et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24624006/); [Vacek et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27217522/)).

That does not mean anatomy is irrelevant. Some knee-specific geometry measures also have support in the literature. An updated meta-analysis found smaller femoral notch width in ACL injury cases than controls, and a matched case-control study found that in females, a narrower femoral notch and steeper lateral compartment cartilage slope were independently associated with higher ACL injury risk. ([Li et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32444935/); [Sturnick et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25583759/)).

Still, newer prospective data caution against overclaiming. A 4.5-year cohort of female team-sport athletes found no significant association between non-contact ACL injury and several commonly proposed baseline exam variables, including anterior-posterior knee laxity, hyperextension, generalized joint hypermobility, femoral anteversion, hamstring extensibility, and navicular drop; however, the study was powered to detect moderate to strong associations, so smaller effects may have been missed. ([Pasanen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39943896/)).

### Hormones and ligament laxity

Hormonal effects are biologically plausible, but the human evidence is mixed and not strong enough to make estrogen or cycle phase the main explanation. A 2017 systematic review/meta-analysis found low-strength evidence that menstrual-cycle phase may relate to ACL injury and that oral contraceptives may be protective; it also found greater laxity in the ovulatory than follicular phase in the subset available for meta-analysis. ([Herzberg et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28795075/)).

A newer 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis updated that picture. It found no effect of menstrual-cycle phase on knee laxity or anterior tibial translation in meta-analysis, even though estradiol altered ACL fibroblast collagen-related biology in vitro. That makes the hormonal story plausible mechanistically, but much less decisive clinically than many summaries imply. ([Gilmer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39887996/)).

### Strength imbalances and movement patterns

The evidence supports neuromuscular contributors, but not a single “quad-dominant versus weak hamstrings/glutes” explanation. A prospective study found that athletes who later sustained non-contact ACL injuries had lower hip abduction and external-rotation strength at baseline. A matched case-control study suggested that female athletes who later tore the ACL had lower hamstring strength, while not differing in quadriceps strength, relative to matched male controls; it did not show a clean future-injured-versus-uninjured female difference. ([Khayambashi et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26646514/); [Myer et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19124976/)).

Mechanism-focused systematic review evidence is consistent with a broader movement explanation: poor core stability, heel-strike landings, weak hip abduction strength, and increased dynamic knee valgus may all contribute to ACL risk. But simple screening tests are not enough; in a large prospective cohort of elite female soccer and handball players, the vertical drop jump did not work well as a stand-alone screening test for new ACL injuries. ([Larwa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33917488/); [Krosshaug et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26867936/)).

### What prevention trials show

The clearest practical intervention evidence comes from prevention trials and meta-analyses. A 2026 systematic review/meta-analysis of 18 studies found that athletes in ACL injury-prevention programs were significantly less likely to sustain ACL rupture, with a pooled risk ratio of 0.46 overall and 0.57 in female-only data. ([Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/)).

A 2025 meta-analysis limited to female team athletes found that neuromuscular training reduced ACL injury risk by 50%. For knee injury outcomes more broadly, higher compliance had the clearest support; sessions longer than 15 minutes and training 2 to 3 times per week were associated with better results in subgroup analyses, but those dose findings were more tentative. ([Gu et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41175154/)).

A cluster randomized trial in 4,564 adolescent female football players found that a 15-minute neuromuscular warm-up done twice weekly reduced ACL injury rate by 64%, although the absolute rate difference did not reach statistical significance. ([Waldén et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22556050/)).

### Limits and open debates

Much of the anatomy and hormone literature is observational, which makes causal claims harder to establish. Some anatomic traits do appear relevant, and hormone biology clearly affects ligament cells, but these lines of evidence are mainly observational and less directly actionable than the training literature. ([Gilmer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39887996/); [Vacek et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27217522/); [Sturnick et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25583759/); [Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/)).

### Quick reference points

Female adolescent athletes: 1.56-fold higher ACL injury risk overall; female soccer relative risk 3.12. ([Childers et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38692337/)).

Team-ball sports: non-contact ACL injuries accounted for 63% of female ACL injuries and 50% of male ACL injuries; female non-contact incidence was 0.14 vs 0.05 per 1000 player-hours. ([Chia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622227/)).

Menstrual-cycle meta-analysis: no meaningful effect on knee laxity or anterior tibial translation in the newer synthesis. ([Gilmer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39887996/)).

ACL-prevention meta-analysis: pooled risk ratio 0.46 overall; female-only risk ratio 0.57. ([Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/)).

Female-team neuromuscular training meta-analysis: ACL risk ratio 0.50; high compliance had the clearest support, while session-length and frequency findings came from subgroup analyses of knee injury outcomes. ([Gu et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41175154/)).

Cluster randomized trial in adolescent female football players: rate ratio 0.36 with a 15-minute warm-up twice weekly. ([Waldén et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22556050/)).

### Practical interpretation

The best-supported reading is that the female excess in ACL injury risk is not well explained by a simple claim that female athletes are born with “weaker knees.” The wider-pelvis/Q-angle claim is overstated, the hormone claim is biologically plausible but clinically unsettled, and the strength-imbalance claim captures part of the story but not the whole thing. A well-supported practical claim is that training and movement patterns matter because neuromuscular prevention programs repeatedly lower ACL injury risk in trials and meta-analyses. This is an inference from intervention evidence, not a proof that anatomy and hormones do not matter at all. ([Watson et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41486737/); [Gu et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41175154/); [Waldén et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22556050/); [Gilmer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39887996/); [Vacek et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27217522/); [Hertel et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24624006/)).
