# Calcium and Osteoporosis Prevention

“Drink milk for strong bones” is often presented as if higher calcium intake reliably prevents osteoporotic fractures. The evidence is more conditional: calcium is part of normal bone physiology, but increasing intake (especially via supplements) has, at best, **small and context-dependent** effects on fracture outcomes. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

## What the question is and is not

Fractures (especially hip and other non-vertebral fractures) are the clinically important outcomes, and many calcium trials/meta-analyses show different results for “any/total fracture” vs hip fracture specifically. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580); [Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([SentiWeb][1])

A common assumption is that raising dietary calcium to “recommended” targets (often >1200 mg/day in older adults) should reduce fractures without the adverse effects seen with supplements, but systematic review evidence does not clearly support that assumption for fracture prevention. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

## Dietary calcium: does eating more calcium prevent fractures?

In a systematic review that included both cohort evidence and randomized trials, there was **no consistent evidence that higher dietary calcium intake is associated with a lower fracture risk** (and only a small number of dietary-calcium RCTs reported fracture outcomes). ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

This is the key reason the “just drink more milk for fewer fractures” claim is hard to support with fracture-endpoint evidence: increasing dietary calcium to target intakes has not consistently translated into fewer fractures in the available trial and cohort literature summarized in systematic reviews. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

## Calcium supplements: what do fracture-endpoint meta-analyses show?

A central finding in the calcium-supplement literature is outcome-specific: calcium supplements may produce **small reductions in total fractures**, but **do not show clear benefit for hip fractures** in the same analyses (hip fractures being among the most clinically consequential endpoints). ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

When focusing on **community-dwelling** older adults (a population that overlaps with many “generally healthy” postmenopausal women living independently, but is not identical), a large systematic review/meta-analysis of randomized trials found that supplementation with calcium, vitamin D, or both **was not associated with a lower risk of fractures**, and concluded these findings do not support routine use in that population. ([Zhao et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2017.19344)) ([JAMA Network][2])

Taken together, the fracture-endpoint evidence supports a narrower interpretation than most public messaging: **routine calcium supplementation is not reliably fracture-preventive in broadly healthy, community-dwelling women**, and any average benefit (if present) appears small and not clearly present for hip fracture (and in some analyses attenuates in the largest/lowest-bias trials). ([Zhao et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2017.19344); [Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([JAMA Network][2])

## Calcium plus vitamin D: where the fracture signal is strongest

Across high-quality summaries, **vitamin D plus calcium** performs differently from vitamin D alone, and the benefit appears **more pronounced in higher-risk (institutionalized) older adults** than in community settings. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([Cochrane][3])

In the Cochrane review (trials up to December 2012), vitamin D **plus calcium** produced a **small reduction in hip fracture risk** (RR 0.84, 95% CI 0.74–0.96) and a **small reduction in any fracture** (RR 0.95, 95% CI 0.90–0.99), with larger absolute benefit estimates in institutionalized vs community-dwelling populations. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([Cochrane][3])

An updated meta-analysis of RCTs commissioned by the National Osteoporosis Foundation reported statistically significant reductions for **total fractures** (SRRE 0.85) and **hip fractures** (SRRE 0.70), but also showed that the total-fracture estimate differed by setting (community-dwelling SRRE ~0.95 vs institutionalized SRRE ~0.67). ([Weaver et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00198-015-3386-5)) ([Springer Nature][4])

This “setting dependence” is one of the most consistent ways to reconcile competing claims: **combined Ca+D is most defensible as fracture prevention in frailer, higher-risk older adults (often institutionalized and/or more likely to have low baseline intake/status)** rather than as a universal intervention for healthy women. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4); [Weaver et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00198-015-3386-5)) ([Cochrane][3])

## Does vitamin D matter more than calcium?

A strong version of “vitamin D matters more than calcium” (vitamin D alone prevents fractures broadly) is not supported by major evidence syntheses.

In the Cochrane review, vitamin D **alone** was unlikely to prevent hip fracture (RR 1.12, 95% CI 0.98–1.29) or any fracture (RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.96–1.11) in the studied formats/doses, while vitamin D **plus calcium** showed small reductions. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([Cochrane][3])

A systematic review/meta-analysis that evaluated RCTs of vitamin D alone vs vitamin D with calcium concluded that vitamin D alone (intermittent or daily at standard doses) was not associated with reduced fracture risk, while **daily vitamin D plus calcium** appeared more promising. ([Yao et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2019.17789)) ([JAMA Network][5])

More recent RCT meta-analysis in healthy adults ≥60 years (search through November 2023) similarly found **no reduction in total fractures** with vitamin D vs placebo (RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.93–1.14), and reported an **increased hip fracture risk in women** in subgroup analysis (RR 1.34, 95% CI 1.06–1.70), highlighting that regimen/population details can matter and that vitamin D alone is not a simple “more is better” strategy for fracture prevention (with subgroup signals best treated as hypothesis-generating). ([de Souza et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-024-08933-1)) ([Springer Nature][6])

An umbrella review of meta-analyses in adults concluded that results across vitamin D and Ca/D meta-analyses are discrepant in magnitude and significance, with Cochrane reviews among the higher-quality syntheses, reinforcing that the most reliable evidence does not support broad fracture prevention from vitamin D alone. ([Chakhtoura et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab742)) ([OUP Academic][7])

## Supplements vs diet: harms and trade-offs

Even when fracture benefits exist, supplementation involves trade-offs that are often omitted from public messaging.

In Cochrane’s synthesis (including trials of vitamin D and vitamin D analogues), vitamin D with or without calcium increased **hypercalcemia** (RR 2.28, 95% CI 1.57–3.31), slightly increased **gastrointestinal symptoms** (RR 1.04, 95% CI 1.00–1.08), and increased **renal disease** (RR 1.16, 95% CI 1.02–1.33). ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([Cochrane][3])

In the BMJ systematic review, adherence/persistence with calcium supplements in trials was low (roughly 40–60%), and gastrointestinal side effects (including constipation) were cited as a common reason for discontinuation; the same review also notes trial evidence of kidney stone increases in a Ca+D group alongside fewer fractures in that trial context. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])

Cardiovascular outcomes remain an area of uncertainty and publication-/analysis-dependent signals: the BMJ review summarizes that cardiovascular risks reported in calcium trials/meta-analyses can be similar to or exceed fracture-prevention benefits in some analyses. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580); [Bolland et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.c3691); [Bolland et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.d2040)) ([SentiWeb][1])

More recent meta-analyses of placebo-controlled RCTs disagree: one meta-analysis reported about a **15% increased CVD risk** in healthy postmenopausal women with calcium supplements, while another trial-collaboration meta-analysis reported **no significant excess risk** for CHD/stroke outcomes and argued large excess risks could be excluded. ([Myung et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu13020368); [Huo et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cdnut.2023.100046)) ([MDPI][8])

## Quick reference: what the evidence supports (fracture outcomes)

* **Higher dietary calcium intake → fewer fractures:** not supported as a reliable association/effect in systematic review synthesis (and dietary-calcium RCT fracture data are sparse). ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580)) ([SentiWeb][1])
* **Calcium supplements → fewer fractures:** at most small effects on total fracture; no clear hip-fracture benefit in key reviews/meta-analyses, and routine use in community-dwelling older adults is not supported by a large RCT meta-analysis. ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580); [Zhao et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2017.19344)) ([SentiWeb][1])
* **Vitamin D alone → fewer fractures:** not supported in Cochrane and other syntheses; some newer evidence suggests possible harm signals for hip fracture in women in subgroup analysis depending on regimen/trials included. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4); [Yao et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2019.17789); [de Souza et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-024-08933-1)) ([Cochrane][3])
* **Vitamin D + calcium → fewer fractures:** small but more consistent reductions (hip and any fracture), with larger absolute benefit in institutionalized/higher-risk older adults; community-dwelling effects are smaller and sometimes null depending on analysis. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4); [Weaver et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00198-015-3386-5)) ([Cochrane][3])

## Practical interpretation

* If the goal is **fracture prevention in healthy, community-dwelling women**, the best available RCT meta-analytic evidence does **not** support routine calcium (or vitamin D) supplementation as a broadly effective strategy. ([Zhao et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2017.19344)) ([JAMA Network][2])
* If the goal is **fracture prevention in older, higher-risk adults**, especially **institutionalized** populations, the evidence is more favorable for **combined calcium + vitamin D**, though the average effect is still modest. ([Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4); [Weaver et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00198-015-3386-5)) ([Cochrane][3])
* The claim “dietary calcium works but supplements don’t” is not strongly supported for fracture outcomes; rather, **dietary calcium increases are not clearly fracture-preventive either**, while supplements carry clearer adverse-event trade-offs (GI symptoms, renal outcomes; and debated cardiovascular signals). ([Bolland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h4580); [Avenell et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD000227.pub4)) ([SentiWeb][1])
