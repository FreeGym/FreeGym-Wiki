# Why Is Fat Distributed Differently on Women's Bodies?

Fat distribution is not just cosmetic. Where fat is stored changes how strongly it is linked to insulin resistance, blood lipids, inflammation, vascular disease, and type 2 diabetes risk. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286); [Britton et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23850922/); [Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061); [Lotta et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.19329)) The main pattern is real: before menopause, many women store a larger share of fat subcutaneously in the hips, buttocks, and thighs; after menopause, fat storage shifts more centrally. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755)) But the simple "estrogen sends fat to the hips, testosterone sends it to the belly" version is too clean. Sex hormones matter, but so do genetics, age, total energy balance, sleep, stress physiology, and exercise. ([Finkelstein et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1206168); [Klaver et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1111/and.12660); [Rask-Andersen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-08000-4); [Pulit et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1093/hmg/ddy327); [Merlotti et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2017.31); [Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415); [Covassin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.01.038); [Tenk et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.014))

## The basic biology

Adipose tissue is not one uniform organ. Subcutaneous fat sits under the skin; visceral fat sits deeper inside the abdomen around organs; ectopic fat accumulates in tissues like liver, pancreas, skeletal muscle, and around the heart. Visceral and ectopic fat are generally more strongly associated with cardiometabolic risk than lower-body subcutaneous fat. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286); [Britton et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23850922/); [Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061))

Estradiol appears to help maintain a more "female" fat-partitioning pattern: more lower-body subcutaneous storage and less visceral/central storage. Evidence comes from menopause-transition cohorts, menopausal hormone therapy studies, and gender-affirming hormone therapy studies. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755); [Salpeter et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1463-1326.2005.00545.x); [Sites et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2004-1479); [Klaver et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1111/and.12660); [Slukova et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcte.2024.100379)) In the SWAN cohort, body composition worsened during the menopause transition even when body weight and BMI did not fully capture the change; fat mass increased, lean mass declined, and the percentage of body weight as fat increased. Separate SWAN regional-fat analyses show that android and visceral fat also increase across the menopause transition. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755))

Androgens are more complicated. In men, very low testosterone can increase fat mass, and estradiol deficiency also contributes to fat gain because some testosterone is aromatized into estradiol. In women, androgen excess states such as PCOS are often associated with central obesity and insulin resistance, but this relationship is bidirectional and does not mean testosterone alone "causes belly fat" in a simple one-way manner. Hormone effects depend on sex, dose, tissue sensitivity, menopause status, and baseline metabolic state. ([Finkelstein et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1206168); [Lim et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1093/humupd/dms030); [Escobar-Morreale and San Millan, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tem.2007.07.003); [Klaver et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1111/and.12660))

## Why hip and thigh fat can be protective

Gluteofemoral fat - fat stored around the hips, buttocks, and thighs - is often associated with a more favorable metabolic profile than abdominal visceral fat. One reason is that lower-body subcutaneous fat may act as a relatively safe long-term storage depot for fatty acids, keeping them away from liver, pancreas, skeletal muscle, and visceral compartments where they are more metabolically disruptive. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286))

That does not mean "more fat is always healthier." It means that, for a given amount of total body fat, the storage pattern matters. In the Dallas Heart Study, visceral fat was associated with higher incident cardiovascular disease risk, while lower-body fat was associated with lower risk among adults with obesity. ([Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061)) In Framingham, visceral fat was associated with incident cardiovascular disease and cancer after adjustment for standard risk factors. ([Britton et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23850922/))

Genetic evidence points in the same direction. A JAMA genetic study found that genetic variants linked to less gluteofemoral fat or more abdominal fat were associated with higher cardiometabolic risk, including type 2 diabetes and coronary disease. This supports the idea that lower-body fat storage is not merely a passive marker; it may be part of a safer partitioning pattern. ([Lotta et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.19329))

## What changes at menopause

The menopause transition is associated with a shift toward higher total fat mass, lower lean mass, and more central fat storage. In SWAN, fat mass gain accelerated during the menopause transition, lean mass declined, and the percentage of body weight as fat increased, while weight and BMI alone missed some of the body-composition change. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865))

Regional-fat data from SWAN show the central shift more directly. Android fat increased more rapidly during the menopause transition, and visceral fat began increasing during the transition. ([Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389)) In SWAN Heart, women experienced an accelerated increase in visceral adipose tissue beginning about two years before menopause, and this increase was associated with greater carotid atherosclerosis risk. ([Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755)) Heart-adjacent fat depots also appear to rise after menopause; in SWAN cardiovascular-fat data, late peri- and postmenopausal women had more epicardial and paracardial fat than pre- and early perimenopausal women, independent of age and obesity measures. ([El Khoudary et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1210/JC.2015-2110))

The claim that central adiposity is "the main reason" women's cardiometabolic risk catches up after menopause is plausible but overstated. Central fat gain is an important mediator, but estrogen loss also affects vascular function, blood lipids, insulin sensitivity, inflammation, and blood pressure. Menopause-related risk is therefore better understood as a combined shift in hormones, body composition, vascular biology, and aging rather than as belly fat alone. ([El Khoudary et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000912); [Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755))

## Sex-hormone interventions and fat partitioning

Gender-affirming hormone therapy gives useful, though imperfect, evidence because it changes sex-steroid exposure more directly than ordinary aging studies. A meta-analysis found that feminizing hormone therapy increased fat mass and reduced lean mass, while masculinizing therapy reduced fat mass and increased lean mass. ([Klaver et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1111/and.12660))

More recent MRI-based evidence suggests that feminizing therapy can shift the visceral-to-subcutaneous fat ratio downward. In a small 2025 MRI/MRS study, six months of feminizing hormone therapy in transgender women significantly reduced the visceral/subcutaneous abdominal fat ratio. The study was small and also observed reduced insulin sensitivity in transgender women, so it is mechanistically useful but not definitive. ([Slukova et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcte.2024.100379))

Menopausal hormone therapy studies are mixed. A meta-analysis reported reductions in abdominal fat and several metabolic-syndrome components with hormone replacement therapy, but trials varied in formulation, dose, age, route, timing, and study quality. ([Salpeter et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1463-1326.2005.00545.x)) A randomized placebo-controlled trial of conjugated estrogen plus medroxyprogesterone did not show a clear beneficial change in body composition or fat distribution and reduced insulin sensitivity. ([Sites et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2004-1479)) Hormone therapy should therefore not be framed as a weight-loss or body-shape intervention based on these body-composition studies. ([Salpeter et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1463-1326.2005.00545.x); [Sites et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2004-1479))

## Genetics sets the template, but it does not make behavior irrelevant

Fat distribution is strongly influenced by genetics. Large genome-wide association studies show that waist-to-hip ratio and body-fat distribution have many genetic determinants, with sex-specific effects that are especially prominent in women. Much of this evidence is European-ancestry-heavy, so generalization across populations should be made carefully. ([Pulit et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1093/hmg/ddy327); [Rask-Andersen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-08000-4))

This is why two people can lose the same amount of weight but change shape differently. The body does not draw fat evenly from every depot on command. Weight-loss interventions do not affect all depots equally; meta-analytic evidence shows differential changes in subcutaneous and visceral depots across diet, exercise, weight-loss drugs, and bariatric surgery. ([Merlotti et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2017.31))

Spot-reduction is not supported by the best current controlled evidence. A systematic review and meta-analysis of unilateral limb-training studies found no meaningful local fat-loss effect in the trained limb compared with the untrained limb. Abdominal exercises can strengthen abdominal muscles, but they do not reliably make the body preferentially burn abdominal fat. ([Ramirez-Campillo et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.5114/hm.2022.110373))

## Exercise can reduce visceral fat, even without perfect weight loss

The strongest behavior evidence is for exercise. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that exercise without calorie restriction reduced visceral adipose tissue in adults with overweight, with moderate- to high-intensity aerobic exercise showing especially strong effects. ([Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415))

A network meta-analysis of randomized trials found that high-intensity interval training and aerobic exercise reduced visceral fat, with effective programs often using about three sessions per week for 12 to 16 weeks. Resistance training was less consistently effective for visceral-fat reduction in that analysis, but it remains important because it can help preserve or increase lean mass and improve strength. ([Chang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-021-00767-9); [Lopez et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428)) A newer network meta-analysis of 84 randomized trials found that regular exercise broadly improves visceral adipose tissue in people with overweight or obesity, with vigorous aerobic exercise and HIIT ranking highly. ([Chen et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13666))

So behavior can shift distribution somewhat, but not in the "do this exercise to remove fat from this exact spot" sense. A better framing is: exercise, especially aerobic or HIIT-style training combined with resistance training, can reduce visceral fat and improve body composition even when the scale changes modestly. ([Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415); [Chang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-021-00767-9); [Chen et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13666); [Lopez et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428))

## Sleep and stress: real signals, weaker intervention proof

Sleep restriction can promote central fat accumulation. In a randomized crossover study, experimental sleep restriction increased energy intake and body weight; total abdominal fat increased only during the sleep-restriction condition, with increases in both abdominal subcutaneous and visceral fat. ([Covassin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.01.038))

Stress is biologically plausible because cortisol, sleep disruption, appetite regulation, and reward-driven eating can all affect abdominal fat storage. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that perceived stress correlated with visceral obesity and lipid parameters of metabolic syndrome. ([Tenk et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.014)) Longitudinal evidence also links psychosocial stress to adiposity, though effect sizes are generally modest and heterogeneous. ([Wardle et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1038/oby.2010.241))

Stress-management interventions should not be sold as direct belly-fat reducers. The intervention evidence is much thinner than the exercise evidence. Small trials of mindfulness or stress-eating interventions suggest possible benefits for stress eating, cortisol patterns, and abdominal fat, but these are not strong enough to claim that stress management independently "redistributes" fat in a precise way. ([Daubenmier et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1155/2011/651936))

## Verdict on the competing claims

The claim that hip and thigh subcutaneous fat is metabolically protective is mostly supported. More precisely, lower-body subcutaneous fat is often associated with lower cardiometabolic risk compared with visceral or ectopic fat, especially when comparing people with similar BMI or total fat mass. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286); [Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061); [Lotta et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.19329))

The claim that menopause shifts women toward central adiposity is strongly supported. Longitudinal menopause-transition studies show increases in fat mass, visceral fat, android fat, and cardiovascular fat depots around menopause. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [El Khoudary et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1210/JC.2015-2110); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755))

The claim that this central shift is the main reason women's cardiometabolic risk catches up to men is partly supported but too narrow. Central adiposity is important, but estrogen loss also affects vascular, lipid, inflammatory, and insulin-related pathways directly. ([El Khoudary et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000912))

The claim that fat distribution is largely genetic and hormonal is supported, but "largely" should not mean "fixed." Genetics and hormones set the template; total fat loss, exercise, sleep, and stress-related behaviors can still change visceral fat and metabolic risk. ([Rask-Andersen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-08000-4); [Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415); [Chang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-021-00767-9); [Covassin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.01.038); [Tenk et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.014))

The claim that spot-reduction is not a practical fat-loss strategy is well supported. Local muscle training does not reliably produce local fat loss. ([Ramirez-Campillo et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.5114/hm.2022.110373))

The claim that resistance training, sleep, and stress management influence visceral fat independently of total weight is mixed. Exercise has the strongest evidence, especially aerobic and high-intensity interval training; resistance training is important for lean mass and metabolic health, with less consistent direct evidence for visceral-fat reduction. Sleep restriction has controlled evidence linking it to abdominal and visceral fat gain. Stress has plausible biology and observational support, but direct intervention evidence is weaker. ([Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415); [Chang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-021-00767-9); [Lopez et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428); [Covassin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.01.038); [Tenk et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.014))

## Practical interpretation

A smaller waist, lower visceral fat, and preserved or higher lean mass are generally better cardiometabolic signs than simply weighing less. BMI can miss important changes in fat partitioning, especially during menopause. ([Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061); [Lopez et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428))

The most evidence-based approach is not trying to "target" hip, thigh, or belly fat directly. It is reducing visceral fat risk while preserving muscle: regular aerobic exercise or HIIT, resistance training, adequate sleep, nutrition that supports sustainable fat loss if needed, and stress-management strategies that improve sleep, eating patterns, and adherence. ([Vissers et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0056415); [Chang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41366-021-00767-9); [Chen et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13666); [Lopez et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428); [Merlotti et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2017.31); [Covassin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.01.038); [Tenk et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2018.05.014); [Daubenmier et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1155/2011/651936))

Lower-body fat is not something to treat as a health problem by itself. In many people, especially premenopausal women, hip and thigh subcutaneous fat may be a safer storage depot. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286); [Lotta et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.19329)) The more concerning pattern is increasing waist circumference, visceral or ectopic fat, liver fat, or loss of muscle with age. ([Manolopoulos et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1038/ijo.2009.286); [Britton et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23850922/); [Neeland et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.01.061); [Greendale et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1172/jci.insight.124865); [Greendale et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgab389); [Samargandy et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1097/GME.0000000000001755))
