## Iron deficiency without anemia and fatigue

Fatigue is common, and iron deficiency can exist before hemoglobin falls into the anemic range. The evidence supports iron deficiency without anemia as a plausible cause of fatigue in some people, especially menstruating or premenopausal women with documented low iron stores, but not as a universal explanation for tiredness. Most of the direct symptom-treatment trials were done in women, so the evidence is much thinner outside that population. ([Yokoi and Konomi, 2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"); [Houston et al., 2018](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ..."); [Al-Naseem et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.7861/clinmed.2020-0582 "Iron deficiency without anaemia: a diagnosis that matters")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

## What iron deficiency without anemia means in the trials

The fatigue trials did not use one single definition. [Verdon et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ...") studied non-anemic women with unexplained fatigue, many of whom had low or borderline ferritin. [Vaucher et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ...") required ferritin below 50 ug/L and hemoglobin above 12 g/dL. [Krayenbuehl et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ...") enrolled premenopausal women with ferritin 50 ng/mL or lower and normal hemoglobin. The PREFER trial ([Favrat et al., 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0094217 "Evaluation of a Single Dose of Ferric Carboxymaltose in Fatigued, Iron-Deficient Women - PREFER a Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Study | PLOS One")) used ferritin below 50 ug/L plus transferrin saturation below 20%, or ferritin below 15 ug/L, with normal or borderline hemoglobin. That variation matters, because it means the literature does not establish one universal symptom-treatment cutoff such as ferritin below 30 ug/L. That is not the same as saying ferritin below 30 ug/L lacks diagnostic value: outside inflammatory states, it remains a common diagnostic threshold for iron deficiency in clinical practice, even though some studies and clinical frameworks use higher cutoffs such as 45-50 ug/L. ([Verdon et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."); [Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."); [Krayenbuehl et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ..."); [Favrat et al., 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0094217 "Evaluation of a Single Dose of Ferric Carboxymaltose in Fatigued, Iron-Deficient Women - PREFER a Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Study | PLOS One"); [Al-Naseem et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.7861/clinmed.2020-0582 "Iron deficiency without anaemia: a diagnosis that matters"); [Jäger et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.25692 "Ferritin Cutoffs and Diagnosis of Iron Deficiency in Primary Care")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."))

## Does low iron always make you tired?

No. The clearest summary comes from [Yokoi and Konomi’s meta-analysis](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core") of six randomized trials and six cross-sectional studies. In the randomized trials, iron treatment had a significant beneficial effect on fatigue in people with iron deficiency without anemia. But in the pooled cross-sectional studies, the association between iron deficiency without anemia and fatigue was not significant and was not robust to sensitivity analysis. That pattern fits a practical conclusion: low iron can be one cause of fatigue, but fatigue is too nonspecific for low ferritin to be assumed to be the cause every time. ([Yokoi and Konomi, 2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

## What oral supplementation trials show

In [Verdon et al. (2003)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."), 144 non-anemic women with unexplained fatigue were randomized to 80 mg/day elemental iron or placebo for 4 weeks. Fatigue fell by 29% in the iron group versus 13% in the placebo group, and subgroup analysis suggested benefit mainly in women with ferritin 50 ug/L or lower. ([Verdon et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ...")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."))

In [Vaucher et al. (2012)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."), 198 menstruating women with fatigue, ferritin below 50 ug/L, and hemoglobin above 12 g/dL received 80 mg/day elemental iron or placebo for 12 weeks. Fatigue scores fell 47.7% with iron versus 28.8% with placebo. Hemoglobin, ferritin, and soluble transferrin receptor improved, but quality of life, depression, and anxiety did not. Within that trial, the fatigue effect did not depend on being below versus above 15 ug/L ferritin once women were already in the below-50 ug/L range. ([Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ...")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."))

Taken together, the oral trials support a modest symptom-level benefit in selected iron-deficient, non-anemic women with fatigue. They do not show that iron is a general fatigue treatment, and they do not show consistent improvement across all related outcomes. ([Verdon et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."); [Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."); [Houston et al., 2018](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ...")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."))

## What intravenous trials show

The IV literature points in the same general direction, but with more caution. [Krayenbuehl et al. (2011)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ...") reported a nonsignificant overall fatigue improvement over the initial 6 weeks, with the signal appearing strongest at ferritin 15 ng/mL or lower. ([Krayenbuehl et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ...")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ..."))

In the PREFER trial, a single 1000 mg ferric carboxymaltose infusion improved the proportion of women with reduced fatigue at Day 56, 65.3% versus 52.7% with placebo, and a 50% reduction in fatigue was also more common with iron. Mental quality of life and some cognitive measures also improved. More side effects were reported in the iron group, although overall treatment-emergent adverse events were not significantly different and were mainly mild or moderate. ([Favrat et al., 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0094217 "Evaluation of a Single Dose of Ferric Carboxymaltose in Fatigued, Iron-Deficient Women - PREFER a Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Study | PLOS One")) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0094217 "Evaluation of a Single Dose of Ferric Carboxymaltose in Fatigued, Iron-Deficient Women - PREFER a Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Study | PLOS One"))

The 2022 abridged Cochrane review of intravenous iron pooled 21 randomized trials with 3514 participants. It found improvement in fatigue and a small increase in peak oxygen consumption, but no overall quality-of-life benefit. Mild adverse events were more common with IV iron, serious adverse events were not, and the evidence quality was rated low or very low for all outcomes except fatigue. ([Dugan et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36321348/ "Systematic review and meta-analysis of intravenous iron ...")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36321348/ "Systematic review and meta-analysis of intravenous iron ..."))

## What the performance evidence shows

The performance story is narrower than the fatigue story. [Houston et al. (2018)](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ..."), pooling mixed non-anemic iron-deficient adults, found less self-reported fatigue with iron but no overall improvement in objective physical capacity. By contrast, [Pasricha et al. (2014)](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.113.189589 "Iron supplementation benefits physical performance in women of reproductive age: a systematic review and meta-analysis") found in women of reproductive age that daily iron improved maximal and submaximal exercise performance, including VO2max-related outcomes and submaximal heart-rate measures. A reasonable synthesis is that subjective fatigue improves more consistently than objective performance, and performance effects look stronger in women of reproductive age and exercise settings than in heterogeneous adult populations. ([Houston et al., 2018](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ..."); [Pasricha et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.113.189589 "Iron supplementation benefits physical performance in women of reproductive age: a systematic review and meta-analysis")) ([BMJ Open](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ..."))

## How to think about the ferritin cutoff

The claim that supplementation only helps when ferritin is below 30 is too rigid for the trial data. Successful oral fatigue trials often enrolled women with ferritin below 50, not only below 30. At the same time, some IV data suggest the strongest signal may occur at very low ferritin, such as 15 or lower. That does not mean ferritin below 30 lacks diagnostic value; outside inflammatory states it remains a common diagnostic threshold for absolute iron deficiency, even though treatment-response trials used a range of entry criteria. The best evidence-based summary is not “30 is the only real threshold,” but “benefit is most plausible when biochemical deficiency is documented, symptoms fit, and other causes of fatigue have been considered.” ([Verdon et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."); [Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."); [Krayenbuehl et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21705493/ "Intravenous Iron for the Treatment of Fatigue in Nonanemic ..."); [Favrat et al., 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0094217 "Evaluation of a Single Dose of Ferric Carboxymaltose in Fatigued, Iron-Deficient Women - PREFER a Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Study | PLOS One"); [Al-Naseem et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.7861/clinmed.2020-0582 "Iron deficiency without anaemia: a diagnosis that matters"); [Jäger et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.25692 "Ferritin Cutoffs and Diagnosis of Iron Deficiency in Primary Care")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12763985/ "Iron supplementation for unexplained fatigue in non- ..."))

## Harms and limits

Iron is not harmless. A 2015 systematic review and meta-analysis found that ferrous sulfate significantly increased gastrointestinal-specific side effects in adults. In [Vaucher et al. (2012)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."), adverse events were numerically more common with oral iron but not significantly different. In [Dugan et al. (2022)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36321348/ "Systematic review and meta-analysis of intravenous iron ..."), IV iron increased mild adverse events but not serious ones. The short-term RCT literature is much better at showing symptom effects and tolerability than at answering broader long-term questions. ([Tolkien et al., 2015](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0117383 "Ferrous sulfate supplementation causes significant gastrointestinal side-effects in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis | PLOS One"); [Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."); [Dugan et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36321348/ "Systematic review and meta-analysis of intravenous iron ...")) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0117383 "Ferrous sulfate supplementation causes significant gastrointestinal side-effects in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis | PLOS One"))

## Practical interpretation

Low iron without anemia can be symptomatic, but it does not always make you tired. The best evidence supports a modest fatigue benefit from iron in selected iron-deficient, non-anemic groups, especially menstruating or premenopausal women with low ferritin. Broader claims about objective performance are more mixed. The evidence does not support blaming every fatigue complaint on low ferritin, and it does not support ferritin below 30 as the only threshold at which treatment can work, even though ferritin below 30 remains a common diagnostic threshold for deficiency. The most defensible conclusion is that iron works best as a targeted treatment for documented deficiency, not as a blanket fix for tiredness. ([Yokoi and Konomi, 2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"); [Houston et al., 2018](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/4/e019240 "Efficacy of iron supplementation on fatigue and physical ..."); [Vaucher et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22777991/ "Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic ..."); [Dugan et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36321348/ "Systematic review and meta-analysis of intravenous iron ..."); [Al-Naseem et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.7861/clinmed.2020-0582 "Iron deficiency without anaemia: a diagnosis that matters")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/iron-deficiency-without-anaemia-is-a-potential-cause-of-fatigue-metaanalyses-of-randomised-controlled-trials-and-crosssectional-studies/F7E59D4BFC154E9687E42CDCC4968EAF "Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))
