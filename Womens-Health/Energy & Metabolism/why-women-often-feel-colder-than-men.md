## **Why women often feel colder than men**

Women do, on average, report feeling colder than men, especially in cool indoor environments. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found that women report significantly colder sensations and have lower mean skin temperatures than men in cold environments, even though their core temperatures are slightly higher. The average difference in reported thermal sensation was modest, and the physiological estimates were heterogeneous across studies, which argues against a one-line explanation. The best-supported reading of the literature is that several factors interact: lower absolute heat production that is largely explained by lower lean mass, colder skin and extremities from different vasomotor responses, hormone-related shifts in thermoregulation, and indoor temperature standards that often reflect male-based metabolic assumptions more than female physiology. Iron deficiency and hypothyroidism are also important clinical causes of cold intolerance, but they are better thought of as additional explanations for the individual who feels unusually cold, not the whole explanation for the average sex difference. ([Vellei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.buildenv.2025.112936); [Johnstone et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/82.5.941); [Greenfield et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2023.114126); [Kingma and van Marken Lichtenbelt, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1038/nclimate2741); [Taylor et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736\(24\)01614-3); [Auerbach et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2025.0452); [Brigham et al., 1996](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408399609527748)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360132325004184?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Do women feel colder by nature? ## **Lower muscle mass and metabolic rate**

Absolute resting metabolic rate is higher in men than in women. However, the stronger metabolic studies suggest that much of this difference is explained by fat-free mass rather than sex itself. Johnstone et al. found that variation in basal metabolic rate was explained by fat-free mass, fat mass, age, and thyroxine, but not by sex after adjustment. Buchholz et al. similarly found that the male-female difference in resting metabolic rate became non-significant after adjustment for lean body mass. An older study by Arciero et al. reported a modest residual difference even after controlling for fat-free mass, fat mass, and aerobic fitness. So the popular claim is partly right but too blunt: women usually produce less heat at rest in absolute terms, but much of that appears to be because they tend to have less metabolically active lean tissue, not because female metabolism is uniformly "slower" in some simple categorical sense. These studies concern resting energy expenditure rather than subjective cold perception directly, so they support a plausible mechanism rather than a complete explanation. ([Johnstone et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/82.5.941); [Buchholz et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1079/BJN2001471); [Arciero et al., 1993](https://doi.org/10.1152/jappl.1993.75.6.2514)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16280423/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Factors influencing variation in basal metabolic rate include fat-free ..."))

## **Skin temperature and blood flow**

The thermoregulation literature suggests that feeling cold may be driven less by a lower core temperature and more by lower skin temperature and peripheral blood-flow differences. In the 2025 meta-analysis, women had lower mean skin temperature in cold environments but higher core temperature overall. Greenfield et al. concluded that sex differences in local thermal perception become smaller when body morphology is well controlled, yet differences in early vasomotor responses and subsequent changes in blood flow probably contribute to the greater thermal awareness women report. That fits the common experience of colder hands and feet better than a whole-body "low metabolism" theory alone does. ([Vellei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.buildenv.2025.112936); [Greenfield et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2023.114126)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360132325004184?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Do women feel colder by nature? ## **Hormones and reproductive state**

Hormones matter, but the shorthand claim that estrogen simply "causes cold hands and feet" is too simple. Charkoudian and Stachenfeld concluded that estrogens generally promote lower body temperatures by augmenting heat-dissipation responses, whereas progesterone tends to promote higher body temperature and can augment skin vasoconstriction. Baker et al. reviewed menstrual-cycle physiology and found that core body temperature is typically about 0.3 to 0.7°C higher in the luteal phase than in the follicular phase. The newer meta-analysis likewise found that menstruation, pregnancy, and menopause affect women's thermal assessment, although the amount of data remains limited. So the hormone story is real, but it is dynamic and state-dependent rather than a single fixed estrogen effect. ([Charkoudian and Stachenfeld, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autneu.2015.11.004); [Baker et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1080/23328940.2020.1735927); [Vellei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.buildenv.2025.112936)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1566070215300321?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Review Sex hormone effects on autonomic mechanisms of ..."))

## **Thermostats, buildings, and social context**

The evidence does not support a clean "cultural, not biological" explanation. Karjalainen's review found that women are more likely than men to report thermal dissatisfaction in the same indoor environments, especially in cooler conditions, even though many studies do not show large sex differences in neutral temperature itself. At the same time, buildings clearly matter. Kingma and van Marken Lichtenbelt argued that standard metabolic-rate values used in thermal comfort models are based on an average male and may overestimate female metabolic rate by up to 35%, which would bias recommended temperatures downward for women. Parkinson et al. then used two large US office datasets to show that office temperatures are less comfortable for women largely because of overcooling. The most defensible conclusion is that biology contributes to the difference, and indoor standards and workplace norms can magnify it. ([Karjalainen, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1600-0668.2011.00747.x); [Kingma and van Marken Lichtenbelt, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1038/nclimate2741); [Parkinson et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-03121-1)) ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1600-0668.2011.00747.x?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Thermal comfort and gender: a literature review - Karjalainen"))

## **Iron deficiency and thyroid disease**

These explanations matter most at the individual level. Hypothyroidism classically presents with fatigue, lethargy, weight gain, and cold intolerance, and the most common cause is chronic autoimmune thyroiditis. A systematic review estimated the incidence of autoimmune hypothyroidism at about 350 per 100,000 per year in women versus 80 per 100,000 per year in men. Iron deficiency is also common in women of reproductive age; a 2025 JAMA review estimated that in high-income countries about 38% of nonpregnant reproductive-age women have iron deficiency without anemia and about 13% have iron-deficiency anemia. The thermoregulation literature here is older and is strongest for iron-deficiency anemia, though some reviews discuss impaired temperature regulation in iron deficiency more broadly. So persistent or newly worse cold intolerance should prompt consideration of thyroid testing and iron studies in the right clinical context, not just arguments about the thermostat. ([Taylor et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736\(24\)01614-3); [McGrogan et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2265.2008.03338.x); [Auerbach et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2025.0452); [Brigham et al., 1996](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408399609527748)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39368843/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Hypothyroidism"))

## **Limits and open debates**

The biggest unresolved question is how much of the average sex difference remains after body size, lean mass, and body morphology are carefully matched. Some studies find little or no independent sex effect after adjustment, while others find a small residual difference. The hormonal literature is also more complicated than popular summaries make it sound, because estrogen and progesterone can shift different thermoregulatory mechanisms in different directions. And for iron, the strongest evidence on cold intolerance concerns iron-deficiency anemia rather than every case of isolated low ferritin. ([Johnstone et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/82.5.941); [Buchholz et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1079/BJN2001471); [Arciero et al., 1993](https://doi.org/10.1152/jappl.1993.75.6.2514); [Greenfield et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2023.114126); [Charkoudian and Stachenfeld, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autneu.2015.11.004); [Brigham et al., 1996](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408399609527748)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16280423/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Factors influencing variation in basal metabolic rate include fat-free ..."))

## **Practical interpretation**

Most women are not colder than men for one single reason. The best evidence points to a combination of lower absolute heat production that is largely explained by less lean mass, lower skin temperature and different peripheral blood-flow responses in the cold, hormone-related changes across the menstrual cycle and reproductive life stages, and indoor environments that are often tuned around male-based comfort assumptions. Thyroid disease and iron deficiency, especially iron-deficiency anemia, are important medical considerations when cold intolerance is disproportionate, persistent, or newly worse. ([Vellei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.buildenv.2025.112936); [Greenfield et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2023.114126); [Charkoudian and Stachenfeld, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.autneu.2015.11.004); [Parkinson et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-03121-1); [Taylor et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736\(24\)01614-3); [Brigham et al., 1996](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408399609527748)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360132325004184?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Do women feel colder by nature?
