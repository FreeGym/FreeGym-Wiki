# Do Women's Metabolisms Actually Adapt Differently to Dieting?

The physiology often described as "metabolic damage" is more precise when separated into **adaptive thermogenesis** and other predictable changes after weight loss. Adaptive thermogenesis is a drop in energy expenditure that is greater than predicted from the loss of fat mass and fat-free mass. It is real, but the evidence below does not establish the strongest version of the claim that ordinary dieting permanently and cumulatively damages women's resting metabolism. The more defensible view is that weight loss lowers energy needs through smaller body size, some lean-mass loss, temporary or sometimes persistent adaptive thermogenesis, and a larger appetite or behavioral drive to regain weight. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/oby.21538 "Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after The Biggest Loser competition | Obesity | Wiley Online Library"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30321282/ "No consistent evidence of a disproportionately low resting energy expenditure in long-term successful weight-loss maintainers | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32386226/ "Metabolic adaptation is not a major barrier to weight-loss maintenance | PubMed"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? Quantification of the feedback control of human energy intake | PubMed"))

## What adaptive thermogenesis is

Adaptive thermogenesis is not simply "burning fewer calories after weight loss." A smaller body burns fewer calories, and fat-free mass is a major determinant of resting energy expenditure. Adaptive thermogenesis refers to the **extra** reduction in resting, sleeping, or total daily energy expenditure after accounting for changes in fat mass and fat-free mass. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

That distinction matters because many claims about "metabolic damage" mix together three different things: expected lower energy needs from weighing less, loss of metabolically active tissue, and a true adaptive drop beyond body-composition changes. In research terms, the key question is not whether energy expenditure and appetite biology change during dieting; they do. The question is how much of the expenditure change is predicted by body composition, how much is adaptive, how long it persists, and whether the pattern differs by sex. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12986-021-00587-8 "Metabolic adaptation is associated with less weight and fat mass loss in response to low-energy diets | Nutrition & Metabolism | Springer Nature Link"), [Canadian Science Publishing](https://cdnsciencepub.com/doi/10.1139/apnm-2022-0372 "Weight loss induces changes in adaptive thermogenesis in female and male physique athletes | Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism"))

## How big is adaptive thermogenesis?

A systematic review of 33 adult studies including 2528 participants found adaptive thermogenesis in 27 studies, but it also emphasized major methodological heterogeneity. The review concluded that adaptive thermogenesis may occur after weight loss, but values are often small or non-significant in higher-quality designs, and the effect appears attenuated or absent after weight stabilization or neutral energy balance. The review was supported by the Portuguese Foundation for Science and Technology, and the authors reported no conflicts of interest. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

In a controlled low-energy-diet study of 71 adults with obesity, participants lost about 14 kg after 8 weeks on 1000 kcal/day diets and showed metabolic adaptation of about -92 kcal/day immediately after weight loss. In that study, a 50 kcal/day greater absolute magnitude of metabolic adaptation was associated with about 0.5 kg less weight and fat-mass loss, after adjustment for adherence, physical activity, sex, and body composition. The interaction between metabolic adaptation and sex was not significant, which argues against a clear "women adapt more" conclusion in that dataset. The study was funded by NTNU and the Liaison Committee for education, research and innovation in Central Norway, and the authors declared no competing interests. ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12986-021-00587-8 "Metabolic adaptation is associated with less weight and fat mass loss in response to low-energy diets | Nutrition & Metabolism | Springer Nature Link"))

Another study from the same research group found that metabolic adaptation was about -92 kcal/day immediately after weight loss, fell to about -38 kcal/day after a 4-week stabilization phase, and was not present at 1 year in the subset with complete data. It also did not predict weight regain. This supports the idea that some measured "adaptation" reflects being in an active energy deficit rather than a permanently lowered metabolism. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522008929 "Metabolic adaptation is an illusion, only present when participants are in negative energy balance | American Journal of Clinical Nutrition | ScienceDirect"))

## Does it persist long term?

Some classic studies do show persistent energy-expenditure suppression after large sustained weight loss. Leibel, Rosenbaum, and Hirsch found that maintaining body weight at least 10% below usual weight was associated with reduced total energy expenditure per kg fat-free mass. Rosenbaum and colleagues later reported lower energy expenditure in people who had maintained at least 10% weight loss for more than 1 year. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7632212/ "Changes in energy expenditure resulting from altered body weight | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18842775/ "Long-term persistence of adaptive thermogenesis in subjects who have maintained a reduced body weight | PubMed"))

The "Biggest Loser" follow-up is the most dramatic example. Six years after the competition, participants had regained much of the lost weight but still showed substantial metabolic adaptation; the authors reported persistent adaptation of roughly -499 kcal/day. This is real evidence against the idea that all adaptation rapidly disappears, but it came from an extreme weight-loss competition, so it should not be treated as typical dieting physiology. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/oby.21538 "Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after The Biggest Loser competition | Obesity | Wiley Online Library"))

Other long-term data are less dramatic. In long-term successful weight-loss maintainers, Ostendorf and colleagues found no consistent evidence of disproportionately low resting energy expenditure compared with prediction equations. In 171 overweight women followed for 2 years after weight loss, metabolic adaptation at the level of resting metabolic rate was minimal under weight-stable measurement conditions and did not predict weight regain; in a subset with complete data, adaptation was present after weight loss but not at 1 or 2 years. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30321282/ "No consistent evidence of a disproportionately low resting energy expenditure in long-term successful weight-loss maintainers | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32386226/ "Metabolic adaptation is not a major barrier to weight-loss maintenance | PubMed"))

## Are women different?

The cited evidence does not show that women have a uniquely "damaged" resting metabolism after dieting. In the 71-person low-energy-diet study, metabolic adaptation affected weight-loss response, but the effect was independent of sex. In drug-free physique athletes, both female and male competitors showed reduced body-composition-adjusted resting energy expenditure, lower T3, and lower resting heart rate during contest preparation; the responses moved back toward baseline after recovery. Female leptin fell more in absolute terms, but that was partly because women started with higher leptin. ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12986-021-00587-8 "Metabolic adaptation is associated with less weight and fat mass loss in response to low-energy diets | Nutrition & Metabolism | Springer Nature Link"), [Canadian Science Publishing](https://cdnsciencepub.com/doi/10.1139/apnm-2022-0372 "Weight loss induces changes in adaptive thermogenesis in female and male physique athletes | Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism"))

A clearer sex-specific issue in the cited evidence is the reproductive axis. In regularly menstruating young women, Loucks and Thuma found that luteinizing-hormone pulsatility was unaffected at energy availability of 30 kcal/kg lean body mass/day, but below that threshold LH pulse frequency decreased and pulse amplitude increased. That is direct evidence that sufficiently low energy availability can disrupt reproductive signaling in women. ([Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/88/1/297/2846067 "Luteinizing hormone pulsatility is disrupted at a threshold of energy availability in regularly menstruating women | Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism"))

Low energy availability may also affect bone-related physiology. In a randomized crossover study of 11 eumenorrheic women and 11 men, 5 days at 15 kcal/kg lean body mass/day decreased bone formation and increased bone resorption markers within women but not within men; however, the authors also reported no significant sex-specific differences between men and women for the bone-marker area-under-the-curve comparisons, so the study supports caution but not a simple "women are always more disrupted" rule. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S8756328217303137 "Effects of reduced energy availability on bone metabolism in women and men | Bone | ScienceDirect"))

## Hormones are real, but they are not the same as permanent damage

Diet-induced weight loss changes appetite-regulating hormones. In a study of 50 overweight or obese adults without diabetes, a 10-week very-low-energy diet reduced leptin, peptide YY, cholecystokinin, insulin, and amylin, increased ghrelin and other appetite-related hormones, and increased subjective appetite. Several of these differences from baseline were still present 1 year later. The study was funded by the National Health and Medical Research Council and others. ([Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"))

These hormonal changes help explain why dieting can feel harder over time, especially after substantial weight loss. But they do not prove that a woman's metabolism has been permanently damaged. They show that the body defends against weight loss through both energy-expenditure and appetite pathways. ([Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? Quantification of the feedback control of human energy intake | PubMed"), [Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

## Muscle loss is a major confounder

Some of the "lower metabolism" after dieting is not adaptive thermogenesis at all; it is the expected consequence of reduced body mass and body-composition changes, especially reduced fat-free mass. That component is partly preventable. In overweight premenopausal women, resistance training conserved fat-free mass, resting energy expenditure, and strength after weight loss compared with aerobic training or no training. In older overweight women, resistance training helped preserve fat-free mass during energy-restricted weight loss. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18356845/ "Resistance training conserves fat-free mass and resting energy expenditure following weight loss | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19247271/ "Resistance training preserves fat-free mass without impacting changes in protein metabolism after weight loss in older women | PubMed"))

Protein intake also matters. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 47 studies found that higher protein intake significantly helped prevent muscle-mass decline in adults with overweight or obesity attempting weight loss, although it did not significantly prevent declines in strength or physical function. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405457724001761 "Enhanced protein intake on maintaining muscle mass, strength, and physical function in adults with overweight/obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis | Clinical Nutrition ESPEN | ScienceDirect"))

This supports the second competing claim: part of the persistent reduction in energy needs after dieting is body-composition related, and muscle-preserving strategies can reduce that component. It does not mean adaptive thermogenesis is imaginary; it means the phrase "metabolic damage" often over-attributes everything to a mysterious broken metabolism. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18356845/ "Resistance training conserves fat-free mass and resting energy expenditure following weight loss | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19247271/ "Resistance training preserves fat-free mass without impacting changes in protein metabolism after weight loss in older women | PubMed"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405457724001761 "Enhanced protein intake on maintaining muscle mass, strength, and physical function in adults with overweight/obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis | Clinical Nutrition ESPEN | ScienceDirect"))

## Appetite and behavior may matter more than resting metabolism

A major reason weight regain happens is that the body increases the drive to eat. Polidori, Sanghvi, Seeley, and Hall model-estimated that weight loss produced an appetite or energy-intake feedback signal of about 100 kcal/day per kg of lost weight, more than threefold larger than the corresponding energy-expenditure adaptations. This study used data from a Janssen-sponsored canagliflozin trial; one author was a Janssen employee, and other disclosures included consulting, research, or patent-related interests, so the findings should be interpreted with that conflict context in mind. It should not be the sole evidence for the claim, but it aligns with independent hormonal evidence showing persistent appetite-related changes after weight loss. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? Quantification of the feedback control of human energy intake | PubMed"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"))

This is why the "it's just adherence" framing is too harsh, but the "my metabolism is permanently ruined" framing is too strong. Adherence is affected by biology: hunger, reduced spontaneous movement, and lower energy needs all push in the same direction. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? Quantification of the feedback control of human energy intake | PubMed"))

## Competing claims, graded

**Repeated dieting causes cumulative, irreversible metabolic damage:** not established by the cited evidence. Persistent adaptive thermogenesis can occur after large sustained losses, but studies in weight-stable women and long-term maintainers do not show a universal permanent suppression of resting metabolism. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/oby.21538 "Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after The Biggest Loser competition | Obesity | Wiley Online Library"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30321282/ "No consistent evidence of a disproportionately low resting energy expenditure in long-term successful weight-loss maintainers | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32386226/ "Metabolic adaptation is not a major barrier to weight-loss maintenance | PubMed"))

**Adaptive thermogenesis is real but often reverses with stabilization/refeeding:** broadly supported, with caveats. The best current synthesis is that adaptive thermogenesis is detectable in many studies during active weight loss, but it is smaller or absent in better-controlled weight-stable conditions. Some extreme or sustained weight-loss states show persistence. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522008929 "Metabolic adaptation is an illusion, only present when participants are in negative energy balance | American Journal of Clinical Nutrition | ScienceDirect"), [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/oby.21538 "Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after The Biggest Loser competition | Obesity | Wiley Online Library"))

**Women experience greater hormonal disruption during caloric deficit:** partly supported. Women have a sex-specific reproductive vulnerability to sufficiently low energy availability, and menstrual-cycle disruption can be a meaningful low-energy-availability warning sign. But current evidence does not clearly show that women's resting metabolic rate adapts more strongly than men's after adjusting for body composition and energy balance. ([Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/88/1/297/2846067 "Luteinizing hormone pulsatility is disrupted at a threshold of energy availability in regularly menstruating women | Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism"), [British Journal of Sports Medicine](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/17/1073 "2023 International Olympic Committee's (IOC) consensus statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs) | British Journal of Sports Medicine"), [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12986-021-00587-8 "Metabolic adaptation is associated with less weight and fat mass loss in response to low-energy diets | Nutrition & Metabolism | Springer Nature Link"), [Canadian Science Publishing](https://cdnsciencepub.com/doi/10.1139/apnm-2022-0372 "Weight loss induces changes in adaptive thermogenesis in female and male physique athletes | Applied Physiology, Nutrition, and Metabolism"))

**The "metabolic damage" narrative is overstated:** supported. A deficit is not futile, and the cited evidence does not show that dieting permanently breaks metabolism in the usual sense. The better message is that weight loss creates predictable biological resistance, so successful plans should account for muscle preservation, recovery or maintenance phases when needed, and strategies for hunger and adherence. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522008929 "Metabolic adaptation is an illusion, only present when participants are in negative energy balance | American Journal of Clinical Nutrition | ScienceDirect"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30321282/ "No consistent evidence of a disproportionately low resting energy expenditure in long-term successful weight-loss maintainers | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32386226/ "Metabolic adaptation is not a major barrier to weight-loss maintenance | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18356845/ "Resistance training conserves fat-free mass and resting energy expenditure following weight loss | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19247271/ "Resistance training preserves fat-free mass without impacting changes in protein metabolism after weight loss in older women | PubMed"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405457724001761 "Enhanced protein intake on maintaining muscle mass, strength, and physical function in adults with overweight/obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis | Clinical Nutrition ESPEN | ScienceDirect"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? ## Practical interpretation

In practical terms, these studies support focusing less on the idea that one diet permanently destroys metabolism and more on measurable risks: lean-mass loss, hunger, lower energy needs, reduced spontaneous activity, and reproductive-axis disruption under sufficiently low energy availability. Those effects can make further weight loss harder and maintenance more demanding. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/does-adaptive-thermogenesis-occur-after-weight-loss-in-adults-a-systematic-review/726FC60518DA67349B9C3EC1D75A7156 "Does adaptive thermogenesis occur after weight loss in adults? A systematic review | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/88/1/297/2846067 "Luteinizing hormone pulsatility is disrupted at a threshold of energy availability in regularly menstruating women | Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism"), [Monash University](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/long-term-persistence-of-hormonal-adaptations-to-weight-loss/ "Long-term persistence of hormonal adaptations to weight loss | Monash University"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18356845/ "Resistance training conserves fat-free mass and resting energy expenditure following weight loss | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19247271/ "Resistance training preserves fat-free mass without impacting changes in protein metabolism after weight loss in older women | PubMed"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405457724001761 "Enhanced protein intake on maintaining muscle mass, strength, and physical function in adults with overweight/obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis | Clinical Nutrition ESPEN | ScienceDirect"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27804272/ "How strongly does appetite counter weight loss? Quantification of the feedback control of human energy intake | PubMed"))

The most directly supported protective strategies in the cited literature are resistance training and adequate protein for preserving fat-free mass. The stabilization data also support considering maintenance or recovery phases when a person is coming out of a large deficit, and the low-energy-availability literature supports attention to reproductive-axis signals. Because sufficiently low energy availability can disrupt LH pulsatility in regularly menstruating women, and because REDs consensus guidance treats menstrual function as part of the low-energy-availability risk picture, menstrual-cycle disruption during aggressive dieting should be treated as a potential physiological warning sign rather than as proof of willpower or proof of permanent metabolic damage. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18356845/ "Resistance training conserves fat-free mass and resting energy expenditure following weight loss | PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19247271/ "Resistance training preserves fat-free mass without impacting changes in protein metabolism after weight loss in older women | PubMed"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405457724001761 "Enhanced protein intake on maintaining muscle mass, strength, and physical function in adults with overweight/obesity: A systematic review and meta-analysis | Clinical Nutrition ESPEN | ScienceDirect"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522008929 "Metabolic adaptation is an illusion, only present when participants are in negative energy balance | American Journal of Clinical Nutrition | ScienceDirect"), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/88/1/297/2846067 "Luteinizing hormone pulsatility is disrupted at a threshold of energy availability in regularly menstruating women | Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism"), [British Journal of Sports Medicine](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/17/1073 "2023 International Olympic Committee's (IOC) consensus statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs) | British Journal of Sports Medicine"))

## Evidence gaps

Future studies would be more useful if they measured resting energy expenditure, total daily energy expenditure, lean mass, appetite, reproductive function, thyroid hormones, leptin, and physical activity in the same women across weight loss, stabilization, regain, and repeat dieting attempts. More sex-stratified trials that control for menstrual-cycle phase, hormonal contraception, menopause status, protein intake, and resistance training would also help separate resting-metabolism questions from reproductive, appetite, and body-composition questions. Current evidence is strong enough to reject simplistic "metabolism permanently broken" claims, but not strong enough to say women's dieting physiology is identical to men's in every domain.
