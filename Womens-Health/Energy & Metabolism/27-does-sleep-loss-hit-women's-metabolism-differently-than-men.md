## Does Sleep Loss Hit Women's Metabolism Differently Than Men's?

Sleep restriction clearly affects metabolism. The sex-specific answer is more cautious: women show measurable hits to insulin sensitivity and glucose handling after restricted sleep or overnight wakefulness, but current human evidence does not yet show that women are consistently hit harder than men, or that the luteal phase reliably magnifies the metabolic damage of the same sleep loss. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594); [Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156); [Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926); [Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002))

## Bottom line

Sleep restriction worsens metabolic regulation at the group level. In randomized-trial meta-analyses, short sleep increases hunger, energy intake, and body weight in some protocols, and reduces insulin sensitivity across several measurement methods. The clearest group-level finding is impaired insulin sensitivity; the classic "ghrelin up, leptin down" story is much less consistent. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594))

Women are affected. A six-week randomized crossover trial in 38 women found that reducing sleep by about 1.5 hours per night increased fasting insulin and HOMA-IR, with a stronger HOMA-IR increase in postmenopausal women; a small clamp study in postmenopausal women also found reduced insulin sensitivity after four nights of sleep restriction. ([Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156); [Singh et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.23739))

But "women are hit harder than men" is not proven. Men-only and mixed-sex experiments also show insulin-resistance effects after sleep loss, and many studies are too small, male-heavy, or not designed to test sex-by-sleep interactions. ([Buxton et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.2337/db09-0699); [Donga et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2009-2430); [Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926))

The menstrual-cycle claim is biologically plausible but under-tested. Insulin resistance varies modestly across the menstrual cycle, brain insulin action appears stronger in the follicular phase than the luteal phase, and cortisol responses to sleep restriction differ by menstrual phase. However, those findings do not yet establish that sleep restriction causes a larger glucose or insulin-sensitivity hit in the luteal phase. ([Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w); [LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002))

## What is well established

Experimental sleep restriction is a metabolic stressor. A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that sleep restriction increased subjective hunger, increased energy intake by about 253 kcal/day, produced small short-term weight gain in partial sleep-restriction protocols, and reduced insulin sensitivity; it did not find strong mean effects on leptin, ghrelin, or energy expenditure. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002))

A separate systematic review and meta-analysis focused on insulin sensitivity found that sleep restriction reduced insulin sensitivity measured by OGTT/IVGTT and HOMA-IR, with clamp data also suggesting reduced whole-body insulin sensitivity. This supports a causal effect of insufficient sleep on insulin action, even though exact effect sizes vary by protocol, sleep dose, feeding control, and measurement method. ([Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594))

These findings answer the broad question: sleep loss can impair metabolic regulation. They do not, by themselves, answer whether the impairment is larger in women, because pooled trial averages often combine men and women or include mostly male samples. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594); [Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926))

## Glucose and insulin sensitivity in women

The strongest women-specific chronic sleep-restriction trial is [Zuraikat et al.](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156). In this randomized crossover trial, women aged 20 to 75 years completed two six-week phases: adequate sleep and sleep restriction by delaying bedtime 1.5 hours per night. Sleep restriction reduced total sleep time by about 1.34 hours per night and increased fasting insulin and HOMA-IR; the HOMA-IR effect was more pronounced in postmenopausal than premenopausal women, and adiposity changes did not explain the effect. ([Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156))

A smaller randomized crossover clamp study in postmenopausal women reported that 14 women underwent the protocol and nine were analyzed. Four nights of sleep restriction to 60 percent of habitual sleep reduced insulin sensitivity and fat oxidation under eucaloric feeding. Clamp-based insulin sensitivity is a stronger physiologic measure than fasting HOMA-IR, but the analyzed sample was small. ([Singh et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.23739))

Earlier healthy-women data also support the relevance of partial sleep deprivation to energy balance and insulin sensitivity, although protocols and sample sizes differ. ([Bosy-Westphal et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1159/000158874))

A newer controlled within-subject study in reproductive-aged women found that one night of wakefulness, modeled as a night-shift scenario, increased next-morning post-OGTT glucose at 60 minutes and peak glucose, while fasting glucose and HOMA-IR did not differ. The progesterone-to-estradiol ratio was not significantly associated with fasting or postprandial glucose outcomes in that study, which argues against a simple "higher progesterone equals worse response" explanation for acute night-shift glucose effects. This was not a direct luteal-phase versus follicular-phase sleep-loss trial. ([Xue et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgag038))

The key limitation is comparison. These women-only studies show that women are metabolically vulnerable to sleep loss, but they cannot prove women are more vulnerable than men. Men-only and mixed-sex protocols also show insulin-resistance effects after short sleep, including reduced insulin sensitivity after one week of 5-hour nights in healthy men and reduced glucose disposal after one night of partial sleep deprivation in a small mixed-sex clamp study. ([Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156); [Singh et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.23739); [Buxton et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.2337/db09-0699); [Donga et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2009-2430); [Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926))

## Menstrual cycle and sleep-loss metabolism

There is a reasonable biological basis for expecting menstrual phase to matter. In the [BioCycle Study](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702), 257 healthy premenopausal women showed modest but significant HOMA-IR variation across the menstrual cycle, rising from midfollicular to early luteal phase, with the change mainly reflecting insulin rather than glucose. ([Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702))

Mechanistic data also support phase-dependent insulin biology. In a [Nature Metabolism study](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w), intranasal insulin improved peripheral insulin sensitivity during the follicular phase but not the luteal phase, and hypothalamic insulin responsiveness was also phase-dependent. This suggests that brain insulin action may contribute to the relatively lower insulin sensitivity seen in the luteal phase. ([Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w))

Sleep-loss endocrine responses can also be phase-dependent. In healthy young women, one night of partial sleep restriction altered cortisol responses in the follicular phase but not the luteal phase. That is important because cortisol is metabolically relevant, but it is still not direct evidence that glucose tolerance or insulin sensitivity worsens more after sleep restriction in the luteal phase. ([LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002))

So the menstrual-cycle claim should be stated carefully: cycle phase probably modifies some endocrine and insulin-related physiology, and it may modify metabolic responses to sleep loss, but direct cycle-phase sleep-restriction trials with OGTT, clamp, continuous glucose monitoring, or standardized meal testing remain too sparse to draw a confident phase-specific conclusion. ([Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w); [LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002); [Xue et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgag038); [Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926))

## Appetite hormones and food intake

The appetite-hormone story is not as clean as "short sleep raises ghrelin and lowers leptin." In the RCT meta-analysis, sleep restriction increased hunger and energy intake but did not consistently change leptin or ghrelin. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002))

Sex-specific appetite-hormone findings are mixed. In one controlled men-and-women study conducted in the context of negative energy balance, short sleep increased ghrelin in men but not women, while GLP-1 was lower in women but not men; this suggests potentially different appetite pathways rather than a single shared leptin-ghrelin mechanism. The same protocol did not find a sleep-duration effect on glucose, insulin, or leptin profiles. ([St-Onge et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.5665/sleep.2198))

Other food-intake trials also separate intake effects from classic hormone effects. [Calvin et al.](https://doi.org/10.1378/chest.12-2829) found increased caloric intake after sleep restriction without changes in activity energy expenditure, leptin, or ghrelin. A male-only trial found increased ghrelin and higher snack intake during sleep restriction, with ghrelin increases correlating with sweet-food calories. ([Calvin et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1378/chest.12-2829); [Broussard et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.21321))

A 2025 systematic review specifically asking whether sex modifies appetite- and weight-regulating hormone responses found only eight eligible human experimental studies with sex-specific analyses, totaling 302 participants. It reported possible sex-related variation for several hormones, including leptin, insulin, GLP-1, ghrelin, adiponectin, and thyroxine, but the results were inconsistent and showed no clear pattern. ([Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

Behavioral eating may be an important pathway, especially in women, but it is not yet proven to be the main sex difference. In women, poor sleep quality has been associated with emotional eating, and short sleep combined with high emotional eating predicted greater snack intake in a lab setting. That evidence is observational and self-report based. In a within-subject study of 60 normal-weight females, partial sleep deprivation increased high-energy food choices and intake, apparently by reducing liking for low-energy foods and weakening food-choice conflict detection. ([Dweck et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.appet.2013.10.001); [Benjamins et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodqual.2020.104074); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

## Why sex-specific conclusions are still uncertain

The strongest current conclusion is that the field is still underpowered for sex-specific claims. A 2024 review on sex differences in sleep, circadian rhythms, and metabolism notes that much remains unknown because females have often been underrepresented in sleep and circadian research. ([Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

Methodology is also inconsistent. Studies differ in sleep-loss dose, number of nights, lab versus home restriction, controlled versus ad libitum feeding, time of blood sampling, use of OGTT versus clamp versus fasting indices, menstrual-cycle verification, oral-contraceptive exclusion, menopausal status, adiposity, and whether sex was a prespecified interaction rather than a post hoc subgroup. Those differences are large enough to explain why some studies find hormone changes and others do not. ([Gallegos et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.coemr.2020.10.006); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

## Best read on the competing claims

The claim that acute and chronic sleep restriction produces larger glucose-tolerance and insulin-sensitivity hits in women than in men is not established. Women clearly show insulin-sensitivity impairment in controlled trials, but comparable male data also show impairment, and direct adequately powered sex-by-condition tests are limited. ([Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156); [Singh et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.23739); [Buxton et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.2337/db09-0699); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594))

The claim that the luteal phase amplifies the metabolic hit from lost sleep is plausible but not proven. Menstrual phase affects insulin-related physiology, and sleep restriction can produce phase-dependent cortisol responses, but direct phase-stratified sleep-restriction glucose trials are still inadequate. ([Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w); [LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002); [Xue et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgag038))

The claim that ghrelin and leptin disruption is comparable between sexes is too simple. Average leptin and ghrelin effects are inconsistent, and some studies suggest different appetite signals in men and women, such as ghrelin changes in men and GLP-1 changes in women. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [St-Onge et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.5665/sleep.2198); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

The claim that the literature is too male-dominated and methodologically inconsistent for confident sex-specific conclusions is the best-supported claim. Current evidence supports "sleep loss harms metabolic regulation in both sexes, with plausible sex- and hormone-related modifiers," not "women are definitively hit harder." ([Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

## Quick reference evidence

Group-level RCT evidence: sleep restriction increases hunger and energy intake and reduces insulin sensitivity; leptin and ghrelin effects are inconsistent. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594))

Women-only chronic restriction: six weeks of mild sleep restriction increased fasting insulin and HOMA-IR in women, more strongly in postmenopausal women. ([Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156))

Postmenopausal clamp data: four nights of sleep restriction reduced insulin sensitivity and fat oxidation in nine analyzed participants. ([Singh et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.23739))

Menstrual-cycle insulin biology: HOMA-IR varies modestly across the cycle; brain insulin action improves peripheral insulin sensitivity in the follicular phase but not the luteal phase. ([Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w))

Sleep-loss cycle interaction: partial sleep restriction produced different cortisol responses by menstrual phase, but this is endocrine evidence, not direct glucose-tolerance evidence. ([LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002))

Appetite hormones: sex-specific hormone data are inconsistent; a 2025 systematic review found only eight eligible studies with sex-specific analyses. ([Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

Behavioral food intake: poor sleep and sleep deprivation can shift food choices and intake in women, including emotional-eating and high-energy-food pathways, but this does not yet prove a larger female response than male response. ([Dweck et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.appet.2013.10.001); [Benjamins et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodqual.2020.104074); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039))

## Practical interpretation

Sleep loss is metabolically relevant for women and men. The evidence is strong enough to treat chronic short sleep as a modifiable risk factor for poorer insulin sensitivity and higher energy intake. ([Zhu et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2019.02.002); [Sondrup et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2022.101594); [Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156))

The sex-specific evidence is not strong enough to recommend different sleep targets for women versus men, or different sleep targets by menstrual phase. A more defensible interpretation is that sex hormones, menstrual phase, and menopause may modify vulnerability, but the field needs better trials before translating that into phase-specific guidance. ([Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039); [Yeung et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2010-0702); [Hummel et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-023-00869-w); [LeRoux et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2014.06.002); [Zuraikat et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2337/dc23-1156))

The ideal next study would randomize women and men to the same sleep-restriction protocol, control feeding, verify menstrual phase hormonally, include premenopausal and postmenopausal strata, measure glucose with OGTT or clamp plus continuous glucose monitoring, and prespecify sex-by-condition and phase-by-condition interactions. ([Lok et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2024.101926); [Alfikany et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/clockssleep7030039); [Gallegos et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.coemr.2020.10.006))
