# What Can a Consumer Wearable Physically Measure?

A consumer wearable physically measures signals. It does not directly measure most of the health states shown on its dashboard. The safest distinction is: **PPG measures optical pulse-wave signals; accelerometers measure movement; ECG electrodes measure an electrical rhythm strip; temperature sensors measure local skin or peripheral temperature patterns; bioimpedance sensors measure electrical impedance; CGM sensors measure interstitial glucose.** Everything beyond that is an inference layer, sometimes reasonable and sometimes unsupported. The include/exclude verdicts below apply one operating assumption: on export, context may be stripped and a downstream AI system may treat column names as ground truth. They are not claims that each cited source directly studied downstream AI ingestion. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4); [Carrier et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1644082); [Dávila-Ruales et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/20420188241304459))

## The measurement layer

Most physiological data in consumer smartwatches and rings come from three sensor families: photoplethysmography (PPG), accelerometry, and, on some devices, single-lead ECG. PPG uses reflected light to detect changes in blood volume near the skin surface; accelerometers measure acceleration and movement; single-lead ECG records an electrical rhythm strip, usually for a short on-demand period rather than continuously. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))

Other sensors are narrower than their marketing labels suggest. Electrodermal activity sensors measure skin conductance, which is then used to infer stress. Bioimpedance sensors measure tissue resistance to a small electrical current, which is then used to estimate body composition. Temperature sensors in consumer wearables should be treated as local skin or peripheral temperature sensors unless a separately validated core-temperature algorithm is named. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Vos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijmedinf.2023.105026); [Carrier et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1644082))

CGMs are a separate category. In this article, CGM means adhesive, minimally invasive real-time continuous glucose monitoring systems, not wrist-worn optical glucose labels and not direct blood glucose. CGMs measure interstitial glucose. A 2024 systematic review of ambulatory real-time CGM systems found an average mean absolute relative difference of 9.4%, with better performance in euglycemia and hyperglycemia than in hypoglycemia; no included device met ISO hypoglycemia criteria. ([Dávila-Ruales et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/20420188241304459))

## The inference layer

The dashboard layer is where the evidence problem begins. Heart rate from PPG is an inference from pulse timing. Step count is an inference from accelerometer patterns. Sleep duration is an inference from movement plus physiological signals. Sleep stages, stress, recovery, readiness, strain, energy scores, VO2 max, body composition, calories, cardiovascular age, biological age, and metabolic fitness are higher-order inferences, usually built from proprietary models. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001))

This does not mean all inference is bad. Clinical measurement also uses algorithms. The more precise distinction is whether the full chain has been verified, analytically validated, and clinically validated for the intended use. The V3 framework separates those steps: verification asks whether the sensor works, analytical validation asks whether the algorithm accurately measures the intended construct, and clinical validation asks whether the measure is meaningfully associated with the clinical or behavioral outcome it claims to represent. ([Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4))

For AI export, that distinction becomes stricter. A user may remember that a number was “just a wearable estimate,” but an exported column named `recovery_score` or `cardio_age` can present the label as a health state unless the schema prevents it. The verdict below is therefore assigned to the **exported metric label**, not to the sensor in isolation. ([Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4))

## Why the export problem is worse than the app problem

Consumer devices often do not export the raw measurement layer. A 2025 review noted that no smartwatch or smart ring manufacturer currently allows user export of continuously recorded raw PPG, three-dimensional accelerometer, or temperature signals, except for ECG; exported data are usually aggregated time series, and even instantaneous heart rate is filtered or averaged in ways that are not fully transparent. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))

That matters because context is part of validity. PPG heart rate is stronger at rest than during vigorous movement. Sleep staging performs differently from sleep/wake detection. SpO2 may show wide limits of agreement and can misclassify hypoxic ranges. Energy expenditure estimates remain inconsistent. If the export strips device model, firmware, placement, motion, contact quality, population, and measurement condition, the metric can become a false health claim at scale. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))

The table is a schema decision. A narrow label can be included when the evidence supports that exact label or a narrow trend label. A broader label is excluded.

|`validated_af_screening_flag`|**Include**|Wrist-worn ECG and PPG devices have peer-reviewed evidence for atrial fibrillation detection, and some devices are cleared for pre-diagnostic AF detection. The supported claim is narrow: AF screening, not diagnosis of all arrhythmias. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|

It should not be relabeled as total activity, fitness, mobility health, or energy expenditure. |`heart_rate_bpm` without rest/exercise/contact/motion context|**Exclude**|Broad heart-rate exports mix conditions where PPG performs differently. |`stress_score`|**Exclude**|Stress scores infer psychological or physiological stress from HRV, heart rate, electrodermal activity, and other proxies. A systematic review found major problems with labeling, statistical power, biomarker validity, and generalization. ([Vos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijmedinf.2023.105026); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`recovery_score`, `readiness_score`, `strain_score`, `body_battery`, `energy_score`|**Exclude**|These are proprietary composite health scores. A 2025 review found that exact algorithms were not disclosed and peer-reviewed validation was limited. ([Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001))|
|`device_sleep_duration_trend`|**Exclude**|Sleep duration is more defensible than sleep stages, but current validation does not establish context-stripped longitudinal sleep-duration trend export across typical-use populations. PSG validation studies also show that devices can overestimate total sleep time and underestimate wake after sleep onset. ([Schyvens et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/sleepadvances/zpaf021); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))|
|`sleep_stage_minutes`, `sleep_score`, `sleep_quality`|**Exclude**|Wearables can overestimate sleep and struggle with wakefulness and stage classification. Independent PSG validation shows low wake specificity and only fair-to-moderate agreement for many stage outputs. ([Schyvens et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/sleepadvances/zpaf021); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`calories_burned` or `energy_expenditure`|**Exclude**|Multiple reviews find insufficient validity for consumer wearable energy expenditure. One systematic review found no brand accurate enough across settings, and an Apple Watch meta-analysis found inconsistent performance with frequently large errors. ([Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))|
|`vo2max`, `cardio_fitness`, `fitness_age`|**Exclude**|Smartwatch VO2 max is estimated from anthropometrics, heart-rate signals, workload, and proprietary algorithms. Validation studies are often small and healthier than the populations likely to rely on the metric. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`blood_pressure_estimate` or cuffless BP|**Exclude**|Wrist wearables generally do not physically measure blood pressure. Cuffless approaches estimate it from PPG or pulse-transit features and show limited agreement with cuff-based ambulatory blood pressure. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`spo2` from consumer smartwatch/ring export|**Exclude**|Reflectance PPG can estimate oxygen saturation, but smartwatch SpO2 has wide limits of agreement and needs more validation before clinical use; errors are especially consequential in hypoxic ranges. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))|
|`respiratory_rate`|**Exclude**|Wearables infer respiratory rate from modulation in PPG or ECG signals. Validation is not broad enough for context-stripped AI export, and accuracy can degrade in clinically relevant populations such as severe obstructive sleep apnea. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`skin_temperature`, `body_temperature`, or `fever_flag`|**Exclude**|Wrist or ring sensors measure local skin or peripheral temperature patterns. Exported temperature health claims depend on conditions such as environment, contact, timing, baseline modeling, and whether the output is being relabeled as body temperature or fever. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))|
|`body_fat_percent`, `muscle_mass`, or `body_composition` from watch bioimpedance|**Exclude**|Bioimpedance measures impedance, then estimates body composition. A 2025 validation of a commercial smartwatch bioimpedance approach found mixed results and weak agreement for skeletal muscle mass, which is not enough for broad AI export. ([Carrier et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1644082))|
|`biological_age`, `cardiovascular_age`, `vascular_age`, `healthspan`, `metabolic_fitness`|**Exclude**|These labels inflate narrow sensor signals into broad biological constructs. The physical sensors do not directly measure age, healthspan, vascular aging, or metabolic fitness, and proprietary models do not supply enough transparent validation. ([Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001); [Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4))|
|`noninvasive_glucose` from a watch or ring|**Exclude**|The supported consumer glucose signal here is interstitial glucose from a CGM. The CGM evidence cited in this article does not validate a wrist-worn optical glucose export. ([Dávila-Ruales et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/20420188241304459))|

## The key gap: brands market health states, but sensors measure signals

“Heart health” is broader than rhythm-strip ECG, resting heart rate, or an AF screening flag. The sensor-supported claim is narrower: a device may record a single-lead rhythm strip, estimate resting pulse, or flag possible AF. It does not measure cardiac health, all arrhythmias, coronary disease, autonomic health, or future cardiovascular risk from those signals alone. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))

“Recovery” is broader than sleep duration, resting heart rate, HRV-like pulse variability, and activity history. A 2025 review of composite health scores found common inputs such as HRV, resting heart rate, activity, and sleep duration, but also found that exact algorithms were not disclosed and peer-reviewed validation was sparse. The supported claim is not “recovery”; it is a proprietary score derived from several weaker and stronger inputs. ([Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001))

“Stress” is broader than electrodermal activity, heart rate, and HRV. Electrodermal activity measures skin conductance, and PPG-derived HRV-like metrics estimate pulse timing variability. A systematic review of wearable stress detection found problems with labeling, biomarker validity, statistical power, and generalizability. The supported claim is not “stress level”; it is “a model output trained on proxy signals.” ([Vos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijmedinf.2023.105026); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))

“Sleep quality” is broader than device-estimated sleep duration. PSG validation studies show that consumer devices can identify sleep better than wake and can misclassify wake after sleep onset and sleep stages. The supported claim inside an app is at most a device-estimated sleep measure under specified validation conditions; on export, context-stripped sleep-duration trend remains excluded unless separately validated as a trend. ([Schyvens et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/sleepadvances/zpaf021); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6))

“Calorie burn” is broader than movement plus heart-rate modeling. Systematic reviews repeatedly find energy expenditure to be one of the least valid consumer wearable outputs. The physical signals support movement detection and pulse estimation; they do not support context-free calorie truth. ([Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))

## How the competing claims resolve

The measurement-inference distinction is fundamental. A dashboard that presents “heart rate: 62 bpm” and “recovery: 67%” with the same visual confidence creates a false equivalence between a relatively direct physiological estimate and an opaque composite model. The evidence base supports treating those as different classes of outputs. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001))

It is also true that all measurement involves inference at some level. A pulse oximeter, ECG algorithm, or PPG heart-rate estimator is not “pure reality.” The practical question is whether the chain from sensor to output has been validated for the intended use. The V3 framework captures that better than a simple “direct versus indirect” binary. ([Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4))

The strongest evidence reviewed here does not prove that brands deliberately blur the line for business reasons. That claim is plausible as market analysis, but the peer-reviewed evidence more directly shows the operational result: proprietary scores are common, exact algorithms are not disclosed, raw data exports are limited, and validation is incomplete. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001))

For a human user, accuracy and actionability may matter more than the sensor pipeline. For an AI pipeline, the sensor pipeline matters because context is stripped. A narrow metric can remain true after export; a broad metric can become a false health claim once the app explanation, measurement condition, and proprietary model caveats disappear. ([Goldsack et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-020-0260-4))

## Practical interpretation

Consumer wearables can physically measure a small number of signals. Those signals are useful when the exported label stays narrow: ECG rhythm strip, validated AF screening flag, resting or sleep heart-rate trend, device step-count trend, and CGM interstitial glucose time series. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Dávila-Ruales et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/20420188241304459))

The dashboard’s broadest labels should be excluded from AI ingestion. Recovery, readiness, stress, strain, sleep duration trend, sleep quality, VO2 max, calories, body composition, blood pressure, SpO2, respiratory rate, biological age, cardiovascular age, healthspan, and metabolic fitness either rely on proprietary inference, degrade under real-world conditions, lack broad independent validation, or make claims wider than the physical sensor can support. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Doherty et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1515/teb-2025-0001); [Lambe et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-02238-1))

The clean rule is: **export the signal, not the story.** A downstream AI system can use `device_step_count_trend`; it should not receive `fitness_score`. It can use `resting_or_sleep_heart_rate_trend`; it should not receive `recovery_score`. It can use `single_lead_ecg_rhythm_strip`; it should not receive `heart_health`.
