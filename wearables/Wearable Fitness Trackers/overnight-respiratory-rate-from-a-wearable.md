# Overnight Respiratory Rate From a Wearable

Overnight respiratory rate is one of the more technically plausible wearable signals because sleep reduces motion relative to wakefulness, and published studies have estimated breathing frequency during sleep from respiratory sinus arrhythmia in heart-beat interval data, photoplethysmography (PPG) modulation, accelerometer-derived breathing motion, or complementary PPG and accelerometer signals. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

**Verdict: exclude.** The metric is plausible and sometimes accurate, but available validation is device-specific, algorithm-specific, and population-limited; performance can degrade in severe obstructive sleep apnea, and independent free-living data show weak tracking of short-term day-to-day respiratory-rate fluctuations. For a context-stripped export, that is not enough to treat a generic wearable-derived "overnight respiratory rate" column as ground-truth respiratory physiology for AI use. ([Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

## What the device is actually measuring

Wearables do not usually measure airflow. In published validation studies, Fitbit inferred respiratory rate from respiratory sinus arrhythmia in heart-beat interval power spectra, while the Samsung Galaxy Watch validation used complementary PPG and accelerometer information during sleep. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

That makes the metric different from a respiratory belt, nasal cannula, capnography, or direct observation. It is an inferred periodicity signal, not a direct measure of ventilation, tidal volume, airway obstruction, dyspnea, or gas exchange. The Fitbit validation compared its estimate with nasal-cannula sleep-study data, and the Samsung validation compared watch-derived respiratory rate with polysomnography-derived nasal-thermocouple respiratory rate. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

Mechanistically, this is more defensible during stable sleep than during daily life. The Fitbit paper limited estimation to sleep and noted that the technique cannot be applied when there are motion artifacts; the Samsung paper also noted that unconscious movement, watch position, apnea, and hypopnea can create challenges for PPG and accelerometer-based respiratory-rate estimation during sleep. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

## Validation against reference methods

The best validation evidence is device-specific, not metric-general.

A Fitbit respiratory-rate algorithm was validated against nasal-cannula reference data in 52 measurements from 28 participants with apnea-hypopnea index below 30. The reported root mean square error was 0.648 breaths/min and mean absolute error was 0.46 breaths/min. That is encouraging for average overnight estimation in selected users, but severe sleep apnea was excluded, the algorithm was proprietary, and all authors were Fitbit-affiliated and funded by Fitbit. The paper also states that raw data and proprietary code were not publicly available. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6))

A Samsung Galaxy Watch sleep-clinic validation compared wearable respiratory rate with polysomnography-derived nasal-thermocouple respiratory rate in 195 participants. Average overnight respiratory rate had RMSE 1.13 breaths/min and bias 0.39 breaths/min; continuous respiratory rate had RMSE 1.62 breaths/min and bias 0.37 breaths/min. Accuracy degraded in severe obstructive sleep apnea: average overnight accuracy fell to 79.45 percent and continuous respiratory-rate accuracy to 75.8 percent. This trial received sponsorship from Samsung Electronics, and two authors were affiliated with Samsung Electronics, so the favorable findings should be interpreted with that in mind. ([Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

A 2024 free-living observational study in patients with COPD and healthy controls found that Fitbit Charge 4 respiratory rate had good agreement with a Polar H10 reference device for average values: ICC 0.84 in COPD and 0.77 in healthy controls. But day-to-day fluctuation tracking was weak: r=0.47 in COPD and r=0.50 in healthy controls. The authors concluded that Fitbit lacked accuracy for short-period day-by-day fluctuations in respiratory rate, and they emphasized that Fitbit's proprietary algorithms are a black box. This study declared no conflicts of interest and was funded by Flemish research bodies, making it an important independent counterweight to manufacturer-linked studies. ([Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

## The trend claim is stronger than the absolute-number claim

The strongest argument for overnight respiratory rate is not that a single value is clinically meaningful. It is that a user's own baseline is often stable, so a sustained upward deviation may signal physiologic stress. In a WHOOP study of 25,000 members with 750,000 nights of data, within-person respiratory rate was more stable than resting heart rate or heart-rate variability, with a mean intraindividual standard deviation of 0.51 respirations/min over 30 nights. ([Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693))

In symptomatic users, respiratory-rate deviations have shown some illness-detection signal. The WHOOP COVID-19 study reported that a respiratory-rate-based model identified 20 percent of COVID-positive individuals before symptom onset and 80 percent by the third day of symptoms. However, the negative symptomatic group also had many false alerts: 34.2 percent had at least one positive classification across the same six-day window. The authors also noted limitations including self-reported COVID-19 status, uncertain symptom-onset timing, lack of asymptomatic evaluation, and the fact that final diagnoses from COVID-negative symptomatic users were not collected. Several authors were WHOOP employees or shareholders, and one author's university position was sponsored by WHOOP. ([Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693))

Fitbit's COVID-19 respiratory-rate study found that from one day before to five days after symptom onset, 36.4 percent of symptomatic COVID-positive users and 23.7 percent of asymptomatic COVID-positive users had at least one respiratory-rate measurement at least 3 breaths/min above their baseline. But at low population prevalence, the modeled positive predictive value was very low: at 1 infection per 1000 users, requiring at least three elevated measurements gave an estimated PPV of 2.641 percent. All authors were Fitbit-affiliated and funded by Fitbit. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6))

A newer prospective randomized crossover trial, COVID-RED, tested a wearable-based early-detection algorithm using the Ava bracelet plus symptom diary. The bracelet measured respiratory rate, heart rate, heart-rate variability, wrist-skin temperature, and skin perfusion during sleep. The wearable condition generated earlier alerts than the symptom-policy control, but it did so with very low specificity: the experimental algorithm had 93.8 to 99.2 percent sensitivity but 0.8 to 4.2 percent specificity for detecting infections during a specified period, and 45 to 52 percent sensitivity but 38 to 50 percent specificity for per-day detection. The authors concluded that the algorithm overestimated infections and noted that low specificity contributed to a trial economic evaluation suggesting that the wearable device and experimental algorithm would likely not be cost-effective in the general population. Several authors disclosed employment ties to organizations involved in the study, including Julius Clinical, Ava AG, P95 CVBA, Dr Risch Medical Laboratory, Haleon, and Roche Diagnostics consulting outside the current work. ([Zwiers et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0325116))

## Healthy users versus chronically ill users

For healthy users, the clinical utility is limited. Respiratory rate is stable most nights in the WHOOP dataset, but illness-detection studies show that deviations are nonspecific and can generate many false positives when used as screening signals. In the Fitbit COVID-19 respiratory-rate analysis, modeled PPV was low at low prevalence; in COVID-RED, the wearable-based algorithm alerted earlier but had very low specificity. ([Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693); [Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Zwiers et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0325116))

For chronically ill users, the stakes are higher, but the export case is weaker, not stronger. The independent COPD study found decent agreement for average respiratory rate but weak day-to-day fluctuation tracking. The Samsung sleep-clinic study found degraded performance in severe obstructive sleep apnea. Those are not minor caveats; they directly affect populations where context, signal quality, and disease-specific physiology matter most. ([Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

## Comparison with wearable SpO2

Respiratory rate may be more technically forgiving than wrist SpO2 in one narrow sense: it is detecting rhythmic pattern rather than estimating an absolute oxygen-saturation value. The cited respiratory-rate algorithms infer breathing frequency from respiratory sinus arrhythmia, PPG modulation, accelerometer-derived motion, or a combination of PPG and accelerometer signals, while the independent COPD study found better agreement for Fitbit respiratory rate than for Fitbit oxygen saturation. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

The independent COPD study illustrates this contrast: Fitbit respiratory-rate agreement was moderate-to-good, while oxygen saturation agreement in COPD was poor, with ICC 0.32 and overestimation of SpO2. ([Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

That does not make respiratory rate includeable. A more plausible signal is still not a validated export variable. The metric can be technically sound enough for a user-facing trend while still being unsafe as a generic AI-ingested health feature. The evidence base remains device-specific, algorithm-dependent, and sensitive to sleep quality, motion artifact, and severe obstructive sleep apnea. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

## The marketing gap

The sensor supports a narrow claim:

**"This device may estimate overnight breathing frequency under favorable sleep and signal-quality conditions, and sustained within-person deviations may sometimes reflect physiologic stress."** ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

The broader interpretation would be inflated:

**"This metric reflects respiratory health, recovery, illness risk, or cardiopulmonary status."**

That broader claim is not supported by the validation literature. Respiratory rate does not identify why breathing changed, whether ventilation is adequate, whether oxygenation is impaired, whether sleep apnea occurred, or whether the user is becoming ill. The cited studies validate specific algorithms against specific reference methods, and the illness-detection studies show nonspecificity, false positives, and low specificity in screening contexts. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693); [Zwiers et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0325116))

## Quick reference

- **Absolute overnight respiratory rate from a wearable:** exclude. Device-specific validation is not enough to treat the exported value as ground truth. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

- **Respiratory-rate trend from a validated device:** still exclude for context-stripped AI export unless the export explicitly preserves device, algorithm version, signal quality, sleep window, and trend-only interpretation. Fitbit and Samsung validations are algorithm-specific, and the Fitbit Charge 4 free-living study found weak day-to-day fluctuation tracking. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

- **Illness detection from respiratory-rate deviations:** exclude. The signal exists, but sensitivity, specificity, lead time, and positive predictive value are not reliable enough for general screening. ([Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693); [Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Zwiers et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0325116))

- **Chronic disease monitoring:** exclude. This is where the metric would matter most, but the evidence is not strong enough, and performance can degrade in clinically important subgroups such as severe obstructive sleep apnea; in COPD, the Fitbit Charge 4 showed good average agreement but weak day-to-day respiratory-rate fluctuation tracking. ([Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))

- **Human-facing personal awareness:** acceptable as a cautious trend prompt, not as a diagnostic or clinical variable. The evidence supports limited trend awareness more than clinical classification. ([Miller et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0243693); [Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Zwiers et al., 2025](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0325116))


## Practical interpretation

Overnight respiratory rate is not junk. It is one of the more defensible wearable signals from a measurement standpoint because published algorithms can estimate sleep breathing frequency from PPG-derived heart-beat intervals, PPG modulation, accelerometer motion, or complementary PPG and accelerometer features. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976))

But the standard for AI export is higher than "plausible" or "often serviceable." The right verdict is **exclude** because the exported column overstates the evidence. It converts a device-specific, proprietary, sleep-conditioned, trend-sensitive estimate into an apparent physiologic fact. At scale, that would manufacture false confidence because the cited studies show that performance depends on device, algorithm, reference method, sleep conditions, signal quality, and population. ([Natarajan et al., 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-021-00493-6); [Jung et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/18/7976); [Hermans et al., 2024](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2024/1/e56027))
