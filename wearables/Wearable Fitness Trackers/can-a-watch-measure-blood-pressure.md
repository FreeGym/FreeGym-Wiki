# Can a Watch Measure Blood Pressure?

A watch can estimate blood-pressure-related patterns, but current cuffless blood pressure devices estimate blood pressure indirectly rather than measuring arterial pressure directly. Most rely on sensors such as photoplethysmography (PPG) or tonometry, often combined with pulse transit time, pulse arrival time, and algorithms; outputs often represent blood pressure change relative to calibration rather than direct measurement. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Stergiou et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35708294/))

The practical verdict is simple: **watch-derived blood pressure values should be excluded** from AI-consumed health datasets unless the field is explicitly a non-measurement alert that only prompts cuff confirmation. Current expert reviews conclude that patients and clinicians should use validated cuff devices until cuffless blood pressure devices are appropriately tested and validated, because inaccurate values could contribute to under-treatment or over-treatment. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857))

A column named "systolic blood pressure" or "diastolic blood pressure" should only contain a validated blood pressure measurement or a clearly provenance-tagged non-measurement estimate. Cuffless validation statements identify calibration quality, recalibration frequency, post-calibration stability, device position, awake/asleep accuracy, exercise response, and ability to track blood pressure change as validation conditions; if those conditions are absent from the export, the number is not independently interpretable as blood pressure. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Stergiou et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35708294/); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))

## Metric verdict

- Watch-derived systolic blood pressure: **exclude**. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

- Watch-derived diastolic blood pressure: **exclude**. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

- Watch-derived blood pressure trend: **exclude**. Trend use requires independent validation of blood pressure tracking under the intended conditions of use; current validation recommendations specifically require evidence for tracking blood pressure changes, post-calibration stability, device position, awake/asleep accuracy, exercise response, and recalibration stability. ([Stergiou et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35708294/); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))

- Watch-derived hypertension notification: **exclude as a blood pressure metric**. It may be stored as a device-generated screening alert that prompts cuff confirmation, but it is not systolic or diastolic blood pressure data. ([Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296); [Cohen et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2844767))


These verdicts are driven by the same problem across devices: wrist optical sensors infer pressure from surrogate signals, often after cuff calibration, and a bare export can omit the conditions that determine whether the estimate is valid. The American Heart Association scientific statement says cuffless devices estimate blood pressure indirectly, commonly using PPG or tonometry plus pulse transit or pulse arrival features and machine learning, and that outputs often represent change relative to calibration rather than direct measurement. It also highlights real-world susceptibility to motion, sensor position, body posture, and hydrostatic pressure. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

## What a watch is actually sensing

Blood pressure is a pressure signal that clinical devices estimate or measure through defined measurement methods. Most automatic cuff devices use cuff inflation and deflation, sense pressure waveforms generated by cardiac contraction and relaxation through the inflated cuff, and estimate systolic and diastolic blood pressure with device-specific algorithms. ([Sharman et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41371-022-00693-x))

A wrist optical sensor does not measure arterial wall force. It measures changes in reflected light from blood-volume changes in the skin and then uses an algorithm to infer a pressure value. ([Avolio, 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41440-023-01269-z); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

That distinction matters. A commentary in _Hypertension Research_ describes commercially available wrist watch-type cuffless devices as using PPG signals, sometimes with ECG, and states that PPG-based devices determine blood pressure through surrogate metrics and algorithms related to pulse propagation time or pulse contour, not by measuring force. The same article notes that current devices face major challenges in producing reliable, well-calibrated blood pressure readings and in faithfully reflecting physiological blood pressure changes. ([Avolio, 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41440-023-01269-z))

## What systematic reviews show

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis in _European Heart Journal - Digital Health_ included 16 studies, 15 devices, and 974 participants. Most devices used PPG. The pooled mean differences looked modest on average: about 3.4 mmHg for systolic blood pressure and 1.2 mmHg for diastolic blood pressure. But the review also found high heterogeneity and concluded that standardization and real-life validation were needed before clinical use. ([Islam et al., 2022](https://academic.oup.com/ehjdh/article/3/2/323/6576559))

The same review explains why average error is not enough. Validation studies often used controlled settings, resting measurements, limited age ranges, and incomplete algorithm transparency. The authors noted that movement, body temperature, physiological state, undisclosed algorithms, and lack of daily-life validation make it difficult to generalize results to real use. ([Islam et al., 2022](https://academic.oup.com/ehjdh/article/3/2/323/6576559))

A newer 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis compared wearable cuffless blood pressure devices with 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring. Daytime differences were smaller, but nighttime results were worse: nighttime systolic blood pressure differed by about 4.5 mmHg and nighttime diastolic blood pressure by about 5.6 mmHg. The authors concluded that current wearable cuffless blood pressure devices are not reliable for nighttime blood pressure measurement, which matters because nocturnal blood pressure is clinically important. ([Lee and Chang, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666667725004179))

A 2025 _JAMA Cardiology_ narrative review reached the conservative clinical conclusion: patients and clinicians should use validated cuff devices until cuffless blood pressure devices are appropriately tested and validated. The review explicitly names the risk of inaccurate cuffless blood pressure causing under-treatment or over-treatment. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857))

## Calibration is not the same as accuracy

Cuff calibration can make a device look accurate near the calibration point. It does not prove that the device remains accurate when motion, sensor position, body posture, hydrostatic pressure, temperature, sleep state, exercise response, or blood pressure level changes. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))

A 2022 study of the Samsung Galaxy Watch Active 2 against 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring found that low systolic blood pressure was overestimated and high systolic blood pressure was underestimated. The authors concluded that smartwatch blood pressure showed systematic bias toward the calibration point and was not ready for clinical use. The study reported public/noncommercial research funding and declared no commercial or financial relationships that could be construed as a conflict of interest. ([Falter et al., 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cardiovascular-medicine/articles/10.3389/fcvm.2022.958212/full))

A 2023 real-world prospective observational study of Samsung Galaxy Watch users included 760 participants and 35,797 readings over roughly 22 days. The average pre-post calibration error for systolic blood pressure was 6.8 +/- 5.6 mmHg and increased at higher systolic blood pressure levels. The authors concluded that post-calibration stability and optimal recalibration intervals needed further investigation. This study thanked Samsung Electronics employees for technical assistance, and one author disclosed honoraria, consulting services, and research grants from manufacturers of blood pressure monitoring technology, including cuffless-device manufacturers and Samsung Research America; this conflict should reduce confidence in favorable interpretations. ([Han et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41440-023-01215-z))

A 2026 prospective validation study of Samsung Galaxy Watch 5 over 28 days was more favorable: in 37 participants, the watch had small mean bias and minimal drift over the calibration period when compared with a validated Omron cuff. But accuracy worsened as the reference blood pressure moved away from the calibration point; when reference blood pressure was 10 mmHg away from calibration, mean error was about 3.4 mmHg for systolic blood pressure and 5.1 mmHg for diastolic blood pressure. The cohort was small and mostly normotensive, and the authors advised cuff confirmation when blood pressure fluctuates substantially or when diagnostic or treatment decisions are planned. The study declared no conflicts and reported public/university grant funding. ([Walzel et al., 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20552076261415923))

That favorable 2026 study does not rescue the metric for export. It supports a narrower claim: in a small, selected, mostly normotensive sample, one smartwatch model may track relatively stable blood pressure near its calibration point. It does not validate a generic exported "blood pressure" column across the broader conditions identified in validation statements, including diverse populations, motion, body position, sleep/awake conditions, exercise response, calibration quality, and blood pressure changes. ([Walzel et al., 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20552076261415923); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))

## The screening argument is different from measurement

There is a reasonable screening argument that even imperfect wearables might prompt people to check their blood pressure with a real cuff. That is not the same as saying the wearable measured blood pressure. Apple says its hypertension notification feature analyzes optical heart-sensor data over 30-day periods to look for patterns of possible hypertension, then prompts users to use a third-party cuff for seven days. Apple also states that the feature is not intended to diagnose, treat, or aid management of hypertension, and that not all people with hypertension will receive a notification. Manufacturer documentation is useful here only for describing the claim and disclaimer, not as validation evidence. ([Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296))

A 2026 _JAMA_ research letter analyzing Apple's hypertension notification feature reported that, in a validation study against 30-day home blood pressure monitoring, 41.2% of people with undiagnosed hypertension received an alert and 58.8% did not; among those without hypertension, 92.3% did not receive an alert and 7.7% received an inappropriate alert. That translates to moderate rule-in value and weak rule-out value. This supports "go check with a cuff" more than "the watch knows your blood pressure." ([Cohen et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2844767))

So the verdict stays: a hypertension notification is **exclude as a blood pressure metric**. It can be stored, if needed, as a device-generated screening alert with clear provenance. It should not be merged into blood pressure data or used as a proxy for systolic or diastolic blood pressure. ([Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296); [Cohen et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2844767))

## User comprehension is not good enough to protect the metric

A 2023 population survey of cuffless smartwatch blood pressure use found that only 31.8% of participants said the values were "very accurate and helpful," while 63.5% said values were slightly lower or higher than a standard home blood pressure monitor. About 40% of participants had hypertension even though the authors noted smartwatch blood pressure use was not recommended for people with underlying conditions in relevant position papers and by the manufacturer. The study reported no funding and no competing interests, but thanked Samsung Electronics employees for technical assistance with Galaxy Watch instructions. ([Jang et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40885-023-00242-9))

This is the export problem: the number can be separated from the measurement context that validation papers treat as essential. A blood pressure field without provenance does not show calibration timing, cuff accuracy, user skill in cuff calibration, recalibration frequency, device position, body posture, motion, sleep/awake state, exercise response, sensor contact, or whether the value is near or far from the calibration point. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/); [Walzel et al., 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20552076261415923))

## The marketing gap

The inflated claim is "blood pressure monitoring." The supported claim is narrower: **some devices can produce cuff-calibrated blood pressure estimates or hypertension-risk notifications under constrained conditions, and those outputs may sometimes prompt cuff confirmation.** Samsung's support page says compatible Galaxy Watch models can be used to measure blood pressure when coupled with a Galaxy smartphone and cuff-based blood pressure monitor, provides systolic and diastolic values after cuff calibration, and requires recalibration every 28 days. Apple frames its feature differently: it looks for patterns of possible hypertension and prompts third-party cuff confirmation rather than providing systolic or diastolic blood pressure values. ([Samsung Support, 2024](https://www.samsung.com/sg/support/mobile-devices/how-to-measure-blood-pressure-with-galaxy-watch/); [Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296))

That is not the same thing as measuring blood pressure. ([Avolio, 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41440-023-01269-z); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

Samsung-style cuffless blood pressure features are closest to a numeric estimate, but the evidence shows calibration-point dependence, drift concerns, limited performance at blood pressure extremes, opaque algorithms, and insufficient validation across real-world populations and conditions. ([Falter et al., 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cardiovascular-medicine/articles/10.3389/fcvm.2022.958212/full); [Han et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41440-023-01215-z); [Walzel et al., 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20552076261415923); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

Apple-style hypertension notifications are more honestly framed as screening prompts, but they still should not be exported as blood pressure values. The alert may be useful precisely because it tells the user to check with a cuff; it does not replace the cuff. ([Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296); [Cohen et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2844767))

## Practical interpretation

- Use validated upper-arm cuff devices or ambulatory blood pressure monitoring for diagnosis, medication titration, and clinical decision-making. Current expert reviews and statements do not support cuffless watch blood pressure as a replacement for validated cuff measurement. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857); [Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254))

- Do not include watch-derived systolic or diastolic blood pressure in AI health pipelines as blood pressure. ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))

- Do not include watch-derived blood pressure trend unless the exported field preserves device identity, calibration protocol, measurement context, and the intended "trend only" interpretation. Since those caveats are required for interpretation, the default verdict is **exclude**. ([Stergiou et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35708294/); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/); [Walzel et al., 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/20552076261415923))

- Treat watch hypertension alerts as prompts for cuff measurement, not as diagnosis, management data, or blood pressure values. ([Apple Support, 2025](https://support.apple.com/en-in/117296); [Cohen et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2844767))


## Bottom line

A watch can estimate blood-pressure-related signals. It cannot be trusted as an exported blood pressure measurement. ([Cohen et al., 2025](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000254); [Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857))

**Verdict: exclude watch-derived blood pressure.** ([Yang et al., 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2832857); [Stergiou et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37303198/))
