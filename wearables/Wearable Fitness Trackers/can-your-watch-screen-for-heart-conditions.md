# Can Your Watch Screen for Heart Conditions?

Yes - but only in a narrow sense. Consumer wearables can screen reasonably well for atrial fibrillation (AF), especially when the device can record a single-lead ECG rhythm strip. That does not make the watch a general "heart health" monitor, because the cited validation evidence is AF-specific. ([Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

## The narrow claim that holds up

Atrial fibrillation is the consumer-watch heart feature in this article with the clearest clinical validation. A 2025 systematic review and diagnostic meta-analysis of smartwatch AF detection included 26 studies and 17,349 patients, reporting pooled sensitivity of 95 percent and specificity of 97 percent, with device-specific estimates of 94 percent sensitivity and 97 percent specificity for Apple Watch and 97 percent sensitivity and 96 percent specificity for Samsung devices. The same meta-analysis reported comparable overall accuracy between PPG-based and ECG-based smartwatch models, but the interpretation still depends on the exact use case and confirmation pathway. ([Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133))

That evidence supports an AF-specific screening claim. It does not by itself support a broad claim that the watch can screen for "heart conditions," "cardiovascular health," coronary artery disease, heart failure, valve disease, cardiomyopathy, hypertension, or overall cardiac wellness. ([Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

## What the sensor can actually measure

A single-lead ECG records cardiac electrical activity. That makes it mechanistically suited to rhythm classification, including AF versus sinus rhythm, when the tracing is classifiable and reviewed appropriately. The 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS AF guideline states that AF can be documented by 12-lead ECG, rhythm strips, wearables, or intracardiac electrograms, but that the diagnosis still requires visual confirmation that the rhythm diagnosis is accurate. ([Joglar et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001193))

PPG is different. PPG is an optical pulse-wave signal measured at the skin, not an electrical recording of the heart. A smartwatch using PPG can infer irregular pulse timing, but PPG alone cannot diagnose AF; it can only produce an irregular rhythm screening signal that should be confirmed with ECG. A 2024 systematic review of smartwatch AF validation studies makes the same distinction: ECG-capable smartwatches can support diagnosis when the tracing is examined by a clinician, while PPG-based devices are useful for detecting irregular pulses and cannot be used to make a diagnosis of AF. ([Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5); [Scardulla et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.measurement.2023.113150))

## ECG-based AF classification: include

ECG-capable watches can be included as AF-specific screening tools when the exported metric remains narrow: a device classification from a single-lead ECG rhythm strip, not a diagnosis of heart disease. The 2024 systematic review found that most smartwatch validation measures were above 90 percent, but it also noted that many studies excluded inconclusive readings when calculating performance; when inconclusive readings are included, performance drops. ([Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

This is why the export name matters. `single_lead_ECG_AF_classification` is acceptable. `heart_condition_detected` is not. The first column describes the measurement context; the second turns one rhythm-screening function into a broad disease claim.

## PPG irregular rhythm notification: include as a prompt, not a diagnosis

The Apple Heart Study recruited 419,297 participants and found that 2,161 participants, or 0.52 percent, received an irregular pulse notification over a median of 117 days of monitoring. Among notified participants, 34 percent had AF on subsequent ECG patch readings, and the positive predictive value was 0.84 for observing AF on ECG simultaneously with a later irregular pulse notification. The trial was sponsored by Apple, so the positive findings should be interpreted with that conflict in mind. ([Perez et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1901183))

The Fitbit Heart Study enrolled 455,699 participants. Irregular heart rhythm detections occurred in 4,728 participants, or 1.0 percent; among 1,057 participants with a notification and an analyzable ECG patch, AF was present in 340, or 32.2 percent. When another irregular detection occurred during ECG patch monitoring, the positive predictive value for concurrent AF was 98.2 percent overall and 97.0 percent in participants aged 65 or older. This trial was funded by Fitbit, designed by Fitbit in collaboration with Massachusetts General Hospital investigators, and included Fitbit employees in data collection, interpretation, and manuscript review, so it should not stand alone as independent evidence. ([Lubitz et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.122.060291))

The right metric is therefore `PPG_irregular_rhythm_notification`, not `AF_diagnosis`. The notification is a screening event that can trigger an ECG confirmation pathway. ([Perez et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1901183); [Lubitz et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.122.060291); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

## False positives and low-risk users

False positives matter because AF prevalence changes the meaning of the same test result. The 2024 smartwatch validation review notes that false positives can cause anxiety, emergency department visits, and resource use, and that positive predictive value increases when AF prevalence is higher. In practice, that means an alert in an older hypertensive patient carries a different pre-test probability than an alert in a healthy 25-year-old. ([Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

The same review reports that inconclusive smartwatch readings occurred up to 30 percent of the time in some studies, and that those inconclusive readings can hide both false positives and false negatives. It also notes that real-world PPG is affected by skin pigmentation, LED color, contact force, ambient temperature, motion artifact, ambient light, and battery limitations. ([Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

A separate review of PPG sensors similarly emphasizes that wrist PPG is affected by technological, environmental, and person-level variability, and that lack of standardization limits generalizability and reproducibility. That is a sensor limitation, not just a software limitation. ([Scardulla et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.measurement.2023.113150))

## The clinical pathway

A wearable alert is not the endpoint. The safe pathway is: wearable irregular rhythm alert, then ECG rhythm documentation, then clinician review, then stroke-risk and bleeding-risk assessment, then treatment decision. The 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS guideline supports ECG or rhythm-strip documentation with visual confirmation for AF diagnosis, use of validated stroke-risk scores such as CHA2DS2-VASc, and bleeding-risk assessment to identify and modify bleeding risk factors rather than to exclude anticoagulation by itself. ([Joglar et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001193))

The EQUAL randomized controlled trial tested a version of this pathway in higher-risk adults. Patients aged 65 or older with elevated stroke risk were randomized to six months of Apple Watch monitoring with PPG and single-lead ECG functions or standard care; ECGs were reviewed remotely by an independent eHealth team within 24 hours. New-onset AF occurred in 9.6 percent of the intervention group and 2.3 percent of the standard-care group. Apple loaned watches for the study, and two authors reported Apple travel grants, so the detection benefit is clinically relevant but not fully free of device-related conflict. ([van Steijn et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2025.11.032))

That result supports "smartwatch-assisted AF detection in elevated-risk adults." It does not support "watch screens for heart disease." ([van Steijn et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2025.11.032))

## Does screening reduce stroke?

AF screening can increase AF diagnosis. Whether it consistently reduces stroke at population scale is less settled. ([McIntyre et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjopen/oeac044); [Wahab et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lanepe.2025.101298))

The STROKESTOP randomized trial invited all 75- to 76-year-olds in two Swedish regions to intermittent ECG screening for 14 days. After a median follow-up of 6.9 years, the screened group had a small net benefit on a combined endpoint of stroke, systemic embolism, bleeding hospitalization, and death: 31.9 percent versus 33.0 percent, hazard ratio 0.96. The study had public and foundation funding, but also industry funding from Carl Bennet AB, Boehringer Ingelheim, Bayer, and Bristol Myers Squibb-Pfizer. ([Svennberg et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2821%2901637-8))

A 2022 meta-analysis of randomized AF screening trials found that ECG-based screening was associated with a modest stroke reduction, relative risk 0.91, but the trial sequential analysis did not cross the stopping boundary, and the included trials varied substantially in populations, screening methods, and stroke definitions. ([McIntyre et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjopen/oeac044))

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of non-invasive AF screening devices included 32 studies with 735,542 participants. It found that screening increased new AF diagnosis, but pooled RCT data did not show reduced death, cardiovascular hospitalization, stroke, or bleeding; stroke relative risk was 0.95 with a 95 percent confidence interval of 0.87 to 1.04. This review was funded by the British Heart Foundation and the National Institute for Health and Care Research. ([Wahab et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lanepe.2025.101298))

GUARD-AF tested 14-day continuous ECG patch screening in adults aged 70 or older in primary care. The trial increased AF diagnosis and oral anticoagulation initiation, but it found no evidence that screening reduced stroke hospitalizations; stroke hospitalization was 0.7 percent in the screening group versus 0.6 percent in usual care. The trial was under-enrolled because of COVID-19, but the result is still a warning against assuming that more AF detection automatically means fewer strokes in every screening population. ([Lopes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2024.08.019))

## The marketing gap

The valid claim is "AF rhythm screening." The inflated claim is "heart health monitoring." ([Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

AF detection does not assess plaque in coronary arteries, cardiac pumping function, valve disease, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy, blood pressure control, or future cardiovascular risk. A wearable can produce heart-rate, rhythm, PPG, activity, and sleep signals, but the cited AF-screening evidence does not validate a broad "heart health score." ([Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5); [Scardulla et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.measurement.2023.113150))

The gap is clearest when AF-specific exports sit beside broad labels such as "cardiovascular age," "vascular age," "readiness," "recovery," or "heart health." A 2026 PLOS Digital Health study found that Oura Ring nocturnal PPG could estimate vascular age in 160 mostly healthy adults with mean absolute error of about 6 to 7 years, but the study tested feasibility against a fingertip PPG comparator in a sleep-lab context, not diagnosis of heart disease or clinical decision-making. It also disclosed Oura-related competing interests, including one author on Oura's medical advisory board and two authors in the Oura-NUS Joint Lab, although the authors stated Oura had no role in analysis, interpretation, or writing. ([Yilmaz et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pdig.0001329))

That makes vascular age mechanistically interesting, but it does not clear the export bar as a heart-condition screening metric. ([Yilmaz et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pdig.0001329))

## Practical interpretation

Wearable AF screening is genuinely useful when the claim is kept narrow. It can identify possible AF, especially in older or higher-risk adults, and it can move someone into a clinical confirmation pathway they might not have entered otherwise. The evidence supports AF-specific screening prompts and ECG-based rhythm classification, not broad cardiac diagnosis. ([van Steijn et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2025.11.032); [Barrera et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.102133); [Zarak et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s42444-024-00118-5))

For downstream AI systems, the safest export rule is simple: keep AF-specific columns and delete broad cardiac wellness columns. `PPG_irregular_rhythm_notification` and `single_lead_ECG_AF_classification` can be useful. `heart_health_score`, `cardiovascular_age`, and `watch_detected_heart_condition` should be excluded.
