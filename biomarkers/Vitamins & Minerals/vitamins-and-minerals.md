# Vitamins & Minerals

The most debated questions about vitamin and mineral biomarkers - what the tests actually measure, where they fail, and why the gap between association and intervention is widest here.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## What Vitamin D Level Is Actually Sufficient?

No biomarker has a more contentious sufficiency threshold than vitamin D. The Institute of Medicine (IOM, now National Academy of Medicine) set 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) as sufficient for bone health in 2011, concluding that the evidence for benefits beyond bone was insufficient to justify a higher target. The Endocrine Society set 30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L) as the minimum, with 40-60 as the preferred range. Longevity and optimization clinics often target 50-80 or higher.

The difference between 20 and 30 as a cutoff isn't academic. At a threshold of 20, roughly 25% of the US population is "deficient." At a threshold of 30, that number jumps to over 40%. The cutoff determines who gets supplemented, who gets retested, who worries, and how much healthcare spending is directed at vitamin D.

The IOM and Endocrine Society used the same evidence base and reached different conclusions. The IOM weighted the bone data heavily and found insufficient evidence for extraskeletal benefits. The Endocrine Society weighted observational associations with cancer, cardiovascular disease, and immune function, and set a higher target as a precautionary measure.

Vitamin D also has a seasonal cycle that complicates any fixed threshold. A person at 22 ng/mL in February (nadir) may be at 38 in August (peak). Are they "deficient" half the year and "sufficient" the other half? Or is the winter dip a normal physiological rhythm that doesn't require correction?

**Competing claims:**

1. The IOM threshold of 20 ng/mL is evidence-based and appropriate - the bone health evidence supports it, and raising the threshold to 30 pathologizes a large proportion of the population without proven benefit for extraskeletal outcomes; the supplementation RCTs have not validated higher targets
2. The Endocrine Society's 30 ng/mL is a more prudent target - observational data consistently shows better outcomes at higher levels, the cost and risk of supplementation are low, and waiting for perfect RCT evidence while millions remain at 20-25 ng/mL is excessively conservative
3. Both fixed thresholds are wrong because they ignore individual variation - vitamin D metabolism is affected by genetics (GC gene, CYP2R1 variants), body composition (vitamin D is sequestered in adipose tissue), skin pigmentation, sun exposure, and latitude; a single number applied to everyone is inherently flawed
4. The focus on a specific number distracts from the real question: does supplementing to a higher target improve hard health outcomes? Until RCTs answer that convincingly (and so far they haven't), the threshold debate is less important than it appears

**Evidence needed:** IOM and Endocrine Society evidence reviews and their methodological differences, vitamin D threshold and bone fracture dose-response data, Mendelian randomization on vitamin D levels and extraskeletal outcomes, seasonal variation quantification and clinical significance, genetic determinants of vitamin D metabolism and individual optimal levels.

## Is Low Vitamin D a Cause of Disease or a Marker of Poor Health?

Low vitamin D is associated with nearly everything: cardiovascular disease, cancer (particularly colorectal), type 2 diabetes, multiple sclerosis, depression, respiratory infections, autoimmune disease, dementia, and all-cause mortality. The associations are consistent, large, and reproduced across populations. On the surface, this looks like one of the most important modifiable risk factors in medicine.

But people with low vitamin D tend to share other characteristics: they're more likely to be obese (adipose tissue sequesters vitamin D), less physically active (less time outdoors means less sun exposure), older, sicker, and living in northern latitudes with less UVB radiation. Low vitamin D may be a consequence of poor health rather than a cause of it. An obese, sedentary person with chronic illness will have low vitamin D because their biology and behavior produce it - not because vitamin D deficiency caused their condition.

Reverse causation is a serious concern. Acute and chronic illness itself lowers vitamin D levels: inflammation consumes 25(OH)D, hospitalization eliminates sun exposure, and the acute phase response redistributes vitamin D binding protein. A patient measured in the ICU will have low vitamin D that reflects their illness, not a pre-existing deficiency.

Mendelian randomization - using genetic variants that influence vitamin D levels to test causality - has produced mixed results. Some studies support a causal role for vitamin D in multiple sclerosis and possibly respiratory infections. For cardiovascular disease and cancer, the results have been largely null.

**Competing claims:**

1. The observational associations are largely confounded - low vitamin D is a marker of obesity, inactivity, poor health, and limited sun exposure, not an independent causal factor; this explains why supplementation trials fail to replicate the observational benefits
2. Vitamin D is likely causal for some outcomes (bone health, possibly MS and respiratory infections) but not others (cardiovascular disease, cancer, all-cause mortality) - the mistake is treating "vitamin D" as a single story when the causal relationship is outcome-specific
3. Reverse causation explains a substantial portion of the association in acute settings (hospitalized patients, critical illness), but population-level prospective studies with baseline measurements years before disease onset still show associations, suggesting confounding alone doesn't explain everything
4. The causal debate may be moot for clinical practice - even if low vitamin D is partly or mostly a marker, supplementation is cheap and safe at moderate doses, and the potential benefit (even if small) may justify treatment without requiring definitive proof of causation

**Evidence needed:** Mendelian randomization studies on vitamin D and specific outcomes (MS, respiratory infections, CVD, cancer, mortality), longitudinal studies with vitamin D measured years before disease onset, reverse causation quantification in acute illness, confounding analyses controlling for obesity, activity, and sun exposure simultaneously.

## Why Do Vitamin D Supplementation Trials Keep Failing?

The VITAL trial (2019): 25,871 participants, 2,000 IU/day of vitamin D3, median 5.3 years of follow-up. No reduction in cancer incidence, cardiovascular events, or all-cause mortality. The D-Health trial (2022): 21,315 participants, 60,000 IU/month, 5 years. No reduction in cancer mortality (the primary endpoint). VIDA: no bone benefit in elderly. ViDA: no reduction in cardiovascular disease.

These are large, well-designed, long-duration randomized controlled trials. They represent the highest quality of evidence. And they are, for the most prominent claimed benefits of vitamin D supplementation, negative.

This creates the central paradox of vitamin D: enormous observational associations, failed intervention trials. Either the associations are confounded (vitamin D is a marker, not a cause), or the trials are flawed in ways that prevent them from detecting a real benefit.

**Competing claims:**

1. The trial results are definitive - supplementing vitamin D does not meaningfully reduce cancer, cardiovascular disease, or mortality in the general population; the observational associations are confounded, and the era of recommending vitamin D supplementation for non-bone outcomes should end
2. The trials were designed to fail because they didn't target deficient populations - VITAL enrolled mostly vitamin D-replete individuals (mean baseline 25(OH)D ~31 ng/mL), and supplementing people who are already sufficient wouldn't be expected to show benefit; trials in genuinely deficient populations might show different results
3. The dose, form, or co-supplementation may matter - 2,000 IU/day may not be enough for some individuals, bolus monthly dosing (D-Health) may not replicate daily physiological exposure, and vitamin D without adequate K2, magnesium, or calcium may not produce the same biological effects
4. Subgroup analyses from VITAL and other trials hint at benefits in specific populations (cancer mortality reduction after excluding early follow-up, benefits in lean individuals, benefits in those with low baseline levels) - the null overall result may mask real effects in the people who actually need supplementation

**Evidence needed:** VITAL and D-Health subgroup analyses by baseline vitamin D level, trials in genuinely deficient populations (below 20 ng/mL at baseline), dose-response trials (comparing 1,000 vs 2,000 vs 4,000 IU/day), daily vs bolus dosing comparison, vitamin D with and without K2/magnesium co-supplementation, individual patient data meta-analyses across trials.

## When Antioxidant Supplementation Caused Harm: The Beta-Carotene and Vitamin E Trials

Oxidative stress is implicated in cancer, cardiovascular disease, and aging. Antioxidant vitamins (E, C, beta-carotene, selenium) neutralize free radicals in test tubes. Observational studies consistently showed lower rates of cancer and cardiovascular disease in people with higher antioxidant intake. The intervention logic was straightforward: supplement antioxidants, reduce disease. The trials happened. They were among the most consequential failures in preventive nutrition.

The ATBC trial (Alpha-Tocopherol, Beta-Carotene Cancer Prevention Study, 1994) randomized 29,133 Finnish male smokers to beta-carotene, vitamin E, both, or placebo. The beta-carotene arm showed an 18% INCREASE in lung cancer incidence and an 8% increase in all-cause mortality. Vitamin E had no effect on lung cancer. The finding was unexpected and initially contested.

CARET (Beta-Carotene and Retinol Efficacy Trial, 1996) tested beta-carotene plus vitamin A in 18,314 smokers and asbestos workers. It was stopped 21 months early because of a 28% increase in lung cancer incidence and 17% increase in all-cause mortality. The supplement actively caused the disease it was supposed to prevent in a high-risk population.

SELECT (Selenium and Vitamin E Cancer Prevention Trial, 2008) randomized 35,533 men to vitamin E, selenium, both, or placebo for prostate cancer prevention. Selenium showed no benefit. Vitamin E INCREASED prostate cancer incidence by 17% compared to placebo. The trial was halted, and follow-up analyses sustained the finding.

HOPE and HOPE-TOO tested vitamin E for cardiovascular prevention; HOPE-TOO showed increased heart failure hospitalizations. Physicians' Health Study II showed no benefit for vitamin E or vitamin C on cardiovascular events or cancer. The Women's Health Study showed no cardiovascular benefit for vitamin E. The pattern across large, well-designed antioxidant trials has been remarkably consistent: no benefit at best, harm at worst.

Why did antioxidant supplementation fail? Several hypotheses: free radical signaling plays essential roles in physiology (mitochondrial biogenesis, apoptosis of precancerous cells) that supplemental antioxidants disrupt; high-dose isolated antioxidants behave differently than food-derived antioxidant mixtures; the observational associations reflected healthier lifestyles, not the antioxidants themselves. Mendelian randomization studies have largely not supported causal roles for circulating antioxidant levels in cancer or cardiovascular outcomes.

**Competing claims:**

1. The antioxidant trial failures are among the strongest examples of observational-to-trial reversal in modern nutrition - the consistent pattern across multiple vitamins, populations, and outcomes argues that broad antioxidant supplementation for disease prevention is not evidence-based and in some cases actively harmful
2. The failures apply to supplementation in well-nourished populations with the specific high-dose isolated-nutrient formulations tested - they don't necessarily apply to dietary antioxidants from food, to specific clinical deficiency states, or to populations with documented low antioxidant status
3. The broader lesson for vitamin supplementation is that biomarker improvement (serum vitamin levels rising with supplementation) is not evidence of clinical benefit, and the same scrutiny applied to ATBC, CARET, and SELECT should be applied to currently popular supplements that haven't been tested at this scale
4. The supplement industry has largely ignored these findings - beta-carotene, vitamin E, and selenium remain widely sold for cancer and cardiovascular prevention despite the trial data, and consumers who take them at pharmacologic doses may be accepting risks similar to those ATBC and CARET participants took

**Evidence needed:** ATBC, CARET, and SELECT long-term follow-up analyses, meta-analyses of antioxidant supplementation outcomes, Mendelian randomization on circulating antioxidant levels and disease outcomes, food-derived vs supplemental antioxidant effects, specific-deficiency vs general-population supplementation outcome differences.

## Vitamin D Assays: Why Different Labs Give Different Numbers

Vitamin D status is assessed by measuring serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) - the primary circulating form and the accepted marker of vitamin D stores. But measuring 25(OH)D accurately is technically challenging, and different assay platforms produce meaningfully different results.

Immunoassays (the most common method in clinical labs) and liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS, considered the gold standard) can differ by 10-20% for the same sample. Some immunoassays struggle to accurately capture vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol) separately from D3 (cholecalciferol), potentially underestimating total 25(OH)D in people supplementing with D2. Cross-reactivity with vitamin D metabolites (24,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, 3-epi-25(OH)D3) varies by platform.

The Vitamin D Standardization Program (VDSP) and CDC's Vitamin D Standardization Certification Program have improved inter-laboratory agreement. But variation persists, and most patients don't know which assay their lab used or whether results from different labs are comparable.

When the sufficiency debate hinges on whether someone is at 18 or 24 or 31 ng/mL - and each of those numbers falls into a different clinical category depending on which guideline you follow - assay variation of 10-20% isn't a footnote. It's a classification-changing problem.

**Competing claims:**

1. Assay variability is a significant and underappreciated problem for vitamin D testing - a result of 18 ng/mL at one lab might be 22 at another, moving a patient from "deficient" to "insufficient" or "sufficient" depending on the guideline used; clinical decisions should account for this uncertainty
2. The VDSP and standardization efforts have substantially reduced inter-lab variation for most commercial assays - the problem is smaller than it was a decade ago, and for most clinical purposes, immunoassay results are adequate for classification and monitoring
3. LC-MS/MS should be the standard method for clinical vitamin D measurement, especially when results are borderline or when accuracy matters for clinical decisions - the cost difference is modest and the accuracy improvement is meaningful
4. The assay problem argues for wider treatment ranges rather than precise cutoffs - instead of debating whether 20 or 30 is the right threshold, clinical practice should acknowledge measurement uncertainty and use a range approach (e.g., clearly deficient below 15, clearly sufficient above 35, uncertain in between)

**Evidence needed:** Inter-assay comparison studies (immunoassay vs LC-MS/MS) with clinical reclassification rates, VDSP standardization progress reports, vitamin D2 vs D3 measurement accuracy by platform, cost-effectiveness of LC-MS/MS vs immunoassay in clinical settings, proposals for measurement uncertainty-adjusted clinical guidelines.

## B12: When the Blood Level Lies

Serum vitamin B12 is the standard screening test for B12 deficiency. The reference range at most labs is approximately 200-900 pg/mL (150-670 pmol/L). Below 200 is flagged as deficient. Above 200 is "normal." This is where the trouble starts.

Serum B12 measures total circulating B12, including both active and inactive forms. The biologically active fraction is holotranscobalamin (holoTC) - B12 bound to transcobalamin II, which delivers it to cells. HoloTC represents only 10-30% of total serum B12. The remainder is bound to haptocorrin, which has no known delivery function. A person can have a total B12 of 350 pg/mL that looks reassuring while their holoTC is low and their cells are functionally B12-starved.

Functional markers detect B12 deficiency at the metabolic level, independent of what's circulating in the blood. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) rises when B12-dependent methylmalonyl-CoA mutase can't function. Homocysteine rises when B12-dependent methionine synthase can't function. Both can be elevated in the presence of a "normal" serum B12, confirming functional deficiency that the standard test missed.

The clinical consequence: patients with fatigue, neuropathy, cognitive changes, or macrocytic anemia whose serum B12 is in the low-normal range (200-400 pg/mL) may be told they're "fine" when they're functionally deficient. Neurological damage from B12 deficiency can be irreversible if delayed.

**Competing claims:**

1. Serum B12 alone is an inadequate screening test - it misses functional deficiency in the low-normal range (200-400 pg/mL), and MMA should be checked in any symptomatic patient with serum B12 below 400, regardless of whether the total level is "within range"
2. Serum B12 is sufficient for most screening purposes - very low levels (below 200) reliably identify deficiency, and adding MMA to every borderline result increases cost and complexity for a condition that, while important, affects a relatively small proportion of the screened population
3. Holotranscobalamin (active B12) is the most accurate single marker of B12 status - it reflects the bioavailable fraction directly, responds faster to depletion than total B12, and is increasingly available as a clinical test; it should replace total B12 as the first-line screen
4. The functional approach (MMA + homocysteine) is the most definitive but should be reserved for symptomatic patients or high-risk groups - testing MMA in every asymptomatic person is not justified, but dismissing symptoms because total B12 is "normal" is also wrong

**Evidence needed:** Serum B12 vs MMA vs holotranscobalamin diagnostic accuracy for functional B12 deficiency, prevalence of functional deficiency at serum B12 levels of 200-400 pg/mL, neurological outcomes with early vs delayed B12 repletion, cost-effectiveness of MMA or holoTC as first-line screening in high-risk populations, serum B12 cutoff optimization studies.

## Who's Actually at Risk for B12 Deficiency?

B12 deficiency is not random. It concentrates in specific populations with identifiable risk factors - yet screening in these populations is often not systematic.

Vegans and strict vegetarians are the most obvious group. B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products (meat, fish, dairy, eggs). Without supplementation, vegans will inevitably become deficient, often within 1-3 years of eliminating all animal products. Vegetarians who consume dairy and eggs may maintain marginal status, but depletion is common with low intake.

Elderly adults are the largest at-risk group by numbers. Atrophic gastritis (chronic inflammation of the stomach lining that reduces acid and intrinsic factor production) affects an estimated 10-30% of adults over 60. Without adequate acid and intrinsic factor, B12 from food cannot be liberated and absorbed. Pernicious anemia (autoimmune destruction of parietal cells) is the extreme form. B12 deficiency in the elderly often presents as cognitive decline, neuropathy, or fatigue that gets attributed to "aging."

Metformin, the most prescribed diabetes drug in the world, impairs B12 absorption. Studies show that 10-30% of long-term metformin users develop low B12. The mechanism involves interference with calcium-dependent ileal absorption. Despite this well-documented effect, routine B12 monitoring in metformin users is not universally practiced.

Chronic PPI and H2 blocker users reduce gastric acid, impairing B12 liberation from food proteins. Long-term use (especially beyond 2 years) is associated with increased B12 deficiency risk.

**Competing claims:**

1. B12 screening should be routine in all high-risk groups: vegans/vegetarians, adults over 60, metformin users, chronic PPI users, and post-bariatric surgery patients - the test is cheap, deficiency is common in these groups, consequences are preventable, and waiting for neurological symptoms means waiting too long
2. Universal supplementation is more practical than universal screening for some groups - all vegans should supplement regardless of testing, and all metformin users could benefit from prophylactic B12 without the cost of serial monitoring
3. The threshold for concern should be lower than the lab cutoff in high-risk groups - a serum B12 of 250 in a 70-year-old on metformin should trigger MMA testing or empiric supplementation, not reassurance
4. B12 deficiency in the elderly is overdiagnosed by functional markers - MMA and homocysteine both rise with declining kidney function (common in the elderly), potentially falsely suggesting B12 deficiency when the real issue is reduced renal clearance of these metabolites

**Evidence needed:** B12 deficiency prevalence by risk group (vegan, elderly, metformin users, PPI users), metformin and B12 depletion dose-response and timeline, MMA specificity for B12 deficiency in elderly populations with reduced kidney function, cost-effectiveness of routine screening vs universal supplementation in high-risk groups, neurological outcome data from early vs late B12 repletion.

## Serum Magnesium: The Test That Barely Works

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions: energy metabolism, protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood glucose control, blood pressure regulation, and bone development. It's the fourth most abundant mineral in the body. And we have no reliable, practical clinical test for it.

Only about 1% of the body's magnesium circulates in the blood. Roughly 60% is in bone, 39% is intracellular (mostly in muscle and soft tissue), and the remaining 1% is in serum and red blood cells. Serum magnesium is tightly regulated through renal excretion and bone buffering. When intake drops, the body pulls magnesium from bone and tissue to maintain serum levels. This means you can be substantially depleted in total body magnesium while your serum magnesium reads "normal." By the time serum magnesium falls below the reference range (typically 1.7-2.2 mg/dL), depletion is usually severe.

RBC magnesium (measuring magnesium inside red blood cells) is theoretically better because it reflects intracellular stores. But it's still an imperfect proxy - red blood cells aren't representative of all tissues, the test has higher variability, and it's not widely available or standardized.

The ionized magnesium assay (measuring the free, biologically active fraction) is more physiologically relevant but even less available clinically.

**Competing claims:**

1. Serum magnesium is a poor screening tool that misses most magnesium depletion - relying on it creates false reassurance, and clinicians should be willing to treat suspected magnesium deficiency empirically (with oral supplementation) in symptomatic patients without waiting for a low serum level
2. Despite its limitations, serum magnesium still has value at the extremes - a truly low serum magnesium (below 1.7) is clinically significant and actionable, and a very low level (below 1.5) can cause arrhythmias, seizures, and cardiac arrest; the test works for severe depletion even if it misses mild-to-moderate depletion
3. RBC magnesium should be more widely available and used as the standard test rather than serum magnesium - while still imperfect, it captures intracellular stores better and is more likely to detect subclinical depletion
4. The measurement problem means we genuinely don't know how common clinically significant magnesium deficiency is in the general population - claims of widespread deficiency are not verifiable with current testing, and the supplement industry exploits this uncertainty

**Evidence needed:** Serum vs RBC vs ionized magnesium correlation with total body magnesium stores (using balance studies or isotope methods as reference), sensitivity of serum magnesium for detecting subclinical depletion, magnesium depletion symptom studies with verified body stores, standardization and availability data for RBC and ionized magnesium assays.

## Is Magnesium Deficiency as Widespread as Claimed?

The supplement industry's claim: most people are magnesium deficient. The evidence cited: dietary surveys showing that a majority of adults in Western countries consume less magnesium than the Recommended Dietary Allowance (420 mg/day for men, 320 mg/day for women). Magnesium content in soil and food has declined over decades. Modern food processing removes magnesium. Stress, alcohol, caffeine, and certain medications increase magnesium excretion.

The counter: dietary intake below the RDA doesn't equal clinical deficiency. The RDA is set with a safety margin. Absorption varies by source and individual. And as established, we can't reliably measure magnesium status with available tests, so the actual prevalence of clinically significant deficiency is unknown.

The result is a paradox: there's reasonable indirect evidence that many people have suboptimal magnesium intake, but we can't confirm who is actually deficient, which makes both the "everyone is deficient" and the "deficiency is rare" positions unprovable with current tools.

**Competing claims:**

1. Subclinical magnesium deficiency is likely common based on converging indirect evidence: widespread dietary inadequacy, declining food magnesium content, high rates of conditions associated with low magnesium (hypertension, type 2 diabetes, muscle cramps), and the fact that supplementation studies often show benefits in these populations
2. "Most people are deficient" is an unfalsifiable claim that conveniently drives supplement sales - without a reliable biomarker, the prevalence is unknown, and the leap from "dietary intake below RDA" to "clinical deficiency requiring supplementation" is not supported by evidence
3. Magnesium supplementation is cheap, safe at moderate doses (up to 350 mg/day supplemental is the UL, though total intake can be higher from food), and unlikely to cause harm in people who aren't deficient - the precautionary argument for supplementation doesn't require proof of widespread deficiency
4. Specific populations are more likely to be genuinely deficient: type 2 diabetics (renal magnesium wasting from glucosuria), chronic alcohol users, PPI users (impaired absorption), and people with inflammatory bowel disease - targeted supplementation in these groups is more evidence-based than universal supplementation

**Evidence needed:** Dietary magnesium intake surveys with absorption-adjusted estimates, magnesium supplementation RCTs in general population vs specific deficiency-risk groups, historical trends in food magnesium content, magnesium supplementation and blood pressure/glucose/muscle function outcomes, reliable biomarker development research.

## Can You Reliably Measure Zinc Status?

Zinc is essential for immune function, wound healing, protein synthesis, DNA synthesis, cell division, taste and smell, and over 300 enzymatic reactions. Mild zinc deficiency impairs immunity and wound healing. Severe deficiency causes growth retardation, hypogonadism, and skin lesions. But identifying who is actually zinc deficient is remarkably difficult.

Serum zinc (or plasma zinc) is the standard test. It's tightly homeostatic - the body defends circulating zinc levels at the expense of tissue stores, similar to magnesium. This means serum zinc can remain normal despite tissue depletion. When serum zinc does drop, it's often late in the depletion process.

Worse, serum zinc is a negative acute phase reactant. During infection, inflammation, or stress, zinc is redistributed from plasma into the liver and immune cells. A sick person with a CRP of 10 will have a low serum zinc that reflects the acute phase response, not chronic zinc deficiency. Testing zinc during any inflammatory state produces unreliable results.

Additional confounders: serum zinc drops after meals (zinc moves from plasma into tissues during absorption and distribution), varies with time of day, and is affected by albumin levels (much of serum zinc is albumin-bound, so low albumin falsely lowers zinc).

**Competing claims:**

1. Serum zinc is too unreliable to be clinically useful in most contexts - it misses depletion in early stages, drops during any inflammation, and varies with meals and time of day; measuring it in a sick or recently-fed person is essentially meaningless
2. Despite its limitations, serum zinc remains the best available marker and is useful when measured under controlled conditions - fasting, morning, without acute illness; very low values (below 60 mcg/dL) are clinically significant regardless of confounders
3. For populations at high risk of zinc deficiency (vegetarians/vegans, elderly, chronic diarrheal disease, athletes with heavy sweat losses), empiric supplementation at moderate doses (15-30 mg/day) may be more practical than attempting to diagnose deficiency with an unreliable test
4. Functional zinc markers (alkaline phosphatase, which requires zinc as a cofactor, or taste acuity testing) could supplement serum zinc but are nonspecific and not validated for zinc deficiency diagnosis in clinical practice

**Evidence needed:** Serum zinc sensitivity for tissue-level depletion, inflammation-adjusted zinc interpretation methods, fasting vs non-fasting zinc variability, zinc depletion-repletion studies with functional outcomes, alternative zinc status biomarker validation (hair zinc, RBC zinc, alkaline phosphatase).

## MTHFR Variants: When Genetics Became a Marketing Tool

MTHFR (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) is an enzyme in the folate cycle that converts 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate to 5-methyltetrahydrofolate, which is required for homocysteine remethylation to methionine. Two common variants - C677T and A1298C - reduce enzyme activity. The C677T homozygous variant (TT genotype) reduces enzyme activity by approximately 70%. It's found in 10-15% of many populations, with higher prevalence in Southern European and Hispanic populations.

In the 2010s, MTHFR testing exploded through direct-to-consumer genetic testing and functional medicine. A narrative emerged: MTHFR variants impair methylation, causing elevated homocysteine, impaired detoxification, neurotransmitter problems, and increased risk for depression, anxiety, cardiovascular disease, recurrent miscarriage, and neural tube defects. Treatment protocols involve methylfolate (instead of folic acid), methylcobalamin, SAMe, TMG, and other "methylation support" supplements. Some practitioners build elaborate treatment protocols around MTHFR status.

The evidence base for most of this is thin. The C677T TT genotype does modestly increase homocysteine, particularly when folate intake is low. In populations without folic acid fortification, the variant is associated with increased neural tube defect risk. But in folate-replete populations (like the US, where food is fortified), the clinical impact of MTHFR variants is minimal for most carriers. Homocysteine is often normal. The variant is extremely common - calling it a "mutation" that requires treatment medicalizes a normal genetic variation.

**Competing claims:**

1. MTHFR variants are clinically significant for a subset of carriers - those with the TT genotype, low folate intake, elevated homocysteine, or recurrent pregnancy loss may genuinely benefit from methylfolate over folic acid and from B vitamin optimization; dismissing all MTHFR concerns as hype ignores the subset who are affected
2. The vast majority of MTHFR carriers have normal homocysteine and no clinical consequences - routine MTHFR testing and methylation support protocols for everyone with a variant are not evidence-based, and the elaborate protocols marketed by functional medicine practitioners and supplement companies far exceed what the science supports
3. MTHFR status is irrelevant if homocysteine is normal - the actionable question isn't "do you have the variant" but "is your methylation impaired as measured by functional markers (homocysteine, MMA)"; treating the genotype rather than the phenotype is backwards
4. The MTHFR testing industry has created a self-sustaining market: genetic test identifies common variant, variant is framed as pathological, patient is sold supplements to "fix" it, supplements are taken indefinitely - the entire loop operates outside evidence-based medicine and profits from genetic anxiety

**Evidence needed:** MTHFR genotype and homocysteine levels in folate-fortified vs non-fortified populations, methylfolate vs folic acid clinical outcome studies (not just homocysteine reduction), MTHFR and recurrent pregnancy loss meta-analyses, neural tube defect risk by genotype and folate status, cost-effectiveness and clinical utility of routine MTHFR testing.
