# Why Isn't Fasting Insulin a Standard Test?

Fasting insulin is a biologically sensible early marker of metabolic strain. When insulin-sensitive tissues become less responsive, pancreatic beta cells can often preserve normal fasting glucose by secreting more insulin. In that state, glucose may look "normal" while insulin is already elevated. The problem is not that this biology is imaginary. The problem is that fasting insulin has not yet become a standardized, portable, outcome-linked clinical decision tool. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

The strongest version of the case for fasting insulin is that hyperinsulinemia with normal glucose should not be ignored. The strongest version of the case against routine testing is that a biomarker needs analytical validity, clinical validity, and clinical utility before it becomes a standard screening tool: the number should mean roughly the same thing across laboratories, map to validated decision thresholds, and change management in a way shown to improve outcomes. ([Pletcher and Pignone, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21403122/); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

## What fasting insulin is trying to detect

Fasting insulin is an indirect marker of the insulin concentration required to maintain fasting glucose homeostasis. In compensated insulin resistance, fasting glucose can remain normal because beta cells increase insulin secretion enough to offset reduced insulin sensitivity. That makes fasting insulin attractive: it may expose metabolic compensation before fasting glucose or A1C crosses diagnostic thresholds. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Tabák et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22683128/))

But fasting insulin is not a pure measurement of "insulin resistance." It reflects a heterogeneous phenotype rather than one mechanism, including insulin secretion, insulin clearance, beta-cell function, adiposity, fasting physiology, and assay behavior. Because not all manufacturers have recalibrated insulin assays to a reference method, results also need interpretation in the context of the specific assay's calibration. Fasting insulin-based indices also reflect fasting, mostly hepatic, insulin sensitivity more than whole-body dynamic insulin action. The hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp remains the reference method for measuring insulin sensitivity, but it is time-consuming, expensive, and impractical for routine care. ([Goodarzi et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21632466/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## Why glucose-based tests became standard first

Current diabetes diagnosis and screening are built around fasting plasma glucose, 2-hour plasma glucose during an oral glucose tolerance test, random plasma glucose in symptomatic patients, and A1C. The 2026 ADA Standards of Care continue to define diagnostic testing through plasma glucose and A1C criteria, not fasting insulin. ([ADA Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes))

That does not mean insulin is metabolically irrelevant. It means glucose and A1C have standardized methods, reproducible diagnostic thresholds, decades of outcome linkage, and clinical action pathways. Laboratory guidance likewise states that, in most people with diabetes or at risk for diabetes or cardiovascular disease, routine testing for insulin or proinsulin is not recommended and these assays are useful primarily for research purposes. ([Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## The assay standardization problem is real

The central technical problem is that insulin is usually measured by immunoassay, and different assays do not always agree. Antibody specificity, calibration material, proinsulin cross-reactivity, matrix effects, unit conversion, and manufacturer-specific traceability chains can all shift the reported value. ([Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/); [Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/))

In the American Diabetes Association insulin assay standardization work, 12 commercial insulin methods from 9 manufacturers were compared. Within-assay coefficients of variation ranged from 3.7% to 39.0%, and among-assay coefficients of variation ranged from 12% to 66%. The same work reports a median among-assay CV around 24%, with only partial agreement even after using a common reference preparation. ([Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/))

A later study compared 10 commercial insulin methods against an isotope-dilution liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry reference measurement procedure. Only 4 of 10 methods had at least 95% of individual serum results within a 32% total-error goal, and 7 of 10 methods showed bias greater than 15.5% for a substantial fraction of samples. The important finding was not that insulin cannot be standardized; it was that traceability to a reference procedure using suitable native serum materials could improve comparability. ([Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/))

More recent work still finds meaningful disagreement. A 2022 comparison of four ELISA kits and three automated chemiluminescent immunoassays found substantial discrepancies across kits, with relative standard deviations ranging from 1.7% to 23.2%, and concluded that traceability chains and unit-conversion factors were not harmonized. ([Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/))

## Why "normal fasting insulin" is not one number

Reference intervals vary by assay, population, age, sex, BMI selection, and how "healthy" is defined. In a healthy Iranian adult sample, the overall 95% reference interval was 2.11 to 12.49 µU/mL. In healthy nondiabetic Chinese adult men, it was 1.57 to 16.32 µU/mL. A large Brazilian laboratory database proposed 2.52 to 13.14 µU/mL for fasting insulin and 0.39 to 2.86 for HOMA-IR. A 2026 Nepalese cross-sectional study reported 2.63 to 14.56 µIU/mL. ([Tohidi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24530467/); [Li et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23268156/); [Schrank et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39529982/); [Paudel et al., 2026](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/16/3/e109523))

These are reference intervals, not universal disease thresholds. A reference interval describes the distribution in a selected reference population, while a clinical decision limit is linked to risk, diagnosis, treatment, or consensus thresholds. The upper end of a reference interval therefore does not automatically prove optimal physiology or establish a treatment trigger. This is why a fasting insulin of 10 µU/mL may be interpreted differently depending on the lab, the population, and the clinical context. ([Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/))

A fasting insulin around 20 µU/mL with normal glucose is different from a borderline value. That value sits above the upper interval reported in many carefully selected "healthy" cohorts, so it is worth rechecking and interpreting in clinical context. But it still should not be treated as a standalone diagnosis of insulin resistance without considering the assay, repeatability, body composition, triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, blood pressure, waist circumference, medications, and glycemic markers. ([Tohidi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24530467/); [Li et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23268156/); [Schrank et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39529982/); [Paudel et al., 2026](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/16/3/e109523); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## HOMA-IR helps, but it does not solve the problem

HOMA-IR uses fasting insulin and fasting glucose to estimate insulin resistance. The common formula is:

**HOMA-IR = fasting insulin × fasting glucose / 22.5** when glucose is in mmol/L, or approximately **fasting insulin × fasting glucose / 405** when glucose is in mg/dL. ([Matthews et al., 1985](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3899825/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

HOMA-IR is useful because it contextualizes insulin relative to glucose. A fasting insulin of 15 µU/mL with glucose of 75 mg/dL and the same insulin with glucose of 105 mg/dL do not represent the same metabolic state. HOMA-IR captures some of that difference. ([Matthews et al., 1985](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3899825/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

But HOMA-IR inherits the insulin assay problem. Endotext notes that the lack of standardized insulin assays makes universal cut points difficult to define, and it frames fasting surrogate indices such as HOMA-IR as tools used extensively in epidemiology and clinical trials rather than definitive individual diagnostic tests. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

Cutoffs also vary by population and method. In a Spanish general adult population, the HOMA-IR threshold was 3.46 using a 90th-percentile criterion but 2.05 when metabolic syndrome components were used. In a 15-year Chinese cohort, cutoffs around 1.4 and 2.0 corresponded closely to the 75th and 90th percentiles among people with persistent normal glucose tolerance. ([Gayoso-Diz et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24131857/); [Lee et al., 2016](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0163424))

So HOMA-IR is more physiologically contextualized than fasting insulin alone for research and risk stratification, but it is not a fully validated universal clinical diagnostic threshold. It is also not uniformly superior to fasting insulin alone for every outcome. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Gast et al., 2012](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0052036); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

## What outcome studies show

The outcome evidence is strongest for HOMA-IR and broader insulin resistance markers, not for a single universal fasting insulin cutoff.

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of 38 studies including 215,878 participants found that higher HOMA-IR was associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and non-fatal major adverse cardiovascular events. The reported hazard ratios were 1.87 for type 2 diabetes, 1.35 for hypertension, and 1.46 for non-fatal major adverse cardiovascular events. The same analysis did not find clear associations with cardiovascular mortality or cancer mortality and concluded that more research is needed to define HOMA's value for metabolic and cardiovascular outcomes. ([González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/))

A 2012 meta-analysis of 65 studies and more than 516,000 adults without diabetes compared fasting glucose, fasting insulin, and HOMA-IR for incident cardiovascular disease. HOMA-IR showed a stronger association with coronary heart disease than fasting insulin alone; fasting insulin's association was weaker and not clearly significant in some analyses. The authors reported no funding support and no competing interests. ([Gast et al., 2012](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0052036))

A separate meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies found that higher fasting insulin or hyperinsulinemia was associated with increased risk of hypertension and coronary heart disease, but not stroke. ([Xun et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24132974/))

For hypertension, a 2017 meta-analysis of 11 studies including 55,059 participants and 10,230 incident hypertension cases found that people in the highest fasting insulin category had a 54% higher risk of incident hypertension, while those in the highest HOMA-IR category had a 43% higher risk. The authors reported no competing financial interests. ([Wang et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27836689/))

Prospective cohort data also support fasting hyperinsulinemia as a risk marker for future diabetes. In Pima Indians with normal glucose tolerance, high fasting plasma insulin predicted type 2 diabetes independent of clamp-measured insulin resistance and acute insulin response. That is strong biological evidence, but it comes from a specific high-risk population and does not automatically create a universal clinical cutoff. ([Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/))

## Why this has not become routine clinical testing

Fasting insulin is not a standard metabolic screening test because several conditions remain unmet.

First, the assay is not sufficiently harmonized. A value from one platform may not be interchangeable with a value from another platform. That weakens both cross-sectional interpretation and long-term tracking when labs change methods. ([Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/); [Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/))

Second, reference ranges are not clinical decision limits. Many population reference intervals cluster with upper limits around 12 to 16 µU/mL, but these are not universally validated "treat above this" thresholds. ([Tohidi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24530467/); [Li et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23268156/); [Schrank et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39529982/); [Paudel et al., 2026](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/16/3/e109523); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/))

Third, fasting insulin is physiologically ambiguous. A high value may mean insulin resistance with compensation. A low or normal value may mean good insulin sensitivity, but it may also reflect reduced beta-cell capacity in some contexts. This is why fasting insulin alone cannot cleanly distinguish compensation from failure. ([Goodarzi et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21632466/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

Fourth, HOMA-IR remains cutoff-dependent. It is useful for ranking risk in populations, but its threshold varies by ethnicity, age, sex, glycemic status, and assay method. ([Gayoso-Diz et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24131857/); [Lee et al., 2016](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0163424); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

Fifth, clinical action pathways are underdeveloped. For glucose and A1C, diagnostic categories lead to defined follow-up and treatment pathways. For fasting insulin, current laboratory guidance does not provide a universally accepted algorithm that says what to do differently at 8, 12, 20, or 30 µU/mL in a normoglycemic patient. ([ADA Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

Sixth, direct cost-effectiveness evidence is thin. The available cost-effectiveness literature is more developed for insulin-free surrogate indices than for routine fasting insulin screening. One related cost-effectiveness analysis compared the triglyceride-glucose index with HOMA-IR for diagnosing insulin resistance and found the triglyceride-glucose index more cost-effective, largely because it uses routine lipid and glucose measures rather than insulin. ([Nevárez-Sida and Guerrero-Romero, 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37315510/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

## The standardization problem is solvable

The assay problem is not scientifically impossible. Earlier ADA-associated work proposed insulin assay standardization, and later analyses showed that agreement could improve through traceability to isotope-dilution LC-MS/MS reference measurement procedures using appropriate serum materials. ([Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Staten et al., 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2797975/))

Laboratory medicine reviews have described HbA1c and C-peptide as examples where reference systems and harmonization or standardization efforts improved comparability. Insulin harmonization has been treated as a continuing need rather than a completed implementation. ([Hörber et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30802485/); [Staten et al., 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2797975/))

A mature fasting insulin system would likely need: a reference measurement procedure, commutable reference materials, manufacturer recalibration, consistent units, external proficiency testing, population-specific reference intervals, outcome-derived decision thresholds, and clinical algorithms showing that insulin-guided management improves outcomes. ([Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Staten et al., 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2797975/); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/); [Pletcher and Pignone, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21403122/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

## Where fasting insulin can still be useful

Fasting insulin can be informative as an adjunctive risk signal, especially when interpreted conservatively rather than as a diagnostic endpoint. ([Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

A persistently high fasting insulin with normal glucose, high triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol, central adiposity, hypertension, fatty liver risk, family history of type 2 diabetes, or features of a condition in which insulin resistance is clinically suspected can strengthen the suspicion of compensated insulin resistance. It can also help identify a person whose glucose remains normal only while insulin output is high. ([Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/); [Wang et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27836689/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/))

The safer use case is trend-based and context-based: measure fasting insulin with fasting glucose, use the same laboratory and assay when possible, repeat unexpected results, and interpret the number alongside A1C, triglycerides, HDL cholesterol, blood pressure, waist circumference, medications, and clinical history. ([ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

The riskier use case is treating a single fasting insulin value as a universal diagnosis. A value of 20 µU/mL is above many published healthy-cohort reference intervals, but a value of 9, 12, or 15 µU/mL cannot be interpreted cleanly without assay and context. ([Tohidi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24530467/); [Li et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23268156/); [Schrank et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39529982/); [Paudel et al., 2026](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/16/3/e109523); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## Evidence gaps

The evidence base still has important gaps.

There are good assay-comparison studies showing that non-standardization is real. There are cohort studies and meta-analyses showing that fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, or insulin resistance predict diabetes, hypertension, and some cardiovascular outcomes. But there is less direct evidence showing that routine fasting insulin screening changes clinical decisions in a way that improves hard outcomes beyond existing risk markers. ([Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/); [Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/); [Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [Pletcher and Pignone, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21403122/))

Clinician confidence is also an implementation question: without harmonized assays, validated thresholds, and defined action pathways, a test is harder to interpret consistently in routine care. ([Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## Quick reference

Fasting insulin is biologically meaningful but analytically fragile. ([Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/); [Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/))

HOMA-IR is more physiologically contextualized than fasting insulin alone, but it inherits insulin assay variability and lacks universal cutoffs. ([Matthews et al., 1985](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3899825/); [Gayoso-Diz et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24131857/); [Lee et al., 2016](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0163424); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

A markedly elevated fasting insulin with normal glucose is not "nothing." It is often compatible with compensated insulin resistance, especially when other metabolic risk markers are present. ([Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/))

The reason fasting insulin is not standard is not that insulin resistance is unimportant. It is that insulin testing lacks the standardization, threshold validation, treatment algorithms, and cost-effectiveness evidence that glucose and A1C have. ([Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADA Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

## Practical interpretation

The right conclusion is not "never measure fasting insulin." It is also not "fasting insulin should replace glucose and A1C." ([Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory); [ADLM, 2024](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/optimal-testing-guide-to-lab-test-utilization/g-s/insulin))

A better conclusion is: fasting insulin is a useful adjunctive signal, especially when clearly elevated, but it is not yet ready to serve as a universal diagnostic or screening standard. The number can be clinically meaningful, but it is not portable enough across assays or validated enough across populations to stand alone. ([Weyer et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11118012/); [Marcovina et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/); [Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Rosli et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35622611/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))

The standardization problem should be solved. Until then, fasting insulin is best used as a contextual marker: helpful when interpreted carefully, misleading when treated as a universal cut-point. ([Miller et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19325009/); [Staten et al., 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2797975/); [Ozarda et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30047297/); [Sacks et al., 2023](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/46/10/e151/153425/Guidelines-and-Recommendations-for-Laboratory))
