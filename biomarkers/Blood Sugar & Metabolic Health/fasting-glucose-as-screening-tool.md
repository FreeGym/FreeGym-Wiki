# Fasting Glucose as a Screening Tool

Fasting plasma glucose is a useful, standardized, high-specificity first-pass test for fasting hyperglycemia and diabetes. It is not a complete stand-alone screen for total dysglycemia, and it is a weak stand-alone screen for early insulin resistance. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Duong et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902703/); [Kaur et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0242415); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

Strictly, a basic metabolic panel includes a glucose value; it is only a fasting plasma glucose if the sample is drawn after at least 8 hours without caloric intake. Current ADA criteria for nonpregnant adults classify fasting plasma glucose below 100 mg/dL as normal, 100-125 mg/dL as impaired fasting glucose/prediabetes, and 126 mg/dL or higher as diabetes, with confirmatory testing required unless hyperglycemia is unequivocal. ([MedlinePlus, 2024](https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/basic-metabolic-panel-bmp/); [American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes))

## What fasting glucose measures

Fasting glucose measures plasma glucose in the fasting state. That is clinically useful, but it is not the same thing as directly measuring insulin resistance, post-load glucose handling, beta-cell reserve, or early metabolic compensation. The ADA notes that fasting plasma glucose, 2-hour glucose during an OGTT, and HbA1c reflect different aspects of glucose metabolism and identify groups with incomplete concordance; compared with fasting glucose and HbA1c cut points, the 2-hour OGTT value diagnoses more people with prediabetes and diabetes. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

This means a normal fasting glucose is reassuring about fasting glycemia at that moment. It is not proof that insulin sensitivity is normal. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

## Why fasting glucose can stay normal

The central biological problem is compensation. In early insulin resistance, the pancreas can maintain normal glucose by secreting more insulin. Fasting glucose rises later, when compensation becomes insufficient. The Whitehall II cohort supports this timing, although its insulin sensitivity estimates were model-based rather than clamp-derived: changes in glucose, insulin sensitivity, and insulin secretion became apparent years before diabetes diagnosis, with insulin sensitivity abnormalities preceding overt diagnostic hyperglycemia. ([Tabák et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19515410/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

A later review of the same natural-history pattern reported that insulin sensitivity was already reduced about 13 years before diabetes onset, while glucose remained tightly regulated until roughly 2-6 years before diagnosis, when glucose rose more abruptly. ([Tabák et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3891203/))

A Whitehall II analysis comparing South Asian and white participants found that insulin sensitivity and secretion trajectories differed before diabetes diagnosis, and that South Asian participants had lower insulin sensitivity and faster rises in fasting glucose before diagnosis. This reinforces the same general point: fasting glucose can look stable for a long period while the underlying compensatory system is under strain. ([Hulman et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00125-017-4275-6))

## How much fasting glucose misses

Against OGTT-defined diabetes, fasting glucose at the diagnostic threshold is specific but not very sensitive. A 2023 systematic review and network meta-analysis of 73 studies found that fasting plasma glucose 126 mg/dL or higher had sensitivity of 0.49 and specificity of 0.98 versus 2-hour OGTT 200 mg/dL or higher; combining HbA1c 6.5% or higher or fasting glucose 126 mg/dL or higher improved sensitivity to 0.64 but lowered specificity to 0.95. ([Duong et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902703/))

A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis using OGTT or 2-hour post-load glucose as the reference standard found that the optimal fasting glucose threshold for detecting undiagnosed diabetes was about 104 mg/dL, with sensitivity of 82.3% and specificity of 89.4%. That does not mean 104 mg/dL should be used as a diagnostic threshold; it means a lower threshold can improve case-finding at the cost of more false positives, while the ADA diagnostic threshold of 126 mg/dL is more specific. ([Kaur et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0242415); [American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes))

Fasting glucose also misses isolated impaired glucose tolerance: people with normal fasting glucose but abnormal 2-hour glucose. A review of impaired fasting glucose and impaired glucose tolerance found that IFG, IGT, and combined IFG/IGT vary by population and ethnicity, which is one reason fasting-only screening does not map cleanly onto total dysglycemia burden. ([Yip et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5707745/); [American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes))

## The "high-normal fasting glucose" problem

A fasting glucose of 95-99 mg/dL is not prediabetes by ADA criteria. But it should not always be treated as metabolically meaningless. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Tirosh et al., 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16207847/))

In a cohort of young men with fasting glucose below 100 mg/dL, higher fasting glucose within the normal range independently predicted future type 2 diabetes. ([Tirosh et al., 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16207847/))

The practical interpretation is not "95-99 mg/dL means disease." It is: high-normal fasting glucose should be interpreted in context. If it occurs alongside abdominal adiposity, high triglycerides, low HDL-C, hypertension, fatty liver, prior gestational diabetes, PCOS, family history, high-risk ancestry, or rising values over time, it is a reason to look deeper rather than file the result as simply "normal." ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/risk-factors/index.html); [Tirosh et al., 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16207847/); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

## Does fasting insulin solve the problem?

Fasting insulin and HOMA-IR are biologically informative because they can estimate insulin resistance and can sometimes identify compensatory hyperinsulinemia before fasting glucose rises. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that higher HOMA-IR was associated with higher future risk of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and non-fatal major adverse cardiovascular events, although not with all mortality outcomes examined. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Tabák et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19515410/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/))

But that does not automatically mean fasting insulin should be added to every screening panel. A 2024 cohort study of 5,578 mostly male Japanese workers found that adding fasting glucose to a model with age, sex, BMI, smoking, dyslipidemia, hypertension, and HbA1c improved diabetes prediction, but adding HOMA-IR and HOMA-beta did not materially improve performance further. The authors also declared no competing interests. ([Hu et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38191206/))

So fasting insulin is better viewed as a second-line or phenotyping tool, not a universally proven population screen. It can be useful when the clinical question is early insulin resistance, unexplained metabolic risk, or discordant markers, but fasting insulin and HOMA-IR have not earned the same screening status as fasting glucose, HbA1c, or OGTT. This is partly because HOMA-based methods are surrogate measures, HOMA-IR cutoffs vary across populations, and at least one professional recommendation advises against using fasting or OGTT insulin measurements as routine clinical estimates of insulin sensitivity. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Gayoso-Diz et al., 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1472-6823-13-47); [Endocrine Society of Australia, 2015](https://www.racp.edu.au/evolve/recommendations/endocrine-society-of-australia); [Hu et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38191206/))

## Cost-effectiveness

Recent economic evaluations generally support targeted or stepwise type 2 diabetes screening rather than indiscriminate expansion of every biomarker. A 2025 systematic review of economic evaluations found that risk-score-based screening, targeted screening, and combinations of risk scores with biomarkers often represented better value than universal biomarker-only screening, while the cost-effectiveness of screening designs varied by setting and implementation details. ([Jin et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1098301525000191))

The key gap is that this evidence base is not the same as evidence for universal fasting insulin screening. The current case for adding fasting insulin broadly is biologically plausible, but outcome and cost-effectiveness evidence is still weaker than for established dysglycemia screening and lifestyle intervention in high-risk groups. In the Diabetes Prevention Program, intensive lifestyle intervention and metformin reduced diabetes incidence in high-risk participants, with lifestyle intervention producing the larger reduction. ([Jin et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1098301525000191); [Endocrine Society of Australia, 2015](https://www.racp.edu.au/evolve/recommendations/endocrine-society-of-australia); [Hu et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38191206/); [Knowler et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11832527/))

## Physically active people

Exercise improves insulin-stimulated glucose disposal. A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials using hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp methods found that exercise training lasting at least 4 weeks significantly increased insulin-stimulated glucose disposal. ([Rebello et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36828899/))

That makes fasting glucose modestly tricky to interpret in active people, but the strongest defensible claim is limited: physical activity can improve glucose handling enough that fasting glucose alone still should not be treated as a complete metabolic assessment. The stronger claim, that fasting glucose is uniquely more misleading in physically active populations, should be treated as a hypothesis rather than a broad screening rule unless supported by population-specific evidence. ([Rebello et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36828899/); [American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

## What catches more

OGTT catches post-load dysglycemia that fasting glucose misses. HbA1c adds a longer-term glycemic marker, though it also has sensitivity limits and can be distorted by anemia, hemoglobin variants, kidney disease, pregnancy, and altered red-cell turnover. Fasting insulin or HOMA-IR can show compensation, but cutoffs and clinical action thresholds are less standardized than glucose and HbA1c. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [NIDDK, 2018](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/diagnostic-tests/a1c-test); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [Gayoso-Diz et al., 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1472-6823-13-47))

The best screening strategy is therefore tiered:

Fasting glucose is useful as a standardized first-pass marker. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Duong et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902703/))

HbA1c plus fasting glucose often improves dysglycemia case-finding compared with either test alone, though the tradeoff is lower specificity and more follow-up testing. ([Duong et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902703/))

OGTT is better when the question is missed post-load dysglycemia, impaired glucose tolerance, prior gestational diabetes, discordant markers, or high suspicion despite "normal" fasting glucose. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Yip et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5707745/); [NIDDK, 2018](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/diagnostic-tests/a1c-test))

Fasting insulin or HOMA-IR may be useful when the question is early insulin resistance or compensatory hyperinsulinemia, but it should not be presented as an outcome-proven universal replacement for glucose-based screening. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/); [Hu et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38191206/); [Endocrine Society of Australia, 2015](https://www.racp.edu.au/evolve/recommendations/endocrine-society-of-australia))

## Practical interpretation

A normal fasting glucose means fasting glucose regulation is currently intact. It does not mean insulin sensitivity is normal. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

A fasting glucose of 100-125 mg/dL should not be framed as merely "early risk." By current ADA criteria, it is already impaired fasting glucose/prediabetes, although the underlying metabolic process may have begun years earlier. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Tabák et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3891203/))

A fasting glucose of 95-99 mg/dL is not diagnostic, but in a high-risk person it should prompt context-based follow-up rather than automatic reassurance. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Tirosh et al., 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16207847/); [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/risk-factors/index.html))

If fasting glucose and HbA1c are normal but suspicion remains high, OGTT is the most direct next test for missed post-load dysglycemia. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Yip et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5707745/))

If the concern is early insulin resistance rather than diabetes diagnosis, fasting insulin/HOMA-IR can add useful information, but the evidence for universal screening is not yet strong enough. ([Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/); [González-González et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36181637/); [Hu et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38191206/); [Endocrine Society of Australia, 2015](https://www.racp.edu.au/evolve/recommendations/endocrine-society-of-australia))

## Bottom line

Fasting plasma glucose is adequate as a standardized, high-specificity first-pass screen for fasting hyperglycemia and diabetes. It is not adequate as a stand-alone screen for early insulin resistance or total dysglycemia. ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Duong et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9902703/); [Kaur et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0242415); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))

The real failure is not that fasting glucose exists. The failure is treating "normal fasting glucose" as synonymous with "normal metabolic health." ([American Diabetes Association Professional Practice Committee, 2026](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes); [Muniyappa et al., 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK278954/))
