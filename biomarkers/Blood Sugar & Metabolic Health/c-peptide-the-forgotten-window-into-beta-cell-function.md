# C-Peptide: The Forgotten Window Into Beta Cell Function

C-peptide is a marker of **endogenous insulin secretion**, not a general “blood sugar” marker. Its main value is that it answers a narrower question than glucose, HbA1c, or fasting insulin: **how much insulin is the pancreas still producing?** ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23413806/ "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6373457/ "C-peptide as a measure of the secretion and hepatic extraction of insulin: Pitfalls and limitations"))

## What C-peptide measures

Insulin and C-peptide are produced from the same precursor, proinsulin, and are secreted by pancreatic beta cells in roughly equal molar amounts. That is why C-peptide can serve as a proxy for endogenous insulin secretion. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23413806/ "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"))

After secretion, insulin and C-peptide behave very differently. Peripheral insulin is a noisy proxy for pancreatic secretion because insulin undergoes substantial and variable hepatic extraction before it reaches the systemic circulation. C-peptide has negligible hepatic extraction, a longer half-life, and more stable peripheral concentrations, making it a cleaner marker of beta-cell output than a peripheral insulin measurement. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6373457/ "C-peptide as a measure of the secretion and hepatic extraction of insulin: Pitfalls and limitations"))

This distinction matters most when the clinical question is **endogenous secretion** rather than **glycemic exposure**. HbA1c and glucose tell you how much hyperglycemia is present; C-peptide helps tell you whether the problem is mainly insulin resistance with preserved secretion, insulin deficiency, or a mixed state. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11823364/ "Exploring the potential role of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes management"))

## C-peptide is not one test with one meaning

C-peptide can be measured fasting, random/non-fasting, or after stimulation with glucagon or a mixed-meal tolerance test. Stimulated C-peptide often gives a better estimate of beta-cell reserve than a fasting value because it tests secretion under demand rather than at baseline. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9543865/ "C-peptide determination in the diagnosis of type of diabetes and its management"))

Interpretation depends on the **paired glucose level**. A low C-peptide during hyperglycemia means something very different from a low C-peptide during hypoglycemia, where beta-cell secretion should be suppressed. In hypoglycemia workups, insulin, C-peptide, proinsulin, glucose, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and sulfonylurea/meglitinide testing are interpreted together. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.994707/full "The optimal diagnostic criteria of endogenous hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia based on a large cohort of Chinese patients"), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/94/3/709/2596247 "Evaluation and management of adult hypoglycemic disorders"))

Kidney function also matters. C-peptide is cleared largely outside the liver, with renal handling playing an important role, so reduced kidney function can raise circulating C-peptide and make secretion look higher than it really is. ([Wiley Online Library](https://dom-pubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/dom.14785 "C-peptide determination in the diagnosis of type of diabetes and its management"))

Timing also matters. C-peptide measured during or soon after DKA, HHS, or another hyperglycemic emergency can underestimate beta-cell reserve; clinical guidance advises not testing C-peptide within 2 weeks of a hyperglycemic emergency when classification or insulin withdrawal decisions depend on the result. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

## Where C-peptide clearly changes interpretation

The strongest clinical use is in patients using insulin. Injected insulin does not contain C-peptide, so C-peptide can distinguish endogenous pancreatic insulin production from exogenous insulin exposure. This is one of the clearest advantages over a standard insulin assay. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.12159 "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"))

The second strong use is unexplained hypoglycemia. In endogenous hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia, insulin secretion is inappropriate for the low glucose level, so C-peptide and proinsulin are usually not suppressed. In exogenous insulin use, insulin may be high while C-peptide and proinsulin are suppressed. In sulfonylurea or meglitinide exposure, insulin and C-peptide can both be inappropriately high, so drug testing is important. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.994707/full "The optimal diagnostic criteria of endogenous hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia based on a large cohort of Chinese patients"), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/94/3/709/2596247 "Evaluation and management of adult hypoglycemic disorders"))

The third strong use is diabetes classification, especially in insulin-treated or atypical patients. A person labeled as having type 2 diabetes but showing low C-peptide, leanness, rapid treatment failure, ketosis, or autoimmune features may have type 1 diabetes, LADA, or another insulin-deficient form rather than classic insulin-resistant type 2 diabetes. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36641115/ "The predictive ability of C-peptide in distinguishing type 1 diabetes from type 2 diabetes"), [Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

## Diabetes subtype classification

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis found that C-peptide can help distinguish type 1 from type 2 diabetes, with values around **≤0.20 nmol/L** supporting type 1 diabetes/severe insulin deficiency and values around **≥0.30 nmol/L** supporting type 2 diabetes/preserved secretion in pooled analyses, though heterogeneity across studies was substantial. In practice, values in the lower intermediate range should not be treated as diagnostic by themselves. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36641115/ "The predictive ability of C-peptide in distinguishing type 1 diabetes from type 2 diabetes"))

More conservative clinical classification guidance treats very low C-peptide as strong evidence of severe insulin deficiency, but treats **0.20-0.60 nmol/L** as context-dependent rather than definitive. Values around **≥0.60 nmol/L** provide stronger evidence of preserved endogenous secretion, though they still do not prove classic type 2 diabetes by themselves. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

For LADA, an international expert panel proposed using random C-peptide categories to guide treatment intensity: **<0.3 nmol/L** suggests treating similarly to type 1 diabetes with insulin; **0.3-0.7 nmol/L** is a gray zone; and **>0.7 nmol/L** supports a more type-2-like treatment algorithm, with repeat testing if glycemic control worsens. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/diabetes/article/69/10/2037/16062/Management-of-Latent-Autoimmune-Diabetes-in-Adults "Management of latent autoimmune diabetes in adults"))

C-peptide can also help triage possible MODY, especially when diabetes occurs in a young or lean person with preserved endogenous insulin secretion and negative autoantibodies. Urinary C-peptide/creatinine ratio and fasting or stimulated C-peptide can help separate MODY from long-duration type 1 diabetes, but they do not replace genetic testing when MODY is strongly suspected. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13098-020-00557-9 "Update on clinical screening of maturity-onset diabetes of the young"))

## Type 2 diabetes progression

In type 2 diabetes, C-peptide is often high early because beta cells are compensating for insulin resistance. Over time, some patients lose beta-cell reserve, and C-peptide can fall as endogenous insulin secretion declines. This is why a high C-peptide is not automatically “good” and a single value is not enough to define trajectory. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11823364/ "Exploring the potential role of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes management"))

Longitudinal evidence supports the idea that lower C-peptide is associated with insulin requirement, but the strongest version of the claim - that C-peptide trajectory is the single best predictor of future insulin dependence - is too strong. A 2025 review of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes found that studies mainly show associations between lower fasting or non-fasting C-peptide and insulin requirements; it also highlighted the need for better prospective evidence before C-peptide can become a routine management algorithm for all type 2 diabetes. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11823364/ "Exploring the potential role of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes management"))

The GRADE study adds useful nuance. Glucose-stimulated C-peptide responses improved initially and then gradually declined over time, and higher stimulated responses were linked to better early HbA1c response and slower glycemic progression. That supports C-peptide as a marker of beta-cell reserve, but it does not prove that routine C-peptide monitoring improves outcomes in ordinary primary-care type 2 diabetes. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/47/4/580/154105/Longitudinal-Effects-of-Glucose-Lowering "Longitudinal effects of glucose-lowering medications on beta-cell function and glycemia"))

A smaller outpatient study found that C-peptide-related indices were lower in people already treated with insulin and that a lower casual C-peptide index predicted future insulin treatment over four years. This supports the trajectory concept, but it remains observational evidence rather than a definitive treatment rule. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9060140/ "Casual C peptide index: Predicting the subsequent need for insulin therapy"))

## C-peptide versus fasting insulin

The theoretical advantage of C-peptide over fasting insulin is strong: insulin is distorted by hepatic first-pass extraction, while C-peptide is not. That makes C-peptide a better peripheral marker of pancreatic insulin secretion. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6373457/ "C-peptide as a measure of the secretion and hepatic extraction of insulin: Pitfalls and limitations"))

There is also a laboratory issue. An ADA workgroup evaluating commercial insulin assays found major lack of standardization, with wide variation in assay performance across methods. This is one reason fasting insulin can be difficult to use as a precise clinical marker across different laboratories. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17272483/ "Standardization of insulin immunoassays: Report of the American Diabetes Association Workgroup"))

C-peptide assays are not perfect either. A multicenter laboratory comparison found that C-peptide results varied across methods and laboratories, especially at higher concentrations, although calibration against a reference method improved comparability. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18420730/ "Standardization of C-peptide measurements"))

More recent mass-spectrometry work suggests C-peptide can be measured reproducibly under standardized laboratory conditions. In one interlaboratory LC-MS/MS comparison, median imprecision was lower for C-peptide than insulin for both individual and replicate measurements, but this does not automatically apply to every clinical immunoassay. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38549041/ "Interlaboratory comparison of antibody-free LC-MS/MS measurements of C-peptide and insulin"))

In metabolic-index studies, C-peptide-based measures can perform well, but the answer depends on the index and the setting. In one reproducibility study using repeated OGTTs, C-peptide-based measures of beta-cell function had favorable coefficients of variation and discriminant ratios for several secretion indices, while some insulin-sensitivity indices performed better with insulin-derived formulas. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0258476 "Reproducibility and discrimination of different indices of insulin sensitivity and insulin secretion"))

## Weight loss, remission, and beta-cell recovery

Weight loss can improve insulin sensitivity and beta-cell function in type 2 diabetes, but a single fasting C-peptide may not show the full picture because it reflects both beta-cell output and the insulin demand imposed by insulin resistance. Stimulated or dynamic measures may be more informative than a single fasting value when the question is beta-cell responsiveness. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9543865/ "C-peptide determination in the diagnosis of type of diabetes and its management"), [Enlighten Publications](https://eprints.gla.ac.uk/163673/ "Remission of human type 2 diabetes requires decrease in liver and pancreas fat content but is dependent upon capacity for beta-cell recovery"), [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.933118/full "Weight loss improves beta-cell function independently of dietary carbohydrate restriction in people with type 2 diabetes"))

In mechanistic data from the DiRECT program, remission was associated with reduced liver fat, reduced pancreatic fat, and recovery of first-phase insulin response; responders tended to have shorter diabetes duration than non-responders. This supports the idea that beta-cell dysfunction can be reversible in at least some people with relatively early type 2 diabetes. ([Enlighten Publications](https://eprints.gla.ac.uk/163673/ "Remission of human type 2 diabetes requires decrease in liver and pancreas fat content but is dependent upon capacity for beta-cell recovery"))

A randomized diet trial in people with type 2 diabetes and obesity found that about 6 percent weight loss improved insulin sensitivity and beta-cell function, while carbohydrate reduction lowered HbA1c but did not improve beta-cell function or glucose tolerance beyond the effects of weight loss. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.933118/full "Weight loss improves beta-cell function independently of dietary carbohydrate restriction in people with type 2 diabetes"))

Bariatric-surgery studies also suggest that preoperative C-peptide or C-peptide response can help predict diabetes remission, likely because preserved beta-cell reserve is necessary for remission after major weight loss. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27435450/ "Preoperative beta cell function is predictive of diabetes remission after bariatric surgery"))

## What C-peptide does not prove

A high C-peptide does not prove healthy beta cells. It can mean the pancreas is over-secreting insulin to compensate for insulin resistance. In cohort data, higher C-peptide has been associated with future type 2 diabetes risk, even after adjustment for insulin and other metabolic factors. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/9/9/3001 "Plasma C-peptide and risk of developing type 2 diabetes"))

A low C-peptide does not automatically diagnose autoimmune diabetes. It can reflect long-duration diabetes, glucotoxicity, low current glucose, recent fasting, medication effects, recent hyperglycemic emergency, or advanced beta-cell failure from non-autoimmune causes. Autoantibodies, phenotype, diabetes duration, glucose at the time of testing, kidney function, and sometimes genetic testing are needed for classification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36641115/ "The predictive ability of C-peptide in distinguishing type 1 diabetes from type 2 diabetes"), [Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

C-peptide is also not a replacement for HbA1c, glucose, weight, renal function, cardiovascular risk assessment, or complication screening. It answers a different question: **how much endogenous insulin secretion remains?** ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.12159 "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"))

## Routine metabolic workup versus targeted use

The case for targeted C-peptide testing is strong. It is useful in insulin-treated patients, unexplained hypoglycemia, uncertain diabetes subtype, suspected LADA, possible MODY, lean or rapidly progressive diabetes, and selected cases where insulin withdrawal or insulin initiation is being considered. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.12159 "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"), [Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

The case for ordering C-peptide in every “thorough metabolic workup” for suspected insulin resistance or early prediabetes is weaker. It can provide mechanistic information, but there is not strong evidence that routine C-peptide testing in broad primary-care metabolic screening improves outcomes or is cost-effective. Reviews describe possible roles in type 2 diabetes management, but the evidence is still mostly associative rather than trial-based. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11823364/ "Exploring the potential role of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes management"))

The best middle position is that C-peptide is under-used for **classification and beta-cell reserve**, but over-sold when presented as a universal screening test. It is most valuable when the result would change the next decision: autoimmune testing, genetic testing, insulin initiation, insulin de-escalation, hypoglycemia investigation, or prognosis in atypical type 2 diabetes. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.12159 "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"))

## Quick reference values

- **Very low C-peptide:** values around **≤0.20 nmol/L** support severe insulin deficiency/type 1 diabetes in the right clinical context. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36641115/ "The predictive ability of C-peptide in distinguishing type 1 diabetes from type 2 diabetes"))

- **Intermediate C-peptide:** values around **0.20-0.60 nmol/L** should usually be interpreted as context-dependent rather than diagnostic by themselves, especially in adults with atypical diabetes, long-duration diabetes, or insulin-treated type 2 diabetes. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

- **More clearly preserved C-peptide:** values around **≥0.60 nmol/L** provide stronger evidence of preserved endogenous secretion, though this does not by itself prove type 2 diabetes. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))

- **LADA treatment categories:** random C-peptide **<0.3 nmol/L**, **0.3-0.7 nmol/L**, and **>0.7 nmol/L** have been proposed as treatment-guiding strata by an international expert panel. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/diabetes/article/69/10/2037/16062/Management-of-Latent-Autoimmune-Diabetes-in-Adults "Management of latent autoimmune diabetes in adults"))

- **Hypoglycemia workup:** during documented hypoglycemia, non-suppressed C-peptide supports endogenous insulin secretion or secretagogue exposure; suppressed C-peptide with high insulin supports exogenous insulin exposure. Interpret alongside insulin, proinsulin, beta-hydroxybutyrate, sulfonylurea/meglitinide testing, and glucose. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2022.994707/full "The optimal diagnostic criteria of endogenous hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia based on a large cohort of Chinese patients"), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/94/3/709/2596247 "Evaluation and management of adult hypoglycemic disorders"))

- **Serial monitoring:** trend interpretation is strongest when testing conditions are consistent: same lab/assay, similar fasting or stimulated state, paired glucose, kidney function, and medication context. ([Wiley Online Library](https://dom-pubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/dom.14785 "C-peptide determination in the diagnosis of type of diabetes and its management"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18420730/ "Standardization of C-peptide measurements"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38549041/ "Interlaboratory comparison of antibody-free LC-MS/MS measurements of C-peptide and insulin"))

- **Timing after hyperglycemic emergencies:** avoid relying on C-peptide measured during or within 2 weeks after DKA, HHS, or another hyperglycemic emergency. ([Diabetes Journals](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/49/Supplement_1/S27/163926/2-Diagnosis-and-Classification-of-Diabetes "2. Diagnosis and classification of diabetes: Standards of Care in Diabetes - 2026"))


## Practical interpretation

- C-peptide is a better peripheral marker of pancreatic insulin secretion than fasting insulin because it avoids hepatic first-pass extraction and has more stable circulation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6373457/ "C-peptide as a measure of the secretion and hepatic extraction of insulin: Pitfalls and limitations"))

- In established type 2 diabetes, treatment decisions are still usually driven by HbA1c, symptoms, weight, kidney function, cardiovascular risk, hypoglycemia risk, and complications; C-peptide adds the most when insulin deficiency or diabetes subtype is uncertain. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.15469 "Exploring the potential role of C-peptide in type 2 diabetes management"))

- In a lean adult, rapidly progressive diabetes, early insulin requirement, ketosis, or treatment failure out of proportion to weight/insulin resistance, C-peptide plus autoantibodies can prevent misclassification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36641115/ "The predictive ability of C-peptide in distinguishing type 1 diabetes from type 2 diabetes"))

- In prediabetes or suspected insulin resistance, high C-peptide can indicate compensatory hyperinsulinemia, but routine testing is not yet supported by strong outcome or cost-effectiveness evidence. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/9/9/3001 "Plasma C-peptide and risk of developing type 2 diabetes"))

- The strongest claim is not “everyone needs C-peptide”; it is that C-peptide is a useful, under-used test when the clinical question is **beta-cell reserve, endogenous insulin production, or diabetes subtype**. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/dme.12159 "The clinical utility of C-peptide measurement in the care of patients with diabetes"))
