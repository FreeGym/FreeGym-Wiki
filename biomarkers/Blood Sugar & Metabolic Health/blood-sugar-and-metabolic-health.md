# Blood Sugar & Metabolic Health

The most debated questions about glucose, insulin, metabolic screening, and the tools used to measure them.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Is Fasting Glucose a Good Screening Tool?

Fasting glucose is on every basic metabolic panel. It's the default screening tool for metabolic health. A value under 100 mg/dL is "normal," 100-125 is "pre-diabetic," 126+ is diabetic. Simple, cheap, universal. And it catches metabolic dysfunction at the latest possible stage.

The problem is biological. Insulin resistance develops gradually over years. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin to keep glucose in range. Fasting glucose stays normal as long as the pancreas can keep up. When fasting glucose finally rises, the compensatory mechanism is already failing - beta cell function has declined, and the disease is well-established. A normal fasting glucose doesn't mean normal metabolic health. It means the system hasn't broken down yet.

Studies suggest insulin resistance can precede abnormal fasting glucose by 10-15 years. During that entire window, the person's fasting glucose is "normal" and they're told they're fine. This is not a minor gap - it's the difference between catching metabolic dysfunction when lifestyle changes work best and catching it when the pancreas is already damaged.

**Competing claims:**

1. Fasting glucose is a late marker that misses the majority of the insulin resistance timeline - relying on it as a primary screen means millions of people are told they're metabolically healthy when they're already years into a disease process that's far easier to reverse early
2. Fasting glucose is adequate as a population screening tool - it's cheap, standardized, reproducible, and when combined with HbA1c, catches most clinically significant dysglycemia; adding insulin testing to every screening panel would create complexity without proven outcome benefit
3. The real failure isn't the marker but the follow-up - a fasting glucose of 95-99 should prompt further investigation (insulin, OGTT, or HOMA-IR), but in practice it gets filed as "normal" without any additional workup
4. For physically active and metabolically healthy populations, fasting glucose is even more misleading - exercise improves glucose disposal so effectively that active people can maintain normal fasting glucose despite developing insulin resistance in other tissues

**Evidence needed:** Timeline studies mapping insulin resistance progression to fasting glucose changes, missed diagnosis rates when fasting glucose is the sole screening marker, cost-effectiveness of adding fasting insulin to standard panels, comparative screening strategies (glucose alone vs glucose + insulin vs OGTT) and their diagnostic yield.

## Why Isn't Fasting Insulin a Standard Test?

If insulin resistance is the engine of metabolic disease and fasting glucose catches it too late, the obvious solution is to measure fasting insulin. Elevated fasting insulin in the presence of normal glucose is the hallmark of compensated insulin resistance - the body is maintaining glucose homeostasis but at the cost of hyperinsulinemia. This combination is detectable years before glucose itself rises.

The logic is straightforward. The practice isn't. Fasting insulin is not standardized across laboratories. Different assays (chemiluminescence, ELISA, radioimmunoassay) produce different absolute values for the same sample. There is no universally agreed-upon reference range - "normal" fasting insulin might be below 5, 8, 12, or 25 mIU/L depending on the lab, the assay, and who you ask. Inter-assay variability can be 20-40%. This means a fasting insulin of 10 at one lab might read as 14 at another, and a "normal" result at one lab could be "elevated" at another.

**Competing claims:**

1. The standardization problem is real but not a reason to avoid testing - even with assay variability, a fasting insulin of 20+ mIU/L with a normal fasting glucose is clinically meaningful regardless of which assay produced it, and the information value far outweighs the noise
2. Without standardization, fasting insulin creates more confusion than clarity in clinical practice - physicians don't know what to do with the number, reference ranges are unreliable, and patients fixate on a value that may not be comparable across labs or over time
3. HOMA-IR (which requires fasting insulin) partially addresses the problem by contextualizing insulin relative to glucose, but it inherits the same assay variability and adds its own cutoff ambiguity - it's a research tool being used clinically without validation for that purpose
4. The standardization problem is solvable and should be solved - insulin assay harmonization efforts exist but lack the funding and institutional urgency that glucose standardization received decades ago; the barrier is institutional, not scientific

**Evidence needed:** Insulin assay comparison studies across platforms, proposals for insulin assay harmonization (CDC, IFCC efforts), diagnostic accuracy of fasting insulin at various cutoffs for predicting future diabetes and cardiovascular disease, physician survey data on confidence interpreting fasting insulin, cost-effectiveness of adding insulin to metabolic panels.

## HbA1c: Gold Standard or Flawed?

Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) measures the percentage of hemoglobin molecules with glucose attached. Because red blood cells live approximately 120 days, HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over the preceding 2-3 months. No fasting required. A single draw. Stable and reproducible. It's the primary tool for diabetes diagnosis (6.5%+ = diabetes, 5.7-6.4% = pre-diabetes) and monitoring. By many measures, it's an excellent marker.

But HbA1c doesn't measure glucose. It measures a consequence of glucose exposure on hemoglobin, and anything that changes hemoglobin or red blood cell lifespan changes the result independent of actual glucose levels.

Iron deficiency anemia extends red blood cell lifespan, increasing glycation time and falsely elevating HbA1c. Iron supplementation in anemic patients can drop HbA1c by 0.5-1.0% without any change in actual glucose control. Hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell trait, thalassemia trait) alter glycation rates or assay interference depending on the method used. Recent blood loss or transfusion disrupts the measurement. Pregnancy, chronic kidney disease, and liver disease all affect it.

The race/ethnicity question is particularly contentious. At the same measured average glucose (from CGM data), Black individuals tend to have HbA1c values 0.3-0.4% higher than white individuals. This isn't a small difference - it's enough to cross diagnostic thresholds and change clinical decisions. The mechanism is debated: differences in RBC lifespan, glycation rates, or hemoglobin variants may all contribute.

**Competing claims:**

1. HbA1c is the best single marker for diabetes screening and monitoring despite its limitations - no alternative offers the same combination of convenience, standardization, and clinical validation, and the conditions that meaningfully distort it are identifiable and relatively uncommon
2. HbA1c misclassifies a significant number of individuals, particularly those with iron deficiency, hemoglobinopathies, or certain racial/ethnic backgrounds - using it as the sole diagnostic criterion for diabetes causes real harm through false diagnoses and missed diagnoses
3. HbA1c's biggest flaw is what it hides: it flattens glucose variability into a single average, which means two patients with identical HbA1c can have dramatically different glucose patterns (stable vs highly variable) with potentially different health consequences
4. HbA1c should always be interpreted alongside at least one other marker (fasting glucose, OGTT, or CGM data) rather than standing alone as a diagnostic tool - the multi-marker approach reduces misclassification and captures information that HbA1c misses

**Evidence needed:** Studies on HbA1c discordance with CGM-measured average glucose by race/ethnicity, iron deficiency and HbA1c bias quantification, diagnostic misclassification rates when HbA1c is used alone vs in combination with other markers, hemoglobinopathy and HbA1c interference by assay method.

## Medications That Distort Glucose Testing

Glucose and HbA1c look like simple, robust markers. But multiple medications affect them directly (changing actual glucose handling) or indirectly (through effects on red blood cells, pancreatic function, or insulin sensitivity), and interpreting the results without knowing the medication context produces predictable errors.

Glucocorticoids are the most clinically important confounder. Oral, intravenous, injected, and high-dose inhaled steroids raise fasting glucose, postprandial glucose, and HbA1c. Steroid-induced hyperglycemia is common enough to have its own diagnostic category, and the effect is dose- and duration-dependent. A patient started on prednisone for an autoimmune condition may develop "diabetes" that resolves within weeks of tapering off.

Second-generation antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine at higher doses) produce insulin resistance, weight gain, and hyperglycemia. The effect is mechanism-specific and can develop over weeks to months of treatment.

Thiazide diuretics modestly raise glucose through effects on insulin secretion and potassium depletion; the effect is small but measurable. Beta-blockers can mask hypoglycemic symptoms and mildly affect glucose handling.

Statins slightly increase HbA1c and new-onset diabetes risk (approximately 10% relative increase, smaller absolute effect). The mechanism is incompletely understood but reproducible across trials. The risk-benefit for most patients still favors statins, but the HbA1c rise should not be mistaken for worsening diabetes when starting statin therapy.

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide, liraglutide) lower glucose and HbA1c through weight loss, delayed gastric emptying, and direct beta-cell effects. They also delay post-meal glucose peaks, which affects CGM patterns and OGTT results in ways that can be misinterpreted if the medication isn't known.

Metformin reduces fasting glucose and HbA1c through hepatic glucose output suppression and modest insulin sensitization. Its effect on HbA1c (typically 0.5-1.5%) is well-characterized. The B12 depletion over years of use (covered in the vitamins section) is a separate medication-related lab consideration.

Iron deficiency and its treatment both affect HbA1c (covered in the HbA1c section).

**Competing claims:**

1. Medication-induced glucose changes should be distinguished from true metabolic dysfunction before starting or escalating diabetes treatment - starting metformin for steroid-induced hyperglycemia in a patient who will taper off steroids in 6 weeks is treatment misallocation
2. The threshold for "reversible vs real" diabetes is blurred - a patient with undiagnosed metabolic dysfunction exposed to glucocorticoids may cross into diabetes range and stay there even after steroid withdrawal, because the steroid exposed an existing vulnerability
3. Patients on medications that affect glucose should have baseline testing before initiation and periodic monitoring during treatment - this is standard practice for antipsychotics and glucocorticoids but not universal
4. GLP-1 agonists are changing the interpretation of OGTTs, CGMs, and post-meal glucose patterns - a patient on semaglutide has a different glucose physiology than the same patient off it, and postprandial patterns should be interpreted with this in mind

**Evidence needed:** Steroid-induced hyperglycemia reversibility studies, antipsychotic-related diabetes onset and progression, statin and HbA1c dose-response meta-analyses, GLP-1 effects on CGM patterns and OGTT interpretation, medication review integration into diabetes workup.

## The Oral Glucose Tolerance Test: Forgotten Gold Standard?

The 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) was once the primary method for diagnosing impaired glucose tolerance and diabetes. You drink 75g of glucose, then blood sugar is measured at 1 and 2 hours. A 2-hour value below 140 mg/dL is normal, 140-199 is impaired glucose tolerance, 200+ is diabetes.

The OGTT catches something that fasting glucose and HbA1c both miss: impaired glucose tolerance - the inability to handle a glucose load normally even when fasting values and averages look fine. Some studies suggest 30-40% of people who are "normal" by fasting glucose and HbA1c criteria have impaired glucose tolerance on OGTT. These individuals are at significantly elevated risk for progression to diabetes and cardiovascular events.

Despite this, the OGTT has been largely replaced by HbA1c in clinical practice. It's inconvenient (requires a 2-hour lab visit), has poor day-to-day reproducibility (the same person can get different results on different days), is affected by recent carbohydrate intake and physical activity, and causes nausea in some patients. The convenience of HbA1c won.

**Competing claims:**

1. The OGTT is the most physiologically informative glucose test available - it reveals how the body actually handles a glucose challenge, which is what matters for real-world metabolic health; its replacement by HbA1c for convenience has led to missed diagnoses at scale
2. The OGTT's poor reproducibility makes it unreliable for individual diagnosis - a test that can give a different result on a different day is not a gold standard, regardless of its physiological appeal
3. The OGTT should be reserved for specific populations where fasting glucose and HbA1c are known to be insufficient - gestational diabetes screening (where it remains standard), high-risk individuals with normal fasting markers, and patients with discordant results
4. CGMs may eventually replace the OGTT by providing continuous real-world glucose data that captures post-meal responses without the artificial 75g glucose load - a 2-week CGM captures more metabolic information than a single OGTT ever could

**Evidence needed:** Prevalence of impaired glucose tolerance in individuals with normal fasting glucose and HbA1c, OGTT reproducibility studies (day-to-day coefficient of variation), head-to-head comparison of OGTT vs CGM for detecting early glucose dysregulation, cardiovascular outcome data for OGTT-only-detected impaired glucose tolerance.

## What Is HOMA-IR and Should You Care?

HOMA-IR is calculated from two numbers you may already have: fasting glucose and fasting insulin. The formula (glucose in mg/dL x insulin in mIU/L / 405) produces a single number meant to represent insulin resistance. Higher values mean more resistance. It was developed in 1985 as a research tool for epidemiological studies and has since migrated into clinical practice and biohacker culture.

The appeal is obvious: it captures the glucose-insulin relationship that either number alone misses. A fasting glucose of 90 with an insulin of 4 (HOMA-IR ~0.9) is a completely different metabolic state than a fasting glucose of 90 with an insulin of 18 (HOMA-IR ~4.0). The glucose panel says they're identical. HOMA-IR says one is insulin sensitive and the other is markedly insulin resistant.

The limitations are also real. HOMA-IR reflects only fasting, hepatic insulin resistance - it doesn't capture how you handle meals (postprandial insulin resistance) or how well muscles take up glucose (peripheral insulin resistance). The gold standard for insulin resistance is the hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp, which is a research procedure, not a clinical test. HOMA-IR correlates with clamp measurements, but imperfectly. And because it requires fasting insulin, it inherits all of insulin's assay variability and reference range ambiguity.

**Competing claims:**

1. HOMA-IR is the most accessible and informative marker of insulin resistance available in clinical practice - it's cheap, calculable from routine labs, and captures metabolic information that glucose alone completely misses; cutoffs around 1.0-1.5 as optimal and above 2.0-2.5 as concerning are clinically useful even if imperfect
2. HOMA-IR was designed for population studies and should not be used for individual clinical decisions - it correlates with clamp measurements at a group level but has too much individual variability, and the lack of standardized insulin assays makes absolute values unreliable across labs
3. HOMA-IR is better than glucose alone but still misses postprandial and peripheral insulin resistance - someone with a normal HOMA-IR can still have impaired glucose tolerance, and someone with an elevated HOMA-IR may respond normally to meals; it's a step forward, not the full picture
4. The value of HOMA-IR is in trending, not in absolute values - tracking your HOMA-IR over time at the same lab, using the same assay, reveals changes in insulin sensitivity that neither glucose nor insulin alone would show

**Evidence needed:** HOMA-IR vs euglycemic clamp correlation studies by population, HOMA-IR cutoff validation for predicting diabetes and cardiovascular outcomes, inter-assay variability impact on HOMA-IR reproducibility, HOMA-IR in physically active vs sedentary populations, longitudinal HOMA-IR tracking and outcome prediction.

## Pre-Diabetes: Real Condition or Over-Diagnosis?

Pre-diabetes is defined by the American Diabetes Association as fasting glucose 100-125 mg/dL, or HbA1c 5.7-6.4%, or 2-hour OGTT value 140-199 mg/dL. By these criteria, approximately 38% of American adults - over 96 million people - are pre-diabetic. The label is intended to motivate early intervention: lifestyle changes to prevent progression to type 2 diabetes.

But the scale of the label raises questions. When a third of the adult population qualifies for a disease precursor, is the threshold identifying a genuine at-risk group or medicalizing normal variation? The fasting glucose threshold was lowered from 110 to 100 in 2003, instantly reclassifying millions. The HbA1c criterion of 5.7% was added in 2010. The WHO uses different criteria (fasting glucose 110-125, no HbA1c-based pre-diabetes category), which means you can be pre-diabetic in the US and "normal" under WHO criteria.

Not everyone with pre-diabetes progresses. Only about 5-10% per year convert to diabetes, and many revert to normal without intervention. Meanwhile, the label carries psychological weight, insurance implications, and in some cases unnecessary medication.

**Competing claims:**

1. Pre-diabetes is a legitimate and useful clinical category - it identifies individuals at elevated risk for diabetes and cardiovascular disease, and the evidence that lifestyle intervention in this group prevents progression is strong (Diabetes Prevention Program trial showed 58% risk reduction)
2. The thresholds are set too low, particularly fasting glucose of 100 and HbA1c of 5.7 - these values capture a population so large that the label loses clinical utility, and many people labeled pre-diabetic are at very low absolute risk of ever developing diabetes
3. The ADA and WHO disagreement on thresholds reveals that the cutoffs are more arbitrary than scientific - glucose and metabolic risk exist on a continuum, and creating hard categories imposes false precision on a gradient
4. The pre-diabetes label serves the interests of pharmaceutical companies who market drugs (metformin, GLP-1 agonists) to this enormous population - the broader the definition, the larger the market, and the 2003 threshold change significantly expanded it

**Evidence needed:** Progression rates from pre-diabetes to diabetes by entry criteria (ADA vs WHO), Diabetes Prevention Program long-term follow-up, reversion rates to normal glucose tolerance without intervention, number needed to treat/screen analyses for pre-diabetes interventions, pharmaceutical industry influence on diagnostic threshold setting.

## CGMs for Non-Diabetics: Useful Data or Expensive Anxiety?

Continuous glucose monitors were engineered for people with diabetes - particularly type 1 - to manage insulin dosing and avoid dangerous highs and lows. They measure interstitial glucose every 1-5 minutes, producing a continuous trace of glucose throughout the day and night. They're a genuinely transformative tool for diabetes management.

Now they're marketed to healthy people. Companies sell CGM-based "metabolic health" programs to non-diabetics, promising insights into how foods, exercise, stress, and sleep affect blood sugar. The pitch: your fasting glucose and HbA1c can look normal while your real-time glucose tells a different story. And this is sometimes true - CGMs have revealed genuinely impaired glucose patterns in people whose standard bloodwork is clean.

But CGMs also produce a firehose of data that most people lack the context to interpret. A healthy person sees their glucose spike to 160 after rice and panics. They see a dip to 65 overnight and worry about hypoglycemia. They start food-combining, meal-timing, and restricting carbs based on a device designed for a disease they don't have. The line between metabolic insight and glucose anxiety is thin.

**Competing claims:**

1. CGMs reveal metabolic reality that standard bloodwork hides - postprandial spikes, overnight patterns, dawn phenomenon, and real-time responses to food and exercise provide information that fasting glucose and HbA1c structurally cannot capture; this data is valuable for anyone, not just diabetics
2. Most healthy people cannot meaningfully interpret CGM data without clinical guidance - the device creates a false sense of precision, the "optimal glucose" ranges promoted by CGM companies aren't evidence-based, and the result is anxiety-driven dietary restriction in people who were metabolically healthy to begin with
3. CGMs are most valuable for a specific non-diabetic population: people at high metabolic risk (family history, overweight, pre-diabetes, PCOS) who want to identify which foods and behaviors affect their glucose most - for low-risk healthy people, the signal-to-noise ratio is too low to justify the cost
4. The CGM wellness industry has created demand for a device to solve a problem most non-diabetics don't have, while simultaneously under-serving the diabetic population who genuinely needs better access and coverage

**Evidence needed:** CGM-detected glucose abnormalities in populations with normal standard bloodwork, behavioral change studies in non-diabetic CGM users, comparison of CGM-guided vs standard dietary advice for metabolic outcomes, psychological impact (anxiety, disordered eating) of CGM use in healthy populations, cost-effectiveness of CGM screening in high-risk non-diabetics.

## Do Post-Meal Glucose Spikes Matter in Healthy People?

CGM data has made post-meal glucose visible for the first time outside research labs. And what it shows is that even metabolically healthy people routinely spike to 140, 160, sometimes 180+ mg/dL after high-carbohydrate meals. These spikes typically resolve within 1-2 hours as insulin does its job. This is normal physiology. Or is it?

The "glucose spikes are harmful" camp argues that each spike represents a burst of oxidative stress, glycation, and endothelial damage. Repeated spikes over years, even if each resolves normally, may contribute to vascular damage, accelerated aging, and increased disease risk. They advocate minimizing spikes through food order (vegetables and protein before carbs), post-meal walking, food pairing (adding fat or fiber to blunt the curve), and carbohydrate restriction.

The "this is normal physiology" camp argues that transient postprandial glucose rises are exactly how the system is designed to work. Insulin is secreted, glucose is cleared, homeostasis is restored. The body has robust mechanisms to handle acute glucose loads, and pathologizing normal responses creates unnecessary food anxiety, especially in young, healthy, metabolically flexible individuals.

**Competing claims:**

1. Postprandial glucose spikes above 140 mg/dL cause measurable oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction even in healthy people - minimizing them through dietary strategies is a reasonable longevity-oriented practice even without diagnosed disease
2. Transient glucose rises after meals are normal human physiology - the threshold of 140 mg/dL as "harmful" in a non-diabetic is not evidence-based, and healthy people do not develop vascular damage from eating carbohydrates and having a normal insulin response
3. The spike itself matters less than recovery: how quickly glucose returns to baseline is a better indicator of metabolic health than peak height - someone who spikes to 160 and returns to 90 within 60 minutes may be healthier than someone who spikes to 140 but stays elevated for 3 hours
4. The "minimize spikes" advice is often correct in practice (eating fiber first, walking after meals) but for the wrong reason - these behaviors improve metabolic health through multiple mechanisms (satiety, digestion, insulin sensitivity), not because a post-meal glucose of 150 is inherently damaging in a healthy person

**Evidence needed:** Oxidative stress and endothelial function studies during controlled postprandial glucose excursions in healthy subjects, CGM-derived spike thresholds and long-term health outcomes in non-diabetics, glucose recovery time as a health marker, intervention studies on meal order and post-meal walking with hard health endpoints (not just glucose curves).

## Does Glycemic Variability Matter Beyond Average Glucose?

HbA1c captures average glucose. It says nothing about the pattern. Two people with HbA1c of 5.5% (estimated average glucose ~111 mg/dL) can have very different daily profiles: one holds between 85-120 all day, the other swings from 65 to 175 repeatedly. HbA1c sees them as metabolically identical.

Glycemic variability - the magnitude and frequency of glucose fluctuations - has been proposed as an independent risk factor for complications, separate from mean glucose. The biological rationale: repeated swings between high and low glucose may cause more oxidative stress than sustained moderate elevation, because the antioxidant response can't keep up with rapid oscillations. This has been demonstrated in cell culture and some animal models.

In people with diabetes, CGM-derived variability metrics (coefficient of variation, standard deviation, time in range, MAGE) are increasingly used alongside HbA1c. The question is whether variability matters in non-diabetics whose average glucose is normal, and whether reducing variability (even with a normal mean) improves long-term health.

**Competing claims:**

1. Glycemic variability is an independent contributor to vascular damage and oxidative stress - reducing it matters beyond reducing average glucose, and CGM-derived variability metrics should become standard in metabolic health assessment
2. The evidence for variability as an independent risk factor in non-diabetics is weak - most human outcome data is in diabetic populations, and extrapolating to healthy people with normal means and normal variability is premature
3. Variability is most informative as a marker of metabolic flexibility - how quickly and smoothly you return to baseline after a glucose challenge reflects insulin sensitivity, beta cell function, and counter-regulatory response, which are themselves predictive of future metabolic disease
4. Focusing on variability in non-diabetics risks missing the forest for the trees - for most people, the fundamentals (total calorie quality, exercise, sleep, weight management) will improve both mean glucose and variability, making variability a downstream marker rather than a direct optimization target

**Evidence needed:** Glycemic variability and cardiovascular outcomes in non-diabetic populations, oxidative stress studies comparing stable vs variable glucose at the same mean, CGM variability metrics and long-term metabolic disease prediction, intervention studies targeting variability specifically (not just mean glucose).

## Is Insulin Resistance the Root of Metabolic Disease?

Insulin resistance sits at the center of a web of conditions: type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, NAFLD/NASH, PCOS, some cancers, and possibly Alzheimer's. Some researchers view it as the upstream driver - the root cause from which everything else follows. Others see it as a downstream consequence of energy surplus and adiposity that happens to co-occur with these conditions.

The carbohydrate-insulin model argues: excess carbohydrates drive hyperinsulinemia, hyperinsulinemia drives fat storage and prevents fat burning, fat accumulation worsens insulin resistance, and the cycle spirals into metabolic disease. The solution is carbohydrate restriction.

The energy balance model argues: chronic energy surplus causes fat accumulation, particularly visceral and ectopic fat, which causes inflammation and insulin resistance. Carbohydrates aren't uniquely problematic - any macronutrient in excess causes the same downstream effects. The solution is caloric deficit and weight loss.

This isn't an academic debate. It determines whether a metabolically unhealthy person should prioritize carb restriction or caloric deficit, whether insulin or glucose should be the primary monitoring marker, and how the entire metabolic disease epidemic should be framed and treated.

**Competing claims:**

1. Insulin resistance is the primary upstream driver of metabolic disease - it precedes and predicts obesity, diabetes, CVD, and NAFLD; treating insulin resistance directly (through carbohydrate restriction, exercise, or insulin-sensitizing drugs) should be the first priority
2. Insulin resistance is a consequence of energy surplus and ectopic fat deposition, not a root cause - weight loss from any dietary approach improves insulin sensitivity, which argues against carbohydrates being uniquely causal
3. The carbohydrate-insulin model and the energy balance model aren't mutually exclusive - insulin resistance likely involves both hormonal signaling and energy balance, and the relative contribution varies by individual (some people are more carbohydrate-sensitive than others)
4. Framing insulin resistance as a single root cause oversimplifies a multifactorial process - genetics, sleep, stress, microbiome, environmental toxins, and physical inactivity all contribute independently, and the "one root cause" narrative is more marketable than accurate

**Evidence needed:** Isocaloric studies varying carbohydrate content and measuring insulin resistance, weight loss studies comparing macronutrient compositions for insulin sensitivity outcomes, longitudinal studies tracking insulin resistance preceding vs following weight gain, Mendelian randomization on insulin resistance and downstream metabolic disease.

## Can You Reverse Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes was long considered progressive and irreversible. You managed it - first with lifestyle, then metformin, then additional oral drugs, and eventually insulin. The trajectory was one-directional. Your A1c crept up, your medications increased, and the goal was slowing progression, not reversal.

That narrative is changing. Bariatric surgery data showed remission (HbA1c below 6.5% without medication) in 60-80% of patients, particularly those with shorter disease duration and greater weight loss. The DiRECT trial demonstrated that aggressive dietary weight loss (~15kg via very low calorie diet) produced remission in 46% of participants at 1 year. GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are producing substantial weight loss and diabetes remission in clinical trials at a scale previously only seen with surgery.

**Competing claims:**

1. Type 2 diabetes is reversible in many patients through sufficient weight loss (typically 10-15%+ of body weight) - the key mechanism is reduction of ectopic fat in the liver and pancreas, allowing beta cell function to recover; the earlier you intervene, the more likely remission is possible
2. "Reversal" is the wrong word - "remission" is more accurate because the underlying susceptibility remains and diabetes returns if weight is regained; calling it reversal implies a permanent cure that doesn't exist for most people
3. There is a point of no return: once beta cells are sufficiently damaged (through years of glucotoxicity and lipotoxicity), remission becomes unlikely regardless of weight loss - this explains why diabetes duration is the strongest predictor of remission failure
4. GLP-1 agonists are changing the calculus by making 10-15% weight loss achievable for millions of people who couldn't sustain it through diet alone - but they also raise questions about lifelong medication dependence, cost, access, and what happens when treatment stops

**Evidence needed:** DiRECT trial long-term follow-up, bariatric surgery remission durability data (5-10+ years), GLP-1 agonist diabetes remission rates and durability after discontinuation, beta cell function recovery studies, diabetes duration thresholds for remission likelihood, ectopic fat reduction and remission correlation data.

## When Lowering HbA1c Didn't Help: ACCORD and the Glycemic Targeting Reversal

For decades, the standard message in type 2 diabetes was clear: lower HbA1c is better. DCCT (type 1 diabetes, 1993) and UKPDS (type 2 diabetes, 1998) established that intensive glycemic control reduced microvascular complications (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy). The logic extended naturally: if lower HbA1c reduces small-vessel complications, it should reduce macrovascular complications (heart attacks, strokes, death) too. Push HbA1c as low as safely possible.

ACCORD (2008) tested this directly. It randomized 10,251 high-risk type 2 diabetics with established cardiovascular disease or multiple risk factors to an intensive target (HbA1c below 6.0%) vs standard (7.0-7.9%). The intensive arm was stopped early after 3.5 years due to a 22% increase in all-cause mortality. ADVANCE (also 2008) used a less aggressive intensive target and found neutral results. VADT (2009) in veterans was similarly neutral. The mortality signal wasn't replicated in ADVANCE and VADT, but the "lower is always better" paradigm couldn't survive ACCORD.

The interpretation remains debated. One explanation is that hypoglycemia (more frequent in the intensive arm) directly caused cardiovascular events. Another is that aggressive polypharmacy in the intensive arm introduced harms from multiple concurrent drugs. A third is that rapid glucose lowering in patients with longstanding uncontrolled diabetes destabilized vasculature that had adapted to chronic hyperglycemia. The subsequent shift in practice: individualized HbA1c targets. Younger, healthier patients with shorter disease duration can aim lower; older patients with established complications and shorter life expectancy should target 7.5-8.0%.

Rosiglitazone tells a parallel story. Thiazolidinediones improved HbA1c, fasting glucose, and insulin sensitivity markers robustly. They became widely prescribed. Steven Nissen's 2007 meta-analysis suggested a 43% increase in myocardial infarction, triggering an FDA advisory committee review. The RECORD trial produced mixed results. FDA restrictions were imposed in 2010 and partially lifted in 2013 after reanalysis. The controversy wasn't fully resolved, but the episode taught that glycemic markers improving on a drug doesn't guarantee the drug is safe - outcome trials are required. Since 2008, the FDA has required cardiovascular outcome trials for new diabetes drug approvals.

GLP-1 agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors subsequently demonstrated that it IS possible to lower glucose AND improve cardiovascular outcomes, but the benefits appear to come partly through mechanisms independent of glycemic control. The era of assuming that any glucose-lowering drug is good because it lowers glucose is over.

**Competing claims:**

1. ACCORD permanently changed diabetes care by establishing that HbA1c targeting must be individualized - one number doesn't fit all patients, and aggressive targeting in high-risk older diabetics can be net harmful regardless of how much it improves the HbA1c marker
2. The ACCORD mortality finding was likely driven by hypoglycemia and polypharmacy rather than glucose level itself - newer agents that lower glucose without hypoglycemia (GLP-1s, SGLT2 inhibitors) may permit aggressive glycemic targets without the ACCORD risk profile, but this hasn't been tested in a comparable population
3. The rosiglitazone episode underscores that glycemic markers are necessary but not sufficient for diabetes drug evaluation - cardiovascular outcome trials are now the standard, and glycemic-marker-only approval should be historical
4. The broader lesson extends beyond diabetes: any lifestyle or pharmacologic intervention evaluated primarily on HbA1c, fasting glucose, or CGM metrics in non-diabetic populations rests on unproven surrogate assumptions - the burden of proof for outcome benefit is not met by showing glucose improvement alone

**Evidence needed:** ACCORD long-term follow-up and mortality mechanism analyses, ADVANCE/VADT reconciliation with ACCORD, rosiglitazone reanalyses post-RECORD, cardiovascular outcome trial requirement impact on diabetes drug pipeline, GLP-1 and SGLT2 cardiovascular benefit mechanism studies (glycemic vs extra-glycemic).

## Metabolic Syndrome: Useful Diagnosis or Artificial Cluster?

Metabolic syndrome is defined by the presence of three or more of: waist circumference above 102cm (men) or 88cm (women), triglycerides above 150 mg/dL, HDL below 40 mg/dL (men) or 50 mg/dL (women), blood pressure above 130/85, and fasting glucose above 100 mg/dL. It was designed to identify individuals at high risk for cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes whose individual markers might each look only "borderline."

The cluster concept is the key insight: five borderline-abnormal markers together paint a picture of metabolic dysfunction that any single marker alone would miss. A person with a triglyceride of 155, HDL of 38, fasting glucose of 105, blood pressure of 135/88, and a waist of 104cm has no dramatically abnormal individual result - but collectively they describe a metabolic state that dramatically increases risk.

The criticisms are also real. The thresholds are arbitrary. Why is 150 the triglyceride cutoff and not 130 or 180? The components are weighted equally - but elevated fasting glucose may carry different risk than elevated waist circumference. Multiple competing definitions exist (ATP III, IDF, WHO), which don't fully agree. And some analyses suggest metabolic syndrome doesn't predict cardiovascular events better than its individual components entered separately into a risk model.

**Competing claims:**

1. Metabolic syndrome captures a unified metabolic state - insulin resistance driving a cluster of downstream markers - that has more clinical significance than the sum of its parts; the diagnosis motivates holistic intervention rather than treating each marker separately
2. Metabolic syndrome is a statistical artifact with arbitrary thresholds and equal weighting of unequal risks - entering the five components individually into a risk model predicts outcomes as well or better, and the syndrome label adds nothing clinically
3. The value of metabolic syndrome is in primary care communication and patient motivation - "you have metabolic syndrome" is a more actionable message than "your triglycerides are 155 and your HDL is 38 and your waist is 104cm," even if the predictive math is similar
4. The biggest limitation is what's missing from the definition - fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, inflammatory markers, and liver fat (which is central to the pathophysiology) aren't included, making the diagnosis less physiologically grounded than it should be

**Evidence needed:** Predictive value of metabolic syndrome diagnosis vs individual component models, insulin resistance as unifying mechanism research, threshold sensitivity analyses (how outcomes change at different cutoffs), comparisons of metabolic syndrome definitions (ATP III vs IDF vs WHO) for cardiovascular prediction, proposals for updated criteria including insulin and liver fat.
