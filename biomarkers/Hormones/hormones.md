# Hormones

The most debated questions about hormonal biomarkers - testosterone, thyroid, cortisol, estrogen, and growth factors.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Is Male Testosterone Really Declining?

Population-level data shows a significant decline in average male testosterone over the last several decades. A 2007 study in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that total testosterone in American men declined by roughly 1% per year between 1987 and 2004, independent of age. Similar trends have been observed in European and other populations. At the same age, a man today has substantially lower testosterone than his father did.

The "male hormone crisis" narrative has become mainstream. Low T clinics, testosterone optimization podcasts, and hormone replacement marketing all cite this decline. But the decline tracks closely with several confounders: rising obesity rates (adipose tissue aromatizes testosterone to estrogen and produces inflammatory cytokines that suppress the HPG axis), reduced smoking prevalence (nicotine increases SHBG and total testosterone), changing sleep patterns, increased environmental endocrine disruptors, and shifts in the populations being tested.

Disentangling "genuine endocrine decline" from "metabolic deterioration expressing itself hormonally" is the central challenge. If the decline is primarily driven by obesity and lifestyle, then calling it a testosterone crisis misframes a metabolic crisis as an endocrine one.

**Competing claims:**

1. The testosterone decline is real, significant, and not fully explained by obesity - even after adjusting for BMI, age, and smoking, studies show residual decline suggesting environmental or biological factors (endocrine disruptors, microplastics, lifestyle changes) are independently suppressing male testosterone
2. The decline is primarily a metabolic phenomenon - rising obesity, worsening sleep, increased stress, and reduced physical activity explain most of it, and framing it as a mysterious hormonal crisis distracts from the actual causes
3. The decline is partly a measurement artifact - changes in assay methodology, reference populations, and testing practices over decades make historical comparisons less reliable than they appear
4. Regardless of cause, the functional consequences are real - men today have lower testosterone and experience symptoms (reduced libido, energy, muscle mass, bone density) that affect quality of life, and the cause matters less than the clinical impact

**Evidence needed:** Longitudinal testosterone studies with full adjustment for BMI, sleep, smoking, and comorbidities; endocrine disruptor and testosterone research (phthalates, BPA, PFAS); assay standardization analyses across decades; studies in populations with stable obesity rates to isolate non-metabolic decline; environmental exposure and reproductive hormone data.

## Total Testosterone vs Free Testosterone: Which Matters?

A standard testosterone test reports total testosterone - the sum of all testosterone in the blood. But approximately 98% of circulating testosterone is bound: roughly 60-70% to sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG, tightly bound, biologically inactive) and 20-30% to albumin (loosely bound, weakly bioavailable). Only about 2% is truly free - unbound and able to enter cells and activate androgen receptors.

This means total testosterone can be misleading. A man with total T of 550 and high SHBG may have less bioavailable testosterone than a man with total T of 400 and low SHBG. The first man looks better on paper but may have more symptoms. SHBG varies significantly with age (rises), obesity (falls), liver disease, thyroid status, and medications. Conditions that shift SHBG change the clinical picture without changing total T.

Measuring free testosterone directly is technically difficult. Equilibrium dialysis is the gold standard but expensive and not widely available. Most labs use either calculated free T (using total T, SHBG, and albumin in formulas like Vermeulen) or analog free T assays (which are cheap but inaccurate). The calculated methods depend on which formula is used, and different formulas can produce meaningfully different results.

**Competing claims:**

1. Free testosterone is the clinically relevant fraction - it's what's biologically active, and total T without SHBG context is frequently misleading; free T (or at minimum, total T plus SHBG to calculate free T) should be the standard clinical assessment
2. Total testosterone is sufficient for most clinical decisions - the cases where total and free T lead to different conclusions are a minority, and adding SHBG and free T calculations to every testosterone evaluation adds complexity and cost without proportionate clinical benefit
3. The measurement problem undermines free testosterone's clinical utility - analog assays are inaccurate, calculated values depend on the formula, and even equilibrium dialysis has inter-lab variability; a measurement you can't trust isn't more useful than a simpler one you can
4. Bioavailable testosterone (free plus albumin-bound) may be more clinically relevant than free T alone, since albumin-bound testosterone dissociates easily and is available to tissues - but this fraction is even harder to measure and less commonly reported

**Evidence needed:** Symptom correlation studies comparing total T vs free T vs bioavailable T, SHBG variability impact on clinical decision-making, analog vs calculated vs equilibrium dialysis free T comparison studies, clinical outcomes using free T vs total T as treatment thresholds.

## What Testosterone Level Is Actually "Low"?

The Endocrine Society defines male hypogonadism as total testosterone below 300 ng/dL (on two morning measurements) plus symptoms. But 300 is a single cutoff applied to all adult men regardless of age. The reference ranges at most labs extend from roughly 250-300 on the low end to 900-1100 on the high end. This enormous range means a 28-year-old at 310 is "within range" despite being near the bottom for his age, while a 65-year-old at 310 may be completely normal for his.

The reference range problem compounds the issue. As population testosterone has declined, some labs have widened their ranges to accommodate the shift. A lower limit of 250 today may reflect the declining population average rather than a biologically meaningful threshold. A young man at 320 would have been flagged a generation ago.

Optimization clinics use tighter, age-stratified "optimal ranges" - often targeting 500-700+ for younger men. Conventional endocrinology argues there's no evidence that pushing testosterone from 350 to 600 improves hard outcomes in men who don't meet the clinical definition of hypogonadism.

**Competing claims:**

1. The 300 ng/dL cutoff is too low for younger men - age-stratified thresholds are needed because a testosterone level that's normal for a 60-year-old may represent genuine deficiency in a 25-year-old, and the single-cutoff approach fails to account for the age-related distribution
2. Symptom burden should matter as much as the number - a man at 280 with no symptoms may not need treatment, while a man at 350 with significant fatigue, libido loss, and body composition changes may genuinely benefit; the rigid numerical threshold overrides clinical judgment
3. The evidence doesn't support treating men with "low-normal" testosterone (300-500) who have symptoms - the symptom overlap with depression, poor sleep, stress, and deconditioning is enormous, and attributing these symptoms to testosterone when the level is technically adequate medicalizes normal variation
4. The declining reference ranges are themselves the problem - labs should use age-matched healthy-population data, not the general tested population (which includes obese, sick, and medicated men), and doing so would reveal that many "normal" results are actually below what a healthy male of that age should have

**Evidence needed:** Age-stratified testosterone normative data from healthy, non-obese populations; symptom-testosterone correlation studies at the 300-500 range; treatment response data in men with "low-normal" testosterone and symptoms; historical comparison of reference ranges and the populations used to derive them.

## Who Actually Needs TRT?

Testosterone replacement therapy is one of the fastest-growing prescriptions in medicine. TRT prescriptions in the US tripled between 2001 and 2011, and growth has continued. Low testosterone clinics now operate as a consumer-facing industry, advertising directly to men who feel tired, have lower libido, or struggle with body composition.

Some of these men genuinely need TRT. Primary hypogonadism (testicular failure from Klinefelter syndrome, injury, infection, or radiation) produces irreversibly low testosterone that requires replacement. Secondary hypogonadism from pituitary or hypothalamic disease may also require treatment. These are clear-cut medical indications.

But many men presenting to low T clinics have testosterone that's low from reversible causes: obesity, sleep deprivation, chronic stress, caloric restriction, overtraining, opioid use, or poorly managed chronic illness. For these men, TRT doesn't fix the problem - it masks it. Exogenous testosterone suppresses the HPG axis, reducing endogenous production and impairing fertility (via suppressed LH, FSH, and spermatogenesis). Stopping TRT after prolonged use can leave a man with lower natural testosterone than he started with, during a recovery period that takes months.

**Competing claims:**

1. TRT is overprescribed because the business model of low T clinics incentivizes treatment over investigation - a man with reversible causes of low T should have those causes addressed first, and starting TRT without a thorough workup is a clinical failure that creates iatrogenic dependence
2. The reversible-cause argument, while correct in principle, is used to deny TRT to men who genuinely suffer - not everyone can fix their sleep, lose 30kg, and eliminate stress on command, and making TRT contingent on perfecting lifestyle factors means some men suffer indefinitely while pursuing an unrealistic standard
3. TRT should include a clear diagnostic pathway: two morning testosterone measurements, LH/FSH to distinguish primary from secondary hypogonadism, assessment of reversible causes, and a trial of addressing those causes before committing to exogenous testosterone
4. The fertility implications are underdiscussed - TRT effectively acts as male contraception in most users, and young men are not always informed that starting testosterone may require HCG co-administration or future fertility interventions if they want children

**Evidence needed:** TRT prescription trend data and diagnostic workup rates, outcomes of addressing reversible causes vs starting TRT directly, fertility recovery after TRT discontinuation timelines, LH/FSH testing rates prior to TRT initiation, patient-reported outcomes comparing treat-first vs investigate-first approaches.

## Can You Meaningfully Raise Testosterone Without Drugs?

The natural testosterone optimization claim is pervasive: fix your sleep, lose weight, lift heavy, manage stress, and correct micronutrient deficiencies (vitamin D, zinc, magnesium). The evidence supports each of these individually. The question is whether the combined effect is large enough to matter clinically.

Sleep is the strongest lever. A week of 5-hour sleep can reduce testosterone by 10-15% compared to 8 hours. This is a large, reproducible, and reversible effect. Chronic short sleep likely produces sustained suppression.

Weight loss in obese men can raise testosterone substantially - some studies show increases of 50-100+ ng/dL with significant weight loss, particularly when starting from severe obesity. The mechanism is clear: reduced aromatization in adipose tissue, reduced inflammatory suppression of the HPG axis, and often reduced SHBG (though SHBG sometimes rises with weight loss, complicating the free T picture).

Resistance training has a modest chronic effect on resting testosterone (the acute post-exercise spike is transient and likely doesn't affect tissue exposure). Micronutrient repletion matters only if deficient - supplementing zinc when zinc-replete does nothing.

**Competing claims:**

1. Lifestyle optimization can produce clinically meaningful testosterone increases in men whose low T is driven by modifiable factors - sleep, weight loss, and exercise combined can raise T by 100-200+ ng/dL in the right population, which for some men is the difference between symptomatic and asymptomatic
2. The magnitude of natural optimization is overstated by the wellness industry - for a man with total T of 250, even aggressive lifestyle changes are unlikely to bring him to 450, and the "just fix your lifestyle" message delays effective treatment for men who genuinely need TRT
3. The interventions that raise testosterone the most (sleep, weight loss) also improve every symptom attributed to low T through independent mechanisms - it's impossible to know whether the symptom improvement comes from higher T or from better sleep, lower inflammation, and improved fitness directly
4. "Testosterone boosting supplements" (ashwagandha, tongkat ali, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid) are marketed aggressively but the evidence for clinically meaningful T increases is weak, inconsistent, and usually from small, short-term, industry-funded studies

**Evidence needed:** Sleep restriction and testosterone dose-response studies, weight loss magnitude and testosterone change data stratified by starting BMI, resistance training and resting testosterone meta-analyses (chronic effect, not acute), supplement RCTs with meaningful sample sizes and testosterone as primary outcome, combined lifestyle intervention testosterone outcomes.

## TRT and Cardiovascular Risk: Settled or Still Open?

The cardiovascular safety of TRT was questioned for years. A 2010 trial in elderly men with mobility limitations was stopped early due to excess cardiovascular events in the testosterone group. A 2013 VA observational study and a 2014 PLoS ONE study raised further concerns. The FDA added a cardiovascular warning to testosterone products in 2015.

The TRAVERSE trial (2023) was designed to settle the question. It randomized 5,246 men aged 45-80 with hypogonadism (total T below 300) and established or high-risk cardiovascular disease to transdermal testosterone or placebo, with a primary endpoint of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE). Over a mean follow-up of ~33 months, testosterone did not increase MACE (hazard ratio 0.96). This was the largest and most rigorous cardiovascular safety trial for TRT.

But "not harmful" isn't the same as "completely safe." TRAVERSE found signals for increased rates of atrial fibrillation, acute kidney injury, and pulmonary embolism in the testosterone group. The follow-up was relatively short for a drug intended for lifelong use. And the population was specific - older men with cardiovascular risk factors. The results may not generalize to younger, healthier men using testosterone at higher doses.

**Competing claims:**

1. TRAVERSE largely settles the cardiovascular safety question for the studied population - TRT does not increase major cardiovascular events in older hypogonadal men at high cardiovascular risk, which is the population where concern was greatest
2. Three years of follow-up is too short to conclude long-term safety - atherosclerosis develops over decades, and a 33-month trial cannot rule out cumulative cardiovascular effects of decades of exogenous testosterone
3. The secondary signals (atrial fibrillation, pulmonary embolism, acute kidney injury) deserve serious attention and further study - dismissing these as incidental findings while emphasizing the reassuring primary endpoint is selective interpretation
4. TRAVERSE studied physiologic replacement doses in older hypogonadal men - it tells us nothing about the safety of higher doses used in optimization clinics, or about cardiovascular risk in younger men using TRT at supraphysiologic or near-supraphysiologic levels

**Evidence needed:** TRAVERSE long-term extension data, secondary endpoint analyses (AF, VTE, AKI), cardiovascular outcomes in younger TRT populations, dose-response cardiovascular data, hematocrit and thrombotic risk in TRT users, comparison of physiologic replacement vs supraphysiologic dosing and cardiovascular markers.

## Is TSH Alone Enough to Assess Thyroid Function?

TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) is the standard screening test for thyroid dysfunction. The logic is elegant: TSH is produced by the pituitary in response to thyroid hormone levels. If thyroid hormones are low, TSH rises (hypothyroidism). If thyroid hormones are high, TSH falls (hyperthyroidism). TSH is sensitive to even small changes in thyroid function, often shifting before T4 and T3 leave their reference ranges. Most guidelines recommend TSH as the first-line screen, with additional testing only if TSH is abnormal.

This approach works well for primary thyroid disease. But it systematically misses several clinically important scenarios.

Central hypothyroidism (pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction) produces low or inappropriately normal TSH despite low thyroid hormones - the feedback loop is broken at the top, so TSH doesn't rise even though the thyroid is underproducing. This is rare but not negligible.

Poor T4-to-T3 conversion is more common and more contentious. The thyroid primarily produces T4 (inactive precursor). Peripheral tissues convert T4 to T3 (the active hormone) via deiodinase enzymes. Some people may have normal TSH and normal T4 but low T3 - adequate production but inadequate activation. Whether this is a clinically meaningful condition or a normal variant is debated.

Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's) can be detected early through TPO antibodies, often years before TSH becomes abnormal. Testing antibodies allows early identification of autoimmune thyroid disease and may predict future hypothyroidism.

**Competing claims:**

1. TSH alone is sufficient for initial thyroid screening in the general population - it catches the vast majority of clinically significant thyroid dysfunction, and routine full panels (TSH + free T4 + free T3 + TPO antibodies) in asymptomatic people generate false positives and unnecessary treatment
2. TSH alone misses central hypothyroidism, T4-to-T3 conversion problems, and early autoimmune thyroiditis - a full panel (TSH, free T4, free T3, TPO/TG antibodies) should be standard, especially in symptomatic patients whose TSH is "normal" but whose symptoms suggest thyroid dysfunction
3. The "poor converter" hypothesis (normal TSH and T4, low T3) is not well-established as a clinical entity - T3 levels are affected by non-thyroidal illness, caloric restriction, and circadian variation, and low T3 is more often a sign of systemic illness or energy deficit than a thyroid conversion problem
4. TPO antibodies should be tested at least once, especially in women and those with family history of autoimmune disease - a positive result with normal TSH changes monitoring strategy and may warrant closer follow-up even without current dysfunction

**Evidence needed:** Prevalence of central hypothyroidism in symptomatic patients with normal TSH, T4-to-T3 conversion studies and clinical outcomes, TPO antibody positivity and rate of progression to overt hypothyroidism, cost-effectiveness of full thyroid panels vs TSH-first approach, diagnostic yield of expanded testing in symptomatic patients.

## Subclinical Hypothyroidism: Treat or Watch?

Subclinical hypothyroidism is defined as elevated TSH (typically 4.5-10 mIU/L) with normal free T4. The thyroid is struggling enough that the pituitary is calling for more hormone, but the thyroid is still producing enough T4 to stay within range. It's common - affecting 4-10% of the adult population, more prevalent in women and with increasing age.

Some people with subclinical hypothyroidism have pronounced symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, brain fog, depression, dry skin. Others are completely asymptomatic and are diagnosed only through routine bloodwork. The symptom overlap with dozens of other conditions (poor sleep, stress, iron deficiency, depression) makes attribution to the thyroid uncertain.

The treatment question is genuinely unsettled. A Cochrane review found insufficient evidence that levothyroxine treatment improves symptoms or outcomes in subclinical hypothyroidism. The TRUST trial (2017), the largest RCT in older adults with subclinical hypothyroidism, found no benefit of levothyroxine for symptoms, fatigue, or quality of life. Yet many patients and clinicians report clear improvement with treatment, and some studies in younger patients with TSH above 7-8 show benefit.

**Competing claims:**

1. Subclinical hypothyroidism with TSH above 7-10 should be treated, especially in younger patients, those planning pregnancy, and those with symptoms - the evidence for treatment at this level is reasonable, and untreated subclinical hypothyroidism may progress to overt disease and worsen cardiovascular risk factors
2. The TRUST trial and Cochrane evidence show that treatment doesn't reliably improve symptoms, quality of life, or outcomes - most subclinical hypothyroidism, especially with TSH 4.5-7 in older adults, should be monitored rather than treated; the perceived benefit is often placebo
3. Age and context matter: younger patients with subclinical hypothyroidism, positive TPO antibodies, and symptoms are a different population from elderly patients with mild TSH elevation found incidentally - the treatment evidence is likely heterogeneous, and the TRUST trial's elderly population shouldn't be extrapolated to all subclinical hypothyroidism
4. The "trial of levothyroxine" approach - treating for 3-6 months and reassessing symptoms - is reasonable when symptoms are present and the diagnosis is uncertain; the risk of a monitored treatment trial is low, and the potential benefit of resolving symptoms justifies empiricism

**Evidence needed:** TRUST trial and other RCTs of levothyroxine in subclinical hypothyroidism, age-stratified and TSH-stratified outcome data, TPO antibody status and treatment response, progression rates from subclinical to overt hypothyroidism, cardiovascular risk data at different subclinical TSH levels.

## Where Should the TSH Upper Limit Be?

The standard reference range for TSH is approximately 0.4-4.5 mIU/L, though the exact range varies by lab. This means a TSH of 4.4 is "normal" and a TSH of 4.6 is "high." The cutoff has been debated for over two decades.

The case for lowering it: when NHANES III data was reanalyzed excluding individuals with thyroid antibodies, known thyroid disease, and goiter, the 97.5th percentile for TSH was approximately 4.1 in the 20-29 age group and rose with age. Some endocrinologists have argued the upper limit should be 2.5-3.0, particularly for younger adults and women planning pregnancy.

The case for keeping it: lowering the TSH upper limit from 4.5 to 2.5 would reclassify roughly 20% of the population as having thyroid dysfunction, most of whom are asymptomatic and would not benefit from treatment. The societal cost of mass treatment, overdiagnosis, and medication side effects would be enormous.

TSH also naturally increases with age - this may represent normal physiological adaptation rather than subclinical disease. Some evidence suggests mildly elevated TSH in elderly populations is associated with better, not worse, outcomes, implying that treating it could be harmful.

**Competing claims:**

1. The upper limit should be lowered to 2.5-3.0 for younger adults - the current range includes a contaminated reference population with undiagnosed autoimmune thyroiditis, and a TSH of 4.0 in a 25-year-old is not physiologically normal even if it's statistically common
2. The current range of 0.4-4.5 is appropriate for population screening - lowering it would pathologize millions of people without evidence that treating TSH in the 3.0-4.5 range improves outcomes, and would generate an enormous burden of unnecessary levothyroxine prescriptions
3. Age-specific TSH ranges are the right approach - TSH of 3.5 in a 25-year-old should be interpreted differently than TSH of 3.5 in a 75-year-old, and age-stratified upper limits would be more biologically accurate than a single cutoff applied to all adults
4. In pregnancy or pre-conception, a lower TSH target (below 2.5 in the first trimester) is well-supported - the normal range for pregnancy is different from the general population, and thyroid insufficiency during pregnancy carries specific fetal risks that justify a tighter threshold

**Evidence needed:** NHANES and other population TSH distributions in disease-free cohorts, TSH and age relationship studies, clinical outcomes at TSH 2.5-4.5 in different age groups, pregnancy-specific TSH target validation, reclassification impact analyses (how many people would be affected by threshold changes).

## Drug Effects on Thyroid and Reproductive Hormone Tests

Thyroid and reproductive hormone tests are particularly vulnerable to medication interference, both through genuine physiological effects and through assay artifact.

Biotin supplementation at doses above 5 mg daily (common in hair/nail products containing 5,000-10,000 mcg) causes a characteristic artifact on many thyroid immunoassays: falsely low TSH alongside falsely elevated free T4 and free T3. The pattern mimics hyperthyroidism so closely that patients have been started on antithyroid medication for biotin artifact. Troponin, PTH, reproductive hormones, and cortisol are affected by the same interference on many platforms. Stopping biotin for 2-3 days (or up to a week for very high doses) before testing resolves the interference. The FDA has issued safety communications about this.

Lithium alters thyroid function directly. Roughly 20% of long-term lithium users develop hypothyroidism or subclinical hypothyroidism, with TSH rising over months to years. Monitoring every 6-12 months is standard for lithium users.

Amiodarone (antiarrhythmic) contains 37% iodine by weight and produces complex thyroid effects: some patients develop hypothyroidism (excess iodine suppressing thyroid output), others develop hyperthyroidism (amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis, which can be destructive thyroiditis or iodine-induced Graves-like). Amiodarone also alters TFTs directly - it inhibits T4 to T3 conversion, raising rT3 and total T4 while free T4 may be normal or elevated.

Oral contraceptives and estrogen HRT raise thyroxine-binding globulin, which raises total T4 and total T3 without affecting free hormones or TSH. A woman on OCPs with "elevated" total T4 is almost always euthyroid with elevated TBG. Free T4 and TSH are the correct tests in this context. OCPs also raise SHBG (often doubling it), which shifts calculated free testosterone and free estradiol - comparing testosterone results across OCP status requires the full panel with SHBG.

Glucocorticoids suppress TSH transiently (high-dose) and suppress endogenous cortisol and ACTH, which complicates any adrenal axis testing. Opioids suppress gonadotropins (LH, FSH) and downstream sex hormones; chronic opioid use is a common and underrecognized cause of "hypogonadism" in men.

Finasteride and dutasteride reduce PSA by approximately 50% in men. Any PSA result on these medications must be interpreted with that adjustment (typical convention: multiply by 2 to compare against standard reference ranges).

**Competing claims:**

1. Any abnormal thyroid panel should trigger a "biotin check" before workup - asking about biotin supplementation is trivial and prevents a specific, well-characterized diagnostic error that has caused inappropriate treatment
2. Medication review should be mandatory before interpreting any hormone panel - the list of affecting medications is long, but the highest-yield items (biotin, OCPs/HRT, glucocorticoids, lithium, amiodarone, opioids, 5-alpha reductase inhibitors) cover most clinically significant interferences
3. EHR systems should flag active medications known to affect specific hormone tests at the time of ordering or result review - the information exists in the chart but is rarely surfaced at the right moment
4. Patients on chronic medications that affect hormone testing should have their baseline on-treatment values established before any new complaint is evaluated - a "low testosterone" result in a man on chronic opioids isn't a primary testosterone problem; it's the expected physiology of his medication regimen

**Evidence needed:** Biotin interference prevalence in thyroid testing populations, lithium and amiodarone thyroid monitoring outcomes, OCP and hormone testing misinterpretation rates, opioid-induced hypogonadism prevalence and response to discontinuation, medication review impact on endocrine workup appropriateness.

## Can a Single Cortisol Measurement Tell You Anything?

Cortisol follows one of the strongest circadian rhythms of any hormone. It peaks in the early morning (cortisol awakening response, typically 30-45 minutes after waking), declines through the day, and reaches its nadir around midnight. It also spikes acutely in response to stress (physical, psychological, or metabolic), meals, exercise, pain, and illness. Cortisol secretion is pulsatile - even within an hour, levels fluctuate.

A single serum cortisol captures one point on this rapidly moving curve. An 8am cortisol has a predictable expected range and can identify extremes: very low values suggest adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease), very high values suggest Cushing's syndrome. But mid-range values - which is what most people get - are nearly uninterpretable. A cortisol of 12 mcg/dL at 8am could be normal, or it could be blunted depending on the person's individual rhythm and what happened that morning.

Despite this, single cortisol measurements are routinely ordered, reported with reference ranges that don't specify time of collection, and interpreted by patients and sometimes clinicians as meaningful.

**Competing claims:**

1. A single morning cortisol is adequate for ruling out adrenal insufficiency and Cushing's syndrome - these are the clinically important diagnoses, and a normal 8am cortisol reliably excludes both; more complex testing (24-hour urine, salivary cortisol curves, ACTH stimulation test) is reserved for abnormal results
2. A single cortisol measurement is useless for the question most patients actually want answered: "is my cortisol too high from chronic stress?" - this question requires a cortisol rhythm assessment (multiple timepoints, salivary cortisol diurnal curve, or cortisol awakening response), and acting on a random serum cortisol is clinically meaningless
3. Cortisol should rarely be ordered as a screening test in asymptomatic or non-specifically symptomatic patients - the pre-test probability of true adrenal disease is low, the measurement is noisy, and abnormal results trigger expensive and anxiety-producing workups for conditions most patients don't have
4. For the fitness population specifically, cortisol measurement is almost never informative - exercise, sleep timing, stress, and nutrition all affect cortisol acutely, and a post-training or sleep-deprived cortisol draw tells you about that morning, not about chronic cortisol status

**Evidence needed:** Cortisol circadian rhythm variability in healthy populations, diagnostic accuracy of single morning cortisol for adrenal insufficiency and Cushing's, salivary cortisol curve reliability and clinical utility, cortisol and exercise timing studies, false-positive rates from single cortisol measurements in primary care.

## Is "Adrenal Fatigue" Real?

The concept: chronic psychological or physical stress eventually exhausts the adrenal glands, leading to insufficient cortisol production. The result is persistent fatigue, brain fog, exercise intolerance, salt cravings, light-headedness, poor recovery, and immune dysfunction. Functional medicine practitioners and naturopaths diagnose it frequently. Conventional endocrinology uniformly rejects it.

The Endocrine Society's position is clear: "adrenal fatigue" is not a recognized medical diagnosis. True adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) is a serious, rare autoimmune condition with measurably low cortisol that requires hormone replacement. The adrenal glands don't "fatigue" from stress in healthy people - they're remarkably resilient organs with enormous cortisol-producing reserve.

But the concept isn't entirely without physiological basis. HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis dysregulation is a real, studied phenomenon. Chronic stress can alter the cortisol awakening response (blunted or absent morning spike), flatten the diurnal cortisol curve, and change the sensitivity of the HPA feedback loop. These patterns are documented in burnout, PTSD, chronic fatigue syndrome, and prolonged overtraining. The physiology is real. The label "adrenal fatigue" is the problem - it localizes a brain-level regulatory dysfunction to the adrenal glands and implies a fatigue mechanism that doesn't exist.

**Competing claims:**

1. "Adrenal fatigue" is a medically inaccurate label for a real phenomenon - HPA axis dysregulation from chronic stress is well-documented, and dismissing every patient who presents with fatigue, stress-related cortisol changes, and functional impairment because the label is wrong is throwing out the clinical reality with the terminological bathwater
2. The term "adrenal fatigue" is harmful because it implies a specific pathology (exhausted adrenals) that doesn't exist, which misdirects treatment toward adrenal support supplements and cortisol replacement when the actual intervention should target the stressors, sleep, and recovery practices that regulate the HPA axis
3. The "adrenal fatigue" diagnosis is being used to sell supplements (adrenal glandulars, adaptogens, hydrocortisone microdosing) and salivary cortisol testing panels without evidence that these interventions address the underlying problem or improve outcomes compared to addressing sleep, stress, and training load directly
4. Conventional endocrinology's dismissal of adrenal fatigue is too absolute - there is a clinical gap between "Addison's disease" and "everything is fine" that patients with genuine stress-related neuroendocrine dysfunction fall into, and medicine needs better frameworks for this intermediate space

**Evidence needed:** HPA axis studies in chronic stress, burnout, and overtraining populations; cortisol awakening response as a functional biomarker; adaptogen RCTs with cortisol and symptom endpoints; clinical outcomes for patients diagnosed with "adrenal fatigue" under functional medicine vs conventional approaches; salivary cortisol curve diagnostic utility for stress-related HPA dysfunction.

## Estrogen in Men: When Does It Matter?

Men produce estrogen. This is normal and necessary. Estradiol (E2) in men comes primarily from peripheral aromatization of testosterone in adipose tissue, brain, bone, and other tissues. Estrogen contributes to bone density (estrogen deficiency causes osteoporosis in men), cardiovascular function, brain health, lipid metabolism, and libido. A man with no estrogen would be in serious trouble.

In the testosterone replacement therapy world, estrogen management has become an obsession. Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole) are frequently co-prescribed with TRT to "prevent estrogen from getting too high." The logic: exogenous testosterone can aromatize to estradiol, and elevated estrogen can cause gynecomastia, water retention, and mood changes. Some clinics prescribe AIs preemptively to all TRT patients.

The evidence increasingly suggests this is mostly harmful. Crashing estrogen in men causes joint pain, bone loss, mood disturbance, worsened lipid profiles, and may increase cardiovascular risk. The therapeutic window for estrogen in men is wider than the optimization community assumes - most men on TRT tolerate their natural estrogen levels without problems. Truly symptomatic high estrogen (progressive gynecomastia, significant water retention) may warrant intervention, but routine AI use is being questioned even by clinicians who prescribe TRT.

**Competing claims:**

1. Routine aromatase inhibitor use with TRT is unnecessary and harmful for most men - estrogen is essential for male health, the threshold for symptomatic high estrogen is higher than commonly feared, and AIs worsen bone density, lipid profiles, mood, and possibly cardiovascular risk
2. Some men on TRT do develop genuinely elevated estrogen with symptoms, and short-term or low-dose AI use is appropriate in these specific cases - the problem isn't AIs themselves but their routine use without evidence of estrogen excess
3. Estradiol testing in men on TRT should be standard to guide management - but the target should be "avoid symptoms and extremes" rather than chasing a specific number, because the evidence for an optimal E2 level in men is weak and varies by assay
4. The fixation on estrogen management in TRT reflects the broader "optimize every number" culture rather than evidence-based medicine - most side effects attributed to estrogen in TRT patients are more likely related to dose, hematocrit, or expectations than to estradiol levels

**Evidence needed:** AI use in TRT and bone density outcomes, estrogen and cardiovascular markers in men on TRT with and without AIs, estradiol threshold studies for gynecomastia and other symptoms, clinical outcomes comparing TRT alone vs TRT plus AI, assay-specific estradiol reference ranges for men.

## HRT for Cardiovascular Prevention: The WHI Reversal

Postmenopausal hormone therapy was one of the most widely prescribed interventions in American medicine by the 1990s. The case was built from decades of observational data. The Nurses' Health Study suggested that women on HRT had roughly 40% lower cardiovascular event rates. Mechanistic data was supportive: estrogen improved lipid profiles (LDL down, HDL up), enhanced endothelial function, reduced LDL oxidation, and had favorable effects on coagulation markers. HRT seemed to be protective, the biology was plausible, and the observational data was consistent. Treating menopausal symptoms AND preventing heart disease in one intervention was a best-of-both-worlds proposition.

The Women's Health Initiative changed the story. WHI was a large randomized trial (over 27,000 women total across the estrogen-alone and estrogen-plus-progestin arms) designed to test the observational prediction. The combined estrogen-plus-progestin arm was stopped early in 2002 after 5.2 years of follow-up because the intervention INCREASED cardiovascular events (29% increase in MI), stroke (41%), breast cancer (26%), and venous thromboembolism. The estrogen-alone arm (in women without a uterus) was also stopped early in 2004 for increased stroke. WHIMS, the memory substudy, found increased dementia risk. The intervention that was supposed to prevent disease caused it.

The scientific and clinical fallout was enormous. HRT prescribing dropped by roughly 50% within two years. The hypothesis that HRT protected the cardiovascular system was rejected, at least for older women starting HRT many years after menopause. The biomarker improvements - the LDL, HDL, and endothelial function changes - hadn't prevented events.

Subsequent analysis complicated this conclusion. The "timing hypothesis" proposed that HRT started within 10 years of menopause in younger women (under 60) might have different effects than HRT started in older women (the WHI population's average age was 63). KEEPS and ELITE tested this directly using surrogate markers (carotid intima-media thickness, coronary calcium) and found more favorable results in early postmenopausal starters, though neither trial had cardiovascular events as the primary endpoint. The current nuanced view: HRT for symptom management in younger early-postmenopausal women is reasonable, but HRT specifically for cardiovascular prevention in older women is not supported.

The WHI episode is among the most important examples in medicine of observational data plus mechanistic plausibility plus biomarker improvement not translating to outcome benefit. It also illustrates how hard it is to unwind a widely adopted practice - HRT for "general health" in older women was standard until the trial forced its reversal.

**Competing claims:**

1. The WHI decisively settled that HRT is not cardiovascular protection in older women and that observational-plus-mechanistic evidence was insufficient to establish it - this should be the default lens for interpreting any hormone-based cardiovascular prevention claim, including male testosterone optimization in older men
2. The "timing hypothesis" means WHI shouldn't be extrapolated to all HRT use - younger women starting HRT near menopause may benefit, and the WHI's population-specific findings have been overgeneralized to dissuade appropriate use in the target population for symptom management
3. The biomarker improvements (lipids, inflammation, endothelial function) seen with HRT were real but irrelevant to outcomes - this is a case study in how biomarker improvement can coexist with outcome harm, and it should make clinicians skeptical of "biomarker-better, therefore better" reasoning in other hormone interventions
4. The male equivalent question - whether TRT in older men prevents cardiovascular events, which was the original hope before TRAVERSE - hasn't been answered affirmatively either; TRAVERSE showed TRT is not cardiovascular harmful at physiologic doses in hypogonadal men, but "not harmful" is not the same as "preventive"

**Evidence needed:** WHI long-term follow-up and cause-specific analyses, KEEPS and ELITE trial cardiovascular-event data (when available), timing hypothesis validation in event-driven trials, observational-to-trial reversal pattern analysis across hormone interventions, HRT and cognitive outcome data reconciliation.

## IGF-1: Performance Fuel or Longevity Liability?

Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) is the primary mediator of growth hormone's effects throughout the body. Higher IGF-1 promotes muscle protein synthesis, bone growth, tissue repair, collagen synthesis, and recovery from training. It's the anabolic signal that turns GH secretion into physical adaptation. Athletes and bodybuilders want it higher.

But IGF-1 is also a growth signal for every cell, including potentially cancerous ones. Higher IGF-1 is associated with increased risk of several cancers (colorectal, breast, prostate) in observational data. And some of the most striking longevity data comes from organisms and humans with very low IGF-1.

The Laron syndrome population - people with growth hormone receptor deficiency who produce almost no IGF-1 - have near-zero cancer incidence and may have extended lifespans (though the data is from a small Ecuadorian cohort and the longevity evidence isn't definitive). Caloric restriction, which extends lifespan in many animal models, consistently reduces IGF-1. GH/IGF-1 pathway mutations extend lifespan in worms, flies, and mice. The longevity research community sees lower IGF-1 as potentially protective.

This is possibly the sharpest direct conflict between performance optimization and longevity optimization in all of biomarker science.

**Competing claims:**

1. Higher IGF-1 supports muscle growth, tissue repair, bone density, and recovery, and should be maintained or optimized in physically active people - the cancer association is observational and confounded, and the longevity data is from model organisms and rare genetic syndromes that don't translate to normal human aging
2. Lower IGF-1 may reduce cancer risk and slow aging through reduced cellular proliferation and enhanced autophagy - the evidence from Laron syndrome, caloric restriction, and animal models is consistent enough to suggest that chronically elevated IGF-1 is a trade-off, not a free benefit
3. The dose likely matters: there's probably a range where IGF-1 supports function without excessive growth signaling, and both very low and very high levels are suboptimal - the question is where the sweet spot is, and we don't have enough human outcome data to define it
4. IGF-1 is being used as a proxy for growth hormone status in anti-aging and longevity medicine, but the relationship between GH, IGF-1, and lifespan in normal humans is far less clear than in model organisms - extrapolating from C. elegans and GH-deficient mice to human IGF-1 optimization is premature

**Evidence needed:** IGF-1 and cancer risk Mendelian randomization studies, Laron syndrome cohort long-term follow-up, IGF-1 levels and all-cause mortality dose-response data in normal populations, exercise and IGF-1 longitudinal studies, caloric restriction and IGF-1 in humans (CALERIE trial data), muscle mass and function outcomes at different IGF-1 levels in aging populations.
