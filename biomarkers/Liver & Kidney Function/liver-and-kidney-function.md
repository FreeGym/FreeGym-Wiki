# Liver & Kidney Function

The most debated questions about liver enzymes, kidney markers, and their interpretation - especially in physically active populations where these markers are systematically misread.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Why Are Your Liver Enzymes Elevated After Training?

ALT (alanine aminotransferase) and AST (aspartate aminotransferase) are routinely called "liver enzymes." The label implies they come from the liver. This is misleading.

AST is present in high concentrations in skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, red blood cells, kidneys, and brain - not just the liver. ALT, traditionally considered more liver-specific, is also found in skeletal muscle, though at lower concentrations. When muscle cells are damaged or stressed - as they are during resistance training, eccentric exercise, or high-volume endurance work - both enzymes leak into the bloodstream.

A person who did heavy squats or deadlifts 24-48 hours before a blood draw can easily show ALT of 50-80 U/L and AST of 70-120 U/L. CK (creatine kinase) may be in the hundreds or thousands. All will be flagged on the lab report. The automated interpretation will suggest liver pathology. In some cases, this triggers a hepatitis panel, liver ultrasound, or gastroenterology referral - all for a physiological response to exercise that resolves within 48-72 hours.

This is not a rare scenario. It happens routinely to people who exercise regularly and get blood work without being told to rest before the draw.

**Competing claims:**

1. Exercise-induced enzyme elevation is a well-documented, benign, transient phenomenon that should be recognized as the most common cause of elevated ALT/AST in young, healthy, active individuals - the clinical default for elevated liver enzymes in someone who trains should be "exercise effect" until proven otherwise, not the reverse
2. Exercise elevation is real but shouldn't be used as an automatic excuse to dismiss elevated enzymes - some active people genuinely have liver disease (NAFLD is common even in lean individuals), and assuming every elevation is from training can delay diagnosis of real pathology
3. The solution is standardization: patients should be instructed to avoid intense exercise for 48-72 hours before blood work that includes liver enzymes, the same way they're instructed to fast for lipid panels - this would eliminate most false positives without dismissing real elevations
4. Lab reports should include a flag or footnote for ALT/AST noting that recent exercise can elevate these markers, and requesting clinical correlation - the current system, which flags the number with no context, is the root cause of unnecessary workups

**Evidence needed:** ALT/AST elevation magnitude and duration after different exercise types and intensities, prevalence of exercise-induced enzyme elevation in routine clinical blood work, rates of unnecessary liver workups triggered by exercise-related elevation, studies on rest intervals before blood draws and false-positive reduction.

## How to Tell If Elevated Enzymes Are From Muscle or Liver

The distinction between muscle-derived and liver-derived enzyme elevation is clinically critical and usually simple to make. The key is GGT.

GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase) is a genuinely liver-specific enzyme. Skeletal muscle does not produce or release meaningful amounts of GGT. When liver enzymes are elevated, checking GGT immediately clarifies the source:

- ALT/AST elevated, GGT normal: the elevation is almost certainly from skeletal muscle (exercise, rhabdomyolysis, muscular dystrophy, or other muscle pathology). The liver is fine.
- ALT/AST elevated, GGT elevated: the liver is likely involved. Further workup is warranted (viral hepatitis, alcohol-related liver disease, NAFLD, medication toxicity, bile duct pathology).

CK further confirms muscle origin - if CK is also elevated alongside ALT/AST with normal GGT, the case is essentially closed.

This pattern recognition is straightforward, inexpensive (GGT is a standard lab test), and would prevent the majority of unnecessary liver investigations in active people. Yet it's inconsistently applied because the muscle-enzyme connection isn't emphasized in standard medical training and because many liver panels don't include GGT by default.

**Competing claims:**

1. GGT should be included in every liver panel by default - its ability to distinguish muscle from liver as the source of ALT/AST elevation is simple, cheap, and would prevent unnecessary workups at scale; the current practice of ordering ALT/AST without GGT is an incomplete assessment
2. The ALT/AST/GGT pattern is a useful first step but not definitive - some liver conditions can present with elevated ALT/AST and near-normal GGT (early NAFLD, some viral hepatitis), so a normal GGT doesn't completely rule out liver pathology, especially in patients with risk factors
3. CK should be part of the evaluation whenever ALT/AST elevation is found in an active person - CK is the most specific marker of muscle damage, and adding it to the workup alongside GGT creates a clear diagnostic algorithm: high CK + normal GGT = muscle; normal CK + high GGT = liver; both elevated = investigate further
4. The real problem is not which tests to order but clinical context - if physicians routinely asked "did you exercise in the last 48 hours?" before interpreting liver enzymes, most unnecessary workups could be avoided without changing the test panel at all

**Evidence needed:** GGT sensitivity and specificity for distinguishing muscle vs liver sources of ALT/AST elevation, studies on adding GGT to standard liver panels and impact on workup rates, CK/ALT/AST/GGT algorithms validated in athletic populations, survey data on clinician awareness of exercise-induced enzyme elevation.

## ALT Thresholds: Are the "Normal" Limits Too High?

The upper limit of normal (ULN) for ALT varies by laboratory, typically set at 35-55 U/L for men and 30-45 U/L for women. These limits are derived from reference populations that include people with undiagnosed liver disease - most importantly, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, which affects approximately 25-30% of the adult population and is often asymptomatic.

When researchers recalculated ALT upper limits after excluding individuals with known liver disease risk factors (obesity, diabetes, excessive alcohol, viral hepatitis), the thresholds dropped substantially. Proposed revised limits: approximately 30 U/L for men and 19 U/L for women. Under these tighter thresholds, a "normal" ALT of 40 in a sedentary, overweight man might actually represent early hepatic steatosis or hepatocellular stress that the current reference range misses.

But applying these stricter limits creates a paradox for the fitness population. An active person with ALT of 35 from yesterday's training session would be flagged as abnormal under the revised thresholds, even though the elevation has nothing to do with their liver. Tighter ALT limits improve detection of liver disease in sedentary populations but increase false positives in active populations.

**Competing claims:**

1. The current ALT upper limits are too high because they're contaminated by undiagnosed NAFLD in the reference population - lowering the threshold to 30/19 would catch liver disease earlier in the majority of the population that is sedentary, which is the group most at risk for NAFLD and its progression to fibrosis and cirrhosis
2. Lowering ALT thresholds would dramatically increase false positives and unnecessary workups, especially in active populations - the clinical system is not equipped to handle the volume of "abnormal" results this would generate, and the net effect may be more harm (unnecessary testing, anxiety) than benefit (earlier NAFLD detection)
3. Population-specific thresholds are the right approach: lower thresholds for sedentary/metabolically at-risk populations, and acknowledgment of exercise-induced elevation in active populations - but implementing dual thresholds requires clinical context that lab reports currently don't capture
4. The ALT threshold debate is secondary to the larger problem: NAFLD detection should not rely on ALT at all, because ALT has poor sensitivity for fatty liver even at lower thresholds - imaging-based screening (ultrasound, FibroScan) is fundamentally better for a disease defined by liver fat, not enzyme levels

**Evidence needed:** ALT distribution studies in disease-free, healthy-weight, non-exercising populations, NAFLD detection rates at current vs revised ALT thresholds, false-positive rates at lower thresholds in athletic populations, cost-effectiveness of lowered ALT thresholds vs imaging-based NAFLD screening, comparison of ALT threshold approaches across international guidelines.

## Can Liver Enzymes Detect Fatty Liver Disease?

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - increasingly called metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) - is the most common liver disease in the world, affecting an estimated 25-30% of the global adult population. It ranges from simple steatosis (fat accumulation) to steatohepatitis (NASH/MASH, with inflammation and cell death) to fibrosis and cirrhosis. It's driven primarily by insulin resistance, obesity, and metabolic syndrome.

Given how common NAFLD is, you'd expect liver enzymes to catch it reliably. They don't.

ALT and AST can be completely normal in people with significant hepatic steatosis. Studies using imaging or biopsy as the reference standard show that a substantial proportion of people with NAFLD - including some with NASH and early fibrosis - have ALT within the standard reference range. The sensitivity of ALT for detecting NAFLD is estimated at 40-60% depending on the threshold used. This means a "normal liver panel" provides false reassurance about liver health for millions of people.

The disconnect exists because ALT reflects hepatocyte damage and membrane leakage, not fat content. A liver can be full of fat without acute cell death, producing normal enzymes. By the time ALT rises in NAFLD, the disease may have already progressed to the inflammatory stage.

**Competing claims:**

1. Normal liver enzymes do not rule out NAFLD - clinicians and patients who use a normal ALT as evidence of liver health are being misled by a test that was never designed to detect fat accumulation; screening for NAFLD in metabolically at-risk individuals requires imaging, not blood tests
2. While ALT is insensitive for early NAFLD, it still has value in staging - consistently elevated ALT in someone with known risk factors suggests active hepatocellular injury (NASH rather than simple steatosis) and may help identify those who need more aggressive intervention or monitoring
3. FibroScan (vibration-controlled transient elastography) and the FIB-4 index (calculated from age, ALT, AST, and platelets) should be standard screening tools for NAFLD in at-risk populations - they're more accurate than ALT alone and can identify fibrosis, which is the stage that determines long-term outcomes
4. The push for NAFLD screening needs to be weighed against the lack of approved pharmacotherapy - detecting NAFLD at scale is only useful if there are effective treatments to offer, and currently the primary interventions (weight loss, exercise, metabolic improvement) are the same recommendations these patients are already receiving

**Evidence needed:** ALT sensitivity and specificity for NAFLD by threshold, FibroScan and FIB-4 accuracy for staging in primary care settings, NAFLD prevalence in populations with normal ALT, cost-effectiveness of imaging-based NAFLD screening in metabolically at-risk populations, treatment outcome studies to justify screening.

## GGT: The Most Underrated Marker on a Liver Panel?

GGT is a membrane-bound enzyme concentrated in the liver, bile ducts, and kidneys. It rises with alcohol use (one of the most sensitive markers of heavy drinking), bile duct obstruction, liver disease, and certain medications. On a standard liver panel, it plays second fiddle to ALT and AST.

But GGT may carry more information than its traditional role suggests. A growing body of evidence links elevated GGT - even within the conventional reference range - to cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and all-cause mortality. The associations are independent of alcohol use and persist after adjusting for traditional risk factors.

The proposed mechanism: GGT is involved in glutathione metabolism and may reflect systemic oxidative stress. Higher GGT may indicate that the body is under greater oxidative and metabolic burden, independent of overt liver disease. If this is correct, GGT functions as a broader marker of metabolic health rather than a narrow liver enzyme.

**Competing claims:**

1. GGT is an underutilized marker that provides dual information: liver-specific enzyme status (distinguishing liver from muscle elevation in ALT/AST) AND an independent signal of cardiometabolic risk and oxidative stress - it deserves more clinical attention than it currently receives
2. The cardiovascular and mortality associations with GGT, while statistically robust, don't prove causation and don't yet have a clear clinical application - knowing that GGT is mildly elevated doesn't change management beyond addressing the same lifestyle factors (alcohol, weight, metabolic health) already being recommended
3. GGT is most valuable as part of a pattern: elevated GGT with elevated ALT suggests liver pathology, elevated GGT with normal ALT may reflect alcohol use or metabolic stress, and normal GGT with elevated ALT/AST strongly suggests muscle origin - the pattern matters more than the individual number
4. GGT's sensitivity to alcohol makes it uncomfortable for clinical use - patients may be reluctant to discuss results, and clinicians may avoid ordering it to sidestep the alcohol conversation; this social barrier, not clinical reasoning, explains why GGT is underused

**Evidence needed:** GGT and cardiovascular disease meta-analyses with adjustment for alcohol and metabolic factors, GGT as oxidative stress marker mechanistic studies, GGT trajectory and metabolic disease prediction, clinical utility studies of adding GGT to standard liver panels, GGT in non-drinkers and cardiometabolic outcomes.

## Creatinine and eGFR in Muscular People: Falsely Abnormal?

Serum creatinine is the standard marker of kidney function. It's a waste product of creatine phosphate metabolism in skeletal muscle, filtered by the kidneys and excreted at a roughly constant rate. When kidneys fail, creatinine accumulates. Higher serum creatinine signals worse kidney function.

The problem: creatinine production is proportional to muscle mass. More muscle means more creatine phosphate turnover means more creatinine generated - regardless of kidney function. A lean 90kg man with substantial muscle mass produces significantly more creatinine daily than a 60kg sedentary woman. His "baseline" serum creatinine is higher not because his kidneys work less efficiently, but because they're clearing a larger load.

eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate) is calculated from serum creatinine using equations (CKD-EPI or MDRD) that adjust for age, sex, and sometimes race. They do not adjust for muscle mass or body composition. The equations were derived from populations that include few muscular, athletic, or strength-trained individuals. In these populations, the equations systematically underestimate kidney function.

The clinical consequence: a muscular man with serum creatinine of 1.3-1.4 mg/dL may show an eGFR of 55-65 mL/min - technically stage 2-3a chronic kidney disease. His kidneys are fine. His muscles are just producing more creatinine than the equation expects. This can trigger nephrology referrals, insurance implications, restricted medication choices, and unnecessary anxiety.

**Competing claims:**

1. Creatinine-based eGFR systematically misclassifies muscular individuals as having reduced kidney function - this is a known limitation of the equations and should be flagged on lab reports for patients with high muscle mass or when creatinine is elevated without other signs of kidney disease
2. The misclassification is clinically manageable - an experienced clinician will recognize that a muscular, healthy young man with creatinine of 1.3 and no proteinuria, normal blood pressure, and normal urinalysis does not have kidney disease; the problem is interpretation, not the test
3. Cystatin C-based eGFR should be used whenever creatinine-based eGFR appears reduced in a muscular individual - it resolves the muscle mass confound directly and is the only way to confidently distinguish high creatinine from high production vs impaired clearance
4. eGFR equations should incorporate a body composition variable (lean mass, BMI category, or self-reported activity level) to reduce misclassification - the technology to estimate body composition is increasingly accessible, and failing to account for it in kidney function estimates is an avoidable systematic error

**Evidence needed:** eGFR misclassification rates in athletic and muscular populations, creatinine-based vs cystatin C-based eGFR concordance in strength-trained individuals, body composition-adjusted eGFR equation proposals, clinical impact of false CKD diagnosis in healthy muscular patients (insurance, referrals, medication restrictions), prevalence of true kidney disease in flagged athletic populations.

## Does Creatine Supplementation Fake Kidney Disease on Lab Work?

Creatine monohydrate is one of the most well-studied and widely used supplements in sports nutrition. Standard dosing is 3-5g per day for maintenance. It increases intramuscular creatine phosphate stores, supporting short-duration high-intensity exercise performance. The safety profile is well-established across hundreds of studies.

Creatinine is the spontaneous, non-enzymatic breakdown product of creatine. More creatine in the body means more creatinine is produced. Creatine supplementation reliably raises serum creatinine by 10-30%, depending on dose, muscle mass, and individual metabolism. This creatinine increase is not a sign of kidney stress - it's a predictable consequence of having more substrate.

But on a lab report, the creatinine rise looks identical to impaired kidney function. eGFR drops correspondingly. A person taking 5g of creatine daily may show serum creatinine of 1.4-1.5 mg/dL and an eGFR in the 50s-60s. If the clinician doesn't know the patient takes creatine - or doesn't understand the relationship - this triggers a kidney disease workup.

The irony: creatine is safe for the kidneys in healthy individuals (confirmed in multiple systematic reviews), yet it produces lab results that look like kidney disease. The supplement that's safest for kidneys is the one most likely to fake kidney damage on paper.

**Competing claims:**

1. Creatine-induced creatinine elevation is a well-characterized, benign artifact that should be universally known in clinical medicine - the fact that it still triggers kidney workups reflects a failure of medical education and supplement literacy among clinicians
2. While the effect is usually benign, creatine supplementation in individuals with pre-existing kidney disease has not been studied extensively enough to guarantee safety - advising caution in CKD patients is prudent even if the evidence in healthy individuals is reassuring
3. Patients taking creatine should inform their physician before any blood draw, and physicians should ask about supplement use when interpreting kidney markers - the communication failure is bilateral, and solving it doesn't require changing the test, just the clinical conversation
4. Cystatin C should be used to assess kidney function in anyone taking creatine who has flagged creatinine - it bypasses the confound entirely and gives a clean estimate of kidney function independent of creatine intake or muscle mass

**Evidence needed:** Creatine supplementation and creatinine elevation dose-response studies, systematic reviews of creatine and kidney function in healthy populations, creatine safety data in CKD populations (limited existing data), cystatin C vs creatinine eGFR in creatine users, physician awareness surveys on creatine and creatinine relationship.

## Cystatin C: A Better Kidney Marker for Active People?

Cystatin C is a small protein (13 kDa) produced at a relatively constant rate by all nucleated cells. It's freely filtered by the glomerulus, reabsorbed and catabolized by the tubules, and not secreted. Unlike creatinine, its production is not influenced by muscle mass, diet, protein intake, creatine supplementation, sex, or race in any clinically significant way.

eGFR calculated from cystatin C (eGFR-cysC) or from the combined creatinine-cystatin C equation (eGFR-cr-cysC) is more accurate than creatinine-based eGFR alone, particularly in populations where creatinine production is confounded: muscular individuals, athletes, high-protein dieters, creatine users, elderly adults with low muscle mass (where creatinine-based eGFR overestimates function), and people with extremes of body composition.

The 2021 CKD-EPI equations include a combined creatinine-cystatin C option, which is considered the most accurate eGFR estimate across populations. Cystatin C is particularly valuable as a "tiebreaker" - when creatinine-based eGFR looks abnormal in someone where muscle mass confounding is plausible, cystatin C can confirm or refute true kidney impairment.

**Competing claims:**

1. Cystatin C should be standard in any patient where creatinine-based eGFR is borderline or discordant with clinical context - muscular patients, creatine users, elderly patients with very low muscle mass, and anyone where body composition likely confounds creatinine; the additional cost (~$15-30) is trivial compared to the cost of unnecessary nephrology referrals and workups
2. Cystatin C is not a perfect marker - it can be affected by thyroid dysfunction (hyperthyroidism raises it, hypothyroidism lowers it), high-dose corticosteroids, obesity, inflammation, and possibly smoking; it's better than creatinine for muscle mass confounding but not free of its own confounders
3. The combined creatinine-cystatin C eGFR equation is the most accurate estimate and should be used whenever both values are available - it leverages the strengths of both markers and reduces the error from either alone
4. Making cystatin C widely available requires systemic change - many labs don't offer it, some insurance plans don't cover it routinely, and most primary care physicians are unfamiliar with ordering or interpreting it; the barrier is infrastructure and education, not evidence

**Evidence needed:** Creatinine vs cystatin C vs combined eGFR accuracy studies in athletic populations, cystatin C confounders quantification (thyroid, steroids, inflammation), cost-effectiveness of routine cystatin C in populations at high risk of creatinine confounding, clinical pathway studies (cystatin C reducing unnecessary nephrology referrals), lab availability surveys.

## NSAIDs, Supplements, and Medications That Distort Kidney Markers

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, and others) reduce glomerular filtration through prostaglandin inhibition. Prostaglandins normally vasodilate the afferent arteriole of the glomerulus, maintaining filtration pressure. When NSAIDs block this, afferent dilation is reduced, GFR drops, and serum creatinine rises. The effect is modest in healthy kidneys but can be dramatic in patients with preexisting kidney disease, heart failure, cirrhosis, volume depletion, or those on ACE inhibitors or ARBs (which dilate the efferent arteriole).

A patient who takes ibuprofen daily for chronic pain and shows an eGFR of 55 doesn't necessarily have chronic kidney disease - they may have pharmacologically-induced hyperfiltration suppression that reverses within days of discontinuation. Distinguishing reversible NSAID effect from structural kidney damage requires a trial of NSAID cessation and repeat testing.

Other medications that distort kidney markers without causing actual kidney injury:

- ACE inhibitors and ARBs lower GFR in some contexts (single-functioning kidney, bilateral renal artery stenosis, volume depletion) and can raise creatinine; a modest rise (up to 30%) after starting these drugs is expected and not a reason to stop.
- Trimethoprim (in TMP-SMX) inhibits tubular creatinine secretion without affecting GFR, producing an apparent creatinine rise without real kidney injury.
- Cimetidine similarly blocks creatinine secretion and falsely raises creatinine.
- Cephalosporins can interfere with some creatinine assays.
- Contrast agents (iodinated CT contrast, gadolinium) cause transient changes in kidney function that may or may not represent real injury.

Supplements and foods also matter:

- Creatine monohydrate (covered in depth earlier) raises creatinine without affecting kidney function.
- High protein intake acutely raises BUN and modestly raises creatinine.
- Cooked meat contains creatinine itself; a large meat meal the evening before a blood draw can raise serum creatinine 10-15%.

**Competing claims:**

1. Before diagnosing chronic kidney disease based on an isolated eGFR below 60, a structured medication and supplement review should be performed - NSAIDs, trimethoprim, cimetidine, and creatine supplementation collectively account for a substantial fraction of "new CKD" findings that aren't actually kidney disease
2. The reversibility of medication-induced creatinine changes makes a discontinuation trial the highest-yield diagnostic step for borderline eGFR findings - faster and cheaper than ordering cystatin C or imaging, and directly answers the clinical question
3. Patients on chronic NSAID therapy should have baseline kidney markers documented and monitored periodically - the population risk-benefit for chronic NSAID use factors in kidney function, and starting from unknown baseline makes this assessment impossible
4. The clinical standard "diet before draw" doesn't adequately address the meat consumption and creatinine issue - a 24-hour food log before kidney testing would be more informative than the current practice, but is rarely obtained

**Evidence needed:** NSAID-induced eGFR change reversibility studies, trimethoprim and cimetidine creatinine effect magnitudes, cooked meat consumption and pre-draw serum creatinine, medication review impact on CKD diagnosis accuracy, structured discontinuation trial outcomes for borderline eGFR.

## Does High Protein Intake Damage Healthy Kidneys?

High protein intake increases glomerular filtration rate. The kidneys filter more. Serum creatinine and BUN (blood urea nitrogen) rise. On paper, it can look like kidney stress. The question is whether this hyperfiltration - the kidneys working harder - causes progressive damage over time, or whether it's a normal, sustainable adaptive response.

In people with pre-existing chronic kidney disease, the evidence supports protein restriction. High protein intake in CKD can accelerate the decline in kidney function, and moderate protein restriction (0.6-0.8 g/kg/day) is part of standard CKD management. The damaged kidney cannot safely handle the increased filtration load.

In people with healthy kidneys, the evidence is consistently reassuring. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses of high-protein diets (up to 2.0-3.5 g/kg/day in some studies, sustained over months to years) show no adverse effect on kidney function in healthy adults. GFR increases acutely with high protein intake, but this hyperfiltration does not progress to kidney damage in healthy tissue. The kidney has enormous functional reserve and handles the increased load without structural consequences.

Yet the "protein damages kidneys" claim persists in popular health media and even some clinical settings, partly because the CKD evidence gets extrapolated to healthy populations, and partly because rising creatinine and BUN on lab work look alarming without context.

**Competing claims:**

1. High protein intake is safe for healthy kidneys - the evidence from systematic reviews is clear, the hyperfiltration is physiological and non-damaging in healthy tissue, and protein restriction should only be recommended in documented CKD, not in healthy people pursuing muscle growth or athletic performance
2. The "safe for healthy kidneys" conclusion applies to the study durations we have (months to a few years) - we don't have multi-decade data on sustained intakes of 2.5+ g/kg/day, and lifelong high-protein diets may have effects that shorter studies can't detect
3. The risk isn't binary - some individuals may have undiagnosed subclinical kidney impairment, reduced nephron number (from prematurity, aging, or prior injury), or genetic susceptibility that makes high protein intake more risky for them specifically; "safe for healthy kidneys" assumes you know your kidneys are healthy
4. BUN and creatinine elevation from high protein intake should be expected and communicated to patients - rather than triggering concern, these labs should be contextualized with protein intake information, and clinicians should ask about diet before interpreting kidney markers

**Evidence needed:** Long-term (5+ year) high-protein diet and kidney function studies, meta-analyses of protein intake and eGFR change in healthy adults, protein intake in populations with reduced nephron number or subclinical CKD, BUN and creatinine dose-response with protein intake for establishing expected ranges, kidney outcomes in lifelong high-protein populations (e.g., bodybuilders, high-meat-consuming cultures).
