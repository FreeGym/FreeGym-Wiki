## Is LDL Cholesterol Causal in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease?

For **atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease** - coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, ischemic stroke from atherosclerosis, and peripheral artery disease - the strongest answer is: **LDL is causal, but LDL is not the whole risk model.** ([Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962))

A better framing is not "LDL versus inflammation" or "LDL versus metabolic health." It is: **apoB-containing particles, especially LDL particles, are causal inputs into atherosclerosis; inflammation, insulin resistance, blood pressure, smoking, genetics, thrombosis, and lifetime exposure determine how much risk that LDL burden becomes in a given person.** ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

## What LDL is and is not

LDL-C is the amount of cholesterol carried inside LDL particles. It is not exactly the same thing as LDL particle number, and it is not exactly the same thing as apoB. ApoB is often a closer measure of the number of atherogenic particles, because each LDL, VLDL remnant, IDL, and Lp(a) particle typically carries one apoB molecule. Multivariable Mendelian-randomization work suggests that, when LDL-C, triglycerides, and apoB are modeled together, **apoB is the lipoprotein measure most robustly associated with coronary heart disease risk.** ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962))

The mechanistic model is not simply "cholesterol in the blood is bad." ApoB-containing particles small enough to cross the arterial endothelium can enter the artery wall. LDL is especially important because it is abundant and carries cholesterol into the arterial wall. When retained there, modified LDL and other apoB particles can trigger inflammatory and immune responses, foam-cell formation, and plaque growth. ([Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962))

That distinction matters because someone can have a "normal" LDL-C but a high number of apoB particles, especially in insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, high triglycerides, or small dense LDL patterns. In those states, LDL-C can understate atherogenic particle burden. ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Mottillo et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2010.05.034))

## The genetic evidence

The genetic evidence strongly supports causality. Familial hypercholesterolemia is the natural high-LDL experiment: lifelong elevation of LDL from birth markedly increases premature atherosclerotic disease risk. The mirror-image experiment also exists: people with genetic variants that lower LDL from early life have lower coronary risk. The European Atherosclerosis Society consensus concluded that genetic, epidemiologic, mechanistic, and intervention evidence establishes LDL as a cause of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. ([Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962))

PCSK9 loss-of-function variants are one of the clearest examples. In the ARIC cohort, naturally lower LDL due to PCSK9 variants was associated with substantially lower coronary heart disease risk. This was important because it predicted, before PCSK9 drugs were widely used, that lowering LDL through this pathway should reduce events. ([Cohen et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa054013))

Mendelian-randomization studies also show that genetic variants associated with lower LDL-C through different biological pathways tend to associate with lower coronary heart disease risk in proportion to the LDL-C difference. A 2x2 factorial Mendelian-randomization study of NPC1L1 and HMGCR variants found that lower LDL-C through either pathway, or both together, was associated with lower coronary risk in a way consistent with the absolute LDL-C difference. ([Ference et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2015.02.020))

The key lesson from genetics is exposure time. Lifelong lower LDL appears to produce larger risk reductions per unit LDL-C than starting drug therapy later in life, because atherosclerosis reflects cumulative exposure over decades. ([Ference et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2012.09.017); [Domanski et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.07.059))

## The randomized-trial evidence

Randomized trials show that lowering LDL reduces atherosclerotic cardiovascular events. The Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' meta-analysis of 26 statin trials, including about 170,000 participants, found that each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL-C was associated with about a **22 percent proportional reduction in major vascular events.** ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2810%2961350-5))

In people at lower short-term risk, the same collaboration found similar relative risk reductions but smaller absolute benefits. In participants with a 5-year major vascular event risk below 10 percent, each 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction produced about **11 fewer major vascular events per 1,000 people over 5 years.** That is why statins can be strongly evidence-based while still having modest absolute benefit in low-risk primary prevention. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2812%2960367-5))

A JAMA meta-analysis comparing different LDL-lowering interventions found that statins and nonstatin therapies acting primarily through LDL-receptor upregulation showed similar risk reduction per unit LDL-C lowering. Across 49 trials and 312,175 participants, the relative risk of major vascular events was about 0.77 per 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction for statins and similar for established nonstatin therapies. This does not mean every LDL-C-changing intervention is equivalent; off-target effects and whether atherogenic apoB particle burden is actually reduced still matter. ([Silverman et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2016.13985))

Ezetimibe added to statin therapy after acute coronary syndrome reduced events in IMPROVE-IT, but the absolute benefit was modest: about a 2 percentage-point reduction in the primary endpoint over long follow-up. This trial was funded by Merck, so it should be interpreted with that conflict in mind; its findings are more persuasive because they align with genetic evidence and broader LDL-lowering trial meta-analyses. ([Cannon et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1410489); [Silverman et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2016.13985))

PCSK9 inhibitor trials also support LDL causality. In FOURIER, evolocumab lowered LDL substantially and reduced the primary cardiovascular endpoint from 11.3 percent to 9.8 percent over a median 2.2 years. This trial was funded by Amgen. In ODYSSEY OUTCOMES, alirocumab after acute coronary syndrome reduced the primary endpoint from 11.1 percent to 9.5 percent over a median 2.8 years; this trial was funded by Sanofi and Regeneron. ([Sabatine et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1615664); [Schwartz et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1801174))

VESALIUS-CV later extended evolocumab evidence to high-risk patients without a previous myocardial infarction or stroke, showing a lower risk of first major cardiovascular events in patients with atherosclerosis or diabetes. This trial was funded by Amgen. ([Bohula et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2514428))

Bempedoic acid provides another nonstatin test. In CLEAR Outcomes, statin-intolerant patients assigned to bempedoic acid had lower LDL-C and fewer major adverse cardiovascular events than placebo. The trial did not show significant reductions in fatal or nonfatal stroke, cardiovascular death, or all-cause death, and rates of gout and cholelithiasis were higher with bempedoic acid. This trial was funded by Esperion Therapeutics, so the conflict should be noted; again, the result fits the broader pattern that LDL-lowering through multiple mechanisms reduces atherosclerotic events. ([Nissen et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2215024))

## Why absolute risk reduction can look modest

The strongest criticism of LDL-lowering drugs is not that they fail to work; it is that the **absolute benefit depends heavily on baseline risk, treatment duration, age, and competing risks.** ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2812%2960367-5); [Byrne et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2022.0134))

A 2022 JAMA Internal Medicine meta-analysis emphasized this point. Across 21 statin trials, the absolute risk reductions were about 0.8 percent for all-cause mortality, 1.3 percent for myocardial infarction, and 0.4 percent for stroke, while the corresponding relative risk reductions were larger. That is not a refutation of LDL causality. It means a relative risk reduction applied to a low baseline risk produces a small absolute risk reduction over a few years. ([Byrne et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2022.0134))

This is why the same LDL reduction can be very valuable in someone with established coronary disease, diabetes, high apoB, hypertension, smoking, high Lp(a), chronic kidney disease, or high coronary calcium, but much less compelling in a young person with low short-term absolute risk. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2812%2960367-5); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062))

## Why coronary events happen with "normal" LDL

The observation that many people presenting with coronary disease have "normal" LDL-C is true, but it does not disprove LDL causality. ([Sachdeva et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2008.08.010); [Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144))

In a large analysis of 136,905 patients hospitalized with coronary artery disease, almost half had admission LDL-C below 100 mg/dL, and 17.6 percent had LDL-C below 70 mg/dL. This is often cited as a challenge to the LDL hypothesis. ([Sachdeva et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2008.08.010))

There are several reasons this finding is compatible with LDL being causal. First, "normal" does not mean "no risk"; clinical cutoffs are treatment thresholds, not biological thresholds. Second, a single LDL-C value at hospitalization may not reflect decades of cumulative exposure. Third, LDL-C may miss apoB discordance, remnant particles, or Lp(a). Fourth, coronary events require not only plaque formation, but plaque vulnerability, inflammation, thrombosis, blood pressure stress, smoking, diabetes, and other triggers. ([Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

The base-rate issue also matters. Many more people in the population have "average" LDL than have extreme LDL. Therefore, even if high LDL carries higher individual risk, a large number of events can still occur among people with LDL values near the population average. This is the same logic by which many cardiovascular events occur in people without extreme blood pressure, even though blood pressure is a causal cardiovascular risk factor. ([Sachdeva et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2008.08.010); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9))

## Cumulative LDL-years

Atherosclerosis is better understood as an exposure-over-time disease than as a single-lab-value disease. ([Ference et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2012.09.017); [Zhang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2021.3508); [Domanski et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.07.059))

In pooled cohort data from 18,288 participants, cumulative LDL-C exposure and time-weighted average LDL-C during young adulthood and middle age were associated with later coronary heart disease independently of midlife LDL-C. Compared with the lowest quartile, the highest quartile of cumulative LDL-C had a hazard ratio of 1.57 for coronary heart disease, and the highest quartile of time-weighted average LDL-C had a hazard ratio of 1.69. ([Zhang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2021.3508))

A separate JACC analysis also found that cardiovascular risk depends on the cumulative area under the LDL-C curve and the timing of exposure, supporting the idea that earlier and longer LDL exposure matters. ([Domanski et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.07.059))

This explains why lifelong genetic LDL differences can produce larger risk differences than starting medication later in life. It also explains why a person with high LDL for decades may have risk that is not captured by a single current LDL-C value. ([Ference et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2012.09.017); [Domanski et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.07.059))

## Where inflammation fits

Inflammation is not a fringe issue in atherosclerosis; it is part of the disease process. Retained and modified apoB particles provoke inflammatory responses in the artery wall, and inflammatory activity helps determine plaque progression and rupture risk. ([Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Ridker et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1707914); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

CANTOS tested this clinically by lowering inflammation with canakinumab in patients with prior myocardial infarction and elevated hsCRP. Canakinumab reduced recurrent cardiovascular events without lowering lipid levels, which supports the idea that inflammation is also causal or at least therapeutically actionable in selected high-risk patients. The trial was funded by Novartis, and canakinumab was associated with more fatal infection or sepsis than placebo, so the result is best read as proof of inflammatory biology rather than as a simple broad prevention strategy. ([Ridker et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1707914))

That does not mean every anti-inflammatory strategy works. In CIRT, low-dose methotrexate did not lower interleukin-1beta, interleukin-6, or C-reactive protein levels and did not reduce cardiovascular events. ([Ridker et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1809798))

Colchicine trials provide additional evidence that selected inflammation-targeting strategies can reduce events in atherosclerotic disease. COLCOT found fewer ischemic cardiovascular events after recent myocardial infarction, and LoDoCo2 found fewer cardiovascular events in chronic coronary disease. ([Tardif et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1912388); [Nidorf et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2021372))

Residual inflammatory risk remains clinically important. In a collaborative analysis of statin-treated patients, hsCRP was a stronger predictor of future cardiovascular events and death than LDL-C among people already receiving statin therapy. That finding does not mean LDL is irrelevant; it means that once LDL has been treated, inflammatory risk can remain a major driver of events. ([Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

## Where metabolic health fits

Metabolic dysfunction strongly modifies cardiovascular risk. A meta-analysis found that metabolic syndrome was associated with about a twofold increase in cardiovascular outcomes and a roughly 1.5-fold increase in all-cause mortality. ([Mottillo et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2010.05.034))

The INTERHEART case-control study found that several modifiable factors - abnormal lipids, smoking, hypertension, diabetes, abdominal obesity, psychosocial factors, diet, alcohol use, and physical activity - accounted for most of the population-attributable risk of myocardial infarction across 52 countries. Raised apoB/apoA1 and smoking were among the strongest factors. ([Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9))

Metabolic health and LDL are therefore not competing explanations. Insulin resistance often increases triglyceride-rich lipoproteins, remnants, small dense LDL, and apoB particle burden; it also increases inflammation, blood pressure, glycation, endothelial dysfunction, and thrombosis risk. In that context, a given LDL-C can be more dangerous than it looks, especially if apoB is high. ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Mottillo et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2010.05.034); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9))

## What the critics get right

Critics are right that **LDL-C alone is an incomplete individual risk predictor.** A person's risk depends on apoB particle burden, lifetime exposure, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, insulin resistance, kidney disease, Lp(a), family history, age, sex, plaque burden, inflammation, and thrombosis risk. ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

Critics are also right that **primary-prevention absolute benefits can be modest** in low-risk people, especially over short trial durations. That is a reason to communicate absolute risk reduction and number needed to treat honestly, not a reason to dismiss LDL causality. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2812%2960367-5); [Byrne et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2022.0134))

Critics are also right that **metabolic health deserves more attention.** Treating LDL while ignoring smoking, hypertension, diabetes, obesity, sleep, diet quality, physical inactivity, and insulin resistance is not good prevention. ([Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Mottillo et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2010.05.034))

## What the critics usually get wrong

The fact that some people with high LDL do not develop clinical events does not mean LDL is noncausal. Smoking, hypertension, diabetes, and abnormal lipids all raise cardiovascular risk, but not every person with any one risk factor has an event. Causal risk factors are probabilistic, not deterministic. ([Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144))

The fact that many coronary events occur at "normal" LDL-C does not mean LDL is irrelevant. It means LDL-C thresholds are imperfect, population distributions matter, and events are multifactorial. ([Sachdeva et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2008.08.010); [Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144))

The fact that inflammation-lowering can reduce events does not overturn LDL causality. It shows that atherosclerosis has multiple causal pathways. LDL/apoB particles help build the plaque; inflammation helps determine plaque biology and event risk. ([Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Ridker et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1707914); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))

## Quick reference values

LDL-lowering with statins: about **22 percent relative reduction in major vascular events per 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction** in the Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' meta-analysis. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2810%2961350-5))

Low-risk primary prevention: about **11 fewer major vascular events per 1,000 people over 5 years per 1 mmol/L LDL-C reduction** among people with 5-year risk below 10 percent. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2812%2960367-5))

Statin absolute effects in one 2022 meta-analysis: about **0.8 percent absolute reduction in all-cause mortality, 1.3 percent in myocardial infarction, and 0.4 percent in stroke**, with larger relative reductions. ([Byrne et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2022.0134))

Ezetimibe after acute coronary syndrome: IMPROVE-IT showed event reduction when ezetimibe was added to statin therapy; the trial was funded by Merck. ([Cannon et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1410489))

PCSK9 inhibition: FOURIER and ODYSSEY OUTCOMES both reduced cardiovascular events; FOURIER was funded by Amgen, and ODYSSEY OUTCOMES was funded by Sanofi/Regeneron. ([Sabatine et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1615664); [Schwartz et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1801174))

Bempedoic acid: CLEAR Outcomes reduced major adverse cardiovascular events in statin-intolerant patients, but did not significantly reduce fatal or nonfatal stroke, cardiovascular death, or all-cause death in the primary report; the trial was funded by Esperion Therapeutics. ([Nissen et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2215024))

Cumulative LDL exposure: higher cumulative LDL-C and time-weighted LDL-C from young adulthood through middle age predicted later coronary heart disease independently of midlife LDL-C. ([Zhang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2021.3508))

Coronary disease hospitalizations with "normal" LDL-C: in 136,905 coronary-disease hospitalizations, almost half had LDL-C below 100 mg/dL, and 17.6 percent had LDL-C below 70 mg/dL. ([Sachdeva et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2008.08.010))

Inflammation: CANTOS reduced recurrent cardiovascular events without lowering lipid levels, supporting inflammation as an additional treatment target in selected high-risk patients; the trial was funded by Novartis. CIRT was null for low-dose methotrexate, while colchicine trials showed benefit in selected coronary disease populations. ([Ridker et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1707914); [Ridker et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1809798); [Tardif et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1912388); [Nidorf et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2021372))

## Practical interpretation

The most evidence-consistent position is: **LDL particles and related apoB-containing particles are causal in atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease.** This is supported by genetics, Mendelian randomization, mechanistic pathology, statin trials, ezetimibe trials, PCSK9 inhibitor trials, bempedoic acid, and broader LDL-lowering meta-analyses. ([Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2810%2961350-5); [Silverman et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2016.13985); [Nissen et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2215024))

The strongest nuance is: **LDL-C is not the whole story.** ApoB particle number, cumulative LDL-years, metabolic health, inflammation, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, Lp(a), and existing plaque burden shape individual risk. ([Richardson et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1003062); [Zhang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2021.3508); [Mottillo et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2010.05.034); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9))

So the "LDL is everything" versus "LDL does not matter" debate is a false binary. The better conclusion is: **LDL/apoB exposure is a causal substrate for atherosclerosis; metabolic and inflammatory context determines how much clinical disease emerges from that substrate.** ([Ference et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehx144); [Borén et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurheartj/ehz962); [Yusuf et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2804%2917018-9); [Ridker et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736%2823%2900215-5))
