# LDL-C vs LDL Particle Number vs ApoB

LDL-C measures the mass of cholesterol carried inside LDL particles. ApoB estimates the number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles because LDL, VLDL, IDL/remnant particles, and Lp(a) each carry one ApoB molecule. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels")) LDL-P, usually measured by nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy, estimates the number of LDL particles specifically. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386699/ "Association of apolipoprotein B and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy-derived LDL particle number with outcomes in 25 clinical studies"))

The practical issue is not whether these markers are correlated. They usually are. The issue is what to do when they are discordant. In discordant cases, the weight of evidence generally favors particle number, especially ApoB, over cholesterol mass as the better indicator of atherosclerotic cardiovascular risk. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

## Bottom line

When LDL-C, non-HDL-C, ApoB, and LDL-P are concordant, clinical interpretation is usually similar. In that setting, LDL-C often performs adequately because it is strongly correlated with ApoB and because most lipid-lowering trial thresholds and treatment goals have historically been built around LDL-C. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

When LDL-C and ApoB disagree, risk generally tends to follow ApoB. A recent systematic review of discordance studies found that ApoB outperformed LDL-C in all 9 studies that directly compared them, while LDL-P outperformed LDL-C in 2 of 3 LDL-P comparisons. The review concluded that ApoB is more accurate than LDL-C and non-HDL-C for cardiovascular risk assessment, especially when lipid markers are discordant. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

The strongest practical conclusion is not that LDL-C is useless. It is that LDL-C is an incomplete proxy for the number of atherogenic particles. ApoB is the cleaner measure when the question is, "How many atherogenic particles are circulating and able to enter the artery wall?" ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

## What each marker measures

LDL-C measures cholesterol mass inside LDL particles. It does not count LDL particles. Two people can have the same LDL-C but different numbers of LDL particles if each particle carries a different amount of cholesterol. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

ApoB measures the concentration of ApoB-containing particles. Since each major atherogenic non-HDL particle contains one ApoB molecule, ApoB functions as a particle-number marker for LDL, VLDL, IDL/remnants, and Lp(a), not just LDL. In routine clinical use, ApoB mainly reflects ApoB-100-containing particles; chylomicron-derived particles contain ApoB-48. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

LDL-P measures LDL particle number, typically by NMR spectroscopy. LDL-P and ApoB are related but not identical: LDL-P counts LDL particles, whereas ApoB counts LDL plus other ApoB-containing atherogenic particles. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386699/ "Association of apolipoprotein B and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy-derived LDL particle number with outcomes in 25 clinical studies"))

Non-HDL-C measures the cholesterol mass inside all non-HDL particles. It captures LDL cholesterol plus cholesterol in VLDL, IDL/remnants, and Lp(a), but it is still a cholesterol-content measure, not a particle-number measure. ([EAS](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf "2021 ESC Guidelines on cardiovascular disease prevention in clinical practice"))

## Why discordance happens

Discordance occurs because the cholesterol content per ApoB particle varies. If LDL particles are cholesterol-depleted, a person can have normal or modest LDL-C but high ApoB. If LDL particles are cholesterol-rich, a person can have high LDL-C but less elevated ApoB. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

This matters because atherosclerosis is driven by the retention of ApoB-containing particles in the artery wall. The cholesterol carried inside those particles matters biologically, but the number of particles affects how often those particles enter and are retained in the arterial intima. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"); [ESC/EAS](https://www.escardio.org/guidelines/clinical-practice-guidelines/all-esc-practice-guidelines/dyslipidaemias/ "2025 Focused Update of the 2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines for the management of dyslipidaemias"))

Discordance is especially common when triglycerides are elevated. The Canadian Cardiovascular Society guideline notes that triglycerides above 1.5 mmol/L are associated with small, cholesterol-depleted LDL particles, making LDL-C a less reliable reflection of LDL particle number. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

## Evidence from meta-analyses

A 2011 meta-analysis comparing LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and ApoB found that ApoB was the strongest marker of cardiovascular risk, with LDL-C the weakest and non-HDL-C intermediate. The reported relative risk ratio was 1.43 for ApoB, 1.34 for non-HDL-C, and 1.25 for LDL-C. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21487090/ "A meta-analysis of LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and apolipoprotein B as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

A 2014 meta-analysis of randomized statin trials found that changes in ApoB were more closely associated with coronary heart disease risk reduction than changes in LDL-C. The estimated risk reduction per ApoB change was about 21.6 percent greater than the risk reduction associated with LDL-C change. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24732920/ "Relations of change in plasma levels of LDL-C, non-HDL-C and ApoB with risk reduction from statin therapy"))

However, not every large evidence synthesis shows ApoB clearly beating every alternative marker in every setting. The Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration found that vascular risk assessment can be simplified using either conventional cholesterol measures or apolipoproteins, which supports the idea that LDL-C and standard lipids are often adequate at the population-model level. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3284229/ "Major Lipids, Apolipoproteins, and Risk of Vascular Disease"))

In statin-treated patients from randomized trials, an individual-patient-data meta-analysis found that on-treatment LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and ApoB were all associated with future major cardiovascular events, with non-HDL-C performing strongly. This is one reason guidelines have not universally replaced LDL-C with ApoB as the primary treatment target. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/ "Association of LDL cholesterol, non-HDL cholesterol, and apolipoprotein B levels with risk of cardiovascular events among patients treated with statins"))

The overall pattern is therefore: ApoB is usually the better marker of atherogenic particle burden, especially in discordance; LDL-C remains clinically useful because it is familiar, inexpensive, strongly trial-validated, and usually concordant with ApoB. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

## Evidence from discordance studies

The most important evidence comes from discordance analyses, because those are the cases where the markers disagree and a decision has to be made. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

A 2024 UK Biobank analysis included 293,876 adults followed for a median of 11 years, with 19,982 incident ASCVD events. ApoB was highly correlated with LDL-C and non-HDL-C, but the range of ApoB values at any given LDL-C value was wide. At LDL-C around 130 mg/dL, people with higher ApoB had a 10-year ASCVD event rate of 7.3 percent, compared with 4.0 percent for those with lower ApoB. ([European Heart Journal](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/45/27/2410/7663778 "Discordance among ApoB, non-HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides"))

In that same UK Biobank analysis, residual ApoB remained significantly associated with ASCVD after accounting for LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and triglycerides. Residual LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and triglycerides were not significant once ApoB was included. The authors concluded that LDL-C, non-HDL-C, and triglycerides are not adequate clinical proxies for ApoB in individual patients. ([European Heart Journal](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/45/27/2410/7663778 "Discordance among ApoB, non-HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides"))

A 2024 JAMA Cardiology analysis of NHANES found substantial individual variation in ApoB at the same LDL-C or non-HDL-C levels. Higher-than-expected ApoB was more common in people with metabolic risk factors, but meaningful discordance also occurred in metabolically healthy people. The authors concluded that limiting ApoB testing only to people with hypertriglyceridemia or obvious metabolic risk would miss a substantial minority of discordant patients. This NHANES analysis was cross-sectional and addressed distributional discordance rather than prospective clinical outcomes. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

This is the central argument for ApoB: LDL-C works well as a rough population marker, but ApoB better resolves the individual-level question when cholesterol content and particle number separate. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

## Metabolic syndrome, diabetes, obesity, and high triglycerides

Discordance is not randomly distributed. It is more common in insulin resistance, diabetes, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and hypertriglyceridemia because these states often produce smaller, cholesterol-depleted LDL particles and more remnant particles. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

This means LDL-C can underestimate risk in exactly the people who already carry high cardiometabolic risk. The Canadian guideline explicitly states that when triglycerides are elevated, LDL-C becomes a less reliable reflection of LDL particle number, and non-HDL-C or ApoB becomes more accurate. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

The 2026 ACC/AHA multisociety guideline update moved in the same direction: ApoB may be used to assess residual ASCVD risk and guide treatment in people with cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, high triglycerides, or known cardiovascular disease who have reached LDL-C and non-HDL-C goals. ([ACC/AHA guideline update](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423 "2026 ACC/AHA Multisociety Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

The caveat is that metabolic risk targeting is not perfect. The NHANES analysis found that even metabolically healthy people can have unexpectedly high ApoB for their LDL-C, so a targeted strategy improves detection but does not identify all discordant patients. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

## LDL-P vs ApoB

LDL-P and ApoB answer similar questions because both are particle-number markers. An AACC working group review of 25 clinical studies and 85 outcomes found that both ApoB and NMR-derived LDL-P were associated with cardiovascular outcomes in most studies, were broadly comparable, and were generally stronger risk markers than LDL-C. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386699/ "Association of apolipoprotein B and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy-derived LDL particle number with outcomes in 25 clinical studies"))

In the Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis, when LDL-C and LDL-P were discordant, LDL-related atherosclerotic risk was better reflected by LDL-P than by LDL-C. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21392724/ "Clinical implications of discordance between LDL cholesterol and LDL particle number"))

In the Women's Health Study, discordance between LDL-C and alternative LDL-related measures also changed risk interpretation; LDL-C alone could under- or overestimate coronary risk in women whose LDL-C did not match particle-based or alternative lipid measures. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4501252/ "Discordance of LDL Cholesterol with Alternative LDL-Related Measures and Future Coronary Events"))

The practical distinction is that ApoB is usually easier to standardize, less expensive, and more scalable than NMR LDL-P. The AACC review concluded that although ApoB and LDL-P perform similarly, ApoB is the more practical marker for guideline adoption because of availability, standardization, scalability, and cost. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386699/ "Association of apolipoprotein B and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy-derived LDL particle number with outcomes in 25 clinical studies"))

## ApoB vs non-HDL-C

Non-HDL-C is a major improvement over LDL-C when triglycerides are elevated because it includes cholesterol carried in VLDL, IDL/remnant particles, and Lp(a). It also does not require fasting and avoids some of the calculation problems of LDL-C. ([EAS](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf "2021 ESC Guidelines on cardiovascular disease prevention in clinical practice"))

But non-HDL-C is still a cholesterol-mass measure. It estimates the cholesterol inside atherogenic particles, not the number of particles. ApoB is the more direct particle-count measure. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

In many cohorts, non-HDL-C and ApoB perform similarly because they are highly correlated. The Canadian guideline notes that non-HDL-C and ApoB are both superior to LDL-C in several contexts, while also reporting 8 to 23 percent discordance between ApoB and non-HDL-C. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

The practical hierarchy is therefore: LDL-C is useful, non-HDL-C is often better when triglycerides are elevated, and ApoB is the most direct measure of atherogenic particle number. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

## Cost-effectiveness

The evidence for ApoB is strongest for risk prediction and discordance. The evidence is weaker for direct outcome trials testing an ApoB-guided treatment strategy versus an LDL-C-guided strategy. That distinction matters. A marker can be biologically and prognostically better without yet having randomized trial evidence proving that using it as the primary clinical target improves outcomes. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

A 2026 JAMA cost-effectiveness analysis modeled 250,000 statin-eligible, ASCVD-free adults using LDL-C, non-HDL-C, or ApoB treatment goals. In the base-case model, ApoB-guided treatment produced the most quality-adjusted life-years and was highly cost-effective compared with non-HDL-C, with an incremental cost-effectiveness ratio of about $30,300 per QALY. The authors also found that the cost of ApoB testing itself was a marginal part of total cost. ([JAMA](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2847303 "Cost-Effectiveness of ApoB, Non-HDL-C, and LDL-C Goals for Primary Prevention Lipid-Lowering Therapy"))

This supports the economic plausibility of wider ApoB testing, but it remains model-based evidence rather than a randomized outcomes trial of routine ApoB-guided care. ([JAMA](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/2847303 "Cost-Effectiveness of ApoB, Non-HDL-C, and LDL-C Goals for Primary Prevention Lipid-Lowering Therapy"))

## Guideline comparison

The 2018 ACC/AHA cholesterol guideline treated ApoB mainly as a risk-enhancing factor. ApoB at or above 130 mg/dL, especially with triglycerides at or above 200 mg/dL, was considered a risk-enhancing marker that could support statin therapy in borderline or intermediate-risk patients. ([AHA Journals](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/cir.0000000000000625 "2018 AHA/ACC Cholesterol Guideline"))

The 2026 ACC/AHA multisociety guideline moved further toward ApoB use, especially for residual risk assessment in people with type 2 diabetes, high triglycerides, cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome, or established cardiovascular disease who have already reached LDL-C and non-HDL-C goals. It still does not fully replace LDL-C with ApoB as the universal primary target. ([ACC/AHA guideline update](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423 "2026 ACC/AHA Multisociety Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

The 2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society guideline is more aggressive. It strongly recommends using non-HDL-C or ApoB instead of LDL-C as the preferred lipid parameter when triglycerides are above 1.5 mmol/L, citing high-quality evidence. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

The 2021 ESC prevention guideline keeps LDL-C lowering as the primary treatment focus, while the ESC/EAS dyslipidemia framework provides ApoB secondary goals: ApoB <100 mg/dL for moderate-risk people, ApoB <80 mg/dL for high-risk people, and ApoB <65 mg/dL for very-high-risk people. It also notes that ApoB directly estimates total atherogenic particle concentration and is especially relevant when triglycerides are elevated. ([EAS](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf "2021 ESC Guidelines on cardiovascular disease prevention in clinical practice"); [European Heart Journal](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/41/1/111/5556353 "2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines for the management of dyslipidaemias"))

The 2025 ESC/EAS focused update continues to frame LDL-C and other ApoB-containing lipoproteins as causal in atherosclerosis, while keeping LDL-C lowering as the central therapeutic focus. The update was funded by ESC and EAS without healthcare-industry involvement. ([ESC/EAS](https://www.escardio.org/guidelines/clinical-practice-guidelines/all-esc-practice-guidelines/dyslipidaemias/ "2025 Focused Update of the 2019 ESC/EAS Guidelines for the management of dyslipidaemias"))

## Practical interpretation

If LDL-C and ApoB are both low, risk from ApoB-containing particles is likely low, assuming no major unmeasured risk factor such as very high Lp(a), established ASCVD, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, or strong family history. ([EAS](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf "2021 ESC Guidelines on cardiovascular disease prevention in clinical practice"))

If LDL-C is high and ApoB is high, the interpretation is straightforward: both cholesterol mass and particle number indicate elevated atherogenic burden. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

If LDL-C is normal but ApoB is high, LDL-C is likely underestimating particle burden. This pattern is common in hypertriglyceridemia, insulin resistance, diabetes, obesity, and metabolic syndrome, and outcomes tend to track with ApoB rather than LDL-C. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

If LDL-C is high but ApoB is normal, LDL particles may be relatively cholesterol-rich, and LDL-C may overstate particle number. This does not automatically mean risk is low, but it changes the interpretation: the person has high LDL cholesterol mass without a proportionally high number of ApoB particles. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

If triglycerides are elevated, ApoB or non-HDL-C should be preferred over LDL-C for risk interpretation. ApoB is the more direct particle-number measure; non-HDL-C is a useful lower-cost cholesterol-mass alternative when ApoB is unavailable. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

## Quick reference

- **Best marker of atherogenic particle number:** ApoB. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))

- **Best standard cholesterol measure when ApoB is unavailable:** non-HDL-C, especially when triglycerides are elevated. ([Canadian Journal of Cardiology](https://onlinecjc.ca/article/S0828-282X%2821%2900165-3/fulltext "2021 Canadian Cardiovascular Society Guidelines for the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

- **Most familiar and trial-embedded marker:** LDL-C. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3284229/ "Major Lipids, Apolipoproteins, and Risk of Vascular Disease"))

- **Best interpretation in discordance:** risk generally follows ApoB or LDL-P more than LDL-C. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

- **Highest-yield groups for ApoB testing:** metabolic syndrome, diabetes, obesity, elevated triglycerides, CKM syndrome, established ASCVD, or residual risk despite LDL-C/non-HDL-C goal attainment. ([ACC/AHA guideline update](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423 "2026 ACC/AHA Multisociety Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia"))

- **ApoB vs LDL-P:** both are particle-based and broadly comparable; ApoB is usually more practical, cheaper, and better standardized. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386699/ "Association of apolipoprotein B and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy-derived LDL particle number with outcomes in 25 clinical studies"))

- **Remaining evidence gap:** there is strong risk-prediction and cost-effectiveness evidence, but limited randomized outcomes evidence directly comparing ApoB-guided care with LDL-C-guided care. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))


## Practical conclusion

ApoB is the better marker of atherogenic particle burden. LDL-C is a useful cholesterol marker and remains embedded in clinical trial evidence and many treatment thresholds, but it can misclassify risk when cholesterol content per particle varies. That misclassification is most common in cardiometabolic disease, where accurate risk assessment matters most. ([Journal of Clinical Lipidology](https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874%2825%2900315-0/abstract "ApoB, LDL-C, and non-HDL-C as markers of cardiovascular risk"))

The most defensible clinical position is: LDL-C is acceptable when markers are concordant, non-HDL-C is preferable to LDL-C when triglycerides are elevated, and ApoB should be considered whenever cardiometabolic risk or discordance is plausible. The evidence also increasingly supports broader ApoB testing, because discordance is not limited to easily identifiable metabolic subgroups. ([JAMA Cardiology](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073 "Individual Variation in the Distribution of Apolipoprotein B Levels Across the Spectrum of LDL-C or Non-HDL-C Levels"))
