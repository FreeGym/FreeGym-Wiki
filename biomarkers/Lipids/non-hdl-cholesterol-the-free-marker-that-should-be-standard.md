# Non-HDL Cholesterol: The Free Marker That Should Be Standard

Non-HDL cholesterol should be automatically calculated and prominently displayed on every standard lipid panel. It requires no additional assay and captures cholesterol carried by all major atherogenic lipoprotein classes rather than focusing on LDL-C alone. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/))

The evidence supports elevating non-HDL alongside LDL-C, especially when triglyceride-rich lipoproteins are important. It does not support treating non-HDL and ApoB as interchangeable or abandoning LDL-C entirely. ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

## What non-HDL cholesterol measures

Non-HDL cholesterol is calculated as:

**Non-HDL-C = total cholesterol - HDL-C** ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

Removing the cholesterol carried in HDL leaves cholesterol carried in LDL, intermediate-density lipoproteins, very-low-density lipoproteins, remnant particles, lipoprotein(a), and other ApoB-containing particles. Because total cholesterol and HDL-C are already measured on a standard lipid panel, calculating non-HDL requires no additional laboratory assay. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

Non-HDL and ApoB describe related but different properties. Non-HDL measures the **concentration of cholesterol** inside atherogenic particles. Each major circulating atherogenic lipoprotein particle contains one ApoB molecule, so the plasma ApoB concentration serves as a direct proxy for the **concentration of circulating atherogenic particles**. The cholesterol content of individual particles varies, which is why two people with the same non-HDL cholesterol can have different ApoB concentrations. ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073))

This distinction matters clinically: non-HDL correlates strongly with ApoB, but it does not always track the same biology closely enough to substitute for ApoB in an individual patient. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))

## Why non-HDL improves on LDL-C alone

Routine LDL-C is an operational measure of cholesterol carried predominantly in LDL particles; it also includes cholesterol in Lp(a), and many laboratory methods include IDL cholesterol. Non-HDL additionally captures cholesterol carried in VLDL and triglyceride-rich remnant particles, making it a broader measure of atherogenic cholesterol burden. Neither LDL-C nor non-HDL-C can determine whether Lp(a) concentration is elevated; that requires a dedicated Lp(a) measurement. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Kronenberg et al., 2022](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/43/39/3925/6670882))

This matters particularly in diabetes, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and hypertriglyceridemia, where a larger share of atherogenic cholesterol may be carried in VLDL and remnant particles. In these settings, cholesterol-depleted particles can also make LDL-C, and sometimes non-HDL-C, underrepresent the concentration of atherogenic particles. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073))

Non-HDL also avoids a specific mathematical weakness of calculated LDL-C: the non-HDL-C formula does not contain triglycerides. The Friedewald LDL-C equation estimates VLDL cholesterol from triglycerides, so its errors increase when triglyceride concentrations are high or LDL-C is low. Newer Martin-Hopkins and Sampson equations improve accuracy, but they do not eliminate uncertainty at very high triglyceride levels. ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2785570)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2785570))

In 111,939 patients with triglycerides of 400 to 799 mg/dL, the extended Martin-Hopkins equation placed LDL-C in the correct guideline category in 62.1% of cases, compared with 40.4% for the Sampson equation and 19.3% for Friedewald, using ultracentrifugation-measured LDL-C as the comparator. Even the best-performing equation still produced substantial misclassification, leading the investigators to recommend caution with all calculated LDL-C methods in this triglyceride range. ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2785570)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2785570))

Non-HDL is also suitable for routine nonfasting assessment. A European laboratory consensus concluded that ordinary post-meal changes in lipid concentrations are generally modest and that nonfasting samples can be used routinely, with fasting reassessment reserved for selected circumstances such as markedly elevated triglycerides. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

That advantage is no longer unique to non-HDL, however: modern guidelines generally permit nonfasting standard lipid panels as well. The more specific advantage is that non-HDL avoids the triglyceride-dependent arithmetic used to estimate LDL-C. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

## Head-to-head outcome associations

An individual-participant meta-analysis combined eight statin trials and included 38,153 statin-treated participants. For each 1-standard-deviation increase, the adjusted hazard ratio for a major cardiovascular event was 1.13 for LDL-C, 1.16 for non-HDL-C, and 1.14 for ApoB. Non-HDL was statistically more strongly associated with events than LDL-C or ApoB, although the differences between the hazard-ratio estimates were small. ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Edinburgh Research](https://www.research.ed.ac.uk/en/publications/association-of-ldl-cholesterol-non-hdl-cholesterol-and-apolipopro))

The result supports considering non-HDL alongside LDL-C for estimating residual lipid-related risk in statin-treated populations. It does not establish superior clinical risk classification or show that a treatment strategy targeting non-HDL produces better outcomes, because the trials did not randomize participants to LDL-guided, non-HDL-guided, or ApoB-guided care. ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/))

More recent observational evidence is broadly consistent with the idea that LDL-C alone can miss information. A 2026 analysis of 10,519 adults from three prospective US cohorts recorded 1,103 ASCVD events over a median of 21.3 years. ApoB retained associations with events after accounting for conventional lipid markers, particularly among younger adults; adding it to PREVENT risk estimates produced modest risk-reclassification improvements but did not materially improve discrimination or calibration, and the clinical importance remained uncertain. ([Tang et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2847401)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2847401))

A separate 2026 Copenhagen cohort of 94,398 individuals not taking lipid-lowering medication at baseline found similar overall associations for non-HDL and ApoB: each 1-standard-deviation increase was associated with an ASCVD hazard ratio of 1.16 for non-HDL and 1.14 for ApoB. In analyses using median-based discordance categories, discordantly high levels of either marker carried additional risk, suggesting that atherogenic cholesterol burden and particle concentration can contain complementary information. ([Johannesen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42126847/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42126847/))

The evidence therefore favors a measured conclusion: **non-HDL is generally associated with outcomes at least as strongly as LDL-C and sometimes modestly more strongly, but the cited studies do not show that replacing LDL-C with non-HDL as the sole treatment target improves clinical outcomes.** ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Johannesen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42126847/)) ([Liaigre et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39999862/))

## Non-HDL versus ApoB

Non-HDL and ApoB are highly correlated at a population level, but high correlation does not guarantee agreement in an individual patient. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))

In 293,876 UK Biobank participants followed for a median of approximately 11 years, ApoB correlated with both LDL-C and non-HDL-C at _r_ = 0.96. Nevertheless, ApoB values varied widely at any given non-HDL concentration. Among people with non-HDL around 160 mg/dL, 10-year ASCVD incidence was 6.4% when ApoB was high versus 4.6% when ApoB was low; in that analysis, high and low ApoB meant at least 1 standard deviation above or below the conditional mean ApoB value. Residual ApoB remained associated with events after adjustment for non-HDL, whereas residual non-HDL was not associated with events after adjustment for ApoB. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/))

A nationally representative cross-sectional analysis of 12,688 US adults similarly found a substantial range of ApoB values at fixed LDL-C or non-HDL-C levels. Discordantly high ApoB was more common in people with metabolic risk factors, but it also occurred in apparently metabolically healthy participants. ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/fullarticle/2820073))

These findings argue against treating non-HDL as capturing nearly all of ApoB's value in every patient. Non-HDL is a broader default panel marker than LDL-C alone, but ApoB can reveal a high particle concentration hidden within an apparently acceptable cholesterol concentration. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))

The practical hierarchy is therefore:

- **Non-HDL:** no additional assay; suitable for automatic reporting and routine follow-up. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- **LDL-C:** supported by decades of treatment trials, established thresholds, and guideline infrastructure. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- **ApoB:** an additional assay that provides a direct proxy for atherogenic particle concentration and is most useful when cholesterol mass and particle concentration may be discordant. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))


## Guidelines have already moved

US guidelines have now moved beyond treating non-HDL as a peripheral marker. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

The 2019 European ESC/EAS guideline listed non-HDL as a secondary treatment goal, with targets of less than 130 mg/dL for moderate risk, less than 100 mg/dL for high risk, and less than 85 mg/dL for very high risk. Corresponding ApoB goals were less than 100, 80, and 65 mg/dL. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/41/1/111/5556353))

The 2025 ESC/EAS focused update added or revised recommendations based on evidence published through March 31, 2025. Recommendations outside the scope of the focused update remained governed by the 2019 guideline. ([European Society of Cardiology](https://www.escardio.org/guidelines/clinical-practice-guidelines/all-esc-practice-guidelines/dyslipidaemias/))

More importantly, the US multisociety dyslipidemia guideline released on March 13, 2026, explicitly includes both LDL-C and non-HDL-C treatment goals. It retained percentage LDL-C reduction as a priority but no longer leaves non-HDL at the margins. It also recommends selective ApoB testing after LDL-C and non-HDL goals are reached, especially with triglycerides above 200 mg/dL, diabetes, or achieved LDL-C below 70 mg/dL. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

Representative 2026 US goals include:

- Borderline or intermediate-risk primary prevention when treated: LDL-C below 100 mg/dL and non-HDL-C below 130 mg/dL. ([Wiggins et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2026.02.4872))

- High-risk primary prevention or clinical ASCVD not at very high risk: LDL-C below 70 mg/dL and non-HDL-C below 100 mg/dL. ([Wiggins et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2026.02.4872))

- Very-high-risk secondary prevention: LDL-C below 55 mg/dL and non-HDL-C below 85 mg/dL. ([Wiggins et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2026.02.4872))


Non-HDL has therefore already moved much closer to standard US practice at the guideline level. The remaining questions concern laboratory display, clinician uptake, and whether it should be presented as equal to, or above, LDL-C. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

## What the 30 mg/dL rule means

The familiar non-HDL targets are generally 30 mg/dL higher than the corresponding LDL-C targets. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- LDL-C 55 to non-HDL-C 85 mg/dL. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- LDL-C 70 to non-HDL-C 100 mg/dL. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- LDL-C 100 to non-HDL-C 130 mg/dL. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))


This is a pragmatic guideline crosswalk, not a fixed biological law. The arithmetic difference between non-HDL-C and LDL-C is often used as an estimate of remnant cholesterol, but it is not a chemically isolated measurement and inherits error from the LDL-C method. It chiefly reflects cholesterol not assigned to LDL-C, particularly cholesterol in VLDL and remnant particles; Lp(a)-cholesterol ordinarily contributes to both LDL-C and non-HDL-C and therefore largely does not explain the difference. That contribution varies with triglyceride metabolism, treatment, insulin resistance, and other characteristics; it is not always exactly 30 mg/dL. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/41/1/111/5556353)) ([Kronenberg et al., 2022](https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/43/39/3925/6670882))

The 30 mg/dL offset is useful for communication during transition, but it should not be interpreted as an invariant "normal" remnant-cholesterol concentration. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/))

## Clinical inertia and implementation evidence

There is evidence that familiarity has historically limited non-HDL use, but the direct implementation literature cited here is limited. ([Virani et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4180871/))

A 2011 survey of clinicians attending medical conferences found limited knowledge of non-HDL targets and calculation, particularly outside a lipidology meeting. The response rate was about one-third, and the sample was not nationally representative. The age and setting of the survey limit its relevance to current practice, so the study is better evidence of historical inertia than current prevalence. ([Virani et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4180871/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4180871/))

The studies reviewed here do not establish through a modern randomized implementation trial whether placing non-HDL first on laboratory reports changes prescribing, treatment intensification, goal attainment, or cardiovascular outcomes. The evidence summarized here primarily compares prognostic associations rather than marker-guided clinical strategies. ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/)) ([Johannesen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42126847/))

This is the main evidence gap behind the "primary slot" proposal. The calculation itself requires no additional assay, but changing laboratory reports, electronic records, quality measures, and patient-facing language would still require implementation work. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([Virani et al., 2011](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4180871/))

## Limits and open debates

A prognostic biomarker is not automatically a validated surrogate outcome. A 2025 umbrella review included 20 statin trials and 194,686 participants. Differences in LDL-C or non-HDL reduction between trial groups explained little of the variation in treatment effects on cardiovascular events or mortality; neither marker met the study's criteria for trial-level surrogacy. ([Liaigre et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39999862/))

That result does not mean LDL-C or non-HDL is clinically useless. The review addressed trial-level surrogacy: whether differences in biomarker lowering across the included statin trials predicted differences in treatment effects on clinical outcomes. It did not test the broader causal evidence concerning ApoB-containing lipoproteins and atherosclerosis. ([Liaigre et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39999862/))

The cited studies do not establish a universal clinical winner between non-HDL and ApoB. ApoB is biologically the more direct marker of atherogenic particle concentration, and some cohorts find that ApoB contains the dominant residual information; another found that discordantly high non-HDL and discordantly high ApoB each carried additional risk. The most defensible interpretation is that non-HDL is the most practical no-additional-assay expansion of a standard lipid panel, while ApoB remains valuable when a direct estimate of particle concentration could alter management. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/)) ([Johannesen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42126847/))

## Quick reference values

- Formula: total cholesterol - HDL-C. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

- Measures: cholesterol concentration carried by all major non-HDL, ApoB-containing lipoproteins. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/))

- Additional assay cost: none; it is calculated from the standard panel. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

- Fasting: generally unnecessary for routine measurement. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

- Main advantage over calculated LDL-C: includes cholesterol in VLDL and remnant particles and avoids triglyceride-dependent LDL-estimation formulas. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

- Typical target relationship: non-HDL goal approximately 30 mg/dL above the corresponding LDL-C goal. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423)) ([AHA Journals](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- ApoB is particularly useful after LDL-C and non-HDL-C goals are met when triglycerides are above 200 mg/dL, diabetes is present, or achieved LDL-C is below 70 mg/dL. Discordance can also occur outside these groups. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))


## Practical interpretation

- Non-HDL should be automatically calculated and prominently displayed on every lipid panel. ([Nordestgaard et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4929379/))

- Clinicians should routinely consider it alongside LDL-C, particularly in patients with elevated triglycerides, diabetes, obesity, or metabolic syndrome. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- LDL-C should not be discarded: it remains the marker most directly embedded in randomized treatment evidence, therapeutic thresholds, and established care pathways. ([Mach et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31504418/)) ([Boekholdt et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22453571/)) ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))

- ApoB should not be dismissed as merely an expensive version of non-HDL. It is a proxy for particle concentration and can identify clinically meaningful discordance. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/)) ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))

- The strongest policy is **prominent LDL-C plus non-HDL reporting, with selective ApoB testing**, rather than replacing one universal marker with another. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423))
