# What Does a Standard Lipid Panel Actually Tell You?

A standard lipid panel is a useful first-line cardiovascular-risk screen, but it is not a complete cardiovascular-risk assessment. It reports cholesterol mass in broad lipoprotein categories plus triglycerides; it does not directly count atherogenic particles, identify lipoprotein(a), measure HDL function, or fully characterize triglyceride-rich remnant particles. The most defensible interpretation is that the standard panel is a screening step, not a final verdict. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Erqou et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622820/); [Varbo et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23265341/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## What the standard lipid panel measures

A standard lipid panel usually reports total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, and triglycerides. Total cholesterol is the cholesterol contained across multiple lipoprotein fractions. HDL-C is the cholesterol mass carried inside HDL particles. Triglycerides reflect circulating triglyceride-rich lipoproteins and are also used in common LDL-C calculations. LDL-C is the cholesterol mass assigned to LDL particles, but in routine practice it is usually estimated rather than directly measured. ([Friedewald et al., 1972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4337382/); [Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

The traditional Friedewald equation estimates LDL-C as:

**LDL-C = total cholesterol - HDL-C - triglycerides/5**

This assumes a fixed relationship between triglycerides and VLDL cholesterol. The equation was designed to avoid preparative ultracentrifugation, and laboratories have traditionally avoided reporting Friedewald LDL-C when triglycerides are 400 mg/dL or higher. ([Friedewald et al., 1972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4337382/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

## Why the standard panel is still useful

The standard lipid panel is not obsolete. Large prospective datasets show that standard lipid measures predict vascular risk at a population level. In the [Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/) of 302,430 participants from 68 long-term prospective studies, major lipid measures, including total cholesterol, HDL-C, and non-HDL-C, were associated with vascular outcomes, and risk assessment could often be simplified using total cholesterol and HDL-C or apolipoproteins. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/))

LDL-C is also strongly treatment-relevant. In the [Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21067804/) of 26 randomized statin trials including about 170,000 participants, each 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL-C was associated with an approximately one-fifth proportional reduction in major vascular events. That does not mean LDL-C is the only causal marker, but it explains why LDL-C remains central in prevention guidelines and clinical trials. ([Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21067804/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

So the strongest version of the "standard panel is useful" claim is true: it uses routinely reported lipid measures and is validated for broad risk screening. The weaker version, that it fully characterizes lipid-related risk in an individual person, is not supported by the evidence. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21067804/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## Where calculated LDL-C can mislead

The main problem is not just that LDL-C is calculated. It is that the calculation error is not random: it tends to become more clinically important when LDL-C is low and triglycerides are higher, exactly the setting where treatment decisions may hinge on small differences. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/); [Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

In a large concordance analysis, [Martin and colleagues](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/) compared Friedewald-estimated LDL-C with directly measured LDL-C. The original dataset included 1,340,614 U.S. adults; after excluding triglycerides of 400 mg/dL or higher, 1,310,440 remained for the Friedewald comparison. Among people with Friedewald LDL-C below 70 mg/dL, directly measured LDL-C was 70 mg/dL or higher in 23 percent overall. The discordance increased as triglycerides rose: 39 percent when triglycerides were 150-199 mg/dL and 59 percent when triglycerides were 200-399 mg/dL. The authors concluded that Friedewald LDL-C tends to underestimate LDL-C most when accuracy is especially important, particularly when triglycerides are 150 mg/dL or higher. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/))

The Martin-Hopkins equation was developed to improve this problem by replacing the fixed triglyceride/5 term with an adjustable factor for the triglyceride to VLDL-C ratio. This generally improves LDL-C classification, especially at lower LDL-C and higher triglyceride levels, but it is still an estimate rather than a direct measurement. ([Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

In patients with triglycerides from 400 to 799 mg/dL, [Sajja and colleagues](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/) found that extended Martin-Hopkins LDL-C was more accurate than Friedewald or Sampson LDL-C when compared with a reference ultracentrifugation-based method: 62.1 percent accuracy for extended Martin-Hopkins, 40.4 percent for Sampson, and 19.3 percent for Friedewald. But the same paper emphasized caution: even the better equations were imperfect in severe hypertriglyceridemia. ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Sampson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32101259/))

Newer evidence adds nuance. In pooled alirocumab trial data, Friedewald, Martin-Hopkins, and the NIH/Sampson equation all correlated strongly with beta-quantification LDL-C, and differences were small when triglycerides were below 150 mg/dL. Accuracy worsened as triglycerides rose, especially above 250 mg/dL. ([Ginsberg et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34774485/); [Sampson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32101259/))

A 2026 _Clinical Chemistry_ analysis of 32,533 LDL-C results from dyslipidemic patients compared multiple LDL-C equations with beta-quantification. When triglycerides were 400 mg/dL or lower, Friedewald performed well and was not inferior to Sampson or Martin-Hopkins in that dataset. When triglycerides were above 400 mg/dL, no equation performed adequately, and the authors concluded that LDL-C should not be calculated in that range. ([Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

The practical conclusion is not "Friedewald is always useless." It is: Friedewald can be acceptable in many routine cases, but it is more vulnerable to clinically meaningful underestimation when triglycerides are elevated, LDL-C is low, or results sit near a treatment threshold. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/); [Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Ginsberg et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34774485/); [Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

## Direct LDL-C is not automatically the solution

Direct LDL-C assays exist, but "direct" does not necessarily mean "gold standard." Many direct assays are homogeneous chemical assays, not beta-quantification. Their performance varies by assay, patient population, and triglyceride level. ([Miida et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22980501/); [Meeusen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40899684/))

A multicenter comparison of homogeneous LDL-C assays found that most performed acceptably in non-diseased subjects, but fewer met accuracy criteria in diseased subjects, and hypertriglyceridemia was an important determinant of bias. ([Miida et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22980501/))

A 2025 _Clinical Chemistry_ study comparing three direct LDL-C assays and modern LDL-C equations against beta-quantification found notable variability among direct assays. When triglycerides were below 400 mg/dL, Martin-Hopkins and Sampson estimates showed less bias and misclassification than Friedewald or direct LDL-C. When triglycerides were 400-800 mg/dL, Sampson performed best overall; when triglycerides were extremely high, even the better methods had meaningful limitations. ([Meeusen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40899684/); [Sampson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32101259/))

So direct LDL-C can be useful in selected settings, but replacing calculated LDL-C with routine direct LDL-C does not automatically solve the problem. The reference method in many validation studies is beta-quantification or ultracentrifugation-based measurement, not the ordinary "direct LDL-C" assay on a routine lab report. ([Miida et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22980501/); [Ginsberg et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34774485/); [Meeusen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40899684/); [Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

## HDL-C is not HDL function

HDL-C is the cholesterol mass inside HDL particles. It does not directly measure whether HDL particles are performing reverse cholesterol transport, anti-inflammatory functions, or other proposed protective functions. ([Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Voight et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607825/); [AIM-HIGH Investigators, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/))

This distinction matters. In the [Dallas Heart Study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/), HDL cholesterol efflux capacity, a functional measure of HDL-mediated cholesterol removal from macrophages, was inversely associated with incident cardiovascular events even after adjustment for traditional risk factors, HDL-C, and HDL particle concentration. ([Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/))

Genetic and trial evidence also weakened the old assumption that higher HDL-C is automatically protective. A Mendelian randomization study found that genetic mechanisms that raised HDL-C did not necessarily lower myocardial infarction risk. In [AIM-HIGH](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/), adding niacin to intensive statin therapy raised HDL-C but did not reduce cardiovascular events. ([Voight et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607825/); [AIM-HIGH Investigators, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/))

That does not make HDL-C meaningless. Low HDL-C can still mark a broader adverse metabolic pattern, especially when paired with high triglycerides. But HDL-C should not be interpreted as a direct measure of HDL quality or cardiovascular protection. Cholesterol efflux capacity remains mainly a research or specialized assay, not a routine standard lipid-panel result. ([Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Voight et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607825/); [AIM-HIGH Investigators, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## What the standard panel misses

### ApoB

ApoB is a marker of atherogenic particle number. LDL-C measures the cholesterol mass inside LDL particles; ApoB better reflects how many ApoB-containing particles are present. Each LDL, VLDL, IDL, remnant, and Lp(a) particle generally carries one ApoB molecule. These can be discordant: two people can have the same LDL-C but different numbers of LDL and other ApoB-containing particles. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

In a UK Biobank analysis of 293,876 adults free of cardiovascular disease at baseline, ApoB was highly correlated with LDL-C and non-HDL-C, but there was still a wide range of ApoB values at the same LDL-C, non-HDL-C, or triglyceride levels. At comparable LDL-C, people with higher ApoB had higher 10-year ASCVD event rates than people with lower ApoB. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/))

A 2024 _JAMA Cardiology_ analysis using NHANES data similarly found substantial individual variation in ApoB across LDL-C and non-HDL-C levels. Variability was greatest when LDL-C was estimated using the Friedewald equation, lower with newer equations, and lowest with non-HDL-C. The authors argued that limiting ApoB testing only to people with hypertriglyceridemia may miss some discordant individuals. ([Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/))

### Lipoprotein(a)

Lp(a) is not reported on a standard lipid panel. LDL-C may include some cholesterol carried within Lp(a), but the standard panel does not identify whether a person has a high concentration of Lp(a) particles. ([Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

In a large analysis from the [Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622820/), Lp(a) concentration showed continuous, independent associations with coronary heart disease and stroke risk. In MESA, adding Lp(a) to pooled cohort equation risk prediction improved risk classification, particularly in lower-risk individuals. ([Erqou et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622820/); [Bhatia et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37607461/))

Lp(a) is especially important because it is largely genetically determined and may be high even when LDL-C, HDL-C, and triglycerides look unremarkable. Recent NLA and ACC/AHA guidance recommends measuring Lp(a) at least once in adulthood for ASCVD risk stratification. ([Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

### Triglyceride-rich remnants

Triglycerides are reported on the standard panel, but triglycerides are not the same thing as remnant cholesterol or remnant particle burden. Triglyceride-rich remnants can contribute to atherosclerotic risk, especially in insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, diabetes, and postprandial lipemia. ([Varbo et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23265341/); [Doi et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35710189/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

In Copenhagen population data, elevated nonfasting remnant cholesterol was associated with higher ischemic heart disease risk, and genetic analyses supported a causal interpretation. ([Varbo et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23265341/))

A later _JACC_ analysis found that adding remnant cholesterol to guideline-based risk assessment could reclassify a meaningful share of people who later experienced myocardial infarction or ischemic heart disease. ([Doi et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35710189/))

## When advanced testing is most likely to matter

Advanced testing is not necessary every time a person has a normal, straightforward lipid panel. A once-in-adulthood Lp(a) measurement is now broadly supported for ASCVD risk stratification, but repeated or broader advanced testing is most likely to change interpretation when the standard panel is borderline, internally discordant, or biologically suspicious. ([Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

Common examples include triglycerides 150 mg/dL or higher, especially 200 mg/dL or higher; metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, diabetes, or fatty liver when part of an insulin-resistant pattern; LDL-C near a treatment threshold; unexpectedly low calculated LDL-C in the setting of high triglycerides; strong family history of premature ASCVD; premature ASCVD despite "normal" standard lipids; very high total cholesterol driven by high HDL-C; and discordance between LDL-C and non-HDL-C. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

As a first step, non-HDL-C can be calculated from the standard panel:

**non-HDL-C = total cholesterol - HDL-C**

Non-HDL-C captures cholesterol carried by ApoB-containing particles, including LDL, VLDL, IDL, remnants, and Lp(a). It is not identical to ApoB, but it often performs better than LDL-C when triglycerides are elevated and is emphasized as a useful treatment and risk-assessment measure in modern guidance. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## How the competing claims come out

The claim that the standard lipid panel is a reasonable first-line screening tool is **true**. Large cohort studies and randomized LDL-lowering trials support its usefulness for population-level risk assessment and treatment decisions. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Cholesterol Treatment Trialists' Collaboration, 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21067804/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

The claim that the standard panel is inadequate as a sole risk assessment tool is also **true**, especially for individual decision-making. It can miss ApoB discordance, Lp(a)-related risk, remnant risk, and HDL functional differences. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Erqou et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622820/); [Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Varbo et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23265341/))

The claim that Friedewald should simply be replaced by direct LDL-C is **too broad**. Newer equations often improve accuracy in difficult settings, but routine direct LDL-C assays have their own limitations. The more evidence-aligned position is that laboratories should use validated modern equations in settings where Friedewald is known to be weak, but calculated LDL-C should not be over-interpreted when triglycerides are very high. If triglycerides are above 400 mg/dL and LDL-C precision matters, reference methods, ApoB, and non-HDL-C interpretation are safer than treating any calculated LDL-C as definitive. ([Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Sampson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32101259/); [Meeusen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40899684/); [Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

The strongest overall claim is that the standard panel's biggest problem is how it is used. It is often treated as a definitive green light or red flag, when it should be interpreted as an initial screen that may trigger ApoB, Lp(a), non-HDL-C/remnant-focused interpretation, or other risk assessment when results are borderline, discordant, or clinically inconsistent. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## Quick reference values

Friedewald LDL-C is traditionally not used when triglycerides are 400 mg/dL or higher. ([Friedewald et al., 1972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4337382/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

Friedewald LDL-C can underestimate LDL-C when triglycerides are elevated, especially when calculated LDL-C is low. In one large study, among people with Friedewald LDL-C below 70 mg/dL, direct LDL-C was 70 mg/dL or higher in 23 percent overall, 39 percent with triglycerides 150-199 mg/dL, and 59 percent with triglycerides 200-399 mg/dL. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/))

Martin-Hopkins and Sampson/NIH equations often improve LDL-C estimation in selected settings, but none performs well enough to make calculated LDL-C fully reliable when triglycerides are very high. ([Martin et al., 2013 JAMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24240933/); [Sampson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32101259/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Ginsberg et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34774485/); [Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

Direct LDL-C assays are not the same as beta-quantification and can show assay-dependent bias, especially in dyslipidemia and hypertriglyceridemia. ([Miida et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22980501/); [Meeusen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40899684/))

HDL-C is not HDL function. Cholesterol efflux capacity has shown independent association with cardiovascular events, while simply raising HDL-C pharmacologically has not reliably lowered events. ([Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Voight et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607825/); [AIM-HIGH Investigators, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/))

ApoB, Lp(a), and remnant cholesterol can add information not captured well by the four standard lipid-panel numbers. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Erqou et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622820/); [Doi et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35710189/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

## Funding and conflict-of-interest notes

Several key LDL-C equation studies have relevant disclosures or sponsor-related features. The Martin 2013 JACC analysis used deidentified data from Atherotech, although the authors stated that Atherotech did not pay for the analysis or influence the conclusions. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/))

The Sajja 2021 _JAMA Network Open_ analysis disclosed that the Very Large Database of Lipids was supported by the David and June Trone Family Foundation, and that Seth Martin had a patent pending for LDL-C estimation and multiple industry relationships. ([Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/))

The Ginsberg 2022 _JLR_ analysis used pooled phase 3 alirocumab trial data; the ODYSSEY alirocumab trials were funded by Sanofi and Regeneron. This does not invalidate the findings, but it matters because assay-equation performance can be clinically and commercially important. ([Ginsberg et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34774485/))

## Practical interpretation

A standard lipid panel tells you whether the broad cholesterol and triglyceride pattern is clearly favorable, clearly unfavorable, or ambiguous. It is often enough for initial screening. It is not enough to conclude that cardiovascular risk has been "fully evaluated." ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

Even perfect lipid measurement does not replace full ASCVD risk assessment, which also depends on age, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, kidney disease, inflammatory disease, family history, and other risk-enhancing factors. ([Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

The most evidence-based use is:

1. Interpret LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides, and total cholesterol together, not as isolated numbers. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

2. Calculate non-HDL-C from every standard panel. ([Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

3. Treat Friedewald LDL-C cautiously when triglycerides are elevated, LDL-C is low, or results are near a decision threshold. ([Martin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23524048/); [Sajja et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34709388/); [Paquette et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42047357/))

4. Consider ApoB when LDL-C, non-HDL-C, triglycerides, metabolic risk, or clinical history are discordant. ([Sniderman et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38700053/); [Sayed et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38865115/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

5. Measure Lp(a) at least once in adulthood, with particular importance when inherited risk or premature ASCVD risk matters. ([Koschinsky et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38565461/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))

6. Remember that HDL-C is a concentration, not a functional assay. ([Rohatgi et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25404125/); [Voight et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607825/); [AIM-HIGH Investigators, 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22085343/))


The standard lipid panel is not useless. It is a useful first-line screening tool, and its core calculated LDL-C method traces to a 1972 equation that becomes much more informative when its limitations are understood. ([Friedewald et al., 1972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4337382/); [Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19903920/); [Blumenthal et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824590/))
