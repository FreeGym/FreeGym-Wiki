# Fat

The most debated questions about dietary fat.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Does Dietary Fat Make You Fat?

Fat has more than twice the calories per gram as carbs or protein. For decades, low-fat diets were the default weight loss strategy. Then high-fat diets like keto became popular and people lost weight eating butter.

**Competing claims:**

1. Fat is the most calorie-dense macro, so eating more fat means eating more calories and gaining more fat
2. Dietary fat doesn't spike insulin, so it doesn't trigger fat storage the way carbs do
3. High-fat diets increase satiety and reduce total calorie intake, leading to weight loss despite higher fat intake
4. Fat itself doesn't matter; total calories and food quality determine body composition

**Evidence needed:** Isocaloric feeding studies varying fat intake, ad libitum trials comparing high-fat vs low-fat diets, long-term RCTs on dietary fat and body weight.

## Is Saturated Fat Bad for Your Heart?

This may be the single most debated question in all of nutrition. Government guidelines have warned against saturated fat for decades. Recent meta-analyses have questioned whether the link to heart disease holds up. Both sides cite large bodies of evidence.

**Competing claims:**

1. Saturated fat raises LDL cholesterol, and LDL causes atherosclerosis, so saturated fat causes heart disease
2. Recent meta-analyses show no significant association between saturated fat intake and cardiovascular events or mortality
3. The effect depends on what replaces saturated fat: replacing it with polyunsaturated fat reduces risk, but replacing it with refined carbs does not
4. LDL particle number and type matter more than total LDL, and saturated fat's effect on particle subtypes is more nuanced than "raises LDL"

**Evidence needed:** Diet-heart trial reanalyses, meta-analyses of saturated fat and CVD outcomes, LDL subtype research, replacement nutrient analyses.

## Are Seed Oils Harmful?

Seed oils (soybean, canola, sunflower, corn) have become a major internet health debate. Critics blame them for inflammation and chronic disease. Defenders point out they've been recommended by health organizations for decades.

**Competing claims:**

1. Seed oils are high in omega-6 linoleic acid, which drives inflammation and chronic disease
2. Omega-6 fats are not inflammatory in context of normal human diets, and linoleic acid does not increase inflammatory markers in controlled trials
3. The real problem is that seed oils enable ultra-processed food, not the oils themselves
4. Seed oils are unstable and produce harmful oxidation products when heated, making them dangerous for cooking

**Evidence needed:** RCTs on linoleic acid and inflammatory markers, oxidation product studies, epidemiological data on vegetable oil consumption and disease outcomes.

## How Much Fat Do You Actually Need?

Fat is an essential macronutrient. You will die without it. But recommendations for how much vary enormously, from 20% to 70%+ of calories depending on who you ask.

**Competing claims:**

1. The minimum for health is low (~20-25% of calories), and going higher offers no benefit
2. Higher fat intakes (40%+) are metabolically advantageous for many people
3. Fat needs depend on the type of fat, not just the total amount
4. Essential fatty acid requirements are small, so the "minimum" is much lower than most guidelines suggest, and everything above that is just a calorie source

**Evidence needed:** Studies on minimum fat intake and hormonal/health outcomes, essential fatty acid requirement data, comparisons across fat intake ranges.

## Is Low-Fat or High-Fat Better for Weight Loss?

Low-fat was the dominant weight loss strategy for decades. Then high-fat/low-carb took over. Both camps cite evidence. Most people just want to know which one works.

**Competing claims:**

1. Low-fat diets are better because reducing the most calorie-dense macro creates the largest calorie deficit
2. High-fat diets are better because they control hunger and insulin, making it easier to sustain a deficit
3. Neither is inherently better; adherence and total calorie intake determine outcomes
4. The best approach depends on individual metabolic response, insulin sensitivity, and food preferences

**Evidence needed:** Head-to-head RCTs of low-fat vs high-fat diets, long-term adherence data, individual variability research.

## Does Dietary Cholesterol Raise Blood Cholesterol?

Eggs were vilified for decades because of their cholesterol content. Then guidelines dropped the cholesterol limit. Now some say dietary cholesterol matters again, at least for certain people.

**Competing claims:**

1. Dietary cholesterol significantly raises blood cholesterol and increases heart disease risk
2. The body compensates by reducing its own cholesterol production, so dietary cholesterol has minimal effect
3. Dietary cholesterol raises LDL in some people ("hyper-responders") but not others, and genetic variation explains the inconsistency
4. Eggs and other cholesterol-rich foods are fine because the cholesterol-heart disease link was overstated in the first place

**Evidence needed:** Controlled feeding studies on dietary cholesterol and blood lipids, hyper-responder research, egg consumption and CVD outcome data.

## Are Omega-3 Supplements Worth It?

Fish oil is one of the most popular supplements in the world. The claimed benefits range from heart health to brain function to joint pain. But large trials have produced mixed results.

**Competing claims:**

1. Omega-3 supplements reduce cardiovascular events and are worth taking for most people
2. Large recent RCTs show little to no benefit for primary prevention in healthy people
3. The dose matters: most studies used low doses, and higher-dose EPA trials show real benefit
4. Eating fatty fish is beneficial, but fish oil capsules don't replicate that benefit

**Evidence needed:** Large-scale omega-3 RCTs (VITAL, REDUCE-IT, STRENGTH), dose-response analyses, fish consumption vs supplement comparison data.

## Is Coconut Oil Healthy?

Coconut oil was marketed as a superfood despite being ~82% saturated fat. Health organizations warned against it. Advocates claimed medium-chain triglycerides make it different from other saturated fats.

**Competing claims:**

1. Coconut oil is healthy because its MCTs are metabolized differently and don't raise heart disease risk
2. Coconut oil raises LDL cholesterol more than most other cooking fats and should be limited
3. Coconut oil raises HDL too, so the net effect on cardiovascular risk is neutral
4. The MCT content in coconut oil is too low (mostly lauric acid, not the shorter-chain MCTs used in research) to deliver the claimed benefits

**Evidence needed:** RCTs comparing coconut oil to other fats on lipid panels and health outcomes, MCT chain-length metabolism research.

## Does Fat Timing Matter?

Should you avoid fat before workouts? Eat more fat at certain times of day? Does fat around training impair nutrient absorption or performance?

**Competing claims:**

1. Fat before or during exercise slows digestion and impairs performance
2. Fat timing doesn't matter for most people; total daily intake is what counts
3. Fat slows protein absorption post-workout, reducing the anabolic response
4. High-fat meals before bed impair sleep quality and recovery

**Evidence needed:** Nutrient timing studies involving fat, fat and gastric emptying research, fat co-ingestion with protein and muscle protein synthesis data.

## Are Trans Fats the Only "Bad" Fat?

Industrial trans fats are universally condemned and largely banned. But does that mean all other fats are fine? Or are there other fat types that deserve scrutiny?

**Competing claims:**

1. Trans fats are uniquely harmful, and once removed, fat type doesn't matter much
2. Saturated fat is also harmful, just less so than trans fat
3. Natural trans fats (from ruminant animals, like CLA and vaccenic acid) are different from industrial trans fats and may even be beneficial
4. The dose and dietary context matter more than fat classification; no single fat type is universally "bad" outside of industrial trans fats

**Evidence needed:** Dose-response data on trans fat and CVD, ruminant vs industrial trans fat comparisons, studies on interesterified fats as trans fat replacements.

## Is the Mediterranean Diet's Benefit Really About the Fat?

The Mediterranean diet is the most studied dietary pattern in the world. Olive oil gets much of the credit. But is the benefit actually from the fat, or from everything else the diet includes (and excludes)?

**Competing claims:**

1. The high monounsaturated fat content (especially olive oil) is the primary driver of the Mediterranean diet's health benefits
2. The benefit comes from the overall dietary pattern (vegetables, legumes, fish, whole grains), not specifically from the fat
3. Olive oil's polyphenols and antioxidants, not its fat content, explain the benefit
4. The Mediterranean diet works because of what it displaces (processed food, refined carbs, excess sugar), not because of what it adds

**Evidence needed:** PREDIMED trial data and reanalyses, olive oil intervention studies, Mediterranean diet component analyses.
