# Does Dietary Fat Make You Fat?

Dietary fat is the most energy-dense macronutrient, and many high-fat foods are also highly palatable. That combination can make it easier to overeat-but the best controlled evidence does not support the idea that dietary fat *uniquely* drives body fat gain independent of total energy intake **when total calories and protein are matched**. Instead, dietary fat affects body fatness mainly through how it changes calorie intake (energy density, food choice, satiety, adherence) and only modestly through physiology **in controlled-feeding comparisons** when calories are held constant. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636); [Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2); [Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))

## What the question is and is not

“Does dietary fat make you fat?” can mean at least two different things:

1. **Is fat inherently more “fattening” than carbs when calories are matched?** (an isocaloric question). ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))

2. **Do high-fat diets tend to cause people to eat more calories (and gain fat) in real life?** (an ad libitum / behavior question). ([Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1); [Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008))

Those are different questions with different best evidence.

## Calories per gram is real, but it is not the whole model

Using standard metabolizable energy factors, fat provides about **9 kcal per gram**, while carbohydrate and protein provide about **4 kcal per gram**. This is one reason higher-fat diets *can* be more calorie-dense depending on what replaces what (water, fiber, protein, etc.). ([Roberts & Flaherman, 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmac092))

But “fat has more calories per gram” does **not** automatically mean “higher fat intake causes fat gain,” because people do not eat grams of macronutrients in isolation-they eat foods and patterns that influence total energy intake and adherence. ([Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008); [Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

## Isocaloric feeding evidence: when calories are held constant, fat is not uniquely fattening

A clean way to test “fat vs carbs” is to hold protein and total calories constant (or tightly controlled) and vary carbohydrate vs fat.

In an inpatient metabolic-ward study in people with obesity, Hall and colleagues compared **calorie-matched** energy restriction achieved by either reducing dietary fat or reducing carbohydrate (“calorie for calorie”). The dietary fat-restriction arm produced greater **calculated** body fat loss (from indirect calorimetry-based macronutrient balance) over the short study period than the carbohydrate-restriction arm, **while DXA fat-mass changes were not significantly different over this brief window**. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))

This matters for two competing claims:

* **“Carbs spike insulin, so carbs uniquely drive fat storage.”** If that model were the dominant driver under calorie-matched conditions, you’d expect carbohydrate restriction (lower insulin) to produce clearly greater fat loss than fat restriction. That was not observed in this tightly controlled short-term comparison. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))
* **“Fat is calorie-dense, so it must be inherently more fattening.”** Under calorie-matched conditions, dietary fat was not “automatically” more fattening; in this test, reducing fat led to *more* **calculated** fat loss during the controlled period. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))

The important limitation is that this was **short-term** and tightly controlled-useful for mechanism-testing, not a full model of long-term free-living dieting. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))

## Ad libitum trials: high-fat is not guaranteed to reduce intake versus low-fat in controlled tests

The insulin-centered argument often becomes: **“High-fat / very low-carb diets lower insulin, so they reduce hunger and spontaneous calorie intake.”** A direct test requires feeding people different diets **ad libitum** while measuring intake.

In an inpatient randomized crossover trial, Hall and colleagues compared two **minimally processed diet patterns** for two weeks each:

* a **plant-based low-fat** diet (~10% of energy from fat; high carbohydrate), and
* an **animal-based ketogenic** diet (~76% of energy from fat; very low carbohydrate). ([Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))

Despite the ketogenic diet producing the expected low-carbohydrate physiology, the **low-fat diet led to ~689 kcal/day lower energy intake** than the ketogenic diet across the two-week period. The authors concluded that the predictions of the carbohydrate-insulin model (specifically, that low-carb would reduce ad libitum intake compared with low-fat/high-carb) were inconsistent with what they observed in this setup. ([Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))

So “high-fat increases satiety so you eat less” is **sometimes true in some contexts**, but it is not a rule that **necessarily** beats low-fat diets when diets are carefully constructed and measured. ([Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))

## Low-fat interventions do tend to slightly reduce body fatness over months to years

If “dietary fat makes you fat,” you’d expect reducing fat to reduce body fatness-but the size and context matter.

A Cochrane systematic review and meta-analysis focused on adults **not aiming to lose weight** and examined RCTs (≥6 months) that reduced total fat intake (commonly to ≤30% of energy). Across a very large body of trial evidence, **reducing total fat produced a small but consistent reduction in body fatness**, including an average body weight reduction of about **1.4 kg** (**with heterogeneity and some indications of small-study bias**), with parallel small reductions in BMI, waist circumference, and percent body fat. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636))

This supports a pragmatic interpretation:

* Lowering dietary fat can modestly reduce weight/fatness **even without an explicit weight-loss goal**, likely because it changes food choices and the energy density of the diet in ways that reduce energy intake. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636))

It does **not** imply that dietary fat is uniquely fattening in a calorie-matched vacuum. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636))

## Long-term RCTs: macronutrient ratio is usually a weak predictor of weight loss compared with adherence and food pattern

Over a year or more, differences between “low-fat” and “low-carb/high-fat” weight loss tend to be **small**, and variability between individuals is large.

### Healthy low-fat vs healthy low-carb (DIETFITS)

In the DIETFITS randomized clinical trial (12 months), there was **no significant difference** in weight loss between a “healthy low-fat” and a “healthy low-carbohydrate” diet pattern. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

This directly argues against “fat grams are destiny” *and* against “carbs/insulin are destiny,” at least as population-level rules for free-living weight loss over a year. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

### Different macro compositions, similar long-term weight loss (POUNDS LOST)

In a 2-year randomized trial comparing diets with different targets for fat, protein, and carbohydrate, average weight loss was **similar across the different macronutrient compositions**, with adherence declining over time (a common real-world limiter). ([Sacks et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa0804748))

### Systematic review perspective on low-carb vs balanced-carb weight loss

A Cochrane review comparing low-carbohydrate vs balanced-carbohydrate weight-reducing diets found **little to no difference** in weight change both in the **short term** (roughly a ~1 kg difference on average) and in the **long term** (average difference <1 kg), in people with and without type 2 diabetes. ([Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2))

This is consistent with the idea that macronutrient ratio may shift appetite and water balance for some people, but long-run fat loss is usually driven more by sustained energy deficit and adherence than by fat vs carb ratio per se. ([Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2); [Sacks et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa0804748))

## Why people can lose weight eating “butter”: appetite and food pattern effects can dominate macro math

Two evidence-supported realities can coexist:

1. **Ketogenic / very low-carb diets can reduce appetite in some settings**, or blunt the typical rise in hunger that accompanies weight loss. ([Gibson et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.12230))

2. **High-fat diets can also be easy to overconsume**, especially when they’re energy-dense and hyper-palatable, meaning weight loss is not guaranteed and depends on the overall pattern and intake. ([Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008); [Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))

A useful counterweight to “macro-only” thinking is that food form and processing can strongly affect intake. In an inpatient randomized crossover trial, an **ultra-processed diet caused higher ad libitum calorie intake and weight gain** compared with an unprocessed diet, even though the diets were designed to be matched on presented calories, **energy density**, and key nutrients/macros. ([Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008))

So when someone loses weight “eating butter,” the most evidence-consistent explanations are usually behavioral and dietary-pattern related (satiety, reduced food variety, fewer ultra-processed foods, higher protein, fewer trigger foods), not a special fat-burning effect that violates calorie balance. ([Gibson et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.12230); [Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1); [Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2))

## Quick reference findings

* Metabolizable energy factors: fat ~9 kcal/g; carbohydrate ~4 kcal/g; protein ~4 kcal/g. ([Roberts & Flaherman, 2022](https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmac092))
* Total fat reduction (≥6 months, adults not aiming for weight loss): lower-fat interventions produce a **small but consistent** reduction in body fatness (about **1.4 kg** lower body weight on average, with small improvements in BMI/waist/%fat). ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636))
* Isocaloric metabolic ward test (short-term): calorie-matched fat restriction produced more **calculated** body fat loss (fat balance) than calorie-matched carbohydrate restriction. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021))
* Ad libitum inpatient crossover: a **low-fat plant-based** diet led to **~689 kcal/day lower intake** than an **animal-based ketogenic** diet, contradicting the prediction that low-carb would automatically reduce ad libitum intake relative to low-fat/high-carb. ([Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))
* Long-term free-living RCT: “healthy low-fat” vs “healthy low-carb” showed **no significant difference** in 12-month weight loss. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))
* Cochrane review: low-carb vs balanced-carb weight-reducing diets show **little to no difference** in weight change (about ~1 kg average difference short term; <1 kg long term). ([Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2))
* Food processing signal: ultra-processed vs unprocessed diets (ad libitum inpatient) produced higher calorie intake and weight gain on the ultra-processed pattern. ([Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008))

## Practical interpretation

* **Dietary fat can contribute to fat gain if it increases total calorie intake**, which is common when fat comes packaged in energy-dense, highly palatable foods-but that is a *diet pattern* effect, not a property of fat that overrides calories. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636); [Hall et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008))
* **Lowering dietary fat tends to produce small average reductions in body fatness over months**, even in people not explicitly pursuing weight loss, but the effect size is modest. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013636))
* **“Insulin spikes from carbs” is not sufficient to explain body fat change** in tightly controlled comparisons; calorie-matched studies do not show a consistent fat-loss advantage for carbohydrate restriction over fat restriction. ([Hall et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.07.021); [Hall et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1))
* **High-fat diets can work for weight loss** when they help a person sustainably eat fewer calories (often via appetite effects, food restriction, or reduced ultra-processed food exposure), but they are not reliably superior long-term to well-constructed low-fat approaches. ([Gibson et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.12230); [Naude et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013334.pub2); [Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))
