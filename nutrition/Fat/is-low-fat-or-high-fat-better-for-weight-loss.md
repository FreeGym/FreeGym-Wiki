# Is Low-Fat or High-Fat Better for Weight Loss?

“Low-fat vs high-fat” sounds like a simple head-to-head question, but when you look at the strongest evidence (longer RCTs and meta-analyses of RCTs), the average differences in weight loss are usually small-and often fade with time. In long-term trials, **low-carbohydrate/high-fat tends to produce slightly more weight loss on average in the first 6-12 months**, but **by ~24 months the difference is commonly no longer statistically significant**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

## What “low-fat” and “high-fat” mean in the actual trials

Most of the “low-fat” arms in modern RCTs use thresholds around **<30% of energy from fat** (sometimes much lower), while “high-fat” weight-loss arms often mean **higher-fat paired with lower carbohydrate** rather than “high-fat with high carbohydrate.” ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

That matters because many real-world arguments are actually comparing:

* **Low-fat/high-carb** vs **low-carb/high-fat** (a *carbohydrate restriction* contrast), not “fat up vs fat down” in isolation. ([Jabbour et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.821096); [Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))

The common theoretical argument for low-fat-fat has more calories per gram than carbohydrate or protein-is true in principle (9 vs 4 kcal/g), but long-term RCT data do not show that simply lowering fat reliably produces *more* long-term weight loss than other well-supported approaches **on average**. ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

## What the best head-to-head RCTs show

### DIETFITS (12 months; “healthy low-fat” vs “healthy low-carb”)

In DIETFITS (609 adults, 12 months), mean weight loss was **-5.3 kg (low-fat)** vs **-6.0 kg (low-carb)**, with **no statistically significant difference** between groups. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

DIETFITS also directly tested a popular personalization claim: that **baseline insulin secretion** (and a specific genotype pattern) predicts which diet works better. It found **no diet × insulin secretion interaction** and **no diet × genotype interaction** for 12‑month weight loss. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

A key practical point from DIETFITS is that **individual responses varied widely** within each diet-about a ~40 kg spread in weight change (roughly from large loss to weight gain)-even though the group averages were similar. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

### Bazzano et al. (12 months; low-carb vs low-fat)

In a 12‑month RCT (148 adults), a low-carbohydrate diet was **more effective for weight loss** than a low-fat diet; the reported **between-group mean difference in weight change was -3.5 kg** favoring low carbohydrate. ([Bazzano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.7326/M14-0180))

So, even among strong RCTs, results are not identical: some show **little/no difference** (DIETFITS), while others show a **modest-to-moderate advantage** for low-carb/high-fat at 12 months. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245); [Bazzano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.7326/M14-0180))

## What meta-analyses of RCTs say about the “average” effect

Meta-analyses help answer the question “What happens on average across many head-to-head trials?”-and they consistently suggest: **differences are modest and time-dependent**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

### Long-term (≥1 year) low-fat vs higher-fat interventions

A large systematic review/meta-analysis of RCTs with **≥1 year** follow-up (53 studies; 68,128 participants) found: ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

* In weight-loss trials, **low-carbohydrate interventions produced ~1.15 kg more weight loss** than low-fat interventions on average. ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))
* Low-fat was **not better** than other higher-fat interventions of similar intensity; low-fat looked better mainly when compared to minimal “usual diet” controls. ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

### 6-24 months: low-carb vs low-fat (pairwise meta-analysis)

A meta-analysis of 33 RCTs (≥6 months; n=3,939) reported that compared with low-fat diets, low-carbohydrate diets produced: ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))

* **-1.33 kg greater weight loss** over **6-23 months** (overall). ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))
* A larger difference at **6-11 months** (about **-2.10 kg**), a smaller difference at **12-23 months** (about **-1.21 kg**), and **no statistically significant difference at 24 months**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))

### Time-course: early advantage, later convergence

In a meta-analysis focused on obesity RCTs comparing low-carb with balanced diets, low-carb produced significantly greater weight loss at:

* **3-4 months:** about **-2.59 kg**, and
* **6-8 months:** about **-2.64 kg**,

but the difference was **no longer statistically significant at 10-14 months**, and it **was not statistically significant by 18-30 months**. ([Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709))

### Long-term (≥12 months) macronutrient categories (network meta-analysis)

A network meta-analysis restricted to RCTs with **≥12 months** of active dietary counseling found that multiple approaches led to weight loss vs usual diet, including:

* high-fat/low-carb (HFLC) and
* low-fat/high-carb (LFHC),

with **no consistent large separation** between these two categories in the long term. ([Jabbour et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.821096))

### Newer long-term body composition synthesis (≥12 months)

A 2026 systematic review/meta-analysis focusing on body composition in long-term trials found **no clear difference in fat mass change** between high-fat/low-carb and low-fat/high-carb diets (in the included ≥12‑month RCTs). ([Jabbour et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf297))

## Hunger, insulin, adherence, and “why it works”

### “High-fat works better because it controls hunger”

If high-fat/low-carb reliably produced large appetite advantages, you’d expect consistently larger long-term weight loss differences. Instead, the typical between-diet differences across RCTs are **small** and often **diminish over time**, which is **not consistent with a large, durable appetite advantage on average**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

Direct appetite outcomes are also not a slam dunk for low-fat. A 2025 systematic review of RCTs comparing low-fat (≤30% fat) vs higher-fat diets found **little or no additional benefit on appetite status** overall, with limited and conflicting hunger findings and generally no effect on satiety/desire to eat/palatability-while also noting the evidence base was small and methodologically limited. ([Razmpoosh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-025-23454-0))

### “High-fat works better because it controls insulin”

The strongest direct test of “insulin status predicts which macro split will work better” in a large, 12‑month head-to-head trial did **not** support that claim: DIETFITS found **no interaction** between baseline insulin secretion and diet assignment on weight loss. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

That doesn’t prove insulin biology is irrelevant-only that **baseline insulin secretion (as measured in DIETFITS) didn’t identify a clear winner** between these two diet strategies for weight loss at 12 months. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))

### “Neither is better; adherence determines outcomes”

This is directionally supported by several strands of evidence:

* Long-term RCTs often suffer from substantial dropout and adherence challenges, creating attrition bias risks and shrinking diet separation over time. ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8); [Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))
* In a 12‑month analysis of adherence measures within a low-carb vs low-fat trial, overall adherence indicators were similar between groups, but **greater adherence to the low-carb pattern correlated with more weight and fat loss**, while low-fat adherence indicators were not clearly associated with weight loss in that dataset. ([Hu et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/osp4.23))

A practical synthesis that fits the trial literature is: **macronutrient labels alone don’t guarantee results; sustained implementation appears to matter more**, and many people drift toward a more moderate intake over time, which reduces differences between diet arms. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))

## Quick reference values from higher-level evidence

* **DIETFITS (12 months):** -5.3 kg (low-fat) vs -6.0 kg (low-carb); no significant difference; no insulin/genotype interaction. ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))
* **Bazzano RCT (12 months):** low-carb produced -3.5 kg greater weight loss than low-fat (between-group difference). ([Bazzano et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.7326/M14-0180))
* **Tobias meta-analysis (≥1 year):** low-carb weight-loss interventions averaged **~1.15 kg** more loss than low-fat; low-fat not superior to other well-supported higher-fat interventions of similar intensity. ([Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))
* **Lei meta-analysis (≥6 months):** low-carb averaged **-1.33 kg** more loss than low-fat over 6-23 months; **no statistically significant difference at 24 months**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234))
* **Silverii meta-analysis (obesity RCTs; time course):** low-carb had ~2.6 kg more loss early (3-8 months), but the difference was not significant by ~10-14 months and was **not statistically significant by ~18-30 months**. ([Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709))
* **Jabbour network meta-analysis (≥12 months):** HFLC and LFHC both produced weight loss vs usual diet, with no large separation between them. ([Jabbour et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.821096))
* **Jabbour 2026 (≥12 months; body composition):** no clear advantage of HFLC over LFHC for long-term fat mass change in included RCTs. ([Jabbour et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf297))

## Practical interpretation

* **If you only care about the scale at 12-24 months, neither low-fat nor high-fat is a guaranteed winner.** Average differences are small in meta-analyses, and they often shrink with longer follow-up. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))
* **Low-carb/high-fat has a modest average edge earlier on**, but the advantage is typically on the order of ~1-2 kg in meta-analyses and is often **no longer statistically significant by ~24 months**. ([Lei et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.935234); [Silverii et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14709); [Tobias et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587%2815%2900367-8))
* **Appetite claims are not strongly settled by RCT evidence**, and low-fat does not appear to reliably improve appetite outcomes in the limited RCT literature that directly measured them. ([Razmpoosh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-025-23454-0))
* **“Choose based on insulin sensitivity” is not well supported as a general rule** by the strongest direct evidence in a large 12‑month RCT (no insulin secretion interaction in DIETFITS). ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))
* **Expect large individual variability either way**, and be willing to switch strategies if you’re not trending in the desired direction (wide response range in DIETFITS despite similar averages). ([Gardner et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2018.0245))
