# How Much Fat Do You Actually Need?

“Minimum fat” can mean two very different things: the **physiologic minimum** required to avoid deficiency (mostly **essential fatty acids**) versus a **practical intake range** that supports long-term health outcomes (which depends heavily on **fat type and food pattern**, not just total grams or % of calories). ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

The evidence base suggests that the **true deficiency-prevention minimum is much lower** ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061); [Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009)) than common “20-35% of calories” guidance (Institute of Medicine, 2005), but that **what you do above the minimum can matter a lot** for cardiometabolic risk-especially **what replaces what** (e.g., saturated fat replaced by polyunsaturated fat). ([Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000252); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

## What “minimum fat” is and is not

A strict “minimum” is most defensible when defined as the intake needed to prevent **essential fatty acid deficiency (EFAD)**, because EFAD has identifiable biochemical markers and clinical manifestations in settings of extremely low fat intake. ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061); [Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009))

That deficiency-prevention minimum is **not the same** as the intake that optimizes outcomes like cardiovascular events, lipids, or glycemic control, which depend strongly on **fat type** and the **overall dietary pattern**. ([Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000252); [Estruch et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

## The truly essential requirement: linoleic acid and alpha-linolenic acid

Two polyunsaturated fatty acids-**linoleic acid (LA)** and **alpha-linolenic acid (ALA)**-are considered essential in humans because they cannot be synthesized and must be obtained from the diet. ([Wolff et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/ncp.11278))

In a controlled experiment in healthy adult men, **continuous fat-free feeding** (intravenous or nasogastric) produced biochemical evidence of essential fatty acid deficiency **within days, with changes becoming more pronounced by about 10-14 days**; and the study also suggested that intermittent feeding/fasting periods could partially restore essential fatty acids via mobilization of stores. ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061))

In adults receiving continuous parenteral nutrition, a clinical study reported that EFAD was prevented when **≥3.2% of total calories** were provided as intravenous fat, and the authors reported **7.7 g/day of linoleic acid** (via a soybean-oil lipid emulsion) prevented EFAD **in that PN setting**. ([Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009))

A contemporary clinical review summarizing prevention targets reports adult EFAD-prevention dosing on the order of **~2-4% of total energy from LA** and **~0.25-0.5% of total energy from ALA** (presented as guidance to prevent EFAD). ([Wolff et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/ncp.11278))

## If the essential-fatty-acid minimum is small, why do “minimum fat” recommendations look larger?

**One reason is definitional: commonly cited “20-35% of calories from fat” ranges are typically “acceptable macronutrient distribution ranges” (AMDRs) intended as population-level ranges for overall nutrient adequacy and chronic-disease risk, not EFAD-prevention thresholds. (Institute of Medicine, 2005)**

One reason is practical: even if EFAD can be prevented with a few percent of energy from LA/ALA, diets that are *very* low in fat can make it harder to absorb and utilize certain **fat-soluble compounds** in real meals, especially carotenoids. ([Brown et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/80.2.396); [Goltz et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.201100687); [Unlu et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/135.3.431))

Another is that long-term health outcomes depend not just on “how low can you go,” but on **what macronutrient replaces fat** and **which fats you choose** (e.g., replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat has different outcome data than replacing fat with refined carbohydrate). ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3); [Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000252))

## Fat as a nutrient-delivery tool: absorption of carotenoids rises with meal fat

In a randomized crossover study (n=7), carotenoid appearance in chylomicrons after a salad meal was **negligible with a fat-free dressing (0 g oil)** and increased stepwise with **6 g vs 28 g** of canola oil in the dressing. ([Brown et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/80.2.396))

In another human feeding study, carotenoid absorption was driven more by **the amount of lipid (3 vs 8 vs 20 g)** than by the lipid source, with **20 g** promoting higher absorption for most carotenoids compared with lower-fat conditions. ([Goltz et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.201100687))

In two crossover postprandial studies (n=11 each), adding avocado or avocado oil to salsa/salad-providing **~12-24 g lipid** depending on dose-markedly increased postprandial carotenoid absorption compared with avocado-free control meals. ([Unlu et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/135.3.431))

## Hormones: does “too low fat” meaningfully depress sex hormones?

A 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis of intervention studies in men (6 eligible studies; total n=206) found that **low-fat vs higher-fat diets** were associated with small-to-moderate reductions in several androgen measures, including total testosterone and free testosterone (reported as standardized mean differences). ([Whittaker & Wu, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsbmb.2021.105878)) **(Note: a corrigendum to this paper was published in January 2026; interpret effect sizes in light of that notice.)** ([Whittaker & Wu, 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsbmb.2025.106880))

However, a newer 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (11 trials; n=888) comparing **low-fat diets (≤30% energy from fat)** versus higher-fat diets reported **no significant differences** in multiple circulating sex hormones (including testosterone) and explicitly noted **low certainty** due to the limited evidence base. ([Soltani et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1111/1750-3841.70266))

Taken together, the best current synthesis suggests that any effect of lowering fat on sex hormones is likely **context-dependent and not consistently detectable across trials**, especially when definitions of “low-fat” and background diet quality vary. ([Whittaker & Wu, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsbmb.2021.105878); [Soltani et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1111/1750-3841.70266))

## Cardiometabolic outcomes: the case that “fat type” matters more than “fat %”

A Cochrane systematic review of long-term randomized trials (≥24 months; 15 RCTs; 56,675 participants) concluded that reducing saturated fat intake **reduced combined cardiovascular events** (RR ~0.83) while showing **little or no effect on all-cause or cardiovascular mortality**, and the review reported benefits consistent with replacing saturated fat energy with polyunsaturated fat or carbohydrate in the included trial patterns. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

A systematic review/meta-analysis of RCTs specifically increasing polyunsaturated fat in place of saturated fat (8 trials; 13,614 participants; 1,042 CHD events) reported a pooled **~19% reduction in CHD events** (RR ~0.81) in the higher-PUFA groups. ([Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000252))

A major dietary-pattern RCT (PREDIMED; republished analysis) found that a Mediterranean diet supplemented with **extra-virgin olive oil or nuts** reduced major cardiovascular events compared with a control group receiving low-fat diet advice, supporting that a **higher-fat pattern can be beneficial** when the fat source and overall pattern are favorable. ([Estruch et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389))

## Does “20-25% of calories from fat” look like a true minimum for health?

A large randomized trial of a low-fat dietary pattern in postmenopausal women (Women’s Health Initiative Dietary Modification Trial) reported **no significant reduction** in coronary heart disease, stroke, or overall cardiovascular disease over the intervention period, suggesting that lowering dietary fat to a “low-fat pattern” is **not inherently harmful** but also **not automatically protective** for CVD outcomes. ([Howard et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.295.6.655))

At the same time, EFAD-prevention evidence and clinical dosing targets indicate that the **deficiency-prevention minimum** for essential fatty acids is on the order of **a few percent of total energy**, which is far below 20-25% total fat. ([Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009); [Wolff et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/ncp.11278))

So, “~20-25% fat” is better interpreted as a **practical eating pattern target** used in some dietary strategies, not a biologic minimum dictated by essential fatty acid needs. ([Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009); [Howard et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.295.6.655))

## Are higher-fat intakes (40%+) metabolically advantageous for many people?

In type 2 diabetes, a systematic review/meta-analysis of randomized trials (22 RCTs; n=1391) found that low-carbohydrate diets (which typically raise fat and/or protein relative to low-fat comparators) produced greater short-term improvements in HbA1c and several cardiometabolic markers (e.g., triglycerides and HDL-C), but also reported increases in total cholesterol, with many outcomes showing no clear differences. ([Apekey et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu14204391))

This pattern of mixed-direction changes supports a narrower conclusion: “higher-fat” approaches can be **advantageous for some markers in some populations**, but they are not uniformly superior across risk factors-and the tradeoffs likely depend on **fat type**, the **degree of carbohydrate restriction**, and the baseline metabolic context. ([Apekey et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu14204391); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

## Quick reference values

* **EFAD can develop** in healthy adults exposed to sustained fat-free feeding in controlled conditions (**biochemical markers can appear within days under continuous fat-free feeding, becoming more pronounced by ~10-14 days in classic protocols**). ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061))

* **Clinical EFAD prevention in adults on continuous PN:** EFAD prevented with **≥3.2% of total calories as IV fat**; **7.7 g/day linoleic acid** via soybean-oil emulsion reported as adequate **in that PN setting** in that study. ([Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009))

* **Summarized prevention targets (adult):** roughly **~2-4% energy from LA** and **~0.25-0.5% energy from ALA** as prevention guidance reported in a contemporary clinical review. ([Wolff et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/ncp.11278))

* **Carotenoid absorption from salads:** essentially none with **fat-free dressing**, higher with **reduced-fat**, and highest with **full-fat** dressing in a controlled crossover study. ([Brown et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/80.2.396))

* **Dose effect:** **20 g lipid** promoted higher carotenoid absorption than **3 g** or **8 g** in a human meal study. ([Goltz et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.201100687))

* **Sex hormones:** newer RCT meta-analysis reports **no significant differences** in multiple sex hormones comparing low-fat (≤30% energy) vs higher-fat diets, with **low certainty** noted. ([Soltani et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1111/1750-3841.70266))

* **CVD events and saturated fat:** long-term RCT evidence synthesis suggests reducing saturated fat reduces **combined cardiovascular events** (RR ~0.83), with little/no mortality effect detected. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3))

## Practical interpretation

* If you mean **“minimum to avoid deficiency,”** the best direct human evidence suggests you need **some** dietary LA/ALA, but the required amount to prevent EFAD is **small (a few % of energy)** under clinical prevention targets and classic feeding data. ([Wene et al., 1975](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI108061); [Barr et al., 1981](https://doi.org/10.1097/00000658-198103000-00009); [Wolff et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/ncp.11278))

* If you mean **“minimum to function well on a real diet,”** evidence from controlled meal studies shows that including some fat with plant foods can materially improve absorption of carotenoids (and likely other fat-associated compounds), which makes “near-zero fat” diets harder to execute without nutritional downsides. ([Brown et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/80.2.396); [Goltz et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.201100687); [Unlu et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/135.3.431))

* For long-term cardiometabolic health, the strongest trial-level evidence is less about total fat and more about **fat quality and substitutions** (especially replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat, and Mediterranean-style patterns rich in unsaturated fats). ([Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1000252); [Hooper et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub3); [Estruch et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389))
