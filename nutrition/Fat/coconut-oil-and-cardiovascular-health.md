# Coconut Oil and Cardiovascular Health

Coconut oil is not a cardiovascular “superfood.” In randomized trials, it is generally less LDL-raising than butter, but it usually performs worse than nontropical vegetable oils or other commonly used unsaturated oils when the comparison is atherogenic lipid markers. It often raises HDL cholesterol as well, but the available trials are mostly short and centered on lipid and other risk-marker outcomes rather than direct evidence of fewer cardiovascular events. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/); [Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/); [Khaw et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29511019/))

## What coconut oil is and is not

Coconut oil is a saturated-fat-rich oil whose fatty acid profile is dominated by lauric acid rather than the shorter-chain fats usually meant by “MCT oil.” In one commonly cited comparison, coconut oil was about 47% lauric acid (C12:0), 9% caprylic acid (C8:0), and 7% capric acid (C10:0), whereas manufactured MCT oils were predominantly C8 and C10 with very little lauric acid. A 2021 meta-analysis of MCT oil trials likewise defined MCT oil as consisting almost exclusively of 6:0–10:0 fatty acids. ([Eyres et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26946252/); [McKenzie et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34255085/))

That distinction matters because lauric acid does not behave metabolically like classic C8/C10 MCT oil. Although lauric acid is sometimes grouped as a medium-chain fatty acid by chain length, the majority of it is absorbed with chylomicrons rather than going directly through the portal vein like the shorter-chain MCTs. In the Eyres review, about 70 to 75% of lauric acid was absorbed with chylomicrons, whereas about 95% of medium-chain fatty acids were described as entering via the portal vein. ([Eyres et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26946252/))

## What randomized trials show on lipid panels

The clearest pooled signal comes from a 2020 systematic review/meta-analysis of 16 clinical trials. Compared with non-tropical vegetable oils, coconut oil increased LDL cholesterol by 10.47 mg/dL (95% CI 3.01 to 17.94) and HDL cholesterol by 4.00 mg/dL (95% CI 2.26 to 5.73), with no significant effect on glycemia, inflammation, or adiposity. The authors concluded that coconut oil consumption results in significantly higher LDL cholesterol than nontropical vegetable oils, which should inform choices about coconut oil consumption. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/))

A second 2020 systematic review/meta-analysis reached a similar overall conclusion. Coconut oil significantly increased total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, and HDL cholesterol versus control oils, and subgroup analyses suggested higher LDL than corn, soybean, safflower, and similar oils, but a more favorable pattern than butter. ([Jayawardena et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33096510/))

An updated 2022 meta-analysis of randomized trials found no clinically relevant improvement in LDL, triglycerides, body weight, waist circumference, or body fat compared with other oils/fats, although HDL rose modestly by 3.28 mg/dL. Importantly, the authors judged the overall risk of bias to be high and the certainty of evidence to be very low. ([Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/))

Individual trials help explain why some people come away with mixed impressions. In the 2018 _BMJ Open_ trial, 50 g/day of coconut oil for 4 weeks did not differ from olive oil for LDL change, but it raised HDL more; butter raised LDL substantially more than either oil. In a separate 2018 crossover trial, foods made with corn oil produced a more favorable non-HDL-cholesterol response than foods made with coconut oil, with LDL cholesterol and total cholesterol also tending to favor corn oil. In a 2024 trial in adults with central obesity, extra-virgin olive oil produced lower mean LDL cholesterol than extra-virgin coconut oil after 12 weeks, and hs-CRP did not differ among groups, although the authors concluded that the three oils had broadly similar overall cardiometabolic effects. ([Khaw et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29511019/); [Maki et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30204921/); [Teng et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38372798/))

## Does the HDL increase make the overall effect neutral?

The evidence does not establish that. What the trials show is that coconut oil often raises HDL alongside LDL. What they do **not** show is that this combination is neutral or beneficial for myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular death, because most trials were short and measured lipids rather than hard outcomes. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/); [Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/))

There is one longer randomized trial worth noting: in patients with stable coronary artery disease, coconut oil and sunflower oil used as cooking media for 2 years did not differ significantly in lipids, endothelial function, or cardiovascular events. But that trial was conducted in patients with stable coronary artery disease receiving standard medical care. ([Vijayakumar et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27543472/))

## Why the MCT argument overstates the case

The best-known benefits of MCTs come from trials using **true** MCT preparations, not ordinary coconut oil. Meta-analyses of randomized MCT trials have shown modest reductions in body weight, waist circumference, and fat mass when longer-chain triglycerides are replaced with MCTs. But those trials were explicitly about MCT oils rich in C8 and C10, not about a lauric-acid-dominant oil like coconut oil. ([Bueno et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25651239/); [Mumme and Stonehouse, 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25636220/); [Eyres et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26946252/))

Even for actual MCT oil, the story is not “all saturated fats become harmless.” A 2021 meta-analysis found that MCT oil did not materially change LDL, total cholesterol, or HDL overall, but subgroup analyses suggested that MCT oil could raise LDL and total cholesterol when the comparator was predominantly unsaturated fat. Controlled feeding trials of lauric acid itself also found that it raises total and LDL cholesterol compared with oleic acid, even if its HDL effect is sometimes larger than that of palmitic acid. ([McKenzie et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34255085/); [Denke and Grundy, 1992](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1415008/); [Temme et al., 1996](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8644684/))

## What newer research changes

A newer 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis focused specifically on **virgin** coconut oil adds a VCO-specific perspective. Across 14 RCTs and 1,049 participants, virgin coconut oil significantly lowered triglycerides and increased HDL cholesterol, while showing no significant pooled effect on LDL, total cholesterol, blood pressure, CRP, fasting blood sugar, or anthropometric measures. However, the included studies were still short (2 to 24 weeks), heterogeneous, and compared virgin coconut oil with placebo or a variety of other oils rather than asking a single clean question about replacing unsaturated cooking oils. These findings concern virgin coconut oil specifically and should be interpreted in that context. ([Zhang et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41444640/))

## Limits and open debates

The biggest limitation is not that coconut oil has been understudied on lipids; it is that it has been studied mostly in **small, short, heterogeneous** trials. That is enough to say something useful about LDL, HDL, and triglycerides, but not enough to establish long-term cardiovascular safety or benefit. It also means that differences between virgin and refined coconut oil, background diet, dose, and comparator oil still matter. ([Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/); [Zhang et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41444640/))

A second limitation is that evidence from whole-coconut foods or traditional dietary patterns cannot simply be transferred to isolated coconut oil. The food matrix, fiber, and overall diet differ. For the question “is coconut oil healthy?” the strongest direct evidence still comes from oil-versus-oil randomized comparisons. ([Eyres et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26946252/); [Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/))

## Quick reference values

- Compared with non-tropical vegetable oils, coconut oil increased LDL cholesterol by **10.47 mg/dL** and HDL cholesterol by **4.00 mg/dL** in a 2020 meta-analysis of 16 trials. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/))
    
- In a 2022 meta-analysis of 7 randomized trials, coconut oil showed **no significant pooled effect** on LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, body weight, waist circumference, or body fat, but HDL cholesterol increased by **3.28 mg/dL**; certainty was **very low**. ([Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/))
    
- In the 2018 Khaw trial, coconut oil and olive oil did **not** differ significantly for LDL change over 4 weeks, but coconut oil increased HDL more; butter raised LDL more than either. ([Khaw et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29511019/))
    
- In the 2018 Maki crossover trial, corn oil produced a more favorable response than coconut oil for non-HDL cholesterol, with LDL and total cholesterol also tending to favor corn oil. ([Maki et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30204921/))
    
- In the 2025 virgin-coconut-oil meta-analysis, triglycerides fell by **12.12 mg/dL** and HDL rose by **7.91 mg/dL**, but pooled LDL change was not significant. ([Zhang et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41444640/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the comparison is **butter**, coconut oil often looks less adverse on LDL. ([Khaw et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29511019/); [Jayawardena et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33096510/))
    
- If the comparison is **olive, corn, safflower, sunflower, canola, or similar unsaturated oils**, coconut oil is generally **not** the better choice for lowering atherogenic lipids. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/); [Maki et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30204921/); [Teng et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38372798/))
    
- The argument that coconut oil is healthy because it contains “MCTs” overstates the case. The supportive MCT literature largely concerns **C8/C10-rich MCT oil**, whereas coconut oil is dominated by **lauric acid (C12:0)**, which is metabolized differently. ([Eyres et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26946252/); [McKenzie et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34255085/))
    
- On present trial evidence, coconut oil is better viewed as a saturated fat than as a heart-healthy superfood. When coconut oil compares favorably, that is usually against butter rather than unsaturated oils. ([Neelakantan et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31928080/); [Duarte et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36045407/); [Khaw et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29511019/); [Jayawardena et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33096510/))
