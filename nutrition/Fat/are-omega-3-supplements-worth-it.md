# Are Omega-3 Supplements Worth It?

Omega-3 supplements are not a single intervention. Large outcome trials have tested at least two importantly different strategies: standard mixed EPA+DHA fish-oil capsules, usually around 1 g/day, and prescription high-dose purified EPA (icosapent ethyl) at 4 g/day in statin-treated patients with elevated triglycerides. Those are not interchangeable questions, and treating them as the same is one reason the trial literature can look contradictory. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)). ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)).

## What omega-3 supplements are and are not

The major broad-use cardiovascular trials discussed here tested mixed marine omega-3 products containing both EPA and DHA. By contrast, REDUCE-IT tested icosapent ethyl, a highly purified EPA ethyl ester, at 4 g/day in a statin-treated, higher-risk population. A positive result with purified EPA should therefore not be assumed to apply to ordinary mixed fish-oil capsules. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)). ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)).

## What large primary-prevention trials found

In VITAL, 25,871 adults without prior cardiovascular disease were randomized to 1 g/day of fish oil containing 840 mg of EPA+DHA. Over a median 5.3 years, the primary composite of major cardiovascular events was not significantly reduced (HR 0.92, 95% CI 0.80 to 1.06), although total myocardial infarction was lower (HR 0.72, 95% CI 0.59 to 0.90). ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/ "Marine n-3 Fatty Acids and Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease and Cancer - PMC"))

In ASCEND, 15,480 adults with diabetes but no established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease received 1 g/day of omega-3 or placebo for a mean 7.4 years. Serious vascular events were not significantly reduced (8.9% vs 9.2%; rate ratio 0.97, 95% CI 0.87 to 1.08), and the broader composite including revascularization was also neutral. ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/ "Effects of n-3 Fatty Acid Supplements in Diabetes Mellitus - PubMed"))

Taken together, VITAL and ASCEND argue against the claim that routine low-dose omega-3 supplementation is clearly worthwhile for primary prevention in most healthy or otherwise stable adults. They do not show zero biological effect, but they do show little or no clear benefit on the main cardiovascular endpoints that matter most. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)).

## What high-dose prescription EPA trials found

REDUCE-IT enrolled 8,179 statin-treated patients with triglycerides of 135 to 499 mg/dL and either established cardiovascular disease or diabetes plus additional risk factors. Participants received icosapent ethyl 2 g twice daily (4 g/day total). Over a median 4.9 years, the primary endpoint occurred in 17.2% of the EPA group and 22.0% of the placebo group (HR 0.75, 95% CI 0.68 to 0.83), and cardiovascular death was also lower (4.3% vs 5.2%; HR 0.80, 95% CI 0.66 to 0.98). ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/ "Cardiovascular Risk Reduction with Icosapent Ethyl for Hypertriglyceridemia - PubMed"))

But STRENGTH tested a different high-dose strategy: 4 g/day of a carboxylic-acid formulation of EPA+DHA in 13,078 statin-treated patients at high cardiovascular risk with hypertriglyceridemia and low HDL cholesterol. That trial was stopped early for futility, and the primary endpoint was neutral (12.0% vs 12.2%; HR 0.99, 95% CI 0.90 to 1.09). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/ "Effect of High-Dose Omega-3 Fatty Acids vs Corn Oil on Major Adverse Cardiovascular Events in Patients at High Cardiovascular Risk: The STRENGTH Randomized Clinical Trial - PubMed"))

Interpretation of REDUCE-IT is also complicated by ongoing debate about the mineral-oil placebo. ([Visseren et al., 2021](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf)). ([ESC](https://eas-society.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/2021-Prevention-Guidelines.pdf "2021 ESC Guidelines on cardiovascular disease prevention in clinical practice - PDF"))

This means the strongest positive cardiovascular outcome evidence in this literature is for icosapent ethyl in REDUCE-IT, not for omega-3 supplements in general; mixed EPA+DHA did not reproduce benefit in STRENGTH. ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)).

## Why the results conflict

The simplest explanation is not just that dose matters. Dose probably matters, but formulation and clinical context matter too. Low-dose mixed EPA+DHA trials in primary prevention were neutral on their main endpoints (VITAL, ASCEND), purified EPA was positive in REDUCE-IT, and high-dose mixed EPA+DHA was neutral in STRENGTH. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)). ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)).

A secondary STRENGTH analysis is especially relevant here. Even among participants who achieved the highest plasma EPA or DHA levels on treatment, cardiovascular outcomes were not better than with corn oil placebo. That weakens the argument that the neutral STRENGTH result was simply due to inadequate achieved omega-3 levels within that trial, suggesting that dose alone is unlikely to explain the result. ([Nissen et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33993205/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33993205/ "Association Between Achieved ω-3 Fatty Acid Levels and Major Adverse Cardiovascular Outcomes in Patients With High Cardiovascular Risk: A Secondary Analysis of the STRENGTH Trial - PubMed"))

## What the meta-analyses show

Meta-analyses of randomized trials produce a more nuanced picture than either extreme claim. The 2020 Cochrane review concluded that long-chain omega-3 intake makes little or no difference to combined cardiovascular events, and all-cause mortality was essentially unchanged (RR 0.97, 95% CI 0.93 to 1.01), although it also found modest possible reductions in coronary heart disease mortality (RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.81 to 1.00) and coronary heart disease events (RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.85 to 0.97). ([Abdelhamid et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7049091/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7049091/ "Omega‐3 fatty acids for the primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease - PMC"))

A 2021 formulation-based meta-analysis found that supplementation below 2 one-gram capsules/day was not associated with cardiovascular benefit and argued that any apparent benefit from higher doses still required stronger replication. ([Rizos et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32820013/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32820013/ "Omega-3 supplementation and cardiovascular disease: formulation-based systematic review and meta-analysis with trial sequential analysis - PubMed"))

A larger 2021 meta-analysis of 38 RCTs found modest reductions in cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.93), non-fatal MI (RR 0.87), CHD events (RR 0.91), MACE (RR 0.95), and revascularization (RR 0.91), with larger relative effects in EPA monotherapy than in EPA+DHA trials. The same analysis found higher incident atrial fibrillation with omega-3 therapy overall (RR 1.26, 95% CI 1.08 to 1.48). ([Khan et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34505026/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34505026/ "Effect of omega-3 fatty acids on cardiovascular outcomes: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis reached a similar practical conclusion: omega-3 fatty acids may reduce major cardiovascular events, myocardial infarction, and cardiovascular death on average, but the case is strongest for prescription EPA ethyl ester formulations rather than omega-3 products in general; atrial fibrillation remains a relevant tradeoff, and bleeding concern appears most clearly with prescription EPA ethyl ester formulations rather than uniformly across all omega-3 products. ([Yan et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36103100/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36103100/ "Efficacy and Safety of Omega-3 Fatty Acids in the Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

## Fish intake and fish-oil capsules are not the same evidence question

Evidence favoring fish intake comes mainly from prospective cohort studies, not capsule trials. A 2021 meta-analysis of 25 cohort studies found that higher fish intake was associated with lower cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.85 to 0.98), and the dose-response analysis estimated about 4% lower cardiovascular mortality risk per 20 g/day increase in fish intake. Higher marine omega-3 intake was similarly associated with lower cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.87, 95% CI 0.85 to 0.89). ([Jiang et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34371852/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34371852/ "Intake of Fish and Marine n-3 Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids and Risk of Cardiovascular Disease Mortality: A Meta-Analysis of Prospective Cohort Studies - PubMed"))

That does not prove fish is inherently superior to capsules. It does show that the evidence bases are different: favorable fish data come largely from observational diet studies, whereas the major primary-prevention capsule trials (VITAL and ASCEND) were neutral on their primary cardiovascular endpoints. Habitual fish intake and fish-oil supplementation should therefore not be treated as interchangeable evidence. ([Jiang et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34371852/)). ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)).

## Limits and open debates

The omega-3 literature mixes different molecules, doses, and baseline-risk populations. As a result, the broad statements “omega-3 supplements work” and “omega-3 supplements do not work” are both too crude. The most defensible reading is narrower: routine low-dose supplementation for broad primary prevention is weakly supported (VITAL, ASCEND), while prescription high-dose EPA has the strongest positive outcome evidence (REDUCE-IT); mixed EPA+DHA did not reproduce that benefit in STRENGTH. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)). ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)).

## Practical interpretation

- For most people taking standard fish-oil capsules “just in case,” the major primary-prevention RCTs do not show a clear cardiovascular payoff. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)).
    
- The claim that omega-3 supplements are worth taking for most healthy adults is not supported by the major primary-prevention RCTs. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)).
    
- The claim that “dose matters” is partly true, but incomplete. Formulation and population matter too. High-dose purified EPA was beneficial in REDUCE-IT, but high-dose mixed EPA+DHA was not beneficial in STRENGTH. ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([Nicholls et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33190147/)).
    
- Eating fish is supported by favorable cohort data, but fish-oil capsules have not reliably reproduced a broad primary-prevention benefit in major primary-prevention randomized trials. ([Jiang et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34371852/)). ([Manson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392053/)). ([Bowman et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146932/)).
    
- A well-supported clinical niche for omega-3 therapy is prescription icosapent ethyl as an adjunct to maximally tolerated statin therapy in adults with triglycerides ≥150 mg/dL and established cardiovascular disease, or diabetes plus 2 or more additional cardiovascular risk factors. Even there, atrial fibrillation risk should be part of the discussion, and bleeding should also be discussed, especially in patients taking concomitant antithrombotic therapy. ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415628/)). ([FDA label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2019/202057s035lbl.pdf "VASCEPA prescribing information - FDA"))
