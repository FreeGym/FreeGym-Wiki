# Are Trans Fats the Only “Bad” Fat?

Industrial trans fats are the clearest example of a harmful dietary fat. But once they are removed, fat quality does not become irrelevant. The strongest human evidence supports a more layered view: industrial trans fats are uniquely harmful; saturated fat still matters, especially relative to what replaces it; ruminant trans fats appear less harmful at typical intakes than industrial trans fats but are not clearly beneficial. ([de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/); [Jayedi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37632423/))

## Why industrial trans fats stand apart

In the current evidence base, industrial trans fats remain the clearest “bad” fat. In a systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies, total trans fat intake was associated with higher all-cause mortality, coronary heart disease (CHD) mortality, and total CHD. A newer 2024 systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis did not overturn that pattern: dietary trans fatty acids still showed a modest association with higher risk of coronary events. ([de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/); [Jayedi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37632423/))

The dose-response data also support a causal interpretation. In quantitative analyses combining controlled feeding trials and prospective cohorts, each 1% of energy moved away from trans fat improved blood lipoprotein ratios, and each 2% of energy replaced from trans fat with saturated fat, monounsaturated fat, or polyunsaturated fat was associated with 17%, 21%, or 24% lower CHD risk, respectively. That is why industrial trans fat is not just “a little worse” than other fats; it is consistently worse across multiple comparison models. ([Mozaffarian and Clarke, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19424216/))

## Saturated fat still deserves scrutiny, but replacement matters more

The idea that “once trans fats are gone, fat type doesn’t matter much” is not supported. Long-term randomized trials compiled in the 2020 Cochrane review found that reducing saturated fat lowered combined cardiovascular events by 17%, even though it did not clearly lower all-cause or cardiovascular mortality. The newer 2024 dose-response meta-analysis likewise concluded that replacing saturated fat with linoleic acid or alpha-linolenic acid lowers coronary events. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32827219/); [Jayedi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37632423/))

But saturated fat is not industrial trans fat, and the replacement nutrient changes the answer. In the Nurses’ Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study, replacing 5% of energy from saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat, monounsaturated fat, or carbohydrate from whole grains was associated with 25%, 15%, and 9% lower CHD risk, respectively. In contrast, refined starches and added sugars were positively associated with CHD. A meta-analysis of randomized trials found a 19% lower CHD event risk when polyunsaturated fat replaced saturated fat, corresponding to about 10% lower CHD risk for each 5% of energy shifted. ([Li et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26429077/); [Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20351774/))

This helps explain why broad observational summaries of “total saturated fat” often look weaker than substitution analyses. The 2015 BMJ review found no clear overall association between saturated fat and mortality or total CHD, but it rated the certainty as very low and emphasized heterogeneity. In other words, saturated fat is not best interpreted as a universally toxic nutrient in the way industrial trans fat is; it is better interpreted through the question, “replaced with what?” ([de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/))

## Ruminant trans fats are not the same as industrial trans fats

Ruminant trans fats - those found in dairy fat and beef - do not look identical to industrial trans fats in epidemiology. A 2011 meta-analysis of cohort studies found higher CHD risk for total trans fat intake overall, but ruminant trans fat intake in the observed range was not significantly associated with CHD risk, whereas industrial trans fat trended positively. The 2015 BMJ review similarly found industrial, but not ruminant, trans fats associated with CHD mortality and total CHD. ([Bendsen et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427742/); [de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/))

Short-term randomized evidence at usual-to-moderate doses is also less alarming than the industrial trans fat literature. A systematic review and meta-regression of 13 randomized trials found no relationship between ruminant trans fat intake up to 4.19% of energy and changes in the total-to-HDL or LDL-to-HDL cholesterol ratios. In a 4-week randomized trial in healthy women, about 1.5% of energy from ruminant trans fat did not raise LDL cholesterol, although HDL fell modestly. ([Gayet-Boyer et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25345440/); [Lacroix et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22205319/))

But “different” does not mean “beneficial.” In a quantitative review of human feeding trials, each 1% of energy from ruminant trans fat replacing cis-monounsaturated fat increased the LDL-to-HDL ratio, versus a somewhat larger increase for industrial trans fat; the difference between them was not statistically significant. And in a tightly controlled feeding trial, vaccenic acid also worsened LDL-related markers versus control. So the strongest interpretation is not that ruminant trans fats are “good,” but that they appear less concerning than industrial trans fats at the lower doses people usually eat. ([Brouwer et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20209147/); [Gebauer et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26561632/))

## CLA and vaccenic acid are not a free pass

Vaccenic acid is the predominant ruminant trans fat, and CLA (conjugated linoleic acid) is a trans-fatty-acid isomer found in ruminant fat and often studied as a supplement. Claims that these natural trans fats are cardiometabolically beneficial are stronger than the human evidence supporting them. ([Brouwer et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20209147/))

In a 2024 GRADE-assessed meta-analysis of 14 randomized trials in people at risk of cardiovascular disease, CLA supplementation produced small reductions in body weight, BMI, and body fat percentage, but it did not improve lipid profiles or blood pressure. Another 2023 meta-analysis of 13 randomized trials found that CLA supplementation increased fasting blood glucose and AST. These are not the results one would expect from a clearly cardioprotective fat. ([Esmaeilnejad et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39439191/); [Ghodoosi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37794481/))

These supplement data are not the same as eating dairy fat, but they weaken the broader claim that “natural trans fats may even be beneficial.” At minimum, the human trial evidence does not justify giving CLA or vaccenic acid a blanket health halo. ([Brouwer et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20209147/); [Esmaeilnejad et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39439191/); [Ghodoosi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37794481/))

## Interesterified fats as trans-fat replacements

Interesterified fats are trans-free fats whose fatty acids have been rearranged on the triglyceride backbone to change texture and melting behavior. They are widely used as hard fats in processed foods, especially where food manufacturers need spreadability or structure without partial hydrogenation. ([Ng et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30126103/); [Hall et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38380649/))

The evidence here is mixed. In an early 4-week crossover feeding trial, a stearic-acid-rich interesterified fat and a trans-rich fat both raised the LDL/HDL ratio relative to palm olein. The interesterified fat also increased fasting glucose. ([Sundram et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17224066/))

Later studies have been less uniformly negative. Acute crossover trials found no adverse postprandial effects of interesterified palm oil versus native palm oil or high-oleic sunflower oil, and a 2024 randomized trial found no meaningful acute differences between an interesterified palm-based spread, a non-interesterified spread, and spreadable butter. An 8-week randomized trial likewise found no significant differences in surrogate insulin-resistance markers or fasting lipoproteins versus natural palm olein, although the NatPO diet showed lower changes in leptin and body fat percentage than the IEPalm diet. ([Berry et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17406926/); [Hall et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38380649/); [Ng et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30126103/))

The newest 6-week crossover RCT, published in 2025, found no significant difference in the total-to-HDL cholesterol ratio or other measured cardiometabolic markers between palmitic-rich and stearic-rich interesterified fats at 10% of energy, apart from one inflammatory marker. The authors also noted that long-term effects could not be determined. So interesterified fats are not automatically “the new trans fat,” but neither are they automatically benign just because the label says trans-free. ([Hall et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40975239/))

## Limits and open debates

The biggest mistake in this area is to treat all non-trans fats as interchangeable. The newer literature still supports industrial trans fat as the clearest outlier, but outside that category the best questions are: how much, replacing what, from which food source, and in what processed form? Saturated fat looks more concerning when it displaces polyunsaturated fat than when it is simply compared with refined carbohydrate. Ruminant trans fats look less harmful at usual intakes than industrial trans fats, yet high-dose feeding trials do not support calling them protective. And interesterified fats remain a formulation-specific, mostly short-term evidence base rather than a solved safety question. ([Li et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26429077/); [Jayedi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37632423/); [Bendsen et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427742/); [Hall et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40975239/))

## Quick reference

- **Industrial trans fat:** associated with higher all-cause mortality, CHD mortality, and total CHD; replacing 2% of energy from trans fat with saturated fat, monounsaturated fat, or polyunsaturated fat was associated with 17%, 21%, and 24% lower CHD risk, respectively. ([de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/); [Mozaffarian and Clarke, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19424216/))
    
- **Saturated fat:** not in the same evidence category as industrial trans fat, but reducing it lowers combined cardiovascular events in long-term trials, and replacing it with polyunsaturated fat has the clearest benefit signal. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32827219/); [Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20351774/))
    
- **Ruminant trans fat:** not significantly associated with CHD in cohort meta-analyses at typical observed intakes, and up to 4.19% of energy did not worsen key lipid ratios in meta-regression of trials; that said, high-dose controlled feeding studies can still worsen LDL-related markers. ([Bendsen et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427742/); [Gayet-Boyer et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25345440/); [Gebauer et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26561632/))
    
- **CLA/vaccenic acid benefit claims:** not well established in human cardiometabolic trials; CLA supplementation shows small body-composition effects but no lipid benefit and some adverse glucose/liver-enzyme signals. ([Brouwer et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20209147/); [Esmaeilnejad et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39439191/); [Ghodoosi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37794481/))
    
- **Interesterified fats:** mixed short-term evidence; some formulations looked adverse in early trials, while more recent consumer-relevant trials look largely neutral over weeks, with long-term effects still uncertain. ([Sundram et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17224066/); [Hall et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40975239/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Industrial trans fats are the only major dietary fat class that can reasonably be called **bad with minimal qualification**. ([de Souza et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26268692/); [Mozaffarian and Clarke, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19424216/))
    
- After industrial trans fat, fat type still matters, but mostly through **replacement and context**, not a simple good/bad label. ([Li et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26429077/); [Jayedi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37632423/))
    
- Saturated fat is **less clearly harmful than industrial trans fat**, but replacing some of it with polyunsaturated fat remains the best-supported direction for CHD prevention. ([Hooper et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32827219/); [Mozaffarian et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20351774/))
    
- Ruminant trans fats appear **less concerning at usual intakes** than industrial trans fats, but the evidence does **not** justify calling them broadly beneficial. ([Bendsen et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427742/); [Brouwer et al., 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20209147/))
    
- Interesterified fats show why **“trans-free” is not the same as “proven healthy.”** ([Sundram et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17224066/); [Hall et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40975239/))
