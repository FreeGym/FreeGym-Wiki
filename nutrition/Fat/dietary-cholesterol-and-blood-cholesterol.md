# Dietary Cholesterol and Blood Cholesterol

The newer literature through 2025 does not support either extreme claim. It does **not** overturn the older feeding-trial finding that dietary cholesterol raises blood cholesterol on average, especially LDL-C. But it also does **not** support the idea that eggs are uniformly harmful or that moderate egg intake clearly raises cardiovascular disease risk in everyone. The best-supported summary is: dietary cholesterol usually matters **some**, saturated fat often matters **as much or more** in practical diets, and individual responses differ. ([Berger et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26109578/), [Vincent et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30596814/), [Carson et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31838890/), [Carter et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40339906/))

## What dietary cholesterol does and does not do

Controlled feeding studies do not support the claim that dietary cholesterol has a negligible effect in everyone. A systematic review and meta-analysis of feeding trials found that added dietary cholesterol increased total cholesterol by **11.2 mg/dL**, LDL-C by **6.7 mg/dL**, HDL-C by **3.2 mg/dL**, and the LDL-C/HDL-C ratio by **0.2**, with no significant effect on triglycerides. A later meta-regression of 55 randomized dietary intervention studies likewise found a positive dose-response between added dietary cholesterol and LDL-C, with a predicted LDL-C rise of about **1.9 to 4.6 mg/dL per extra 100 mg/day**, depending on the model used. (Berger et al., 2015; Vincent et al., 2019) ([Berger et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26109578/), [Vincent et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30596814/))

Recent egg-specific meta-analyses point in the same direction. A 2020 meta-analysis of 17 randomized controlled trials in healthy adults found that higher egg intake raised LDL-C by **8.14 mg/dL** and the LDL-C/HDL-C ratio by **0.14**, with somewhat larger effects in interventions lasting longer than 2 months. Another 2020 meta-analysis of 66 RCTs found significant increases in total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, TC/HDL-C, ApoA1, and ApoB100, even though not all lipid measures changed. (Li et al., 2020; Khalighi Sikaroudi et al., 2020) ([Li et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32635569/), [Khalighi Sikaroudi et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32524644/))

An updated 2025 umbrella review did not erase that basic signal. Reviewing meta-analyses published since 2020, it found **very weak** evidence that higher egg intake increases LDL cholesterol, total cholesterol, and ApoB, while also judging the overall certainty of the evidence as **critically low** because the underlying reviews were heterogeneous and methodologically limited. (Formisano et al., 2025) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0939475325000031), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39934049/))

## Compensation is real, but incomplete

The compensation argument is real. A metabolic clinical trial found that dietary cholesterol feeding **modestly downregulates human cholesterol synthesis**, which supports the classic idea that the body responds to higher intake by making less cholesterol itself. But that compensation is not complete enough to erase the average LDL-C rise seen across feeding-trial meta-analyses. (Jones et al., 1996; Berger et al., 2015; Vincent et al., 2019) ([Jones et al., 1996](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8857917/), [Berger et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26109578/), [Vincent et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30596814/))

The compensatory response also appears variable rather than uniform. In a 4-week randomized crossover trial, **600 mg/day** of dietary cholesterol increased total cholesterol and HDL-C **without measurable changes in cholesterol absorption or synthesis**, underscoring that cholesterol homeostasis is real but not expressed identically in every study or every person. (Alphonse et al., 2017) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28112077/))

A 2025 randomized crossover trial helps explain why people talk past one another on this topic. In 61 adults, **two eggs per day** as part of a **low-saturated-fat** diet lowered LDL-C relative to the **high-cholesterol, high-saturated-fat control diet**, and saturated fat intake, but not dietary cholesterol, tracked LDL-C across the three diets. But this **5-week** trial was done in generally healthy adults with baseline LDL-C below 135 mg/dL; **48 participants completed all diet phases**, the egg diet did **not** lower LDL-C relative to the **egg-free high-saturated-fat diet**, and the egg diet increased **small LDL particles**. So the newer literature suggests that saturated fat can dominate short-term LDL responses in realistic whole-diet comparisons; it does **not** show that dietary cholesterol never matters. (Carter et al., 2025; Vincent et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020) ([Carter et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40339906/), [Vincent et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30596814/), [Li et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32635569/))

## Hyper-responders and genetics

The hyper-responder idea is supported by feeding studies, but operational definitions vary across studies. In a crossover trial of 40 healthy men given an extra **640 mg/day** of dietary cholesterol from eggs, hyporesponders did not show increases in LDL-C or HDL-C, whereas hyperresponders showed significant increases in both. In another crossover study of older adults given **three eggs per day**, hyperresponders again showed higher LDL-C and HDL-C than during the egg-substitute phase, while hyporesponders did not. (Herron et al., 2003; Greene et al., 2006) ([Herron et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12672915/), [Greene et al., 2006](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1386675/))

That said, the hyper-responder literature is more nuanced than the slogan implies. In the older-adult crossover study, the LDL increase in hyperresponders was accompanied by **larger LDL and HDL particles** and no significant increase in total lipoprotein particle number. In the male crossover study, the authors concluded that the observed changes did not produce a clearly more atherogenic lipoprotein profile in healthy men. (Greene et al., 2006; Herron et al., 2003) ([Greene et al., 2006](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1386675/), [Herron et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12672915/))

Small genetic studies suggest that some of this variability is biologically grounded. In a randomized crossover trial of 91 healthy adults, people with the **C/C genotype** for an **ABCG5** polymorphism had a greater LDL-C rise during the egg phase than carriers of other genotypes. But the case that common genotypes clearly determine long-term cardiovascular risk from eggs is much weaker. In Finnish prospective cohorts, the **apoE4** phenotype did **not** modify associations of egg or cholesterol intake with coronary artery disease or stroke risk. (Herron et al., 2006; Virtanen et al., 2016; Abdollahi et al., 2019) ([Herron et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16614398/), [Virtanen et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26864369/), [Abdollahi et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31095282/))

## Egg consumption and cardiovascular outcomes

The long-term outcome data are less consistent than the feeding-trial lipid data. In a 2020 updated meta-analysis of prospective cohorts, **one additional egg per day** was **not** associated with overall cardiovascular disease risk (pooled RR **0.98**), with null results in US and European cohorts and a possible inverse association in Asian cohorts. A 2021 dose-response meta-analysis likewise found an inverse association for overall CVD up to about **six eggs per week** and decreased CVD incidence up to **one egg per day**, with no association with stroke, though it did report a higher **heart-failure** risk at one egg per day (RR **1.15**). (Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020; Godos et al., 2021) ([Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32132002/), [Godos et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32865658/))

More recent outcome syntheses are less reassuring. A 2022 updated meta-analysis found that each additional **50-g egg per day** was associated with a small increase in overall CVD risk (pooled RR **1.04**), with the positive association concentrated in US cohorts (RR **1.08**) and not seen in Asian cohorts. The same paper linked higher dietary cholesterol and egg intake with higher overall and CVD-related mortality. (Zhao et al., 2022) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35360933/))

The broadest recent summary still does not support an absolutist conclusion either way. The 2025 umbrella review judged the overall evidence on egg consumption and health outcomes to be **critically low** in strength and reported no association between **high versus low** egg consumption and overall cardiovascular outcomes or all-cause mortality, despite the modest adverse lipid signals seen in intervention meta-analyses. That is closer to **“mixed and low-certainty”** than to either “eggs are harmless for everyone” or “eggs clearly raise heart disease risk in everyone.” (Formisano et al., 2025) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0939475325000031), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39934049/))

## Why the literature looks inconsistent

Different study designs are answering different questions. Feeding trials test whether added dietary cholesterol changes blood lipids over days to weeks; those studies usually show modest average increases in LDL-C or related markers. Cohort studies ask whether people who habitually eat more eggs or dietary cholesterol have more cardiovascular events over years; those results are mixed and differ by region, background diet, and outcome definition. (Berger et al., 2015; Li et al., 2020; Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) ([Berger et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26109578/), [Li et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32635569/), [Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32132002/), [Zhao et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35360933/))

There is also likely real biological heterogeneity. Some people show minimal lipid changes, others show larger LDL-C responses, and small genetic studies support a role for cholesterol-transport variation. But current evidence is still too limited to treat “hyper-responder” status as a clinically settled or broadly standardized category for predicting long-term cardiovascular risk. (Herron et al., 2003; Greene et al., 2006; Herron et al., 2006; Virtanen et al., 2016; Abdollahi et al., 2019) ([Herron et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12672915/), [Greene et al., 2006](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1386675/), [Herron et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16614398/), [Virtanen et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26864369/), [Abdollahi et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31095282/))

## Practical interpretation

- Dietary cholesterol **does** raise LDL-C on average in controlled feeding studies, so “it has minimal effect on blood cholesterol” is too strong as a general claim. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26109578/))
    
- The body **does** compensate, but not completely or uniformly, so “the body fully cancels it out” is also too strong. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8857917/))
    
- Saturated fat can have a larger effect than dietary cholesterol in real whole-diet comparisons, which helps explain why some egg trials look neutral or even favorable when eggs replace foods or patterns richer in saturated fat. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40339906/))
    
- A subset of people clearly responds more strongly, and genetic variation may contribute, but the evidence for using genotype or a hyper-responder label to predict long-term clinical risk is still limited. ([Herron et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12672915/), [Greene et al., 2006](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1386675/), [Herron et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16614398/), [Virtanen et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26864369/), [Abdollahi et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31095282/))
    
- Moderate egg intake, roughly **up to one egg per day**, often looks neutral in general-population cohort meta-analyses, but the outcome evidence is mixed rather than exonerating, and some subgroup data are less reassuring. Older meta-analyses found higher CHD/CVD signals in diabetic subgroup analyses, and a 2025 cohort of US stroke survivors found stronger adverse associations in people with diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, obesity, or older age; that cohort should be interpreted as special-population observational evidence rather than general-population evidence. ([Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32132002/), [Rong et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23295181/), [Shin et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23676423/), [Xia et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41062520/))
