# Alcohol

The most debated questions about alcohol's effects on fitness, body composition, health, and performance. This isn't an anti-alcohol page or a pro-alcohol page - it's an evidence page.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Does Alcohol Kill Your Gains?

"Alcohol destroys muscle protein synthesis" is repeated constantly in fitness circles. The evidence exists, but the magnitude and practical significance depend on dose, timing, and context.

**Competing claims:**

1. Alcohol acutely suppresses muscle protein synthesis - the Parr et al. study showed that post-exercise alcohol consumption (~1.5g/kg, roughly 8-12 drinks) reduced MPS by ~24% even when co-ingested with protein
2. The MPS suppression is dose-dependent and the studies used very high doses - moderate drinking (1-2 drinks) likely has a much smaller or negligible effect on MPS, but this has been less studied
3. The real impact on gains over months and years is probably through indirect mechanisms - worse sleep, missed training sessions, poor food choices, reduced training intensity the next day - rather than the acute MPS effect
4. Plenty of athletes and recreational lifters drink moderately and still build muscle - if protein intake, training, and sleep are otherwise good, occasional moderate drinking is unlikely to meaningfully impair progress

**Evidence needed:** Parr et al. alcohol and MPS data, dose-response studies (moderate vs heavy drinking and MPS), longitudinal studies on alcohol consumption and hypertrophy outcomes, indirect pathway analyses (sleep, adherence, training quality).

## Does Alcohol Lower Testosterone?

Alcohol and testosterone is a common concern, especially among men focused on optimizing hormones. The relationship is real at high doses but overstated at moderate ones.

**Competing claims:**

1. Acute heavy drinking (binge drinking) significantly suppresses testosterone for 12-24+ hours - this effect is well-documented and dose-dependent
2. Moderate drinking (1-2 drinks) has minimal or no measurable effect on testosterone levels - some studies show a small transient increase after light drinking
3. Chronic heavy drinking causes sustained testosterone suppression, testicular atrophy, and increased aromatization (conversion of testosterone to estrogen) - this is a real clinical phenomenon in alcohol use disorder
4. The testosterone fear from occasional moderate drinking is massively overstated in fitness culture - the fluctuation is smaller than normal daily variation and far smaller than the effect of a bad night of sleep

**Evidence needed:** Acute alcohol dose-response and testosterone studies, chronic heavy drinking and hormonal profiles, moderate drinking and testosterone in trained populations, comparison of alcohol-induced testosterone changes to normal diurnal variation.

## How Does Alcohol Affect Recovery?

Beyond MPS, alcohol may impair recovery through multiple pathways: inflammation, sleep quality, glycogen replenishment, and rehydration. The question is how much each of these matters at real-world drinking levels.

**Competing claims:**

1. Alcohol impairs glycogen resynthesis when it displaces carbohydrate intake post-exercise - but if carbohydrate intake is adequate alongside the alcohol, glycogen repletion is largely preserved
2. Alcohol is a diuretic and impairs rehydration after exercise - but at moderate doses with concurrent food and water intake, the net dehydration effect is small
3. Alcohol increases inflammatory markers and may delay the resolution of exercise-induced muscle damage - but the clinical significance (does it actually slow return to performance?) is less clear than the biomarker changes suggest
4. The biggest recovery cost of alcohol is probably sleep disruption - alcohol fragments sleep architecture, reduces REM and deep sleep, and impairs the restorative processes that drive recovery, even at moderate doses

**Evidence needed:** Alcohol and glycogen resynthesis studies (with and without adequate carb intake), alcohol and post-exercise rehydration data, alcohol and inflammatory marker recovery timelines, alcohol and sleep architecture studies with next-day performance outcomes.

## Is Alcohol Fattening?

Alcohol has 7 calories per gram - almost as calorie-dense as fat. But the body processes alcohol differently than other macronutrients, and the relationship between alcohol and body fat is more nuanced than simple calorie math.

**Competing claims:**

1. Alcohol calories count like any other calories - people who drink more consume more total calories and gain more fat, especially from the food choices that accompany drinking
2. The body prioritizes alcohol metabolism over everything else (it can't store alcohol), which temporarily halts fat oxidation - so alcohol doesn't directly become fat, but it pauses fat burning while the body clears the alcohol
3. Epidemiological data is mixed - moderate drinkers are not consistently fatter than non-drinkers, and some studies show J-shaped or U-shaped associations where moderate drinkers have lower BMI than abstainers (likely confounded)
4. The real fattening effect of alcohol is behavioral - it increases appetite, reduces inhibition around food choices, and pairs with calorie-dense foods (bar snacks, late-night eating, next-day hangover meals)

**Evidence needed:** Alcohol and ad libitum calorie intake studies, fat oxidation suppression duration and magnitude data, epidemiological alcohol consumption and body composition associations (controlling for confounders), behavioral pathway analyses.

## Is Moderate Drinking Actually Good for You?

For decades, the "J-curve" suggested that moderate drinkers live longer than abstainers. This was used to justify the idea that a glass of red wine is healthy. Recent methodological critiques have challenged the entire foundation of this claim.

**Competing claims:**

1. The J-curve is real - moderate alcohol consumption (especially red wine) reduces cardiovascular risk through effects on HDL cholesterol, blood clotting, and possibly polyphenols
2. The J-curve is a methodological artifact - the "abstainer" group includes former drinkers who quit due to health problems ("sick quitter" bias), making abstainers look unhealthier than they are
3. Mendelian randomization studies (which avoid confounding by using genetic variants as instruments) generally show linear harm - any amount of alcohol increases health risk, with no protective threshold
4. The 2018 Global Burden of Disease analysis concluded that the safest level of alcohol consumption is zero - a position now endorsed by several national health agencies

**Evidence needed:** J-curve studies and their methodological critiques, Mendelian randomization alcohol and mortality data, 2018 GBD alcohol analysis, "sick quitter" bias reanalyses, red wine polyphenol studies (resveratrol dose reality check).

## How Does Alcohol Affect Sleep?

Many people use alcohol to fall asleep. It works - alcohol is a sedative that reduces sleep onset latency. But what it does to sleep quality is a different story.

**Competing claims:**

1. Alcohol helps you fall asleep faster but fragments the second half of the night - it suppresses REM sleep in the first half and causes rebound wakefulness, lighter sleep, and more frequent awakenings later
2. Even moderate doses (1-2 drinks) measurably reduce sleep quality as assessed by HRV, sleep staging, and next-day subjective ratings - the effect is not limited to heavy drinking
3. The sleep disruption from alcohol may be the single most important mechanism through which alcohol impairs recovery, training adaptation, and next-day performance - worse than the direct MPS or hormonal effects
4. Tolerance develops to the sedative effect but not to the sleep architecture disruption - regular drinkers may feel they sleep fine with alcohol, but objective measures show persistent impairment

**Evidence needed:** Alcohol dose and sleep architecture studies (polysomnography), HRV and wearable sleep data with alcohol consumption, alcohol tolerance and sleep quality longitudinal data, next-day cognitive and physical performance after alcohol-disrupted sleep.

## Does Alcohol Affect Endurance More Than Strength?

The performance effects of alcohol may differ by sport and training type. Endurance athletes and strength athletes may not be equally affected.

**Competing claims:**

1. Alcohol impairs endurance performance more than strength performance - aerobic capacity, thermoregulation, and sustained effort are more sensitive to dehydration, glycogen depletion, and sleep disruption
2. Acute hangover effects impair all types of performance - reaction time, coordination, and motivation are reduced regardless of whether the activity is aerobic or anaerobic
3. Strength and power output may be relatively preserved the day after moderate drinking, but training quality (volume tolerance, effort, focus) still suffers
4. The practical answer depends on timing - drinking the night before a hard endurance session is worse than drinking the night before a rest day, and the sport-specific difference matters less than the dose and timing

**Evidence needed:** Alcohol and aerobic vs anaerobic performance comparisons, hangover and performance studies by sport type, next-day training quality metrics (volume, RPE, heart rate) after drinking, timing of alcohol relative to training and performance outcomes.

## How Much Is "Too Much" for Athletes?

Guidelines for the general population suggest up to 1 drink/day for women and 2 for men. But athletes have different recovery demands, performance goals, and body composition targets. Should the threshold be lower?

**Competing claims:**

1. General population guidelines are a reasonable starting point for recreational athletes - occasional moderate drinking within these limits is unlikely to meaningfully impair fitness goals
2. Competitive athletes should aim for less than general guidelines suggest - the recovery cost, sleep disruption, and calorie load of even "moderate" drinking are more consequential when you're training hard and optimizing performance
3. Complete abstinence is unnecessary for most athletes and may be socially isolating - the dose makes the poison, and rigid all-or-nothing thinking about alcohol can be as counterproductive as excessive drinking
4. The honest answer is context-dependent - a drink with dinner on a rest day is very different from drinking after a hard training session or the night before competition, and blanket rules miss this nuance

**Evidence needed:** Athlete-specific alcohol consumption surveys and health/performance outcomes, dose-response studies in trained populations, social and psychological effects of abstinence vs moderation in athletic populations, sport-specific alcohol policies and their outcomes.
