# Is “Leaky Gut” Real?

“Leaky gut” is a confusing term because it is used for two different ideas. **Intestinal permeability** is a real, measurable biological phenomenon. **“Leaky gut syndrome”** as a broad diagnosis for fatigue, brain fog, food sensitivities, autoimmunity, skin symptoms, and systemic inflammation is not established as a validated clinical syndrome. A 2024 review in _Gastroenterology & Hepatology_ states that leaky gut syndrome cannot be accurately diagnosed by symptoms, blood work, or stool studies. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/))

The best interpretation is: barrier dysfunction can matter in specific diseases, especially gastrointestinal and immune-mediated diseases, but it should not be used as a catch-all explanation that replaces proper diagnosis. A 2024 review emphasizes that causal links between a “leaky gut barrier” and many proposed diseases often remain unclear and require more research. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/))

## What intestinal permeability actually means

The intestinal barrier is selective. It allows absorption of nutrients, water, and electrolytes while limiting entry of foreign antigens and microorganisms. It includes microbial, mucus/biochemical, epithelial, and immune components, with tight junctions between epithelial cells playing an important role in regulating paracellular permeability. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/))

Increased permeability means that the barrier is allowing more passage than expected under a given test condition. That does not automatically mean “holes in the gut,” and it does not automatically mean that every nonspecific symptom is caused by bacterial toxins entering the bloodstream. The biology is real, but the clinical interpretation depends on disease context, test method, and whether the permeability change is a cause, consequence, or marker of inflammation. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/))

## How permeability is measured

The lactulose:mannitol test is one of the most common in vivo methods. A person ingests two sugars: mannitol, a smaller molecule absorbed more readily, and lactulose, a larger molecule that is assumed to cross mainly through paracellular pathways. Urinary recovery is then used to calculate the lactulose:mannitol ratio. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

A meta-analysis of lactulose:mannitol testing in celiac and Crohn’s disease found pooled ratios of 0.014 in healthy controls, 0.133 in untreated celiac disease, 0.037 in treated celiac disease, 0.093 in active Crohn’s disease, and 0.028 in inactive Crohn’s disease. That supports permeability as a measurable disease-associated phenomenon, especially in celiac disease and Crohn’s disease. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

The same meta-analysis also found high heterogeneity, partly due to differences in protocols, and concluded that lactulose:mannitol testing is useful but has significant limitations before widespread clinical use. A standardization study similarly found that variations in collection protocols limit meaningful comparisons between studies and suggested a 2.5- to 4-hour urine collection window to reduce error. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0099256))

A controlled sugar-permeability test can be informative in research or specialist settings, but it is not a general consumer diagnostic test for vague symptom clusters. Results depend on protocol, timing, disease context, and clinical interpretation. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

## Zonulin testing is a major weak point

Zonulin is often marketed as a “leaky gut” blood or stool marker, but commonly used commercial zonulin assays have serious validity problems. One study found that a widely used commercial ELISA did not detect pre-haptoglobin 2, the protein commonly described as zonulin, and instead appeared to recognize related proteins such as properdin. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00022/full))

A separate PLOS One study found that commercial zonulin assays were not detecting actual zonulin/prehaptoglobin-2 and advised caution in using serum zonulin as a marker of mucosal barrier integrity. Another study in first-degree relatives of Crohn’s patients found that commercial serum zonulin/ZO-1 and I-FABP did not correlate with lactulose:mannitol permeability measures. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0210728), [Frontiers](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.645303))

This means that commercial “leaky gut” panels built around zonulin should not be treated as reliable diagnostic tests. A controlled sugar-permeability test can be informative in research or specialist settings, but a single commercial marker or symptom checklist is not enough to diagnose a syndrome. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/))

## Where the evidence is strongest

Celiac disease and Crohn’s disease have some of the clearest evidence for altered permeability. In the lactulose:mannitol meta-analysis, untreated celiac disease and active Crohn’s disease had higher pooled permeability ratios than healthy controls, and treated celiac disease and inactive Crohn’s disease had lower values than active/untreated states, though not always identical to controls. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

Crohn’s disease also has prospective evidence. In a cohort of 1,420 asymptomatic first-degree relatives of people with Crohn’s disease, an abnormal lactulose:mannitol ratio above 0.03 was associated with later Crohn’s diagnosis, with a hazard ratio of 3.03 during follow-up. The association remained significant even when the test was performed more than three years before diagnosis, supporting a model in which barrier dysfunction may contribute to Crohn’s pathogenesis in susceptible people, without proving causation. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016508520350216))

Type 1 diabetes is more complicated. An updated review concluded that human and animal studies imply increased intestinal permeability may be an important disease determinant, but it also emphasized diverse methods and interpretation problems. Older studies are not fully consistent, so type 1 diabetes should be framed as an area of plausible association rather than proof that permeability is the primary cause. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34182210/))

## Cause versus correlation

The key clinical debate is not whether permeability exists. It does. The harder question is whether increased permeability causes disease, results from disease, or reflects a shared inflammatory process. Reviews describe the directionality as controversial: dysbiosis, inflammation, and barrier dysfunction can reinforce one another, but it is often difficult to determine which came first. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/))

Crohn’s disease is one of the stronger examples suggesting permeability may precede disease in some high-risk individuals. That does not justify generalizing the same causal model to every symptom cluster, autoimmune disease, mood symptom, or food reaction. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016508520350216))

## What can increase permeability

NSAIDs and aspirin can affect the small bowel. In a pilot study of 20 healthy volunteers, 100 mg/day of enteric-coated aspirin for 14 days produced new small-bowel mucosal findings on capsule endoscopy in 10 of 20 participants, increased fecal calprotectin, and increased the number of participants above the normal lactulose:mannitol cutoff to 10 of 20. The median lactulose:mannitol ratio change itself was not statistically significant in this small pilot study. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1542356508012500))

Acute psychological stress can alter barrier function under experimental conditions. In healthy volunteers, dichotomous listening stress increased paracellular permeability in rectal mucosa biopsies and modulated immune-related gene expression. This supports stress as a physiological barrier-modulating factor, but it does not mean stress alone creates a diagnosable “leaky gut syndrome.” ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9892435/))

Alcohol, dysbiosis, low-fiber dietary patterns, certain medications, and inflammatory states are also discussed as barrier-disrupting factors in the literature. These factors are broad and context-dependent, so they are better understood as possible contributors to barrier stress rather than simple one-to-one causes of nonspecific symptoms. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/))

Gluten is a special case. In celiac disease, gluten exposure is central to disease biology, and untreated celiac disease is associated with higher permeability. That evidence should not be stretched into a claim that gluten causes clinically meaningful leaky gut in everyone. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

## The LPS claim needs caution

The idea that bacterial lipopolysaccharide, or LPS, can enter circulation and trigger inflammation is biologically plausible. LPS is a Gram-negative bacterial endotoxin that can activate innate immune signaling. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4418/10/6/428))

The measurement problem is important. A 2020 methods paper concluded that the commonly used Limulus amebocyte lysate assay was unsuitable in its current form for detecting the low endotoxin levels typically seen in “metabolic endotoxemia.” So claims that a supplement or diet lowers systemic inflammation by “stopping LPS leakage” should be interpreted cautiously unless the study uses validated methods and clinically meaningful endpoints. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4418/10/6/428))

## Interventions targeting the barrier

The most evidence-based approach is to treat the underlying condition rather than treat “leaky gut” as a standalone disease. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/)) In celiac disease, the standard treatment remains a gluten-free diet. Larazotide acetate, a drug designed to regulate tight junctions in celiac disease, has been studied in randomized trials; a meta-analysis of four RCTs found no significant difference from placebo in lactulose-to-mannitol ratio, though it showed some symptom benefit during gluten challenge. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2210740121001613))

Glutamine has mixed evidence. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of clinical trials found that glutamine supplementation did not significantly affect intestinal permeability overall, although subgroup analyses suggested reductions with doses above 30 g/day and short interventions under two weeks. That is not strong enough to support routine glutamine use as a general “leaky gut cure.” ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11471693/))

Probiotics, synbiotics, and prebiotics look promising but remain difficult to interpret. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found reductions in LPS with pro/synbiotics and prebiotics and reductions in zonulin with pro/synbiotics, but the LPS analysis for pro/synbiotics had very low certainty and high heterogeneity, and zonulin is itself a problematic marker when measured by commonly used commercial assays. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1043661825002051), [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00022/full), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0210728))

Bone broth is even less settled. A 2025 review discusses plausible nutrients in bone broth that may relate to barrier biology, but that is not the same as human RCT evidence showing that bone broth repairs intestinal permeability or treats a clinical syndrome. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40180691/))

## Conflict of interest and funding notes

The strongest permeability-testing evidence cited here is not mainly supplement-company evidence, but conflicts still vary. The lactulose:mannitol standardization study reported public research funding and no competing interests. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0099256))

The prospective Crohn’s cohort disclosed no author conflicts and was supported by Crohn’s and Colitis Canada, CIHR, and the Helmsley Charitable Trust; one author was a former recipient of a fellowship involving Ferring Pharmaceuticals. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016508520350216))

The aspirin pilot study was described as independently supported, but one author disclosed advisory roles with Pfizer, AstraZeneca, and Bayer. That does not invalidate the findings, but it should be noted when interpreting drug-related gastrointestinal research. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1542356508012500))

The zonulin assay validity studies are especially important because they cut against commercial testing claims. Scheffler et al. reported public/noncommercial funding and no commercial or financial conflicts, while Ajamian et al. reported Fulbright support and no competing interests. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00022/full), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0210728))

The probiotic/prebiotic literature should be weighed cautiously because effects are strain-specific, product-specific, and often measured with imperfect biomarkers. Even positive RCT meta-analyses do not prove that a generic probiotic, synbiotic, prebiotic, or supplement stack “seals the gut.” ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1043661825002051))

## Quick reference

- Intestinal permeability is real and measurable. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

- “Leaky gut syndrome” is not a validated catch-all diagnosis for nonspecific symptoms. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/))

- Lactulose:mannitol testing has research value but requires careful protocol standardization and context. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

- Commonly used commercial zonulin tests are not reliable standalone markers of intestinal permeability. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00022/full), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0210728))

- Celiac disease and Crohn’s disease have stronger evidence for altered permeability than most other conditions. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z))

- Glutamine, probiotics, prebiotics, and bone broth should not be presented as proven general treatments for “leaky gut syndrome.” ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11471693/), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1043661825002051), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40180691/))


## Practical interpretation

- Use the term **intestinal permeability** or **barrier dysfunction** when referring to the real biology; avoid using “leaky gut” as a diagnosis unless clearly explaining its limitations. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/))

- Increased permeability can be relevant in diseases like celiac disease, Crohn’s disease, and possibly some immune/metabolic conditions, but it is not proven to be the root cause of every systemic symptom. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12876-021-02082-z), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11438725/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34182210/))

- Commercial testing is the weakest part of the “leaky gut” ecosystem: symptom checklists, stool tests, and serum/stool zonulin should not be used to justify restrictive diets or supplement protocols without proper clinical evaluation. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0210728))

- The most defensible clinical approach is to diagnose and treat the underlying condition: celiac disease, IBD, medication-related enteropathy, infection, IBS, food allergy, malabsorption, or other GI disease, rather than treating “leaky gut” as a universal explanation. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11345991/))
