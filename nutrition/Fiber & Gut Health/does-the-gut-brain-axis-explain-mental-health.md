# Does the Gut-Brain Axis Explain Mental Health?

The gut-brain axis is real, but it does not provide a single-cause explanation for depression or anxiety. The best human reading is that the microbiome may influence mental health through immune, endocrine, neural, and metabolic pathways; psychobiotics show promising but heterogeneous, generally low-to-moderate-certainty effects; and Mediterranean-style dietary interventions currently have more directly actionable clinical evidence as a general adjunct than any specific named probiotic product. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"); [Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047); [Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"); [Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Bayes et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522000612 "The effect of a Mediterranean diet on the symptoms of depression in young males (the AMMEND: A Mediterranean Diet in MEN with Depression study): a randomized controlled trial - ScienceDirect"); [Bizzozero-Peroni et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuad176 "The impact of the Mediterranean diet on alleviating depressive symptoms in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials | Nutrition Reviews | Oxford Academic"))

## What the gut-brain axis is

The gut-brain axis refers to bidirectional communication between the gastrointestinal tract, gut microbiota, immune system, endocrine system, autonomic nervous system, and central nervous system. Proposed routes include microbial metabolites such as short-chain fatty acids, tryptophan metabolism, inflammatory signaling, hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis effects, vagal signaling, and interactions with enteroendocrine cells. A 2023 systematic review/meta-analysis of gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder summarized these routes as tryptophan metabolites, vagal nerve stimulation, HPA-axis changes, neurotransmitter-related microbial products, and immune signaling across a more permeable gut epithelium. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"))

Animal studies are important for mechanism discovery, but they are not enough to establish human treatment claims. In preclinical models, microbiome manipulation and fecal microbiota transplantation can alter depression-like or anxiety-like behaviors, but human depression and anxiety involve clinical heterogeneity and potential confounders such as medication, diet, age, sex, BMI, illness severity, measurement method, and depression classification, which are difficult to model in animals. The same 2023 review notes both the strength of animal findings and the mixed nature of human clinical studies. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"))

## The serotonin claim is often overstated

A common claim is that the gut "makes serotonin," so gut bacteria must directly control mood. The first part is broadly true: enterochromaffin cells in the gut synthesize roughly 90 to 95 percent of total body serotonin. But plasma serotonin does not readily cross the blood-brain barrier, and central and peripheral serotonin pools are functionally distinct. That means gut serotonin is not simply being shipped into the brain to regulate mood. ([Martin et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1210/en.2016-1839))

This does not make the gut irrelevant. It means the pathway is more indirect. Gut microbes can influence enterochromaffin-cell serotonin biosynthesis through metabolites such as short-chain fatty acids and secondary bile acids; peripheral serotonin can affect gut motility, immune tone, metabolism, and endocrine signaling; and those systems may feed back into mood-relevant biology. But the simplified version, "the gut makes serotonin, therefore probiotics directly raise brain serotonin," is not supported by the physiology. ([Martin et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1210/en.2016-1839))

## What microbiome association studies show

Human microbiome studies do show associations between depression and gut microbial composition, but the results are not yet clean enough to define a diagnostic microbiome signature. A 2023 systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression included 44 studies with 2,091 patients and 2,792 controls. It found no significant overall differences in common alpha-diversity indices, Firmicutes, or Bacteroidetes between depressive disorder and controls, but it did find more consistent taxon-level patterns: lower levels of several butyrate-producing genera such as Coprococcus and Faecalibacterium, and higher levels of genera such as Eggerthella, Enterococcus, Flavonifractor, Holdemania, and Streptococcus. The authors emphasized that psychotropic medication and dietary habits may influence microbiota, and that measurement method and depression classification affect findings. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"))

A large microbiome-wide association study of depressive symptoms profiled 1,054 Rotterdam Study participants and replicated findings in 1,539 HELIUS participants. It identified 12 genera and one microbial family associated with depressive symptoms, including Eggerthella, Sellimonas, Coprococcus, Subdoligranulum, and Ruminococcaceae-related taxa. Its Mendelian randomization analysis suggested a possible causal link involving major depression and Eggerthella, but the authors also noted limited MR power and the need for further investigation. ([Radjabzadeh et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-34502-3 "Gut microbiome-wide association study of depressive symptoms | Nature Communications"))

More recent Mendelian randomization work has also attempted to test causal relationships between microbial taxa and depression/anxiety using large GWAS datasets. One 2025 analysis found associations suggesting protective effects for some taxa, including Bifidobacteriales/Bifidobacteriaceae for depression and anxiety, while other taxa were associated with higher risk. The authors concluded that further randomized trials and mechanistic work are needed before these associations can be translated into therapies. ([Lai and Xiong, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0163834325000775 "Analysis of gut microbiota and depression and anxiety: Mendelian randomization from three datasets - ScienceDirect"))

## Psychobiotics: promising, but not proven as general mental-health treatment

Here, "psychobiotics" is used as shorthand for probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, or related microbiome-targeted products studied for psychological outcomes. The strongest human evidence is not "all probiotics improve mood," but rather "some probiotic interventions in some populations show benefits on validated depression or anxiety scales, with high heterogeneity." A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 23 RCTs in clinically diagnosed populations included 1,401 patients. Probiotics reduced depression symptoms with an SMD of -0.96 and anxiety symptoms with an SMD of -0.59, while prebiotics showed a nonsignificant trend for depression. The authors highlighted substantial heterogeneity, including differences in study duration and formulation. Using GRADE, they rated certainty low for depression and moderate for anxiety. ([Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047))

A strain-focused 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis found similarly mixed results. Across 12 RCTs with 707 participants, probiotic intervention was associated with a BDI reduction of -2.69 points, but effects were not significant on HAMD, DASS, or MADRS outcomes. That is a useful warning: the result depends on the symptom scale, population, formula, and trial design. ([Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"))

A notable human RCT tested an 8-week multistrain probiotic as adjunctive treatment in adults with major depressive disorder who were already taking antidepressants. It used validated outcomes including HAMD-17, IDS-SR, HAMA, and GAD-7. The trial randomized 49 participants and found signals on selected depression and anxiety outcomes and timepoints: HAMD-17 at week 4, IDS-SR at week 8, and HAMA at weeks 4 and 8, but not consistently across all measures; GAD-7 effects were not significant. This was explicitly a pilot trial intended to estimate acceptability, tolerability, and effect sizes for a future definitive trial, not to prove clinical efficacy. ([Nikolova et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2806011 "Acceptability, Tolerability, and Estimates of Putative Treatment Effects of Probiotics as Adjunctive Treatment in Patients With Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Trials | JAMA Psychiatry | JAMA Network"))

Funding matters here. The [Nikolova et al. trial](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2806011 "Acceptability, Tolerability, and Estimates of Putative Treatment Effects of Probiotics as Adjunctive Treatment in Patients With Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Trials | JAMA Psychiatry | JAMA Network") used Bio-Kult Advanced, a product from ADM Protexin, and the conflict-of-interest section states that several authors received grants from the Medical Research Council and ADM Protexin during the conduct of the study. This does not invalidate the trial, but it should lower confidence until independently funded replications are available. ([Nikolova et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2806011 "Acceptability, Tolerability, and Estimates of Putative Treatment Effects of Probiotics as Adjunctive Treatment in Patients With Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Trials | JAMA Psychiatry | JAMA Network"))

The review-level funding picture is more reassuring for the two meta-analyses cited above: [Asad et al.](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047) reported NIHR/Oxford Health Biomedical Research Centre-related funding and no declared conflicts; [Rahmannia et al.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link") reported no external funding and no competing interests. Still, review-level independence does not remove the limitations of the underlying RCTs, especially small samples, varied products, and inconsistent outcome measures. ([Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047); [Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"))

## Diet quality has more directly actionable clinical evidence than any specific probiotic product

Diet quality is not the same as a microbiome intervention, but dietary improvement may affect inflammation, oxidative stress, brain plasticity, gut microbiota, and food-related behavioral pathways. For that reason, diet may work partly through the microbiome while also acting through non-microbiome pathways. ([Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

The [SMILES trial](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link") randomized adults with major depression to a 12-week modified Mediterranean-style dietary intervention or social-support control. The diet group had a significantly greater MADRS improvement, with a between-group difference of 7.1 points and Cohen's d of -1.16. Remission occurred in 32.3 percent of the diet group versus 8.0 percent of controls, giving a reported number needed to treat of 4.1. The intervention was delivered by dietitians and focused on dietary improvement rather than weight loss. ([Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

The SMILES trial should not be read without its limitations. It was small, participants could not be blinded to diet assignment, expectancy bias was possible, and completion rates differed between groups. The trial also had several funding and conflict-of-interest disclosures: NHMRC support, Woolworths food vouchers, donated cinema vouchers, donated muesli bars, and Meat and Livestock Australia funding for biochemistry analyses; several authors also disclosed prior industry or pharmaceutical funding. The sponsors were reported to have no role in design, analysis, or publication, but the conflicts should still be noted. ([Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"))

The [AMMEND trial](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522000612 "The effect of a Mediterranean diet on the symptoms of depression in young males (the AMMEND: A Mediterranean Diet in MEN with Depression study): a randomized controlled trial - ScienceDirect") tested a Mediterranean diet intervention in young men aged 18 to 25 with moderate-to-severe depression. In this 12-week open-label RCT, 72 participants completed the study. Compared with befriending therapy, the Mediterranean diet group had a significantly greater BDI-II improvement, with a mean difference of 14.4 points, and a quality-of-life improvement, with a mean difference of 12.7 points. The study was funded by Endeavour College of Natural Health and the University of Technology Sydney, and the funding body was reported to have no role in design, implementation, or analysis. ([Bayes et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522000612 "The effect of a Mediterranean diet on the symptoms of depression in young males (the AMMEND: A Mediterranean Diet in MEN with Depression study): a randomized controlled trial - ScienceDirect"))

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of Mediterranean diet RCTs included five trials and 1,507 initially included participants. Mediterranean diet interventions reduced depressive symptoms with an SMD of -0.53, but heterogeneity was high, the prediction interval crossed the null, risk of bias ranged from "some concerns" to "high," and certainty of evidence was low. This supports diet as a plausible adjunct, not as a stand-alone replacement for evidence-based mental health care. ([Bizzozero-Peroni et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuad176 "The impact of the Mediterranean diet on alleviating depressive symptoms in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials | Nutrition Reviews | Oxford Academic"))

The [MooDFOOD trial](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2726983 "Effect of Multinutrient Supplementation and Food-Related Behavioral Activation Therapy on Prevention of Major Depressive Disorder Among Overweight or Obese Adults With Subsyndromal Depressive Symptoms: The MooDFOOD Randomized Clinical Trial | Obesity | JAMA | JAMA Network") adds an important negative result. In 1,025 overweight or obese adults with subsyndromal depressive symptoms, one year of multinutrient supplementation did not prevent major depressive disorder compared with placebo, and food-related behavioral activation therapy did not significantly reduce MDD episodes compared with no therapy. The trial was funded by the European Union FP7 MooDFOOD project with UK NIHR support, and the authors reported several outside conflicts for some investigators. ([Bot et al., 2019](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2726983 "Effect of Multinutrient Supplementation and Food-Related Behavioral Activation Therapy on Prevention of Major Depressive Disorder Among Overweight or Obese Adults With Subsyndromal Depressive Symptoms: The MooDFOOD Randomized Clinical Trial | Obesity | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

## Does the gut-brain axis explain depression and anxiety?

The gut-brain axis helps explain one part of mental health biology, but it does not explain mental health as a whole. Depression and anxiety are heterogeneous syndromes. In some people, gut microbial changes may contribute through inflammation, metabolic signaling, diet-related pathways, or stress-system regulation. In others, microbiome changes may be downstream of depression-related changes in diet, medication, or gastrointestinal function. The human evidence is strongest for "association plus plausible mechanisms," weaker for "causation," and still weaker for "specific microbiome treatment reliably improves clinical outcomes." ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"); [Radjabzadeh et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-34502-3 "Gut microbiome-wide association study of depressive symptoms | Nature Communications"); [Lai and Xiong, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0163834325000775 "Analysis of gut microbiota and depression and anxiety: Mendelian randomization from three datasets - ScienceDirect"); [Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047); [Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"))

The strongest practical conclusion is not "fix the gut, fix the brain." A better conclusion is: the gut-brain axis is one biologically plausible pathway among many, psychobiotics remain product-, strain-, and population-specific with inconsistent replication, and the Mediterranean-style dietary evidence cited here is more directly actionable as a general lifestyle adjunct than the evidence for any single named probiotic product. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"); [Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047); [Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"); [Nikolova et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2806011 "Acceptability, Tolerability, and Estimates of Putative Treatment Effects of Probiotics as Adjunctive Treatment in Patients With Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Trials | JAMA Psychiatry | JAMA Network"); [Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Bayes et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522000612 "The effect of a Mediterranean diet on the symptoms of depression in young males (the AMMEND: A Mediterranean Diet in MEN with Depression study): a randomized controlled trial - ScienceDirect"); [Bizzozero-Peroni et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuad176 "The impact of the Mediterranean diet on alleviating depressive symptoms in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials | Nutrition Reviews | Oxford Academic"))

([Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047))

- Strain-focused probiotic review: 12 RCTs, 707 participants; BDI mean difference -2.69, but HAMD, DASS, and MADRS findings were not significant. - SMILES dietary RCT: 12 weeks; MADRS between-group difference 7.1 points; remission 32.3 percent vs 8.0 percent; small and unblinded, with multiple sponsor/conflict disclosures. - Mediterranean diet RCT meta-analysis: five RCTs; SMD -0.53; high heterogeneity; low certainty evidence. ## Practical interpretation

- The gut-brain axis is biologically plausible and supported by human association studies, but current evidence does not support a single "mental health microbiome" signature. ([Gao et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02670-5 "Gut microbiota composition in depressive disorder: a systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression | Translational Psychiatry"); [Radjabzadeh et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-34502-3 "Gut microbiome-wide association study of depressive symptoms | Nature Communications"))

- The serotonin story is often simplified: the gut makes most body serotonin, but peripheral serotonin does not readily cross the blood-brain barrier, so mood effects must be indirect. ([Martin et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1210/en.2016-1839))

- Psychobiotics are not all interchangeable. Benefits, when seen, are product-, strain-, dose-, duration-, and population-specific; small trials and industry involvement remain major concerns. ([Asad et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/7/e1504/7934047); [Rahmannia et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13099-024-00634-8 "Strain-specific effects of probiotics on depression and anxiety: a meta-analysis | Gut Pathogens | Springer Nature Link"); [Nikolova et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapsychiatry/fullarticle/2806011 "Acceptability, Tolerability, and Estimates of Putative Treatment Effects of Probiotics as Adjunctive Treatment in Patients With Depression: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Trials | JAMA Psychiatry | JAMA Network"))

- Mediterranean-style dietary improvement has more directly actionable clinical evidence as a general adjunctive mental-health strategy than any single probiotic formula cited here, but the diet evidence is still not definitive and should not be framed as a replacement for psychotherapy, medication when indicated, or clinical care. ([Jacka et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial) | BMC Medicine | Springer Nature Link"); [Bayes et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522000612 "The effect of a Mediterranean diet on the symptoms of depression in young males (the AMMEND: A Mediterranean Diet in MEN with Depression study): a randomized controlled trial - ScienceDirect"); [Bizzozero-Peroni et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuad176 "The impact of the Mediterranean diet on alleviating depressive symptoms in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials | Nutrition Reviews | Oxford Academic"))
