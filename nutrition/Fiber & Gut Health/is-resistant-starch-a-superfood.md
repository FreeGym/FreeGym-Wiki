# Is Resistant Starch a Superfood?

Resistant starch is a real and biologically active part of the diet, but the human evidence does not support treating it as a single, uniform "superfood." The strongest data support a few narrower points: resistant starch is fermented in the colon and can increase butyrate-related fermentation; cooling cooked starches can create some RS3 through retrogradation; and higher-dose supplemental resistant starch, especially RS2, can improve some glycemic markers in some populations. But those effects are heterogeneous by resistant starch type, food matrix, dose, duration, and baseline metabolic status, and long-term disease-outcome evidence is much weaker than the hype suggests. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1118229/full); [APJCN](https://apjcn.qdu.edu.cn/24_4_24.pdf); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27593182/); [Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/effects-of-resistant-starch-on-glycaemic-control-a-systematic-review-and-metaanalysis/95174D9505D6D7C92F88C871DC0D958A))

## What resistant starch is and is not

Resistant starch (RS) is starch that remains undigested in the small intestine and is fermented in the large intestine, producing short-chain fatty acids. It is not one thing. Current classifications describe at least five types: RS1 is physically inaccessible starch in intact plant structures; RS2 is native high-amylose starch such as that in raw potatoes or green bananas; RS3 is retrograded starch formed after cooking and cooling; RS4 is chemically modified starch; and RS5 is a starch-fatty-acid complex. Because these forms differ in structure, digestibility, and fermentability, results from one type or food source should not be assumed to apply to all of them. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

That distinction matters in human trials. In a 2023 systematic review/meta-analysis in people with type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, acute postprandial glucose improved with RS1 and RS2, but not with RS3 in the pooled acute analysis. In the chronic analyses, fasting glucose and fasting insulin improvements were seen mainly with RS2, not cleanly across all RS types. The paper’s conclusion was essentially that different RS types appear to influence glucose homeostasis differently. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1118229/full))

## Why cooling rice and potatoes can increase resistant starch

The "cook and cool" idea is based on real starch chemistry. When starch is gelatinized by cooking and then cooled, some of it retrogrades and becomes RS3. In one rice study, resistant starch rose from 0.64 g/100 g in freshly cooked rice to 1.30 g/100 g after 10 hours at room temperature and to 1.65 g/100 g after 24 hours at 4°C followed by reheating. In the same crossover experiment, the cooled/reheated rice lowered 2-hour glycemic response versus fresh rice in healthy adults (IAUC 125 vs 152 mmol.min/L). ([APJCN](https://apjcn.qdu.edu.cn/24_4_24.pdf))

A food-based potato trial points in the same direction, but it is also a good example of why details matter. In a randomized crossover study, 250 g of baked-then-chilled potatoes produced lower early glucose and insulin responses than boiled potatoes in women with elevated fasting glucose and insulin, and lower insulin and GIP area-under-the-curve values, but not a lower overall glucose area-under-the-curve. This trial was funded by the Alliance for Potato Research and Education, although the paper states that the funders had no role in the study design, data collection, data analysis or interpretation, writing, or publication decision. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/11/9/2066))

## How much cooking and cooling actually changes the dose

The chemistry is real, but the dose issue is where popular summaries often go off the rails. In the rice study above, the absolute increase was about 1 g resistant starch per 100 g rice. That is meaningful as a food-processing effect, but it is small relative to the amounts used in many supplement trials. ([APJCN](https://apjcn.qdu.edu.cn/24_4_24.pdf); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

That gap shows up clearly in pooled intervention data. A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis reported that most included trials used powdered supplements rather than ordinary foods, often commercially manufactured Hi-maize 260. In the supplement subgroup, RS content was about 34 to 60% and doses ranged from 6 to 40 g/day; in food-matrix interventions, RS content was about 3.1 to 21.2% and doses ranged from 4 to 30.9 g/day, with cooking or baking likely reducing the actual RS delivered. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

A 2021 meta-analysis of 19 human trials also found that glycemic effects tended to be larger when the dose exceeded 28 g/day and the intervention lasted more than 8 weeks. That is why "cool your rice" is directionally correct, but it does not reproduce the exposure used in many supplement studies. ([APJCN](https://apjcn.qdu.edu.cn/24_4_24.pdf); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full); [Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/effects-of-resistant-starch-on-glycaemic-control-a-systematic-review-and-metaanalysis/95174D9505D6D7C92F88C871DC0D958A))

## Resistant starch and butyrate production

This is the claim with the cleanest support. A systematic review/meta-analysis of randomized trials in healthy adults found that resistant starch significantly increased fecal wet weight and butyrate concentration and lowered fecal pH. In other words, human supplementation studies do support the idea that resistant starch can increase colonic fermentation in a butyrate-favoring direction. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27593182/))

A more detailed human intervention also found the same pattern, but with an important caveat. In a 7-day study using RS2 from unmodified potato starch, fecal butyrate rose from 8 to 12 mmol/kg wet feces, yet responses varied widely between individuals. This study was supported by Procter and Gamble Company and the Howard Hughes Medical Institute, and company scientists were acknowledged for feedback on data analysis and interpretation, so it should not be treated as stand-alone proof. Its main value is showing that even when butyrate rises on average, the response is highly individual. ([Springer Nature Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40168-016-0178-x))

What does not follow automatically is the rest of the "superfood" story. A rise in fecal butyrate is not the same thing as proven reductions in inflammatory markers or lower cancer incidence in humans. Those downstream outcomes remain much less settled clinically than the fermentation data themselves. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27593182/); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full); [The Lancet Oncology](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanonc/article/PIIS1470-2045%2812%2970475-8/fulltext); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433960/))

## Metabolic health: supplementation trials

The metabolic literature is promising but inconsistent. In the 2021 meta-analysis of 19 human trials and 503 participants, resistant starch significantly lowered fasting plasma glucose and HOMA-IR versus digestible starch, with larger effects when the dose was above 28 g/day or the intervention exceeded 8 weeks. But many other insulin-related endpoints were not significant, and the HOMA-IR result became non-significant when one trial was removed in sensitivity analysis. The overall trial quality was rated moderate, and most available studies were RS2 rather than other types. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/effects-of-resistant-starch-on-glycaemic-control-a-systematic-review-and-metaanalysis/95174D9505D6D7C92F88C871DC0D958A))

The newer type-specific review sharpens that picture. In prediabetes and type 2 diabetes, acute postprandial glucose fell with RS1 and RS2, but not RS3, and chronic fasting glucose and fasting insulin improvements were seen mainly with RS2. Effects also looked stronger in type 2 diabetes than in prediabetes. That is a long way from saying "resistant starch" in general improves metabolic health across all forms and all people. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1118229/full))

A good anchor trial for the inconsistency is a 12-week randomized controlled trial in 68 adults with prediabetes using 45 g/day high-amylose maize RS2. Relative to control, HbA1c fell only slightly and TNF-α fell, but insulin sensitivity, insulin secretion, and most glycemic outcomes did not improve. That makes it especially useful as a counterweight to more enthusiastic findings. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522029525))

## Inflammation and colorectal cancer claims

Inflammation claims are easy to oversell. A 2025 meta-analysis in adults with metabolic-syndrome-related risks found fairly steady effects for some outcomes such as total cholesterol and LDL cholesterol, but reductions in fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, and TNF-α remained heterogeneous and not firmly settled. So the current evidence supports "possible benefit in some contexts" more than "reliably anti-inflammatory." ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

The colorectal-cancer claim is weaker still. The best-known long-term randomized trial is CAPP2 in Lynch syndrome, where participants took 30 g/day resistant starch or placebo for up to 4 years. It found no detectable effect on colorectal cancer incidence. That null result makes it a useful reality check against the common claim that resistant starch clearly lowers colorectal cancer risk in humans. A later planned follow-up reported fewer non-colorectal cancers, especially upper-GI cancers, in the resistant starch group, but that does not change the colorectal-null finding. ([The Lancet Oncology](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanonc/article/PIIS1470-2045%2812%2970475-8/fulltext); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433960/))

## Practical interpretation

- Resistant starch looks more like a useful carbohydrate property than a superfood. The best-supported effect is increased fermentation and butyrate production, not a broad, proven package of downstream clinical benefits. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27593182/); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full); [The Lancet Oncology](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanonc/article/PIIS1470-2045%2812%2970475-8/fulltext); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433960/))

- Cooling and reheating rice or potatoes can increase RS3 and sometimes blunt postprandial response, but the absolute amount added in ordinary foods is usually much smaller than the doses used in supplement trials. ([APJCN](https://apjcn.qdu.edu.cn/24_4_24.pdf); [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/11/9/2066); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

- The metabolic evidence is strongest for supplemental RS2 given at higher doses and for longer durations, especially in people with type 2 diabetes or related metabolic risk, and even there the effects are mixed. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/effects-of-resistant-starch-on-glycaemic-control-a-systematic-review-and-metaanalysis/95174D9505D6D7C92F88C871DC0D958A); [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1118229/full))

- "Resistant starch" is too broad a label for sweeping claims. Type, dose, food matrix, processing, and population all matter, so benefits do not transfer cleanly across rice, potatoes, green bananas, high-amylose maize powder, and chemically modified starches. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1655664/full))

- The current human evidence does not justify claiming that resistant starch clearly reduces colorectal cancer risk, although a later Lynch-syndrome follow-up suggested fewer non-colorectal, especially upper-GI, cancers. ([The Lancet Oncology](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanonc/article/PIIS1470-2045%2812%2970475-8/fulltext); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9433960/))
