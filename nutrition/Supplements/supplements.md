# Supplements

The most debated questions about dietary supplements - from the few that clearly work to the many that probably don't, and the industry problems that make it hard to tell the difference.

For individual vitamin and mineral debates, see [Micronutrients](../Micronutrients/README.md). For omega-3/fish oil, see [Fat](../Fat/fat.md). For BCAAs, see [Protein](../Protein/protein.md).

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

# The Industry

---

## Do You Even Need Supplements?

The supplement industry generates over $50 billion annually in the US alone. The "food first" position says most people eating a reasonable diet don't need any of it. The counter-argument is that modern food, soil depletion, and lifestyle gaps make supplementation rational.

**Competing claims:**

1. A well-designed diet provides everything most people need - supplements are a waste of money for the majority of the population
2. Specific populations genuinely benefit from targeted supplementation: vegans (B12), people with low sun exposure (vitamin D), pregnant women (folate), athletes (creatine, possibly others)
3. Modern food is less nutrient-dense than it used to be due to soil depletion, industrial farming, and long supply chains - supplementation fills a real gap
4. The supplement industry creates demand for products people don't need by manufacturing deficiency fears and redefining "optimal" as "above what food provides"

**Evidence needed:** Nutrient adequacy surveys across dietary patterns, soil depletion and nutrient density trend data, supplement use and health outcome studies, cost-effectiveness analyses of targeted supplementation.

---

## Can You Trust What's in the Bottle?

The supplement industry in the US is regulated under DSHEA (1994), which does not require pre-market approval, proof of efficacy, or standardized testing. Third-party testing programs exist but are voluntary.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most supplements from reputable brands contain what they claim and are safe for consumers
2. Independent testing consistently finds products that don't match label claims - underdosed, overdosed, or containing unlisted ingredients
3. Contamination with banned substances is a real risk for athletes subject to anti-doping testing - WADA-accredited labs regularly find prohibited substances in over-the-counter supplements
4. Third-party certifications (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport, USP) are the minimum standard, but most consumers don't know to look for them

**Evidence needed:** Independent label accuracy testing data (ConsumerLab, Labdoor), WADA contamination studies, DSHEA regulatory framework analysis, third-party certification coverage and limitations.

---

## Are Proprietary Blends a Red Flag?

Many supplements list ingredients under a "proprietary blend" with a total weight but no individual doses. This is legal. The question is whether it's always deceptive.

**Competing claims:**

1. Proprietary blends exist to hide underdosing - companies include effective ingredients at trace amounts and list them on the label to create the impression of a comprehensive formula
2. Some companies genuinely protect formulation IP through proprietary blends - not every blend is underdosed
3. If a company won't tell you how much of each ingredient you're getting, you can't evaluate whether the product can work - transparency should be the default
4. The rise of "fully disclosed labels" in the sports supplement market shows that transparency is commercially viable, making the IP protection argument weaker

**Evidence needed:** Proprietary blend testing studies (actual doses vs effective doses), market analysis of disclosed vs undisclosed label trends, case studies of blend reformulations.

# Performance Supplements

---

## Creatine: The Most Evidence-Backed Supplement?

Creatine monohydrate may be the most studied supplement in sports nutrition history. The evidence for strength and power benefits is strong. The remaining debates are about loading, forms, and non-performance benefits.

**Competing claims:**

1. Creatine monohydrate reliably increases strength, power output, and lean mass when combined with resistance training - the evidence is overwhelming across hundreds of studies
2. Creatine monohydrate is the only form with robust evidence - creatine HCl, buffered creatine, and other forms have no proven advantage and are typically more expensive
3. Loading phases (20g/day for 5-7 days) saturate stores faster but aren't necessary - 3-5g/day reaches the same saturation in 3-4 weeks
4. Emerging research on creatine and brain health (cognitive function, TBI recovery, depression) is promising but not yet strong enough to recommend supplementation for these purposes alone

**Competing claims (safety):**

1. Creatine does not cause kidney damage in healthy individuals - this myth persists despite decades of safety data
2. Creatine causes water retention, which is real but intracellular (in muscle, not subcutaneous bloating) - the weight gain is mostly water initially, then lean mass over time
3. Creatine may cause GI distress at high single doses - splitting the dose or using micronized forms reduces this
4. Long-term safety data (5+ years of continuous use) is limited but existing data shows no adverse effects

**Evidence needed:** ISSN position stand on creatine, creatine and cognitive function meta-analyses, creatine form comparison studies, long-term safety data, kidney function studies in healthy users.

---

## Caffeine: Performance Enhancer or Crutch?

Caffeine is the world's most consumed psychoactive substance and one of the most reliable ergogenic aids in sports science. The debates are about dose, timing, tolerance, and whether habitual use diminishes the performance benefit.

**Competing claims:**

1. Caffeine at 3-6 mg/kg reliably improves endurance performance, strength endurance, and reaction time across a wide range of activities
2. Habitual caffeine users develop tolerance that blunts the ergogenic effect - a caffeine withdrawal period before competition may restore the performance benefit
3. Recent research suggests habitual users still get a performance boost from caffeine, just a smaller one - and withdrawal before competition impairs performance during the withdrawal period, negating the strategy
4. Individual response to caffeine varies enormously due to CYP1A2 gene variants - "slow metabolizers" may not benefit and could experience anxiety, elevated heart rate, or impaired sleep

**Evidence needed:** Caffeine and performance meta-analyses by sport type, habitual use and tolerance studies, CYP1A2 genotype and caffeine response data, optimal dosing and timing protocols.

---

## Beta-Alanine: Does the Tingle Mean It's Working?

Beta-alanine increases muscle carnosine, which buffers acid during high-intensity exercise. The tingling sensation (paresthesia) is its most recognizable effect. But does the performance benefit justify the supplementation?

**Competing claims:**

1. Beta-alanine improves performance in efforts lasting 1-4 minutes (high-intensity, glycolytic activities) by buffering hydrogen ions - the evidence is consistent across meta-analyses
2. The performance improvement is small (2-3% in the specific exercise duration window) and may not be meaningful for recreational exercisers
3. Beta-alanine has no benefit for efforts shorter than 60 seconds or longer than 10 minutes - the "buffering window" is narrow
4. The paresthesia (tingling) is harmless and unrelated to efficacy - it's caused by beta-alanine binding to nerve receptors in the skin, not by the buffering mechanism

**Evidence needed:** Beta-alanine meta-analyses by exercise duration, carnosine loading and washout studies, practical significance of 2-3% improvement for non-elite athletes, paresthesia mechanism data.

---

## Citrulline: Better Than Arginine for Pumps and Performance?

L-citrulline (and citrulline malate) is marketed for nitric oxide production, blood flow, and "pumps." It largely replaced L-arginine in pre-workout formulas after research showed arginine has poor oral bioavailability.

**Competing claims:**

1. Citrulline raises plasma arginine more effectively than arginine supplementation itself because it bypasses first-pass metabolism in the gut and liver
2. Citrulline malate at 6-8g may improve high-rep resistance training performance and reduce perceived exertion - but the evidence is mixed and study quality varies
3. The "pump" from citrulline is real (increased blood flow via nitric oxide) but cosmetic - it doesn't directly cause muscle growth
4. Many pre-workouts underdose citrulline (using 1-3g when studies use 6-8g), making the ingredient present on the label but unlikely to be effective

**Evidence needed:** Citrulline vs arginine bioavailability comparisons, citrulline malate and resistance training performance RCTs, effective dose-response data, nitric oxide and muscle hypertrophy research.

---

## Nitrate/Beetroot Juice: Legitimate Endurance Aid?

Dietary nitrate (primarily from beetroot juice) improves exercise efficiency by reducing the oxygen cost of submaximal exercise. It's one of the few supplements with a clear mechanism and consistent endurance benefits.

**Competing claims:**

1. Beetroot juice (~6-8 mmol nitrate, 2-3 hours pre-exercise) reliably improves endurance performance by 1-3% in recreational athletes through the nitrate-nitrite-NO pathway
2. The benefit diminishes or disappears in elite athletes, possibly because they already have optimized oxygen delivery and utilization
3. The performance window is specific - benefits are clearest in sustained moderate-to-high intensity efforts (time trials, sustained running) and less clear for intermittent or short-duration activities
4. Antibacterial mouthwash blocks the oral bacteria that convert nitrate to nitrite, potentially eliminating the benefit - a practical consideration rarely mentioned

**Evidence needed:** Beetroot juice meta-analyses stratified by training status, elite vs recreational athlete comparisons, mouthwash interference studies, optimal dosing and timing protocols.

---

## Omega-3 for Performance and Recovery

Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) are covered in [Fat](../Fat/fat.md) for general health. But the performance and recovery angle is a distinct set of questions: do omega-3s reduce muscle soreness, enhance muscle protein synthesis, protect the brain in contact sports, or blunt training adaptations through anti-inflammatory effects?

**Competing claims:**

1. Omega-3 supplementation (2-4g EPA+DHA daily) reduces delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and accelerates recovery after damaging exercise in multiple RCTs
2. Omega-3s may enhance muscle protein synthesis by improving the anabolic sensitivity of muscle to amino acids and insulin - the Smith et al. studies show increased MPS rates with omega-3 supplementation in both young and older adults
3. The anti-inflammatory properties that reduce soreness might also blunt the inflammatory signaling needed for training adaptation - the same concern as with NSAIDs and antioxidant supplements
4. The concussion and brain health angle is emerging but preliminary - animal models and small human studies suggest omega-3s may support neuroprotection and recovery from traumatic brain injury, but definitive evidence in athletes is lacking

**Evidence needed:** Omega-3 and DOMS/recovery meta-analyses, Smith et al. MPS studies and replications, omega-3 and training adaptation (do gains actually differ?), omega-3 and TBI/concussion recovery trials in athletes, optimal EPA:DHA ratio and dose for performance outcomes.

---

## Sodium Bicarbonate: The Original Buffering Agent

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) was used as an ergogenic aid before beta-alanine existed. It buffers blood acidity during high-intensity exercise. The evidence is strong, but GI side effects are the major practical barrier.

**Competing claims:**

1. Sodium bicarbonate at 0.2-0.3 g/kg reliably improves performance in high-intensity efforts lasting 1-7 minutes by buffering extracellular acidosis - the evidence base is robust and consistent across meta-analyses
2. GI distress (nausea, diarrhea, bloating) affects a substantial proportion of users and can impair performance more than the buffering helps - this is the primary reason it's not more widely used
3. Serial loading protocols (smaller doses spread over days) and enteric-coated capsules may reduce GI side effects while maintaining the performance benefit, but evidence for these strategies is still developing
4. Sodium bicarbonate and beta-alanine buffer through different mechanisms (extracellular vs intracellular) and may have additive effects when combined - some studies support this, but the combined GI burden can be limiting

**Evidence needed:** Sodium bicarbonate and performance meta-analyses by exercise type and duration, GI side effect prevalence and severity data, serial loading protocol trials, bicarbonate + beta-alanine combination studies.

# Health & Recovery Supplements

---

## Collagen Supplements: Joint Saver or Expensive Gelatin?

Collagen supplements (typically hydrolyzed collagen peptides) are marketed for joint health, skin elasticity, and injury recovery. Sales have exploded. The question is whether ingesting collagen actually reaches and rebuilds connective tissue.

**Competing claims:**

1. Collagen peptides are absorbed and can stimulate collagen synthesis in tendons, ligaments, and skin - studies show benefits for joint pain (especially in athletes), skin hydration, and tendon stiffness
2. Collagen is just protein with a poor amino acid profile (low in essential amino acids) - you'd get better results from whey or a varied protein intake
3. The Keith Baar research model (gelatin + vitamin C before exercise) provides a plausible mechanism: collagen-derived amino acids may preferentially support connective tissue synthesis when combined with mechanical loading
4. Most collagen studies are industry-funded, small, and use subjective pain outcomes - the evidence base is weaker than marketing suggests

**Evidence needed:** Collagen peptide absorption and tissue incorporation studies, Keith Baar gelatin + vitamin C protocol trials, collagen vs whey for joint outcomes, independent (non-industry) collagen RCTs.

---

## Glucosamine and Chondroitin: Still Worth Taking for Joints?

Glucosamine and chondroitin were the original joint health supplements and remain among the highest-selling globally. Early trials were positive. Then large, well-designed trials were negative. The debate has been going back and forth for two decades.

**Competing claims:**

1. Early trials (mostly industry-funded, smaller) showed glucosamine and chondroitin reduced osteoarthritis pain and slowed cartilage loss - these drove widespread adoption
2. The GAIT trial (NIH-funded, large, rigorous) found glucosamine and chondroitin no better than placebo for knee osteoarthritis pain overall, though a subgroup with moderate-to-severe pain showed benefit from the combination
3. Pharmaceutical-grade crystalline glucosamine sulfate (the European formulation, Rottapharm) may be more effective than the glucosamine hydrochloride used in many US studies - the "glucosamine doesn't work" conclusion may partly reflect formulation and quality differences
4. For athletes without diagnosed osteoarthritis, there's no evidence that glucosamine or chondroitin prevents joint problems or offers prophylactic benefit - the "joint support" marketing to healthy people is not evidence-based

**Evidence needed:** GAIT trial data and subgroup analyses, glucosamine sulfate vs hydrochloride comparisons, Cochrane reviews on glucosamine for OA, prophylactic use studies in healthy/athletic populations.

---

## Ashwagandha: Adaptogen or Overhyped?

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is the most popular "adaptogen" supplement. Claims range from reducing cortisol to increasing testosterone to improving strength. It has more human trials than most herbal supplements.

**Competing claims:**

1. Ashwagandha consistently reduces self-reported stress and cortisol levels in RCTs, with moderate effect sizes
2. Some studies show modest improvements in strength, power output, and VO2max when combined with resistance training - but effect sizes are small and study quality is mixed
3. Testosterone increases reported in some studies are modest (10-15%) and may reflect cortisol reduction rather than direct androgenic effects - and the clinical significance of such increases is unclear
4. Safety data is mostly short-term (8-12 weeks) - there are emerging case reports of liver injury with some ashwagandha extracts, and long-term safety is not established

**Evidence needed:** Ashwagandha and cortisol meta-analyses, strength and performance RCTs, testosterone mechanism studies, liver safety case reports and extract standardization data.

---

## Curcumin/Turmeric: Anti-Inflammatory or Poorly Absorbed Spice?

Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, has thousands of published studies. It's marketed for inflammation, joint pain, and general health. The fundamental problem is bioavailability - most curcumin is barely absorbed.

**Competing claims:**

1. Curcumin has potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties in cell and animal studies, and some human trials show benefits for joint pain comparable to NSAIDs
2. Standard curcumin has extremely poor bioavailability - most of what you swallow is metabolized before reaching systemic circulation, making effective dosing difficult
3. Enhanced bioavailability formulations (piperine, liposomal, phytosome) dramatically increase absorption, but each formulation has different pharmacokinetics and the research doesn't transfer cleanly between them
4. The sheer number of claimed benefits (anti-cancer, anti-Alzheimer's, anti-depression, anti-everything) is a red flag - compounds that "do everything" in vitro often do very little in vivo

**Evidence needed:** Curcumin bioavailability comparison studies across formulations, head-to-head trials vs NSAIDs for joint pain, curcumin and exercise recovery RCTs, PAINS compound analysis (curcumin as a false positive in drug screens).

---

## Probiotics: Gut Health Fix or Marketing Machine?

Probiotic supplements are a multi-billion dollar market built on the premise that adding bacteria to your gut improves health. The science is strain-specific and condition-specific, but marketing treats all probiotics as interchangeable.

**Competing claims:**

1. Specific probiotic strains have demonstrated benefits for specific conditions (antibiotic-associated diarrhea, IBS subtypes, certain infections) - but the benefits are strain-specific, not generic
2. Most commercial probiotics use strains chosen for shelf stability and cost, not for clinical evidence - the strain in the study is rarely the strain in the product you buy
3. Probiotic bacteria from supplements may not colonize the gut permanently - they pass through, potentially exerting transient effects but not fundamentally changing the microbiome
4. A diverse, fiber-rich diet does more for the microbiome than any probiotic supplement - fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut) may be more effective than capsules

**Evidence needed:** Strain-specific probiotic RCTs by condition, colonization and persistence studies, commercial product strain vs clinical strain comparisons, prebiotic fiber vs probiotic supplementation for microbiome outcomes.

---

## Tart Cherry Juice: Recovery Aid With Real Evidence?

Tart cherry juice (or concentrate) is one of the few "natural" recovery supplements with a growing evidence base. The proposed mechanism is anthocyanin-mediated reduction in inflammation and oxidative stress.

**Competing claims:**

1. Tart cherry juice reduces muscle soreness and accelerates strength recovery after damaging exercise in multiple RCTs
2. The anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects that reduce soreness may also blunt the training adaptation signal - the same concern raised about vitamin C/E supplementation
3. Most studies use a specific dose (roughly 30ml concentrate twice daily) around the exercise bout - casual consumption of cherry products likely doesn't replicate the effect
4. The calorie and sugar content of tart cherry juice is non-trivial (~120 calories per serving of juice), which matters for people in a calorie deficit

**Evidence needed:** Tart cherry and recovery meta-analyses, adaptation blunting studies (analogous to antioxidant supplement research), dose-response data, tart cherry vs other polyphenol sources.

---

## CBD for Recovery: Evidence or Hype?

CBD (cannabidiol) products are marketed for pain, inflammation, sleep, and anxiety in athletes. WADA removed CBD from the prohibited list in 2018. But the evidence base for most athletic claims is thin.

**Competing claims:**

1. CBD has anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties demonstrated in preclinical models, and anecdotal reports from athletes are overwhelmingly positive
2. Most human CBD research is in clinical populations (epilepsy, chronic pain) at pharmaceutical doses - not in healthy athletes at supplement doses
3. CBD products risk THC contamination, which remains prohibited under WADA - athletes using CBD products have tested positive for THC
4. Sleep improvement is the most plausible athletic benefit, with some RCTs showing modest effects on sleep quality at higher doses - but placebo effects are strong in sleep research

**Evidence needed:** CBD and exercise recovery RCTs in athletes, THC contamination testing of commercial CBD products, CBD and sleep quality meta-analyses, dose-response data in non-clinical populations.

---

## Melatonin: Sleep Supplement or Hormone You Shouldn't Take Casually?

Melatonin is sold as a supplement in many countries but regulated as a medication in others (most of Europe). It's the go-to recommendation for sleep, jet lag, and shift work. But dosing practices are often far above physiological levels.

**Competing claims:**

1. Melatonin is effective for jet lag and circadian rhythm adjustment, and modestly helpful for sleep onset in people with delayed sleep phase
2. Most people take far too much - physiological doses are 0.3-0.5mg, but commercial products commonly contain 3-10mg, which can cause grogginess, vivid dreams, and next-day impairment
3. Melatonin supplement labels are unreliable - one study found actual content ranged from 83% less to 478% more than labeled, and some contained serotonin
4. Long-term exogenous melatonin use may downregulate natural production, though evidence for this is limited and debated

**Evidence needed:** Melatonin dose-response studies (low vs high dose), label accuracy testing (Erland & Saxena study), long-term use and endogenous production data, jet lag and shift work meta-analyses.

# Weight Loss Supplements

---

## Do Fat Burners Work?

"Fat burner" is a marketing category, not a pharmacological one. These products typically combine caffeine, green tea extract, and various thermogenic compounds. The question is whether they add anything beyond what caffeine alone provides.

**Competing claims:**

1. The thermogenic effect of fat burner ingredients is real but tiny - typically 50-100 extra calories per day, which is less than a single apple
2. Caffeine is the only ingredient in most fat burners with meaningful evidence for increasing metabolic rate and fat oxidation - everything else is window dressing
3. Fat burners can be dangerous - products have been pulled from the market for containing undisclosed stimulants, and some ingredients (synephrine, yohimbine) carry cardiovascular risk
4. The psychological effect of taking a "fat burner" may improve diet adherence and motivation, which produces more real-world fat loss than the thermogenic effect itself

**Evidence needed:** Thermogenic supplement meta-analyses, caffeine vs multi-ingredient fat burner comparisons, adverse event reports and FDA enforcement actions, psychological/adherence effects of supplement use on diet outcomes.

---

## L-Carnitine: Fat Transporter or Expensive Placebo?

L-carnitine transports fatty acids into mitochondria for oxidation. The logic is simple: more carnitine = more fat burning. The reality is more complicated.

**Competing claims:**

1. Carnitine is essential for fat metabolism, but the body makes enough and dietary intake (from red meat) is adequate - supplementation doesn't raise muscle carnitine levels through oral dosing alone
2. Co-ingesting carnitine with a large insulin spike (high-carb meal) can increase muscle carnitine uptake - but this requires months of consistent dosing and a protocol most people won't follow
3. Carnitine may have recovery benefits independent of fat metabolism - some studies show reduced muscle damage markers and soreness after supplementation
4. The fat loss claims for carnitine are not supported by the weight of evidence - most RCTs show no effect on body composition

**Evidence needed:** Muscle carnitine uptake studies (Wall et al.), carnitine and body composition RCTs, carnitine and recovery/muscle damage trials, co-ingestion protocols and practical feasibility.

---

## Green Tea Extract: Metabolism Booster or Liver Risk?

Green tea extract (GTE), standardized for EGCG, is a staple in weight loss supplements. The metabolic boost is real but small. The liver toxicity signal is also real and less well-known.

**Competing claims:**

1. EGCG from green tea extract modestly increases fat oxidation and energy expenditure, particularly during exercise - the effect is small but consistent
2. The fat loss attributable to GTE is clinically insignificant for most people - roughly 1-2kg over 12 weeks in the most optimistic studies, and less with caffeine-controlled designs
3. Case reports and systematic reviews have identified green tea extract as a cause of liver injury (hepatotoxicity), particularly in fasted states and at higher doses - several products have been withdrawn
4. Drinking green tea delivers the same catechins in a food matrix with lower risk than concentrated extract capsules - the supplement form creates a dose and absorption pattern the liver isn't adapted to handle

**Evidence needed:** GTE and fat oxidation meta-analyses, GTE-attributed hepatotoxicity case reviews, dose-response and fasting state risk factors, green tea beverage vs extract comparison studies.

# Amino Acid & Protein-Adjacent Supplements

---

## EAAs vs BCAAs: Do You Need Standalone Amino Acids?

BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids) dominated the supplement market for years. Now EAAs (essential amino acids) are marketed as the upgrade. But the fundamental question is whether either is useful if you're already eating enough protein.

**Competing claims:**

1. BCAAs (leucine, isoleucine, valine) stimulate muscle protein synthesis through leucine's activation of mTOR - but BCAAs alone can't sustain MPS because you need all essential amino acids to actually build protein, and BCAAs provide only 3 of 9
2. EAAs provide all 9 essential amino acids and can stimulate a more complete MPS response than BCAAs alone - making them a better option if you're going to supplement amino acids at all
3. Neither BCAAs nor EAAs provide meaningful benefit over whole protein sources (whey, casein, egg, meat) when daily protein intake is adequate - they're an expensive way to get amino acids you could get from food
4. The one plausible use case is during fasted training or for people who can't tolerate whole protein around exercise - EAAs provide the building blocks with minimal calories and fast absorption

**Evidence needed:** BCAAs vs EAAs vs whey for MPS studies, BCAA supplementation in the context of adequate protein intake, EAA dosing and timing studies, cost-effectiveness comparisons of amino acid supplements vs whole protein.

---

## Glutamine: Once the King, Now Dethroned?

Glutamine was the most popular bodybuilding supplement through the late 1990s and 2000s. Claims centered on muscle recovery, immune function, and being "conditionally essential" during stress. The muscle-building claims have largely been debunked, but a gut health angle has emerged.

**Competing claims:**

1. Glutamine supplementation does not increase muscle mass, strength, or body composition in healthy, well-fed individuals - multiple RCTs show no benefit over placebo when protein intake is adequate
2. Glutamine is the primary fuel source for enterocytes (intestinal cells) and immune cells - supplementation may benefit gut barrier integrity and immune function during periods of extreme physical stress (ultra-endurance, overtraining, critical illness)
3. The "conditionally essential" framing is legitimate for ICU patients, burn victims, and possibly severely overtrained athletes - but it doesn't apply to the average gym-goer eating enough protein
4. Dietary protein provides abundant glutamine (it's the most abundant amino acid in the body and in most protein sources) - supplementing on top of adequate protein intake is redundant

**Evidence needed:** Glutamine and muscle outcomes meta-analyses in healthy populations, glutamine and gut permeability/barrier function studies, immune function in ultra-endurance and overreaching contexts, dietary glutamine adequacy from whole protein sources.

---

## HMB: Dramatic Results or Dramatic Conflict of Interest?

HMB (beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate) is a metabolite of leucine. It generated excitement when early studies showed large effects on muscle mass and strength. Then independent replications failed to reproduce the results, raising questions about the original lab's findings.

**Competing claims:**

1. HMB may reduce muscle protein breakdown (anti-catabolic) rather than increase synthesis, making it most useful during caloric restriction, immobilization, or in elderly populations with anabolic resistance
2. The most dramatic HMB results (free acid form) came primarily from one research group (Wilson/Lowery lab), and independent replications have not reproduced effects of similar magnitude - raising concerns about the reliability of the original data
3. In trained, well-fed individuals eating adequate protein, HMB supplementation consistently shows no meaningful benefit for muscle mass or strength gains
4. HMB-FA (free acid) vs HMB-Ca (calcium salt) may have different bioavailability and kinetics, complicating comparison across studies - but even the more bioavailable form hasn't shown consistent benefits in independent trials

**Evidence needed:** Independent HMB replication studies, HMB in caloric restriction/catabolic state RCTs, HMB in elderly/clinical populations, conflict of interest and funding analysis of original HMB research, HMB-FA vs HMB-Ca bioavailability comparisons.

---

## ZMA: Sleep and Testosterone from Zinc and Magnesium?

ZMA (zinc mononethionine aspartate, magnesium aspartate, vitamin B6) is marketed for sleep quality, testosterone, and recovery. The original study that launched the product was conducted by the patent holder. Independent replications have not confirmed the testosterone claims.

**Competing claims:**

1. The original Brilla & Conte study (2000) found ZMA increased testosterone and strength in football players - but the lead author held the patent on ZMA, creating a clear conflict of interest
2. Independent replications (Wilborn et al.) found no effect of ZMA on testosterone, free testosterone, or training adaptations in resistance-trained men with adequate zinc status
3. If you're actually deficient in zinc or magnesium, correcting the deficiency will improve sleep and may support testosterone - but that's an argument for zinc and magnesium, not for the branded ZMA product at a premium price
4. The sleep improvement some users report may be real but attributable to the magnesium component alone - magnesium supplementation has modest evidence for sleep quality independent of ZMA

**Evidence needed:** Original Brilla & Conte study critical analysis, Wilborn et al. replication data, zinc/magnesium deficiency correction and testosterone studies, magnesium and sleep meta-analyses, ZMA vs individual zinc + magnesium cost comparison.

# Red Flags & Controversial Supplements

---

## Testosterone Boosters: Do Any of Them Work?

"Natural testosterone booster" is one of the most profitable supplement categories and one of the least evidence-based. Products typically combine herbs (tribulus, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid) with zinc and vitamin D.

**Competing claims:**

1. No over-the-counter supplement meaningfully raises testosterone in healthy young men with adequate nutrition - the increases seen in some studies are within normal fluctuation and clinically irrelevant
2. Some ingredients (fenugreek, ashwagandha) may modestly increase testosterone or reduce SHBG in specific populations, but the magnitude is too small to produce noticeable muscle-building or performance effects
3. Correcting deficiencies in zinc, vitamin D, magnesium, and sleep will do more for testosterone than any supplement stack - most "low T" in young men is a lifestyle problem, not a supplement problem
4. The category exists because it sells to men's insecurities, not because the products work - marketing leverages the word "testosterone" for its psychological effect

**Evidence needed:** Testosterone booster ingredient meta-analyses (tribulus, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid), deficiency correction vs supplementation studies, clinical significance thresholds for testosterone changes, marketing and regulatory analysis.

---

## SARMs: The Underground Supplement Problem

Selective Androgen Receptor Modulators (SARMs) are sold as "legal alternatives to steroids" online and in some supplement stores. They are not approved for human use, not legal to sell as supplements, and carry real health risks.

**Competing claims:**

1. SARMs are pharmacologically active compounds that do increase muscle mass and strength - they are not "supplements" in any meaningful sense, they are unapproved drugs
2. The safety profile of SARMs in humans is largely unknown - clinical trials were discontinued for several SARMs due to liver toxicity and other adverse effects
3. Products sold as SARMs frequently contain different compounds than labeled, wrong doses, or are contaminated with actual anabolic steroids - quality control is nonexistent
4. Athletes who test positive for SARMs face the same consequences as those who test positive for anabolic steroids, regardless of whether the product was marketed as a "supplement"

**Evidence needed:** SARMs product testing studies (actual vs labeled content), clinical trial safety data and discontinuation reasons, anti-doping positive test data from SARMs, regulatory enforcement actions.

---

## Pre-Workout Supplements: Necessary or Dependency?

Pre-workout supplements are among the best-selling products in sports nutrition. Most are built around caffeine with varying add-ons. The concern isn't just efficacy - it's whether habitual use creates dependency and whether stimulant escalation is a real pattern.

**Competing claims:**

1. The effective ingredients in most pre-workouts (caffeine, citrulline, beta-alanine, creatine) can be bought individually for less money and at effective doses - the pre-workout format is a convenience and marketing play
2. Many pre-workouts contain caffeine at 200-400mg per serving, which is fine for some people but excessive for others - and the trend toward "high-stim" products pushing 500mg+ is a genuine safety concern
3. Habitual pre-workout use creates a psychological dependency where people feel they "can't train" without it - this is a real pattern even if the physiological tolerance is to caffeine, not the product
4. Some pre-workouts have contained unlisted stimulants (DMAA, DMHA, BMPEA) that were later banned - the "pre-workout" category has the worst track record for adulteration in the supplement industry

**Evidence needed:** Multi-ingredient pre-workout efficacy RCTs, caffeine dose escalation and dependency patterns in athletes, adulteration prevalence in pre-workout products, psychological dependency vs physiological tolerance studies.

---

## Supplement Stacking: More Ingredients, More Results?

Taking multiple supplements together ("stacking") is standard practice in fitness culture. The assumption is that benefits are additive. But interactions, redundancy, and diminishing returns are rarely considered.

**Competing claims:**

1. Some combinations have additive or synergistic effects - caffeine + creatine is well-supported, vitamin D + K2 has a plausible rationale, iron with vitamin C improves absorption
2. Many popular stacks are redundant - taking a pre-workout with caffeine, a fat burner with caffeine, and drinking coffee means you're just taking a lot of caffeine from three sources
3. Some combinations may interfere with each other - calcium and iron compete for absorption, high-dose antioxidants may blunt training adaptations, and some herb-drug interactions are clinically significant
4. The more supplements you take, the harder it is to identify what's actually working, what's causing side effects, and what's wasting money - simplicity has diagnostic value

**Evidence needed:** Supplement interaction databases, additive vs synergistic effect studies for common stacks, absorption interference data for concurrent supplement use, cost-benefit analyses of common stacking protocols.
