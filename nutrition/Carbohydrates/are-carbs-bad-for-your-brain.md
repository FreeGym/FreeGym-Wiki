# Are Carbs Bad for Your Brain?

“Carbs” is not a single exposure in the research literature, and many popular claims blend together _carbohydrate amount_ (high-carb vs low-carb) with _carbohydrate quality_ (high glycemic load/sugar/refined vs higher-fiber/lower glycemic). ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03525-6))

Across human studies, the **more consistent signals** for worse brain-related outcomes tend to track with _high-glycemic/high-sugar patterns and impaired glucose regulation_ (especially in metabolically vulnerable groups), rather than with “carbohydrates” as a macronutrient category in the abstract-**though some cohort analyses also report associations with total carbohydrate intake**. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824); [Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Chi et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0289782); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w))

Ketones can be an effective alternative brain fuel in some contexts, but “ketones are superior” is not established as a general rule in humans. ([Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001); [Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Chinna-Meyyappan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/1028415X.2022.2143609))

## What “carbs” usually means in these debates

In practice, the competing claims map onto different measurable exposures used in studies, including dietary glycemic index (GI), dietary glycemic load (GL), added/total sugars, and carbohydrate-to-fiber ratio. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824); [Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03525-6))

GI/GL are commonly used to operationalize **estimated** postprandial glycemic impact from diet (how quickly and how much glucose is _expected_ to appear after meals), which is directly relevant to the “blood sugar swings” claim. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824); [Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182))

Carbohydrate-to-fiber ratio is used in recent cohort work as a proxy for carbohydrate quality (more refined/less fiber vs more fiber per carbohydrate). ([Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w))

## What the brain can run on: glucose and ketones

Human intervention research in cognitive impairment commonly frames ketones as an “alternative energy substrate” when glucose utilization is impaired, especially in Alzheimer’s disease (AD)-related research. ([Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Kellar & Craft, 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1474-4422%2820%2930231-3); [State of the Science, 2024](https://doi.org/10.14336/AD.2023.0814))

In humans with subjective cognitive decline, mild cognitive impairment (MCI), or AD, medium-chain fatty acid/triglyceride (MCFA/MCT) supplementation reliably increases circulating ketone bodies in meta-analyses. ([Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001))

Those same meta-analyses treat ketone availability as biologically relevant to cognition in these populations, but they also emphasize heterogeneity in interventions and cognitive outcomes (which matters for “ketones are superior” claims). ([Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001))

## Claim 1: “The brain prefers glucose, and low-carb diets impair cognition”

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 30 randomized controlled trials (total n≈1,781) found **no significant overall effect** of low-carbohydrate diets on mood disturbance, depression, anxiety, quality of life, or _memory speed_, while noting subgroup signals (including an unfavorable decrease in memory speed in adults older than 55 years in subgroup analyses). ([Arsalandeh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1080/09637486.2024.2430006))

A 2023 systematic review focused on ketogenic diet effects on cognition concluded that findings across studies are heterogeneous rather than showing a consistent “cognitive impairment from low-carb” pattern. ([Chinna-Meyyappan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/1028415X.2022.2143609))

In an obesity-treatment randomized trial comparing higher- vs lower-carbohydrate dieting, cognitive performance across multiple tasks changed similarly over time in both groups, with no consistent between-diet differences reported. ([Makris et al., 2013](https://www.nature.com/articles/nutd201329))

Taken together, randomized evidence does **not** support the strong version of the claim that “low-carb diets impair cognition” **on average in the studied populations and timeframes**, although subgroup concerns (e.g., older adults in some analyses) remain plausible and understudied. ([Arsalandeh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1080/09637486.2024.2430006); [Chinna-Meyyappan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/1028415X.2022.2143609))

## Claim 2: “Ketones are a superior brain fuel, and the brain works better without carbs”

If “superior” means “can support cognition,” ketone-producing strategies clearly _can_ support brain function in some human settings, because MCT/MCFA supplementation increases circulating ketones in meta-analyses of cognitively impaired populations. ([Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001))

If “superior” means “consistently improves cognition compared with carbohydrate-containing diets,” the evidence is not that clean, because systematic reviews/meta-analyses describe mixed cognitive findings and substantial heterogeneity across interventions, durations, and cognitive tests. ([Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001); [Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Chinna-Meyyappan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/1028415X.2022.2143609))

In Alzheimer’s disease and MCI specifically, ketogenic interventions have advanced to randomized trials (including a randomized crossover trial of a modified ketogenic diet in AD and randomized trials of ketogenic/MCT-style approaches in cognitive impairment), which is consistent with a serious research signal but not proof of superiority for everyone. ([Phillips et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13195-021-00783-x); [Fortier et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1002/alz.12206); [Sun et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3233/JAD-230406))

Meta-analyses of MCT/MCFA interventions report that cognition-related outcomes may improve in some subgroups (including findings stratified by APOE genotype in some analyses), which argues for “context-dependent benefit” more than “universal superiority.” ([Sun et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3233/JAD-230406); [Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104))

## Claim 3: “Blood sugar swings from refined carbs impair cognition; stable carbs are fine”

On _acute_ cognitive performance, a 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis found that **lower-GL meal manipulations (most commonly lower-GL breakfasts in the included trials)** had small, domain- and timing-dependent effects on cognition rather than a uniform benefit or harm. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824))

In people with type 2 diabetes, a 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis found that higher acute glycemic variability was associated with worse cognitive performance (pooled association approximately r≈−0.22 across included studies), which is consistent with the idea that larger glucose swings can matter in metabolically vulnerable groups. ([Chi et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0289782))

Relatedly, hypoglycemic episodes are associated with higher dementia risk in meta-analyses, reinforcing that glucose dysregulation (including lows) is not neurologically neutral **in diabetes populations (and especially in older patients treated with glucose-lowering therapies)**. ([Mattishent et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fendo.2021.571568); [Cao et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13098-024-01346-4))

The “stable carbs are fine” framing fits the evidence better when interpreted as “carbohydrate quality and glucose stability may matter more than carbohydrate presence,” especially in people with impaired glucose regulation. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824); [Chi et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0289782))

## Claim 4: “Long-term high carb intake may contribute to dementia risk”

Recent large cohort evidence supports an association between carbohydrate _quality/impact_ (GI/GL) and incident dementia risk. ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182))

In a UK Biobank prospective analysis, the authors report that **low-GI diets were associated with lower risk** of all-cause dementia/AD/vascular dementia, while **high-GL diets were associated with higher risk**, which directly addresses the “high carb → dementia” claim in a more specific (and testable) way than total carbohydrate grams. ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182))

Another UK Biobank analysis using carbohydrate-to-fiber ratio (a carbohydrate quality proxy) found that a lower ratio was associated with reduced dementia risk, which again points toward “refined/high-impact carbohydrate patterns” rather than carbohydrate per se-**although total carbohydrate intake was also associated with risk in that analysis**. ([Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w))

Prospective cohort work also links higher sugar intake patterns with dementia risk, consistent with the idea that added/refined carbohydrate exposures may be the more relevant signal. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03525-6))

These studies are observational (so residual confounding and reverse causality remain possible), but the directionality is coherent across different “carb quality” metrics (GI, GL, sugar exposure, carb-to-fiber ratio) **and, in some analyses, total carbohydrate intake**. ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03525-6))

## Alzheimer’s, insulin resistance, and “type 3 diabetes”

“Type 3 diabetes” is a phrase used in some scientific discussions to emphasize insulin signaling abnormalities and insulin resistance mechanisms in Alzheimer’s disease, but it is not a formal clinical diagnosis category. ([Kellar & Craft, 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1474-4422%2820%2930231-3); [State of the Science, 2024](https://doi.org/10.14336/AD.2023.0814))

A 2024 review/position-style synthesis describes brain insulin resistance as a plausible therapeutic target for cognitive decline due to AD, reflecting the seriousness of the hypothesis while not settling causality. ([State of the Science, 2024](https://doi.org/10.14336/AD.2023.0814))

Separately, diabetes itself is consistently associated with higher dementia risk in meta-analytic evidence (e.g., pooled RR estimates around ~1.6 in a recent meta-analysis), which supports a broad “metabolic health ↔ brain health” link even if it does not prove a carbohydrate-specific causal pathway. ([Cao et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13098-024-01346-4))

## Quick reference takeaways

- Low-carb diets **do not show a consistent overall cognitive impairment signal** across randomized trials and systematic reviews, though subgroup findings (e.g., older adults in some analyses) warrant caution. ([Arsalandeh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1080/09637486.2024.2430006); [Chinna-Meyyappan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/1028415X.2022.2143609); [Makris et al., 2013](https://www.nature.com/articles/nutd201329))
    
- Ketone-raising interventions (especially MCT/MCFA supplementation) reliably increase circulating ketones in meta-analyses, and may improve cognition in some cognitively impaired populations, but effects are heterogeneous and not “universally superior.” ([Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001); [Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Sun et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3233/JAD-230406))
    
- Acute cognition effects from lower-GL strategies are **small and context-dependent** in meta-analysis, not a simple “refined carbs = brain fog” rule for everyone. ([Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824))
    
- Long-term dementia risk signals are more consistently linked to carbohydrate _quality/impact_ (high GL, low fiber relative to carbs, higher sugar patterns) **and, in some analyses, total carbohydrate intake**, than to carbohydrates as a blanket category. ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-024-03525-6))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the question is “Are carbs bad for the brain?”, the most evidence-aligned reframing is: **carbohydrate quality and glucose stability appear more relevant than carbohydrate presence**, especially for long-term risk signals-**with the caveat that some cohort analyses also report associations with total carbohydrate intake.** ([Novau-Ferré et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ije/dyaf182); [Lee et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12883-025-04583-w); [Gaylor et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104824))
    
- If someone experiences “brain fog” after eating, the literature most directly supports concern about **glycemic variability and dysglycemia** in metabolically at-risk groups (e.g., type 2 diabetes), rather than proving a universal effect of carbohydrates in healthy people. ([Chi et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0289782); [Mattishent et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fendo.2021.571568))
    
- If the goal is cognitive support in MCI/AD, ketogenic/MCT-style strategies have enough human trial and meta-analytic literature to be considered “active research with potential,” but not a settled replacement for other evidence-based care. ([Avgerinos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2019.101001); [Castro et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac104); [Sun et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3233/JAD-230406); [Phillips et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13195-021-00783-x))
