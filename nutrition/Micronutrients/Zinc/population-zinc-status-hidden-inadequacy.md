# Population Zinc Status: Hidden Inadequacy?

Available evidence does not support either extreme: low serum zinc is not the norm in the general US population or selected high-income cohorts, but adequate reported intake and a normal serum zinc value do not exclude marginal inadequacy in every individual. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105); [Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072); [Vallboehmer et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2025.06.010))

General-population surveys usually find below-cutoff serum zinc in a minority. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105); [Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296)) Stronger risk signals appear in some vegetarian and vegan populations, institutionalized older adults, very heavy drinkers, and users of certain antihypertensive medications. ([Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179); [Barnett et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.115.115188); [Vatsalya et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2018.05.003); [Braun and Rosenfeldt, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijcp.12040); [Suliburska et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10091284)) The central limitation is measurement: dietary intake, serum zinc, and functional endpoints assess different parts of zinc nutrition, and no single test reliably diagnoses marginal zinc deficiency in an individual. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072); [Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

## Intake adequacy and zinc status are different questions

In one analysis of 26,282 US adults participating in NHANES 2005-2016, 15 percent had food-only zinc intakes below the Estimated Average Requirement, falling to 11 percent when supplements were included. The benchmark was the EAR, not the RDA, and the analysis estimated the proportion of the population whose usual reported zinc intake was below the EAR rather than biochemical or clinical deficiency. ([Reider et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu12061735)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7352522/))

This analysis has a major financial conflict: every author was affiliated with Pharmavite, a dietary-supplement company. The paper reported no external funding, but company employees performed the research, and the company publicly promoted the findings as evidence of nutrient gaps. Its estimates should therefore be interpreted cautiously and should not be treated as independent evidence that supplements are needed. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1735)) ([Pharmavite](https://www.pharmavite.com/news/2020/06/review-study-published-in-nutrients-shows-american-adults-lack-critical-nutrients-to-maintain-a-healthy-immune-system/))

Dietary surveys also cannot fully determine how much zinc was absorbed. Food-composition data, short dietary records, supplement reporting, and variation in phytate content and preparation methods introduce uncertainty. An intake above a reference value does not guarantee high absorption, while an intake below it does not by itself demonstrate depleted tissue zinc or impaired function. ([Gibson and Ferguson, 1998](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/68.2.430S); [Gibson et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuy028); [Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19176739/))

## What general-population serum data show

Recent nationally representative biochemical data come from US NHANES 2011-2014. Among 4,347 participants aged six years or older, 3.8 percent of children under ten, 8.6 percent of males aged ten or older, and 8.2 percent of females aged ten or older were below the study's serum-zinc cutoffs. These percentages used age-, sex-, and collection-time-specific cutoffs, which differ from the single 70 μg/dL threshold used in several subgroup studies discussed below. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/))

Serum zinc varied substantially with factors unrelated to usual zinc intake. Afternoon and evening samples were lower than morning samples, while serum albumin and hemoglobin were positively associated with serum zinc. Hypoalbuminemia, anemia in women, and pregnancy markedly increased the odds of falling below the cutoff. Reported dietary and supplemental zinc intake was not significantly associated with serum zinc in this cross-sectional analysis. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/))

These results support the conclusion that below-cutoff serum zinc is not the norm in the general US population. They do not establish that the remaining population has optimal zinc status, nor can they be assumed to represent every high-income country or every higher-risk subgroup. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105); [Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072))

## Phytate lowers absorption, but not by a fixed 50 percent

Phytate can inhibit zinc absorption, but its effect depends on the amounts of both zinc and phytate, the composition of the complete diet, and the time period being studied. ([Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175); [Kristensen et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1079/BJN20051486); [Gibson et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuy028))

In a randomized dietary experiment involving ten healthy women, phytate intake was approximately 440 versus 1,800 mg per day. Fractional zinc absorption ranged from 32.8 to 39.4 percent on the low-phytate diets and from 26.2 to 26.9 percent on the high-phytate diets. High phytate reduced fractional absorption by about ten percentage points and absolute absorption by approximately 25 percent, or about 1 mg per day. ([Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19176739/))

In a separate randomized crossover study of 12 women, three five-day diets had similarly high phytate content but different amounts and sources of protein and zinc. Fractional absorption was approximately 23 to 27 percent and did not differ significantly among the diets. Total absorbed zinc was lower during the vegetarian diet principally because that diet contained less zinc, not because it produced a large additional reduction in fractional absorption. ([Kristensen et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1079/BJN20051486)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16611387/))

These controlled data confirm phytate inhibition but do not support applying a universal “50 percent or more” absorption penalty to all vegetarian or vegan diets. Large reductions may occur with particular meals or unusually high phytate-to-zinc exposure, but the magnitude cannot be inferred from the label “plant-based” alone. Both experiments were small and short, so they also do not define the full range of long-term absorption effects across habitual diets. ([Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175); [Kristensen et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1079/BJN20051486); [Gibson et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuy028))

## Vegetarian and vegan dietary patterns

A systematic review and meta-analysis included 34 studies, with 26 studies and 48 comparisons entering the quantitative analysis. Habitual vegetarian diets were associated with zinc intake that was, on average, 0.88 mg per day lower and serum zinc approximately 0.93 μmol/L lower than in nonvegetarians. Differences were larger in women, vegans, and participants from developing countries, although inconsistent definitions of vegetarian diets limited the analysis. ([Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179)) ([Macquarie University](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/publications/effect-of-vegetarian-diets-on-zinc-status-a-systematic-review-and/))

Newer cross-sectional studies generally support a subgroup effect. In a 2025 Czech study of 95 vegan, vegetarian, and omnivorous families, adult serum zinc followed a vegan-to-vegetarian-to-omnivore gradient. Twelve adults were below the study's reference range: nine vegans, one vegetarian, and two omnivores. Adult vegans nevertheless reported higher food-only zinc intake than the other groups, illustrating that reported milligrams and biochemical status can diverge. Family clustering accounted for a substantial proportion of serum-zinc variation, and the cross-sectional design could not establish that phytate caused the differences. ([Heniková et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43856-025-01257-z)) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43856-025-01257-z))

In the corrected results of the German VeChi Youth study, vegetarian and vegan participants aged six to eighteen had lower serum zinc than omnivores after adjustment for age, sex, puberty, and other covariates. Reported zinc intake was numerically lower in vegans, but the adjusted between-group intake differences were not statistically significant after correction for multiple testing. Dietary fiber was used only as an indirect proxy for phytate, so the study could not quantify how much of the serum difference resulted from phytate rather than total zinc intake or other dietary factors. ([Simon et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00394-025-03761-3); [Correction, 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00394-025-03803-w)) ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-025-03761-3))

The consistent conclusion is that vegetarian and especially vegan populations have lower average zinc intake or serum zinc in many studies. This identifies a higher-risk distribution, not an inevitable deficiency in every person following those diets. ([Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179); [Heniková et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43856-025-01257-z); [Simon et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00394-025-03761-3))

## Serum zinc: imperfect, but not useless

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated biomarkers that respond to changes in zinc intake lasting at least two weeks. Plasma or serum zinc changed in the expected direction in controlled studies, with a pooled difference of 2.17 μmol/L, but heterogeneity was extremely high at approximately 98 percent. Urinary zinc also responded to intake, while evidence for most proposed functional and cellular biomarkers was insufficient or inconsistent. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

Serum zinc is influenced by recent food intake, time of day, inflammation, hormones, medications, albumin, hemoglobin, and sample handling. It is therefore not a direct measurement of total-body zinc and is less reliable for diagnosing mild deficiency in one person than for comparing well-standardized groups or monitoring a controlled intervention. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072); [Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

However, “imperfect” does not mean “uninformative.” The updated review did not identify a validated functional marker that clearly outperformed plasma or serum zinc. Plasma or serum zinc is the best-supported currently available biomarker for detecting group-level or intervention-related changes in zinc exposure, but its accuracy for diagnosing marginal deficiency in an individual remains uncertain. A normal value should therefore not be treated as definitive proof of optimal status. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

## Functional status and the possibility of hidden inadequacy

A 2025 study of 128 young adults reported mean serum zinc values of 83.0 μg/dL in omnivores, 78.2 μg/dL in vegetarians, and 64.9 μg/dL in vegans. Approximately two-thirds of the small vegan group were below the study's 70 μg/dL serum cutoff. Vegetarians and vegans also had lower ex-vivo interferon-α responses and lower expression of interferon regulatory factor 3. ([Vallboehmer et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2025.06.010)) ([RWTH Publications](https://publications.rwth-aachen.de/record/1014061/files/1014061.pdf))

Vegetarian and vegan participants with serum zinc below 70 μg/dL then received 10 mg of zinc as zinc aspartate daily for 14 days. Mean serum zinc increased from 58.9 to 74.0 μg/dL, accompanied by improvement in the experimental immune readouts. Some participants with serum zinc above the cutoff but a low zinc-food-frequency score also showed an improved interferon response without a clear serum change. ([Vallboehmer et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2025.06.010)) ([RWTH Publications](https://publications.rwth-aachen.de/record/1014061/files/1014061.pdf))

This result is intriguing but preliminary. Supplementation was not randomized or placebo-controlled, there was no concurrent untreated comparison group, participants were selected partly on low baseline measurements, and the “at-risk” classification depended partly on a food-frequency questionnaire. Regression to the mean cannot be excluded. The study measured laboratory immune responses rather than infections or other clinical outcomes, and omnivores with low serum zinc were not included in the supplementation phase. The findings generate the hypothesis that serum zinc and dietary screening may capture different aspects of zinc-related biology, but they do not validate the questionnaire, establish functional deficiency in participants with normal serum zinc, or estimate the prevalence of hidden deficiency in the wider population. The study reported no specific research grant and no competing interests. ([RWTH Publications](https://publications.rwth-aachen.de/record/1014061/files/1014061.pdf))

## Older adults: community living and institutionalization differ

Age alone does not consistently identify zinc deficiency. In the multicenter ZENITH study, 387 healthy, free-living adults aged 55 to 87 from France, Italy, Northern Ireland, and the United Kingdom were assessed. More than 96 percent had zinc intakes judged adequate by the study, and the prevalence of study-defined biological zinc deficiency was reported as below 5 percent. Older participants nevertheless had lower erythrocyte and urinary zinc than the middle-aged group. ([Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16254579/))

Results were markedly different in a selected nursing-home population. Of 53 residents aged 65 or older who were screened for an RCT, 58 percent had serum zinc below 70 μg/dL. Thirty-one residents were randomized to 30 mg or 5 mg zinc per day for three months. The higher dose produced a larger increase in serum zinc and increased the number of circulating T cells, although not every participant reached the reference range and some functional differences weakened when expressed per cell. The 5 mg group was a lower-dose control rather than a zinc-free placebo. ([Barnett et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.115.115188)) ([Immunonutrition Society](https://www.immunonutrition-isin.org/docs/covidEvidence/Zinc%201.pdf))

The contrast suggests that setting and health context may be more informative than chronological age alone. The nursing-home estimate should not be generalized to healthy, community-living older adults. ([Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296); [Barnett et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.115.115188))

## Very heavy alcohol use

In a cohort of 108 very heavy-drinking, alcohol-dependent participants, 41 (approximately 38 percent) had serum zinc at or below 70 μg/dL. The low-zinc group reported more heavy-drinking days and had higher inflammatory and liver-injury markers together with lower albumin. ([Vatsalya et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2018.05.003)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29960116/))

This is evidence for a high-risk clinical subgroup, not for alcohol consumption in general. Because the study was observational and albumin, inflammation, and liver physiology themselves affect circulating zinc, it cannot determine how much of the low serum value represented low intake, impaired absorption, altered distribution, increased loss, or active disease. The authors reported no financial disclosures. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6129416/))

## Diuretics, ACE inhibitors, and other antihypertensive medications

A systematic review found only eight eligible clinical studies of antihypertensive therapy and zinc, five of which had a control group. Increased urinary zinc loss was reported with certain doses or combinations of captopril, enalapril, losartan, hydrochlorothiazide, and furosemide. Decreased serum or erythrocyte zinc was reported with some, but not all, ACE inhibitors, diuretics, angiotensin-receptor blockers, beta blockers, and calcium-channel blockers. Most studies were small and did not measure dietary zinc intake. ([Braun and Rosenfeldt, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijcp.12040)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23279674/))

A later three-month trial followed 98 previously untreated adults with hypertension receiving antihypertensive monotherapy. Diuretic treatment was followed by lower serum and erythrocyte zinc and higher urinary zinc; ACE-inhibitor treatment was followed by lower serum zinc; and calcium-channel blockers reduced erythrocyte zinc. Treatment was assigned according to clinical indications rather than an unrestricted randomized comparison, and each medication group was small. The study was publicly funded by the Polish National Science Centre, and the authors declared no conflicts of interest. ([Suliburska et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10091284)) ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/10/9/1284))

A 2024 Japanese claims-database analysis found higher proportions of low serum zinc among patients prescribed spironolactone or furosemide. However, its 13,100 participants were patients from acute-care hospitals who had already been selected for serum-zinc testing. The study lacked dietary and supplement data, was cross-sectional, and could not adequately separate medication effects from age, illness, inpatient status, or other confounding. It therefore cannot estimate the prevalence of deficiency among all users of these drugs. ([Yokokawa et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-53202-0)) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-53202-0))

The Japanese study also had extensive industry involvement. It was funded by Nobelpharma; three authors were Nobelpharma employees; two authors received consultation fees; and the company funded data analysis, writing, and editorial services. Its favorable recommendation for broader zinc monitoring should be weighed with these conflicts in mind and should not be the sole basis for a screening policy. ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-53202-0))

Overall, some diuretics and ACE inhibitors can plausibly alter zinc measurements or urinary losses, but the evidence does not establish a uniform drug-class effect. None of these studies tested whether routinely screening every medication user improves clinical outcomes. ([Braun and Rosenfeldt, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijcp.12040); [Suliburska et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10091284); [Yokokawa et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-53202-0))

## Assessment of the competing claims

1. **“Zinc deficiency is uncommon in developed countries based on dietary surveys and serum markers.”** Broadly supported for the general US population and selected healthy cohorts, but too broad as a statement about every developed or high-income country. “Uncommon” is not “absent.” Approximately 8 percent of US adolescents and adults were below serum cutoffs, while the available European study found low study-defined biological deficiency in selected healthy, free-living older adults. Intake and serum surveys also measure different things. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105); [Andriollo-Sanchez et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/sj.ejcn.1602296)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/))

2. **“Phytate can reduce absorption by 50 percent or more, placing vegetarians and vegans at higher risk despite adequate intake.”** Supported in direction but overstated as a universal magnitude. Controlled whole-day data found approximately 25 percent lower absolute absorption with high phytate, while habitual vegetarian and vegan populations have modestly lower average serum zinc. Some plant-based groups show lower serum values despite apparently adequate reported intake, but those observations do not prove that phytate alone caused the difference. ([Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175); [Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179); [Heniková et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s43856-025-01257-z)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19176739/))

3. **“Serum zinc is a poor marker, so subclinical deficiency may be substantially undercounted.”** Partly supported. Serum zinc is context-sensitive and cannot fully characterize individual or total-body status, but it responds to intake and remains the best-supported available biomarker for group-level or intervention-related changes. Early functional research suggests that some relevant changes may occur without crossing a serum cutoff, but no validated test currently reveals how common such hidden inadequacy is. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072); [Vallboehmer et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2025.06.010)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

4. **“Older adults, alcohol users, and people taking diuretics or ACE inhibitors have lower zinc status.”** Supported for selected populations, not as a blanket statement. Nursing-home residents and very heavy drinkers show substantially higher proportions of low serum zinc, while healthy community-living older adults may not. Some antihypertensive regimens alter serum, erythrocyte, or urinary zinc, but evidence is small and inconsistent and does not establish universal screening. ([Barnett et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.115.115188); [Vatsalya et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2018.05.003); [Braun and Rosenfeldt, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijcp.12040)) ([Immunonutrition Society](https://www.immunonutrition-isin.org/docs/covidEvidence/Zinc%201.pdf))

## Practical interpretation

- Meeting a zinc intake reference value is reassuring but does not fully account for bioavailability. Conversely, a plant-based diet or intake below a reference value does not establish deficiency without biochemical, dietary, and clinical context. ([Gibson and Ferguson, 1998](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/68.2.430S); [Gibson et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuy028); [Hunt and Beiseigel, 2009](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2008.27175); [Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19176739/))

- Vegetarians and vegans, particularly people consuming little zinc relative to phytate, have a higher average risk of low status, but the available evidence does not show that every person on a plant-based diet requires supplementation. ([Foster et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1002/jsfa.6179); [Gibson et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuy028)) ([Macquarie University](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/publications/effect-of-vegetarian-diets-on-zinc-status-a-systematic-review-and/))

- A serum-zinc result should be interpreted alongside time of collection, recent meals, inflammation, albumin, hemoglobin, pregnancy, medications, and illness. A normal result does not prove optimal status, while an isolated low result does not necessarily prove low dietary intake. ([Hennigar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxy105); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/))

- Selected nursing-home residents, very heavy drinkers, and people receiving particular diuretic or ACE-inhibitor regimens may reasonably warrant closer assessment. Current studies do not demonstrate that universal population screening or routine supplementation of all members of these groups improves health outcomes. ([Barnett et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.115.115188); [Vatsalya et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2018.05.003); [Braun and Rosenfeldt, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijcp.12040); [Suliburska et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10091284)) ([Immunonutrition Society](https://www.immunonutrition-isin.org/docs/covidEvidence/Zinc%201.pdf))

- Routine high-dose supplementation should not be inferred from evidence of uncertain or marginal status. The US adult Tolerable Upper Intake Level is 40 mg per day from all sources, and doses of 50 mg per day or more taken for weeks can interfere with copper absorption. Suspected deficiency, malabsorption, or medication-related loss warrants individualized clinical assessment. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/))
