# Zinc and Testosterone: Real Effect or Bro-Science?

Zinc is not irrelevant to testosterone, but the common supplement claim is usually overstated. The best-supported version is: **zinc deficiency can suppress testosterone, and correcting deficiency can help normalize it.** The weaker version is: **zinc or ZMA meaningfully raises testosterone in already well-fed, zinc-sufficient men.** The clinical evidence supports the first claim much better than the second. ([Te et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtemb.2022.127124 "Correlation between serum zinc and testosterone: A systematic review")) ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults")) ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

## Evidence hierarchy caveat

The evidence base needs some care because the most useful analysis would separate zinc-deficient, marginally deficient, and zinc-sufficient participants. The central synthesis used here is a systematic review of zinc and testosterone, read alongside small human intervention studies, controlled zinc/ZMA trials, and an athlete zinc-status meta-analysis. The systematic review included both clinical and animal evidence, so it should not be read as a definitive human supplementation meta-analysis. ([Te et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtemb.2022.127124 "Correlation between serum zinc and testosterone: A systematic review")) ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults")) ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism")) ([Chu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8 "Lower serum zinc concentration despite higher dietary zinc intake in athletes: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

That matters because the key question is not “does zinc ever affect testosterone?” It is “does supplemental zinc raise testosterone in men who are not zinc deficient?” The evidence is much less supportive for that second question. ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

## The strongest positive signal is deficiency-dependent

A systematic review concluded that severe zinc deficiency can reduce testosterone and that zinc supplementation can improve testosterone. That conclusion is most persuasive when zinc status is low or deficient, because supplementation increased testosterone in marginally zinc-deficient older men, while high-dose zinc supplementation did not significantly increase testosterone in healthy, regularly exercising men with sufficient baseline zinc intake. ([Te et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtemb.2022.127124 "Correlation between serum zinc and testosterone: A systematic review")) ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults")) ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement"))

The [Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults") work should be read carefully. Prasad et al. studied the relationship between zinc status and serum testosterone in healthy adults, including marginally zinc-deficient older men, and reported that supplementation in marginal deficiency increased serum testosterone. That supports zinc repletion, not the idea that zinc acts like a testosterone booster in zinc-sufficient men. ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults"))

## What happens in zinc-sufficient men

In a controlled study of 14 healthy, regularly exercising men aged 22 to 33, baseline zinc intake was already in a sufficient range. High-dose zinc supplementation increased serum zinc and urinary zinc excretion, but it did **not** significantly change total testosterone, free testosterone, or urinary testosterone metabolites. The authors concluded that the supplement had no significant testosterone effect when the diet was zinc sufficient. This small study cannot exclude very small effects, but it does argue against a meaningful testosterone-boosting effect in zinc-sufficient men. ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement"))

A small randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in obese men found that zinc sulfate raised serum zinc, but did not significantly change androgens over one month. This does not answer the athletic ZMA question directly, but it reinforces the point that increasing zinc status does not automatically translate into higher testosterone. ([Gómez-García et al.](https://doi.org/10.4067/S0034-98872006000300002 "Effect of oral zinc administration on insulin sensitivity, leptin and androgens in obese males"))

## ZMA and resistance training

One positive ZMA trial was [Brilla and Conte’s](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength") 2000 study in collegiate football players. Participants took a formulation providing 30 mg zinc monomethionine/aspartate, 450 mg magnesium aspartate, and 10.5 mg vitamin B6 for about 7 to 8 weeks. The study reported increases in free testosterone, IGF-1, and strength outcomes compared with placebo. ([Brilla and Conte](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength"))

That trial has a major conflict-of-interest problem. The paper disclosed that Victor Conte had an equity interest in SNAC System, Inc., that a patent was pending for ZMA, and that SNAC System provided financial support for the research. The trial also does not clearly establish correction of deficiency because reported mean dietary intakes already exceeded RDAs for zinc, magnesium, and vitamin B6. The findings should be interpreted with that commercial context in mind and should not be treated as stand-alone proof. ([Brilla and Conte](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength"))

A later double-blind trial in 42 resistance-trained men tested ZMA during 8 weeks of standardized resistance training. It found no meaningful improvement in anabolic or catabolic hormone status, body composition, strength, muscular endurance, or anaerobic capacity. The article concluded that ZMA did not appear to enhance training adaptations in resistance-trained populations. ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

The [Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism") trial also needs a funding note. The full-text disclosure reports funding from Cytodyne Technologies and states that the investigators independently collected, analyzed, and interpreted the data. That makes it industry-funded, but the direction of bias is not straightforward because the results were negative. ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

A more recent randomized, double-blind, counterbalanced study tested acute ZMA use in trained males after sleep restriction. Participants’ habitual intakes already met micronutrient RDAs, and ZMA did not improve sleep, lifting performance, fatigue, or cognitive outcomes. The paper reported no external funding and no conflicts of interest. This does not directly test chronic testosterone changes, but it is relevant to the broader claim that ZMA reliably improves recovery, sleep, or performance. ([Gallagher et al.](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16020251 "Effects of supplementing zinc magnesium aspartate on sleep quality and submaximal weightlifting performance, following two consecutive nights of partial sleep deprivation"))

## Athletes and zinc status

Athletes may have somewhat lower serum zinc than non-athletes. A systematic review and meta-analysis of cross-sectional studies found that athletes had lower serum zinc despite higher dietary zinc intake, suggesting that some athletes may have higher zinc requirements or greater zinc losses. ([Chu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8 "Lower serum zinc concentration despite higher dietary zinc intake in athletes: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

That finding supports diet assessment and appropriate zinc-status assessment in at-risk athletes. It does **not** prove that routine zinc or ZMA supplementation raises testosterone in athletes who already have adequate zinc status. Serum or plasma zinc is commonly used to assess zinc status, but it is an imperfect marker and can be affected by factors such as age, sex, time of day, and inflammation. ([Chu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8 "Lower serum zinc concentration despite higher dietary zinc intake in athletes: A systematic review and meta-analysis")) ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/ "Zinc Fact Sheet for Health Professionals"))

The controlled zinc/ZMA studies in resistance-trained or regularly exercising men discussed here are generally negative when baseline status appears adequate. ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

## The marketing-evidence gap

The evidence gap is the move from a deficiency-correction claim to an optimization claim. “Zinc deficiency can lower testosterone” is not the same as “extra zinc raises testosterone in well-fed men.” The clinical data support the former much more clearly than the latter. ([Te et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtemb.2022.127124 "Correlation between serum zinc and testosterone: A systematic review")) ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults")) ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

A systematic review of commercial “testosterone boosters” concluded that most proposed testosterone-boosting ingredients fail to increase total testosterone in human studies. ZMA fits that broader pattern: one small, commercially conflicted positive trial is outweighed by later negative evidence in resistance-trained or zinc-sufficient men. ([Morgado et al.](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41443-023-00763-9 "Do testosterone boosters really increase serum total testosterone? A systematic review")) ([Brilla and Conte](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength")) ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

The “identity-signaling” claim is not a measured clinical endpoint in the zinc or ZMA trials. As an interpretation of supplement messaging, it should be kept separate from the clinical evidence. Clinically, for adequately fed men, the evidence does not justify treating ZMA as a reliable testosterone enhancer in zinc-sufficient populations. ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

## Quick reference

- **Zinc-deficient or marginally deficient men:** zinc repletion may improve testosterone, especially when zinc deficiency is part of the cause. ([Te et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtemb.2022.127124 "Correlation between serum zinc and testosterone: A systematic review")) ([Prasad et al.](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0899-9007%2896%2980058-X "Zinc status and serum testosterone levels of healthy adults"))

- **Zinc-sufficient men:** high-dose zinc supplementation increased zinc markers but did not significantly increase total or free testosterone in a small controlled study. ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement"))

- **ZMA positive trial:** Brilla and Conte reported higher free testosterone and strength, but the study was small, commercially conflicted, and not clearly a deficiency-repletion trial. ([Brilla and Conte](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength"))

- **ZMA negative resistance-training trial:** Wilborn et al. found no improvement in hormones, body composition, strength, endurance, or anaerobic capacity over 8 weeks. ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

- **Athlete zinc status:** athletes may have lower serum zinc than controls despite higher intake, but this supports status assessment more than blanket testosterone-boosting claims. ([Chu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8 "Lower serum zinc concentration despite higher dietary zinc intake in athletes: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))


## Practical interpretation

Zinc is worth correcting when intake or status is low. That is most relevant for people with poor dietary intake, low-zinc or restrictive diets, gastrointestinal disease, or athlete contexts where zinc status is a concern. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/ "Zinc Fact Sheet for Health Professionals")) ([Chu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8 "Lower serum zinc concentration despite higher dietary zinc intake in athletes: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

For men who already consume enough zinc, ZMA should not be expected to meaningfully raise testosterone. The better-supported intervention is not “take more zinc,” but “identify and correct deficiency if present.” ([Koehler et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/1602899 "Serum testosterone and urinary excretion of steroid hormone metabolites after administration of a high-dose zinc supplement")) ([Wilborn et al.](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12 "Effects of Zinc Magnesium Aspartate supplementation on training adaptations and markers of anabolism and catabolism"))

High-dose zinc is not risk-free. Adult men have an RDA of 11 mg/day zinc and an adult tolerable upper intake level of 40 mg/day, and chronic supplemental zinc can interfere with copper status. The commonly studied ZMA formulation provides 30 mg zinc before diet or other supplements are counted, so stacking zinc-containing products is not automatically harmless. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/ "Zinc Fact Sheet for Health Professionals")) ([Brilla and Conte](https://www.asep.org/asep/asep/BrillaV2.PDF "Effects of a novel zinc-magnesium formulation on hormones and strength"))

Symptoms of clinically low testosterone should be evaluated with proper blood testing and medical assessment rather than inferred from supplement marketing. Clinical guidelines recommend diagnosing hypogonadism only when compatible symptoms or signs are present and testosterone is consistently low on repeat morning testing. Zinc can be part of nutrition correction, but it is not a substitute for diagnosing the underlying cause of hypogonadism. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/testosterone-therapy "Testosterone Therapy for Hypogonadism Guideline Resources"))
