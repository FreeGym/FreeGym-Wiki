# Zinc in High-Prevalence Regions: South Asia and India

Zinc risk in South Asia is best understood as a **bioavailability and food-system problem**, not just a total-intake problem. In India, adequate calories from cereal-heavy staples can still coexist with inadequate absorbable zinc because rice, wheat, millets, and pulses often come with high phytate exposure and relatively low animal-source zinc intake. The strongest evidence supports elevated risk in specific Indian regions and age groups, especially adolescents when serum zinc is interpreted using the cutoffs applied in the Comprehensive National Nutrition Survey analysis, rather than a simple claim that all Indian subgroups are uniformly zinc deficient. ([Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176); [Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066))

## Why South Asia looks different from Western reference data

A global food-supply modeling study estimated that about 17.3 percent of the world population was at risk of inadequate zinc intake, but the modeled prevalence was much higher in South Asia, around 30 percent. The same analysis found that estimated national zinc inadequacy was lower where animal-source zinc intake was higher and higher where phytate:zinc molar ratios were higher. Diets with phytate:zinc molar ratios above 18 were classified as higher-risk patterns. ([Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568))

This matters because dietary-reference framing from lower-phytate contexts can miss the bioavailability problem in cereal-pulse diets. In India and much of South Asia, zinc guidance has to account for cereal and pulse staples, lower animal-source food intake in many groups, and regional differences in staple patterns. Indian household-consumption modeling found meaningful geographic variation: rice-eating states in the south and northeast had higher estimated zinc inadequacy than wheat-eating states in the north, east, central, and west. ([Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://doi.org/10.17226/10026); [Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176))

## Dietary inadequacy in India is substantial, but not uniform

A modeling study using seven rounds of Indian household consumption data from 1983 to 2011-2012 estimated that the prevalence of inadequate absorbable zinc intake increased from 17.1 percent in 1983 to 24.6 percent in 2011-2012, corresponding to about 82 million additional people at risk. The same paper emphasized that inadequate intake is not the same as confirmed clinical deficiency; it is a population-risk estimate based on modeled absorbable zinc intake. ([Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176))

The 2011-2012 modeled Indian diet contained about 8.72 mg/day of total zinc and 1670 mg/day of phytate, with estimated absorbable zinc of about 2.21 mg/day. Using the molecular weights given by Hambidge et al. for zinc and phytate, that corresponds to an approximate phytate:zinc molar ratio of 19:1, which falls into the higher-risk range used in global zinc-inadequacy modeling. This is a calculation from the reported Indian dietary values, not a directly reported number in the India paper. ([Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176); [Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823); [Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568))

The same Indian modeling study found that dietary change can cut both ways. Total dietary zinc and phytate intakes both declined over time, but phytate declined faster than zinc, improving the phytate:zinc ratio. At the same time, demographic changes, urbanization, and shifts toward rice-heavy dietary patterns contributed to higher estimated inadequacy in some regions. ([Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176))

## What Indian biomarker data show

India's best nationally representative child and adolescent biomarker evidence comes from the Comprehensive National Nutrition Survey 2016-2018. Using the cutoffs and inflammation adjustment applied by Pullakhandam et al., low serum zinc concentration was estimated at 17.4 percent in preschool children, 15.8 percent in school-age children, and 31.1 percent in adolescents after adjustment for inflammation using C-reactive protein. ([Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066))

That pattern is important. Under those cutoffs, the preschool and school-age estimates were below the 20 percent public-health cutoff used by the authors, while the adolescent estimate was high enough to warrant public-health concern and further investigation. Low serum zinc was more common in some vulnerable groups, including rural preschool children, poorer households, and children with severe stunting or underweight. ([Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066))

These serum-zinc estimates are cutoff-dependent. A later India-specific reference-cutoff analysis using CNNS data proposed serum-zinc cutoffs 10-18 µg/dL lower than the IZiNCG cutoffs and found much lower prevalence estimates, while correspondence by McDonald et al. cautioned that lower cutoffs could miss some children with zinc deficiency and raised concerns about the reference sample and laboratory variability. The practical interpretation is that CNNS serum-zinc figures should be read as estimates under specified cutoffs, not as a single settled prevalence number. ([Pullakhandam et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41430-022-01088-4); [McDonald et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41430-022-01152-z); [Pullakhandam et al., 2022 response](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41430-022-01161-y))

Serum or plasma zinc is useful at the population level, but it is an imperfect individual diagnostic marker. The BOND zinc review recommends dietary zinc intake, plasma zinc concentration, and height-for-age as the three most useful population-level indicators, while noting that plasma zinc varies with recent meals, time of day, inflammation, infection, and other physiological factors. ([King et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.220079))

The practical implication is that India should not be treated as "low-risk" simply because Western RDA tables look modest, but it also should not be described as uniformly zinc-deficient across all ages and regions. The evidence points to meaningful heterogeneity: adolescents, poorer households, high-phytate diets, rice-heavy regions, and low animal-source-food patterns deserve more attention. ([Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176))

## South Asia is high-risk, but not one uniform block

Regional South Asian data support concern beyond India, but the burden differs by country, age group, and survey method. Nepal's national micronutrient survey analysis reported zinc deficiency in about 22.9 percent of children aged 6-59 months and 24.7 percent of non-pregnant women aged 15-49 years, placing both groups above the 20 percent public-health threshold used in many zinc-status discussions. ([Mehata et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-94344-9))

Pakistan's serial micronutrient-survey evidence suggests improvement over time but persistent concern: a 2024 review reported that zinc deficiency in children and females declined from roughly 37 percent and 41 percent in 2001 to 18.6 percent and 22.1 percent in 2018. This makes Pakistan a useful cautionary example: prevalence can fall substantially, but women and some subgroups may remain above public-health concern thresholds. ([Lowe et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2024.100181))

## Phytate is central to the South Asian zinc question

Phytate binds zinc in the gut and lowers fractional zinc absorption. This is why the same total zinc intake can have different biological meaning in low-phytate and high-phytate diets. Hambidge et al. modeled that adding 1000 mg/day of phytate could roughly double the estimated zinc requirement, while adding 2000 mg/day could roughly triple it, depending on the dietary context. ([Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823))

This is directly relevant to India because the modeled average Indian diet in 2011-2012 contained about 1670 mg/day of phytate. In a high-phytate dietary pattern, quoting only total zinc intake or importing Western RDA values without adjusting for bioavailability can understate the amount of zinc needed to meet absorbed zinc requirements. ([Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176); [Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823))

A later analysis argued that the U.S. Dietary Reference Intake framework likely overestimated fractional zinc absorption for men, women, and adolescents because the underlying absorption values were based partly on lower-phytate diets. When IZiNCG and Miller-model absorption estimates were applied instead, the author concluded that the EAR and RDA for several groups might need to increase by nearly one-half to meet absorbed zinc needs. ([Armah, 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.225607))

## Why Western RDA framing can mislead

The U.S. adult zinc RDA is 11 mg/day for men and 8 mg/day for women, with an adult tolerable upper intake level of 40 mg/day. These values are useful reference points, but they are not automatically equivalent across diets with very different zinc bioavailability. ([Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://doi.org/10.17226/10026))

For high-phytate Indian diets, the more relevant question is not just "How many milligrams of zinc were consumed?" but "How much zinc was absorbed?" A cereal-pulse diet with a high phytate:zinc ratio can require more total zinc to deliver the same absorbed zinc as a lower-phytate diet containing more animal-source foods. ([Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823); [Armah, 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.225607))

This does not mean plant-based Indian diets are inherently deficient. It means zinc adequacy depends on the full dietary pattern: total zinc, phytate load, processing methods, fermentation, leavening, soaking, food diversity, and inclusion of zinc-dense or zinc-enhancing foods. The public-health point is that RDA-only messaging can be too crude for populations whose staple diets sit in a high-phytate range. ([Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823); [Armah, 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.225607))

## Soil zinc depletion is a food-system driver

Soil zinc depletion is not just an agricultural issue; it can affect the zinc content of staple crops and therefore the zinc density of diets. A 2024 India-focused soil zinc surveillance paper reported that India's national soil-health program analyzed more than 30 million samples between 2015 and 2019 and found that about 39 percent of Indian soils were zinc-deficient for crop production, with state-level deficiency ranging from 2 to 67 percent. ([Khokhar et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoil.2024.1421652))

This supports the claim that "adequate calories from staples" does not guarantee adequate zinc intake. If staple crops are grown in zinc-limited soils, the grain itself may have lower zinc concentration before dietary phytate is even considered. Soil zinc status, crop variety, fertilizer strategy, milling, and household food patterns all sit upstream of individual zinc status. ([Khokhar et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoil.2024.1421652))

A 2025 global meta-analysis of wheat agronomic biofortification found that soil zinc fertilization increased grain zinc by about 29 percent, foliar zinc by about 55 percent, and combined soil-plus-foliar application by about 62 percent. The same paper estimated that global and South Asian wheat grain zinc concentrations were generally below the 40 mg/kg target often discussed for human nutrition, and that foliar or combined approaches were more likely than soil-only approaches to reach that target. ([Hui et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-58397-y))

## Fortification and biofortification are more defensible where baseline risk is high

The case for zinc fortification and biofortification is strongest when baseline inadequacy is common, diets are high in phytate, and staple foods reach a large share of the population. South Asia meets many of these conditions, especially in regions with high cereal dependence, low animal-source-food intake, and soil zinc constraints. However, intervention evidence is mixed when the outcome is not just serum zinc but growth, infection, or other functional endpoints. ([Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176); [Lowe et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2024.100181))

A Cochrane review of zinc fortification found that zinc-fortified foods may improve serum or plasma zinc concentration when zinc is added alone, but the evidence base was small and inconsistent, and effects on underweight and stunting were uncertain. The authors concluded that more evidence was needed before making strong claims about health outcomes. ([Shah et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010697.pub2))

A later systematic review and meta-analysis including 59 zinc-fortification studies found that zinc fortification increased plasma or serum zinc and reduced the odds of zinc deficiency. It also found some improvements in outcomes such as diarrhea and fever, but the authors cautioned that when foods were fortified with multiple micronutrients, benefits could not be attributed solely to zinc. ([Tsang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmab065))

The large Delhi wheat biofortification trial tested high-zinc wheat flour versus lower-zinc wheat flour for six months in women of childbearing age and children. It did not find a significant difference in mean zinc concentration at endline, but it did report fewer days of pneumonia and vomiting among children and fewer fever days among women. The authors noted that the grain zinc difference achieved in the trial was smaller than expected and that longer exposure or higher grain zinc may be needed. ([Sazawal et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12937-018-0391-5))

A more recent Indian randomized trial in schoolchildren compared zinc-biofortified whole wheat, post-harvest fortified wheat, and control wheat in chapattis over 20 weeks. The fortified and biofortified wheat increased zinc intake, but the trial did not find a treatment effect on plasma zinc, growth, or DNA damage markers. ([Signorell et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tjnut.2023.08.013))

A 2025 Pakistan cluster-randomised trial of zinc-biofortified wheat flour in rural Pakistani children and adolescent girls found no major overall effects on height, weight, diarrhea, or lung function, although some secondary respiratory outcomes differed at specific follow-up points. This again supports a cautious interpretation: biofortified wheat can improve zinc exposure, but functional health benefits may depend on baseline deficiency, dose, duration, coexisting infections, and other nutrient limitations. ([Gupta et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17071137))

## What changes for Indian user guidance

For Indian users, zinc guidance should not simply copy Western survey assumptions. The relevant baseline risk is higher in many Indian and South Asian settings, and the dominant dietary pattern often has lower zinc bioavailability. That changes how strongly one should consider population strategies such as staple-food fortification, agronomic biofortification, and targeted screening in vulnerable groups. ([Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176); [Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066); [Lowe et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2024.100181))

At the same time, high baseline risk does not justify overconfident individual supplementation advice. Serum zinc is imperfect for individuals, high-dose zinc can cause adverse effects such as copper deficiency, and fortification trials show clearer effects on zinc biomarkers than on growth or infection outcomes. The best interpretation is that India and South Asia deserve region-specific zinc guidance, not blanket supplement claims. ([King et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.220079); [Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://doi.org/10.17226/10026); [Shah et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010697.pub2); [Tsang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmab065))

## Practical interpretation

- In India and South Asia, zinc risk should be assessed using **dietary pattern plus bioavailability**, not total zinc intake alone. High cereal and pulse intake, low animal-source zinc intake, and high phytate:zinc ratios can make RDA-only framing misleading. ([Wessells and Brown, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050568); [Hambidge et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.108.093823); [Armah, 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.225607))

- The strongest Indian biomarker concern appears in adolescents when CNNS data are interpreted with the cutoffs applied by Pullakhandam et al., while modeled dietary inadequacy points to additional concern in rice-heavy states, poorer households, and high-phytate diets. ([Pullakhandam et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqab066); [Smith et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1177/0379572118825176))

- Soil zinc depletion strengthens the case for food-system interventions, including agronomic biofortification and zinc-aware staple-crop strategies. ([Khokhar et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsoil.2024.1421652); [Hui et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-58397-y))

- Fortification and biofortification are more defensible where baseline inadequacy, high-phytate staple dependence, and staple-food reach are documented, but current trials show clearer effects on zinc intake or biomarkers than on growth and infection outcomes. ([Lowe et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2024.100181); [Shah et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010697.pub2); [Tsang et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmab065))

- For individuals, the evidence supports caution rather than automatic long-term high-dose supplementation: baseline risk, diet quality, pregnancy/lactation status, gastrointestinal disease, recurrent diarrhea, and medical context should guide decisions. ([King et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.3945/jn.115.220079); [Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://doi.org/10.17226/10026))
