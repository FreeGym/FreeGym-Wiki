# Zinc Status Testing: Can You Trust the Number?

Serum or plasma zinc is the standard practical marker for zinc status, but it is not a direct readout of total body zinc stores. It is better treated as a context-sensitive clue than as a standalone verdict. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047); [Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

## What serum or plasma zinc can and cannot tell you

Serum and plasma zinc are the most commonly used biochemical indicators of zinc status, and they remain the practical default in clinical and field settings. But circulating zinc represents a very small fraction of total body zinc, and the value is influenced by recent meals, fasting status, time of day, inflammation, hormones, medications, and collection/sample handling. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047); [Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073))

That means the test is useful, but narrow. It can detect more substantial zinc depletion and can move upward with zinc supplementation, especially at the population level. It is much less reliable for determining whether one individual has mild or subclinical deficiency. Hess et al. concluded that serum zinc is useful for estimating population risk and monitoring population responses to interventions, but that an individual serum zinc value does not reliably predict functional response to zinc. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

The "serum magnesium" analogy is directionally reasonable, but it is only an analogy: a normal circulating value does not necessarily prove that status is optimal. Zinc has its own biomarker evidence and confounders. Zinc is homeostatically regulated, and a mild deficit may be partly missed by the blood value until intake restriction is more severe or prolonged. In depletion studies, serum zinc falls clearly with severe restriction, but moderate restriction can produce little change or only slow, small changes. ([Workinger et al., 2018](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/10/9/1202); [Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

## Mild deficiency and normal results

A 2012 systematic review and meta-analysis found that serum or plasma zinc does respond to zinc intake, but the signal is modest: every doubling of zinc intake was associated with only about a 6 percent difference in serum or plasma zinc concentration. That is enough to matter in research, but small enough that real-world noise can blur the meaning of a single value. ([Lowe et al., 2012](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/relationship-between-zinc-intake-and-serumplasma-zinc-concentration-in-adults-a-systematic-review-and-doseresponse-metaanalysis-by-the-eurreca-network/3F65CE86C88359E42776107FBBBBAB7A))

A newer 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis reached a similar practical conclusion. Plasma/serum zinc and urinary zinc were the biomarkers with the clearest response to changes in intake in studies, but the authors emphasized that serum/plasma zinc must be interpreted carefully because of confounding and that many proposed alternatives still lack strong validation. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

So a low zinc result increases suspicion for deficiency, especially when it was drawn under standardized conditions, but it is not definitive proof if inflammation or sample-handling problems are present. A normal result lowers concern but is not automatically "zinc status is fine." The value is most informative when it agrees with the person's dietary pattern, symptoms, inflammatory state, and repeat testing under standardized conditions. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

## Inflammation and zinc redistribution

Serum zinc is a negative acute-phase reactant. During infection, inflammation, fever, or tissue injury, zinc can shift out of the circulation and into tissues such as the liver, lowering the serum or plasma value without necessarily meaning that total body zinc stores are depleted. ([McDonald et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32266402/); [Likoswe et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1563))

This can materially change classification. In BRINDA analyses of preschool-child surveys where regression correction for inflammation was applied, adjustment reduced estimated zinc deficiency prevalence by a median of about 11 percentage points, with a range of 4 to 18 percentage points. This point is best established in preschool-child surveys; the same analysis found weaker and less consistent inflammation relationships in women of reproductive age. ([McDonald et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32266402/))

The same issue appears in country-level analyses. In a Malawi national micronutrient survey, unadjusted zinc deficiency prevalence was estimated at 62 percent; after inflammation adjustment, it fell to 59 percent with one method and 52 percent with the BRINDA method. ([Likoswe et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1563))

A 2024 analysis in Brazilian children also found that inflammation and recent illness symptoms changed interpretation. After adjustment for inflammation, estimated zinc deficiency prevalence decreased from 17.8 percent to 13.8 percent, and recent diarrhea, fever, or respiratory symptoms were associated with higher apparent zinc deficiency prevalence. ([Berti et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38218540/))

The practical takeaway is simple: a low serum zinc value drawn during illness, fever, recent infection, inflammatory flare, or trauma can overcall deficiency. In that situation, the result is often better interpreted alongside C-reactive protein, alpha-1-acid glycoprotein, albumin, clinical context, and, when the clinical question remains, repeat testing under standardized conditions after recovery is more defensible than treating the result as a standalone verdict. ([McDonald et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32266402/); [Berti et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38218540/); [Hennigar et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/); [Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

## Pre-analytic variables in zinc testing

Zinc testing is unusually sensitive to collection and handling. A 2023 experimental study found that capillary plasma zinc was about 8 percent higher than venous plasma zinc, venous serum zinc was about 5 percent higher than venous plasma zinc, delayed whole-blood processing increased measured zinc by about 5 to 12 percent in plasma and serum, and warmer post-collection holding conditions increased measured zinc by about 0.5 to 7 percent. Those shifts changed the percentage of people classified as at risk of deficiency. ([Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073))

Hemolysis is another major problem. Red blood cells contain much more zinc than plasma or serum, so hemolysis can falsely raise the measured value. A 2017 study identified about 1 g hemoglobin/L as a practical hemolysis threshold that increases plasma or serum zinc by about 5 percent. ([Killilea et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28490675/))

Collection tubes can also contaminate samples. An older but still relevant tube-contamination study found that zinc leaching from rubber stoppers in some evacuated blood collection tubes could artificially increase normal serum zinc by about 250 percent. That is not a subtle effect; it is the kind of artifact that can make a result clinically meaningless. The main lesson is to use appropriate trace-element collection materials, not to assume that all modern tubes behave the same way. ([Keyzer et al., 1983](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02332953))

Time of day and fasting status matter too. In Brazilian children, morning nonfasting and afternoon samples had lower serum zinc than morning fasting samples, and the largest absolute percentile difference between morning fasting and morning nonfasting samples was 20.3 ug/dL. NHANES analyses also found that serum zinc varies by sex, age, and time of blood draw. ([Berti et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38218540/); [Hennigar et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29947812/))

This is why reference cutoffs are not universal in the simple way patients often assume. Hess et al. reported different lower cutoffs depending on whether samples were collected in the morning fasting, morning nonfasting, or afternoon state. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

## Alternative and functional markers

Hair zinc, nail zinc, urinary zinc, erythrocyte zinc, leukocyte zinc, zinc-dependent enzymes, and taste acuity have all been proposed as alternatives or complements. The problem is that none has emerged as a single, practical, well-validated marker that reliably captures subclinical zinc status across settings. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

The 2025 systematic review found that plasma/serum zinc and urinary zinc responded most clearly to changes in intake in studies. Hair and nail zinc showed some signal but did not meet the authors' criteria for stronger biomarker performance, and erythrocyte zinc did not appear to be an effective biomarker. Urinary zinc should therefore be understood as intake-responsive in studies, not as a routine clinical replacement for serum or plasma zinc. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

A large randomized trial in young Lao children illustrates the problem. Plasma zinc responded to zinc-containing interventions, hair zinc did not, and nail zinc showed only preliminary promise. Hair and nail zinc were not correlated with plasma zinc, and the authors emphasized that hair and nail values likely reflect different time frames and do not yet have established reference values for zinc status. ([Wessells et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12011-020-02163-2))

So the answer is not "ignore serum zinc and use hair testing." The better answer is: serum/plasma zinc remains the most practical marker, but it should be interpreted as one part of a structured assessment rather than as a definitive body-zinc gauge. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047); [Wessells et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12011-020-02163-2))

## Using a profile instead of one number

A lower-risk profile is a standardized venous sample collected in an appropriate trace-element tube, processed promptly, with no visible or measured hemolysis, no recent infection or inflammatory flare, and timing/fasting status matched to the lab's reference interval. In that setting, a normal serum or plasma zinc value is more reassuring, though still not perfect. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073); [Killilea et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28490675/); [Keyzer et al., 1983](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02332953))

A higher-risk profile is a low or borderline value collected during illness, after nonstandard sample handling, in a visibly hemolyzed sample, from a potentially contaminated tube, or without regard to fasting/time of day. In that setting, repeating the test under better conditions is often more informative than acting aggressively on the first result. ([Killilea et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28490675/); [Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073); [Keyzer et al., 1983](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02332953))

Dietary context also matters. Populations or individuals with low absorbable zinc intake, especially diets high in phytate and low in animal-source zinc, can have higher zinc deficiency risk even when a single serum value is not dramatically low. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303))

The strongest practical signal is concordance: a low value drawn under standardized conditions, no inflammatory explanation, compatible diet or risk factors, and improvement after appropriate correction is more persuasive than any one isolated lab number. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

## Quick reference points

- Serum/plasma zinc is the most available and most commonly used biochemical marker, but it represents less than 0.1 percent of total body zinc and is affected by multiple confounders. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

- In adults, a doubling of zinc intake was associated with only about a 6 percent difference in serum/plasma zinc concentration in a 2012 systematic review and meta-analysis. ([Lowe et al., 2012](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/relationship-between-zinc-intake-and-serumplasma-zinc-concentration-in-adults-a-systematic-review-and-doseresponse-metaanalysis-by-the-eurreca-network/3F65CE86C88359E42776107FBBBBAB7A))

- In the 2025 systematic review, controlled trials showed that plasma/serum zinc increased with intake, but heterogeneity was very high, limiting simple individual interpretation. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

- Inflammation adjustment can substantially reduce estimated zinc deficiency prevalence, especially in preschool-child surveys, showing that low values during inflammation may overestimate true deficiency. ([McDonald et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32266402/); [Likoswe et al., 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/6/1563); [Berti et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38218540/))

- Sample handling can change classification: serum versus plasma, venous versus capillary sampling, delayed processing, warmer holding conditions, hemolysis, and contaminated tubes can all shift measured zinc. ([Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073); [Killilea et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28490675/); [Keyzer et al., 1983](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02332953))

- Hair and nail zinc are not ready to replace serum/plasma zinc for routine status assessment; hair zinc failed to respond in a large child RCT, and nail zinc remains preliminary. ([Wessells et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12011-020-02163-2))


## Practical interpretation

- Do not overinterpret a single borderline serum or plasma zinc result, especially if it was drawn during illness, inflammation, fever, recent infection, or nonstandard collection conditions. ([McDonald et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32266402/); [Berti et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38218540/))

- A low result is more believable when the sample was collected and processed correctly, inflammation is absent or accounted for, and the clinical/dietary context fits. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073))

- A normal result lowers concern but does not fully exclude marginal zinc deficiency, because serum/plasma zinc is homeostatically regulated and relatively insensitive to mild depletion. ([Hess et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1177/15648265070283S303); [Lowe et al., 2012](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/relationship-between-zinc-intake-and-serumplasma-zinc-concentration-in-adults-a-systematic-review-and-doseresponse-metaanalysis-by-the-eurreca-network/3F65CE86C88359E42776107FBBBBAB7A))

- If the result and clinical picture disagree, the most defensible next step is usually repeat testing under standardized conditions, ideally with inflammatory markers considered, rather than switching to an unvalidated alternative marker. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047); [Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073))


## Conflict of interest and funding notes

The main biomarker evidence cited here does not appear to be primarily driven by zinc-supplement manufacturers. The 2025 systematic review reported no funding or sponsorship and no conflicts of interest. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/article/83/3/e778/7699047))

The 2023 pre-analytical study was funded by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, reported no competing interests, and disclosed that Sarstedt donated blood-collection tubes; that tube donation is relevant to note because tube type was part of the methodology, although it is not the same as a zinc supplement manufacturer funding an efficacy trial. ([Killilea and Schultz, 2023](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286073))

The 2017 hemolysis-threshold paper also reported no conflicts of interest; its relevance here is methodological, not supplement efficacy. ([Killilea et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28490675/))

The Lao child RCT on plasma, hair, and nail zinc was funded by Nutrition International, the Mathile Institute, the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, and a Bristol-Myers Squibb Freedom to Discover Award; the authors disclosed that KHB and the spouse of SYH worked for the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, which provided part of the financial support, and stated that sponsors had no role in the study design, analysis, interpretation, report writing, or publication decision. ([Wessells et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12011-020-02163-2))
