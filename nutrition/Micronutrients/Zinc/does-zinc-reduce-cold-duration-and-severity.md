# Does Zinc Reduce Cold Duration and Severity?

Zinc lozenges may shorten the common cold in adults when they are started early and formulated to release ionic zinc in the mouth and throat. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291); [Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059); [Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205)) The evidence is strongest for duration, weaker for severity, and still limited by heterogeneity, formulation differences, and funding or reporting concerns. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990))

## What the strongest zinc-lozenge claim is

The strongest version of the claim is not "zinc cures colds." It is narrower: zinc acetate or zinc gluconate lozenges providing about 75 mg/day or more of elemental zinc, started soon after symptom onset, may shorten the duration of naturally acquired common colds in adults. A 2017 meta-analysis of seven randomized trials using more than 75 mg/day found that zinc lozenges shortened colds by an average of 33 percent, with no statistically significant difference between zinc acetate and zinc gluconate trials. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

The same analysis found that trials using 80 to 92 mg/day did not look meaningfully weaker than trials using 192 to 207 mg/day, suggesting that going far above 100 mg/day is not clearly supported by the trial data. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

An individual-patient-data meta-analysis of three zinc acetate lozenge trials, using 80 to 92 mg/day, found a recovery rate ratio of 3.1 compared with placebo; by day 5, 70 percent of zinc participants had recovered versus 27 percent of placebo participants. ([Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059))

The 2024 Cochrane review is more cautious. It found that zinc used for treatment may reduce cold duration by about 2.37 days, but rated the certainty as low and reported very high heterogeneity across trials. It found unclear effects on symptom severity and concluded that firm conclusions or recommendations could not be made from the available evidence. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

## Duration and severity are not equally supported

The duration finding is more consistent than the severity finding. In the 2024 Cochrane review, zinc for treatment was associated with shorter cold duration, but severity outcomes were rated very low certainty and were not considered reliable enough for a firm conclusion. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

A 2012 systematic review of randomized trials also found shorter cold duration overall, with a pooled reduction of 1.65 days, but high heterogeneity. In that review, zinc did not significantly reduce overall symptom severity in the main pooled analysis, although adult-only subgroup results suggested possible benefit. ([Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990))

A 2015 meta-analysis of three high-dose zinc acetate lozenge trials found shorter durations for several individual symptoms, including nasal discharge, nasal congestion, scratchy throat, hoarseness, cough, and muscle ache. This supports a symptom-duration effect, but it does not fully settle whether people feel substantially less sick on each day of illness. ([Hemila & Chalker, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12875-015-0237-6))

## Why the lozenge formulation matters

The proposed lozenge-specific mechanism is local exposure of the oropharynx to free ionic zinc from a slowly dissolving lozenge, rather than a systemic effect from swallowed zinc alone. This is why the "lozenge" detail matters: a lozenge that dissolves slowly in the mouth is pharmacologically different from a capsule that is swallowed immediately. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291); [Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059); [Hemila & Chalker, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2024.1470004))

Formulation can affect whether zinc is available as free Zn²⁺. In a saliva-based formulation study, citric acid and a large excess of mannitol plus sorbitol suppressed free zinc ionization from zinc gluconate lozenges, while excess glycine did not show the same interference. ([Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205))

Older solution-chemistry reanalyses also reported that lozenges predicted to release more positively charged zinc species were more likely to show clinical benefit, while lozenges predicted to release little or no free ionic zinc tended not to work. These are mechanistic and reanalysis data, so they should be weighed below randomized clinical outcomes, but they explain why two products with the same zinc dose can plausibly perform differently. ([Eby, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jac.a020864); [Eby, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1023/B:BIRE.0000037754.71063.41))

## Acetate versus gluconate

Zinc acetate is often treated as the preferred salt because acetate binds zinc less strongly than gluconate and should, in theory, release ionic zinc more readily. However, the 2017 meta-analysis comparing zinc acetate and zinc gluconate trials did not find a statistically significant difference between them when the daily elemental zinc dose was above 75 mg/day. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

A fair interpretation is that the salt matters, but not by itself. The product must deliver enough elemental zinc and must avoid excipients that strongly bind zinc under mouth-and-throat conditions. ([Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205); [Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

## Timing matters

The best-supported lozenge trials generally started zinc soon after cold symptoms began. The individual-patient-data meta-analysis concluded that properly composed zinc acetate lozenges above 75 mg/day may be useful when started within 24 hours of symptom onset. ([Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059))

The 2015 zinc acetate symptom meta-analysis also framed the practical evidence around starting high-dose lozenges within 24 hours and using them during the acute cold, not as a late intervention several days into illness. ([Hemila & Chalker, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12875-015-0237-6))

## Capsules, syrups, and swallowed zinc

The evidence for swallowed zinc products is much less clear than the evidence for lozenges. The 2024 Cochrane review included multiple routes and formulations, but most treatment lozenge studies were in the lozenge category, while non-lozenge treatment evidence included far fewer capsule, powder, tablet, syrup, and intranasal studies. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

The current evidence does not establish that swallowed capsules, gummies, or swallowed tablets should be assumed to perform like properly formulated lozenges for shortening colds. Given the local-mechanism argument, formulation data, and formulation heterogeneity summarized in the 2024 Cochrane review, it is not justified to extrapolate the high-dose lozenge findings to ordinary zinc capsules, gummies, or swallowed tablets. ([Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205); [Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Hemila & Chalker, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2024.1470004))

## Why the evidence remains contested

Cochrane's zinc reviews have had an unusually complicated history. An earlier Cochrane review on zinc for the common cold was withdrawn after concerns about calculation and analysis, and the topic was later replaced by the 2024 Cochrane review. ([Singh & Das, withdrawn Cochrane review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25924708/); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

The 2024 Cochrane review concluded that the evidence remains insufficient for firm clinical recommendations, largely because the trials vary by population, route, dose, formulation, outcome definition, and risk of bias. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

A 2024 critique argued that the new Cochrane review diluted the lozenge-specific question by pooling pharmacologically different zinc interventions and by not giving enough weight to local zinc-ion availability. This critique is useful for understanding the methodological dispute, but it is itself a critical commentary rather than a randomized trial or systematic review. ([Hemila & Chalker, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2024.1470004))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

Funding bias is a serious issue in this literature. The 2024 Cochrane review noted that most included studies were either commercially funded, industry-related, or did not report funding. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

The 2012 CMAJ systematic review reported that all included treatment trials were funded by industry, which prevented a meaningful comparison between industry-funded and non-industry-funded treatment trials. This does not automatically invalidate the findings, but it lowers confidence, especially when trials are small, heterogeneous, and vulnerable to blinding problems from taste and gastrointestinal side effects. ([Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

Not all industry-linked findings are positive. A 2000 randomized trial involving zinc gluconate and zinc acetate lozenges found little evidence of benefit for natural colds, and one author was affiliated with Warner-Lambert Consumer Healthcare. This trial is also important because a later high-dose lozenge meta-analysis identified the Turner natural-cold comparison as an outlier that contributed substantially to heterogeneity in the lozenge literature. ([Turner & Cetnarowski, 2000](https://doi.org/10.1086/317437); [Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

A 2020 randomized controlled trial of a commercially available zinc acetate lozenge also found no benefit for treating the common cold, showing that "zinc acetate" on a label does not guarantee clinical effectiveness. ([Hemila et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjopen-2019-031662))

## Quick reference values

- 2024 Cochrane treatment estimate: zinc may shorten cold duration by 2.37 days, but certainty was low and heterogeneity was very high. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2))

- High-dose lozenge meta-analysis: zinc acetate or gluconate lozenges above 75 mg/day shortened colds by 33 percent on average. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291))

- Zinc acetate individual-patient-data meta-analysis: 80 to 92 mg/day increased the recovery rate, with 70 percent recovered by day 5 versus 27 percent on placebo. ([Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059))

- High-dose zinc acetate symptom meta-analysis: total cold duration was reduced by about 42 percent across three trials, with shorter durations for several respiratory symptoms. ([Hemila & Chalker, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12875-015-0237-6))

- Adverse events: the 2024 Cochrane review found more non-serious adverse events with treatment zinc; the 2012 CMAJ review found higher risks of bad taste and nausea. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990))

- Dose context: the adult tolerable upper intake level for zinc is 40 mg/day, so the higher doses used in many cold-lozenge trials should be treated as short-term regimens, not routine daily supplementation. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/))


## Practical interpretation

- Zinc lozenges may shorten colds in adults when the product is well formulated, provides roughly 75 mg/day or more of elemental zinc, dissolves slowly in the mouth, and is started within 24 hours of symptom onset. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291); [Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059); [Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205))

- The evidence is not strong enough to say zinc reliably reduces cold severity. Duration is the better-supported outcome. ([Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990))

- Product formulation matters. Lozenges containing strong zinc-binding excipients, or products that are swallowed quickly, should not be assumed to match the positive lozenge trials. ([Zarembo et al., 1992](https://doi.org/10.1002/jps.2600810205); [Eby, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jac.a020864); [Hemila & Chalker, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2024.1470004))

- The evidence base is weakened by industry funding, incomplete funding reporting, small trials, heterogeneity, and disagreement over how zinc formulations should be pooled. ([Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Hemila & Chalker, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmed.2024.1470004))

- Intranasal zinc should not be treated as interchangeable with lozenges; the lozenge evidence is based on oral dissolution, and intranasal zinc has raised safety concerns in the literature. ([Hemila & Chalker, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12875-015-0237-6); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/))

- Prolonged or repeated high-dose zinc use should be avoided unless medically supervised, because excessive zinc intake can interfere with copper status and cause other adverse effects. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Zinc-HealthProfessional/))


## Bottom line

Zinc lozenges have plausible evidence for shortening common-cold illness, but the defensible claim is narrow. The best-supported use is early, short-term, high-dose, properly formulated zinc acetate or zinc gluconate lozenges in adults. The evidence does not justify a broad claim that any zinc supplement reduces cold duration, and it is even less convincing for reducing severity. ([Hemila, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1177/2054270417694291); [Hemila et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ofid/ofx059); [Nault et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014914.pub2); [Science et al., 2012](https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.111990))
