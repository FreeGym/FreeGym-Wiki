# Zinc and Athletes: Sweat Losses, Zinc Status, and Possible Increased Needs

Athletes can lose zinc through sweat, and training status is associated with altered zinc biomarkers. That supports paying attention to dietary zinc intake and zinc status in higher-risk athletes, but it does not support routine high-dose zinc or ZMA supplementation for athletes who are already zinc-replete. ([DeRuisseau et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/); [Chu et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8); [Xue et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-21528-y); [Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12); [Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466))

## What the question is and is not

The adult zinc RDA is 11 mg/day for men and 8 mg/day for women, and the adult tolerable upper intake level is 40 mg/day. These values are general population reference values, not athlete-specific performance targets. ([Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/10026/dietary-reference-intakes-for-vitamin-a-vitamin-k-arsenic-boron-chromium-copper-iodine-iron-manganese-molybdenum-nickel-silicon-vanadium-and-zinc)) ([National Academies](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10026/chapter/14))

The athlete question has two separate parts: whether training may meaningfully increase zinc losses, turnover, or risk of inadequate zinc status, and whether zinc supplementation improves performance or recovery in athletes who are not deficient. The first question has some biologic and observational support; the second has weak support. ([Chu et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8); [Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12); [Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466)) ([Macquarie University](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/publications/lower-serum-zinc-concentration-despite-higher-dietary-zinc-intake/))

## Sweat and urinary zinc losses

Sweat zinc loss is real and measurable. In a controlled 2-hour cycling study, sweat zinc losses represented about 9 percent of the male RDA and 8 percent of the female RDA, showing that a single prolonged session can account for a meaningful fraction of daily zinc intake. ([DeRuisseau et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/))

Urinary zinc is a plausible status marker because urinary zinc responds to changes in zinc intake, but athlete-specific urinary zinc evidence is much thinner than sweat-loss evidence. A systematic review of zinc-status biomarkers published online in 2024 and appearing in a 2025 issue found that plasma/serum zinc and urinary zinc respond to zinc intake, while also emphasizing that serum/plasma zinc is affected by meal timing, inflammation, drugs, hormones, and other non-zinc factors. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38917458/))

A 6-month training study in elite middle-distance runners measured serum and urinary trace elements and found time-related changes in serum zinc, but the reported urinary changes were clearer for several other trace elements than for zinc. That makes the urinary-loss claim plausible but not strong enough to define an athlete-specific zinc requirement. ([Maynar et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-019-0322-7)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-019-0322-7))

## Athlete zinc status

A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis found that athletes had lower serum zinc than controls by about 0.93 µmol/L, despite reporting higher dietary zinc intake by about 2.57 mg/day. The authors concluded that athletes may have higher zinc requirements, but they also noted that evidence for erythrocyte and urinary zinc was insufficient for meta-analysis. ([Chu et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8)) ([Macquarie University](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/publications/lower-serum-zinc-concentration-despite-higher-dietary-zinc-intake/))

The athlete-status evidence is not uniform across sports. In professional athletes grouped by metabolic sport type, aerobic athletes had lower serum zinc than sedentary controls, while anaerobic and mixed aerobic-anaerobic athletes had higher serum zinc than controls, despite no reported differences in zinc intake. ([Maynar et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-018-0233-z)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-018-0233-z))

A 2025 study comparing professional middle- and long-distance runners, amateur runners, and sedentary adults found that serum zinc was lower with higher training level and was negatively correlated with training level. This supports the idea that high training loads can shift zinc biomarkers, especially in endurance contexts, but it still does not establish a new requirement value. ([Xue et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-21528-y)) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-21528-y))

## Does training prove elevated requirements?

The best cautious interpretation is that heavy training may increase zinc losses, turnover, or risk of inadequate zinc status in some athletes, but current evidence is not strong enough to set a separate athlete zinc RDA. A systematic review of regular exercise and zinc status concluded that the evidence base was incomplete and that physically active people should at least meet the standard recommended intake. ([Chu et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0184827)) ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0184827))

Serum zinc should not be treated as a perfect proxy for total-body zinc. Plasma or serum zinc represents a very small fraction of body zinc and can change with inflammation, recent meals, time of day, and other physiologic factors. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38917458/))

## Supplementation and performance

Low zinc intake can impair exercise-related physiology, so correcting deficiency is a real issue. In a controlled feeding study in men, low dietary zinc reduced zinc-status measures, reduced erythrocyte carbonic anhydrase activity, and impaired cardiorespiratory function during exercise. ([Lukaski, 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15883427/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15883427/))

That does not mean extra zinc improves performance in zinc-replete athletes. In resistance-trained men with normal zinc and magnesium status, 8 weeks of ZMA did not significantly improve total or free testosterone, IGF-1, growth hormone, cortisol, strength, endurance, anaerobic sprint capacity, body mass, or body composition. ([Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2129161/))

A 2024 randomized crossover study in recreationally trained men with adequate baseline zinc intake found that an acute dose of ZMA did not improve objective sleep, subjective sleep, cognitive performance, or repeated-sprint performance the next morning. The authors noted that benefits seen in deficient populations should not be generalized to non-deficient healthy exercising people. ([Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11313716/))

A broader systematic review of micronutrients and athletic performance concluded that a balanced diet is generally sufficient, while athletes with deficiencies, malabsorption, or other specific risks may benefit from targeted supplementation. ([Ghazzawi et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports11060109)) ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4663/11/6/109))

## ZMA and the deficiency-generalization problem

The ZMA deficiency-generalization problem is that a valid premise, zinc is essential and deficiency can impair physiology, can be stretched into a claim that extra zinc improves testosterone, recovery, sleep, or strength in already replete athletes. The best direct ZMA trials do not support that leap. ([Lukaski, 2005](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15883427/); [Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12); [Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2129161/))

Documented sweat losses justify checking dietary intake and risk factors; they do not justify routine high-dose supplementation. This matters because the adult zinc UL is 40 mg/day, and the UL was set partly because high zinc intake can interfere with copper-related status markers. ([DeRuisseau et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/); [Institute of Medicine, 2001](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/10026/dietary-reference-intakes-for-vitamin-a-vitamin-k-arsenic-boron-chromium-copper-iodine-iron-manganese-molybdenum-nickel-silicon-vanadium-and-zinc)) ([National Academies](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/10026/chapter/14))

## Higher-risk athlete subgroups

The strongest practical case for zinc assessment is in athletes with restricted energy intake, weight cutting, high training loads, or limited food variety. A sports-nutrition review notes that athletes can have increased micronutrient turnover and losses, but that deficiency risk rises especially when energy availability is poor or dietary restriction is present. ([Peeling et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4))

Plant-heavy athletes are another plausible higher-risk group because zinc from many plant foods is less bioavailable, largely because phytate can reduce absorption. A review on vegan athletes notes that vegetarians may require up to 50 percent more zinc than omnivores because of lower bioavailability, while emphasizing planning strategies such as soaking, fermenting, sprouting, and careful food selection. ([Rogerson, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-017-0192-9); [Hunt, 2003](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/78.3.633S)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-017-0192-9))

Weight-class and aesthetic-sport athletes are higher-risk mainly because the pathway is dietary: repeated restriction, low energy availability, and rapid weight-loss phases reduce the margin for meeting micronutrient needs. That makes assessment more defensible than assuming either adequacy or deficiency. ([Peeling et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

The 2004 ZMA trial in resistance-trained men was funded by Cytodyne Technologies, which had an interest in the supplement category. The paper reported that study design, collection, analysis, and manuscript preparation were conducted independently, and the result was null rather than favorable to ZMA. The funding should still be noted when interpreting the trial. ([Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2129161/))

The 2024 acute ZMA trial reported no conflicts of interest and listed internal research funding from Liverpool John Moores University. Its findings were also mostly null for sleep, cognition, and repeated-sprint performance. ([Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11313716/))

## Practical interpretation

- Heavy training and sweating make zinc worth paying attention to, especially in endurance athletes and athletes with high sweat volumes. The evidence supports meeting intake needs and assessing risk, not routine high-dose supplementation. ([DeRuisseau et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/); [Chu et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-017-0818-8)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12500986/))

- Supplementation is most defensible when dietary intake is low, zinc status is low, or the athlete has a clear risk factor such as calorie restriction, weight cutting, low energy availability, malabsorption, or a poorly planned plant-heavy diet. ([Peeling et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4); [Rogerson, 2017](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12970-017-0192-9)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-023-01875-4))

- In zinc-replete athletes, ZMA and zinc supplementation have not shown reliable benefits for strength, testosterone, sleep, repeated-sprint performance, or body composition. ([Wilborn et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1186/1550-2783-1-2-12); [Edwards et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152466)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2129161/))

- Serum zinc can be useful, but it should be interpreted with dietary history, timing of blood draw, inflammation status, recent meals, and the athlete's training and weight-management context. ([Ceballos-Rasgado et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae072)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38917458/))
