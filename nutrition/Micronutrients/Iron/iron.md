# Iron: Deep Dive

Iron sits at an unusual point in nutrition: it is the most common micronutrient deficiency in the world, while simultaneously being a pro-oxidant nutrient that causes harm in excess. The same lab number can mean different things depending on inflammation, sex, age, and training status. The result is a contested space where standard cutoffs miss real cases, supplementation regimens are often suboptimal, and the relationship between iron and athletic performance is poorly handled by general medicine.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

## Are Standard Ferritin Cutoffs Too Low?

Ferritin is the workhorse marker for iron stores, but reference ranges vary enormously between labs and populations. Standard "deficiency" cutoffs sit around 12-15 ng/mL in many labs, while functional medicine and athletic settings target much higher floors.

**Competing claims:**

1. The WHO cutoff of <15 ng/mL captures iron deficiency anemia in population settings, and raising the threshold creates unnecessary medicalization
2. Functional iron deficiency without anemia (low ferritin, normal hemoglobin) presents at ferritin levels well above standard cutoffs - many clinicians use 30-50 ng/mL as a working floor, especially in symptomatic patients
3. In endurance athletes, ferritin <50 ng/mL has been associated with impaired aerobic capacity and adaptation, suggesting sport-specific cutoffs are warranted
4. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant that rises with inflammation, infection, obesity, and liver disease - "normal" ferritin in inflamed states can mask functional deficiency, making interpretation more nuanced than a single threshold

**Evidence needed:** Ferritin cutoff dose-response studies, IDA-without-anemia symptomatic outcome trials, athlete-specific ferritin and performance data, soluble transferrin receptor and ferritin index studies in inflammatory states.

## Heme vs Non-Heme: Does the Source Matter?

Iron from animal foods (heme) is absorbed via a different pathway than iron from plants and supplements (non-heme), and absorption efficiency differs by an order of magnitude. The implications for vegetarians, supplement users, and people with gut issues are substantial.

**Competing claims:**

1. Heme iron is absorbed at 15-35% versus 2-20% for non-heme iron, and bypasses regulation by inhibitors (phytates, calcium, polyphenols) - making animal sources structurally more reliable for status maintenance
2. Vegetarians compensate over time through upregulated absorption efficiency, and well-planned plant-based diets can maintain status without supplementation
3. Non-heme iron in supplements (ferrous sulfate, fumarate) at therapeutic doses overcomes the absorption disadvantage but produces GI side effects in 30-70% of users, leading to poor adherence
4. Newer forms (ferric maltol, sucrosomial iron, iron bisglycinate) claim better tolerability and absorption, but head-to-head trials are limited and many are industry-funded

**Evidence needed:** Heme vs non-heme absorption RCTs across iron status levels, long-term iron status in matched omnivore vs vegetarian cohorts, supplement form tolerability and absorption comparisons, GI side effect prevalence by formulation.

## Alternate-Day vs Daily Dosing: Is Standard Practice Wrong?

Iron supplementation has historically been prescribed at 1-3 times daily. Recent work by Moretti, Stoffel and colleagues showed that daily dosing triggers a hepcidin response that blocks subsequent doses, while alternate-day single-morning dosing achieves better fractional absorption with fewer side effects.

**Competing claims:**

1. Single morning doses on alternate days produce equal or greater total iron uptake than split daily dosing, with fewer GI side effects and better adherence
2. The hepcidin response to a single dose lasts ~24 hours, making same-day second doses largely wasted - this challenges decades of standard practice
3. In severely deficient patients with urgent need to restore status, daily dosing may still be preferred because cumulative absolute uptake matters more than fractional efficiency
4. Most clinicians and prescribing references have not updated to reflect the alternate-day evidence, leaving patients on inferior regimens

**Evidence needed:** Stoffel/Moretti et al. trial replications and extensions, real-world adherence and tolerability with alternate-day vs daily protocols, prescribing pattern surveys post-publication, comparative outcomes in pregnancy and severe deficiency.

## Iron and the Gut Microbiome: A Neglected Side Effect

Unabsorbed non-heme iron passes into the colon, where it can favor the growth of pathogenic bacteria over beneficial ones. The clinical significance is debated, but it complicates the standard "iron deficiency = supplement aggressively" framing.

**Competing claims:**

1. Oral iron supplementation shifts the gut microbiome toward pathogenic species (Escherichia, Shigella, Salmonella) and away from beneficial ones (Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus)
2. The microbiome shift is dose-dependent and may be minimal at the lower doses used in alternate-day protocols
3. In malaria-endemic regions, iron supplementation has been linked to increased infection rates - a real-world signal that gut iron exposure has consequences
4. For athletes and otherwise healthy populations in non-endemic regions, the microbiome effects are likely outweighed by the benefits of correcting deficiency

**Evidence needed:** Iron supplementation and microbiome composition RCTs, dose-response data on microbiome shifts, infection rate trials by region and dose, GI symptom correlation with microbiome changes.

## Iron in Athletes: Unique Risk, Unique Mechanisms

Endurance athletes have a documented risk profile for iron depletion driven by mechanisms general populations do not experience: footstrike hemolysis, sweat iron loss, exercise-induced hepcidin elevation, and increased turnover. Standard primary care often misses sub-clinical depletion in this group.

**Competing claims:**

1. Endurance athletes (especially female, distance runners, and adolescents) have substantially higher iron losses and elevated risk of functional deficiency, even without overt anemia
2. The exercise-induced post-workout hepcidin spike (3-6 hours) impairs subsequent iron absorption, suggesting timing of supplementation matters - taking iron in the morning before training, or the day after a hard session, may improve uptake
3. Routine iron screening in athletic populations is justified by deficiency prevalence (estimates 15-35% in female endurance athletes), even though general population guidelines argue against routine screening
4. IV iron, when indicated, restores ferritin and aerobic performance faster than oral - but is over-prescribed in some sports settings without confirming functional deficiency

**Evidence needed:** Athlete iron status prevalence studies stratified by sport and sex, exercise-induced hepcidin time course studies, IV vs oral iron comparison RCTs in athletes, training-load and iron loss quantification data.

## Excess Iron and Hemochromatosis: The Other Side of the Curve

Iron deficiency dominates the conversation, but iron overload is also clinically meaningful. HFE gene variants (the most common single-gene disorder in people of Northern European ancestry), aging men accumulating iron, and inappropriate supplementation all push in the other direction.

**Competing claims:**

1. Hemochromatosis is underdiagnosed - HFE C282Y homozygosity occurs in roughly 1 in 200 people of Northern European descent, but only a fraction are recognized clinically
2. Men past their 40s and post-menopausal women progressively accumulate iron without menstrual losses - the "donate blood for longevity" hypothesis is biologically plausible but not RCT-supported
3. Routine iron supplementation without testing is risky in people who may be heterozygous or homozygous for HFE variants - "iron is good for energy" advice can cause harm
4. Excess iron is pro-oxidant and has been associated with cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers - though confounding by inflammation and other factors makes causality difficult to establish

**Evidence needed:** HFE genotype prevalence and penetrance data, blood donation and longevity outcome studies, iron status and chronic disease prospective cohort analyses, screening cost-effectiveness in high-prevalence populations.

## Iron Markers Beyond Ferritin: A Better Picture

Ferritin alone is a noisy indicator, especially in the presence of inflammation. A panel approach - including transferrin saturation, soluble transferrin receptor, and inflammatory markers like CRP - gives a more reliable status assessment but is rarely ordered in primary care.

**Competing claims:**

1. Transferrin saturation (TSAT) below 20% strongly suggests functional iron deficiency, even when ferritin is in the "normal" range due to inflammation
2. Soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR) and the sTfR/log-ferritin index are inflammation-resistant markers but rarely used outside research and specialty clinics
3. Combining ferritin with CRP allows correction of ferritin readings in inflammatory states - protocols exist (BRINDA, Thurnham) but are inconsistently applied clinically
4. Single-marker ordering (ferritin alone) is a major source of misdiagnosis in primary care, and athletes especially benefit from a panel approach given their inflammatory profile post-training

**Evidence needed:** Multi-marker iron panel performance data, inflammation-adjusted ferritin protocol validation, sTfR clinical utility studies, panel cost-effectiveness in high-risk populations.

## Interactions

Iron's nutrient and drug interactions are clinically substantial. Co-ingestion with other minerals and chelating drugs can reduce absorption by 50%+, and iron itself impairs absorption of several common medications.

**Competing claims:**

1. Calcium, zinc, and polyphenols (tea, coffee, red wine) substantially reduce non-heme iron absorption when co-ingested - separating iron supplements from these by 1-2 hours improves uptake
2. Vitamin C enhances non-heme iron absorption by reducing ferric to ferrous iron and chelating it into a soluble complex - a meaningful enhancer at modest doses (50-100mg) without requiring megadosing
3. PPIs, H2 blockers, and antacids reduce iron absorption through impaired acid-mediated solubilization - chronic users have lower iron status and often need higher supplementation doses
4. Iron chelates with levothyroxine, fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, bisphosphonates, levodopa, and methyldopa - reducing both iron and drug absorption when taken together, requiring deliberate timing separation

**Evidence needed:** Iron-mineral co-ingestion absorption RCTs, vitamin C dose-response iron absorption studies, PPI-iron interaction longitudinal data, drug-iron timing optimization research.
