# What Level Is “Sufficient”? The Vitamin D Threshold War

The vitamin D threshold debate is not just a fight over whether 25-hydroxyvitamin D should be **20 ng/mL** or **30 ng/mL**. It is a fight over whether a single blood level can predict meaningful clinical benefit from supplementation across bone, muscle, immune, mood, metabolic, and aging outcomes.

## The two main threshold frameworks

The National Academies/IOM framework treats **25(OH)D of about 16 ng/mL** as an average-requirement-like value and **20 ng/mL** as the level associated with coverage for nearly all people for bone-health needs, assuming minimal sun exposure. In that framework, **<12 ng/mL** is deficiency risk relative to bone health, **12 to 20 ng/mL** is possible inadequacy for some, **≥20 ng/mL** is sufficient for practically all, **>30 ng/mL** is not consistently associated with added benefit, and **>50 ng/mL** may raise concern for adverse effects. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

The [2011 Endocrine Society guideline](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2011-0385) used a higher framework: **<20 ng/mL** as deficiency, **21 to 29 ng/mL** as insufficiency, and **≥30 ng/mL** as sufficiency. Its rationale included PTH plateau data, calcium absorption data, and fracture-related interpretations, but it also acknowledged that vitamin D thresholds were controversial and that assay variability complicated any single cut point.

The [2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305) no longer endorses the older “≥30 ng/mL sufficiency” target for generally healthy people. The guideline panel concluded that existing clinical trials did not clearly identify outcome-specific 25(OH)D thresholds or target levels for disease prevention, especially because many trials did not enroll people with low baseline levels and were not designed around baseline or achieved 25(OH)D strata.

## Why 20 ng/mL remains the strongest general-population cutoff

The strongest case for **20 ng/mL** is narrow but evidence-grounded: it is mainly a bone-health adequacy threshold for generally healthy populations, not a universal optimization target. The National Academies report linked 20 ng/mL to near-complete population coverage for bone-health needs and warned that using higher cutoffs can overestimate deficiency and push high-dose supplementation in people already above 20 ng/mL. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

The [2024 Endocrine Society communication](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae322) makes the same point more directly: it states that the IOM estimated 20 ng/mL would meet bone-health needs in 97.5 percent of the population, while also noting individual variability. It also says the Endocrine Society no longer endorses its prior 30 ng/mL sufficiency definition because clinical trial evidence does not clearly show that net benefit in generally healthy people is predicted by being below 20 to 24 ng/mL. ([OUP](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1948/7685309 "Vitamin D Insufficiency and Epistemic Humility: An Endocrine Society Guideline Communication"))

A direct RCT challenge to the 30 ng/mL target came from postmenopausal women with baseline 25(OH)D of 14 to 27 ng/mL. High-dose vitamin D maintained levels ≥30 ng/mL and produced mean follow-up levels around 56 ng/mL, but the small calcium absorption increase did not translate into better bone density, muscle function, muscle mass, or falls. The authors concluded that they found no data supporting recommendations to maintain 25(OH)D ≥30 ng/mL in that population. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2422066 "Treatment of Vitamin D Insufficiency in Postmenopausal Women: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Nutrition | JAMA Internal Medicine | JAMA Network"))

## What the 30 ng/mL argument gets right

The 30 ng/mL argument is not nonsense. It comes from plausible biological and surrogate-outcome observations: PTH often falls as 25(OH)D rises, some studies report PTH plateaus around 30 to 40 ng/mL, and an older calcium absorption study suggested improved absorption when postmenopausal women rose from about 20 to 32 ng/mL. That was part of the rationale behind the [2011 Endocrine Society sufficiency cutoff](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2011-0385).

The problem is that PTH, calcium absorption, and observational associations are not the same as patient-important outcomes. The [2024 guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305) prioritized RCT evidence and found no reliable basis for proposing 25(OH)D adequacy thresholds or target levels for disease prevention in generally healthy people.

A 3-year RCT in healthy adults aged 55 to 70 compared 400 IU/day, 4000 IU/day, and 10,000 IU/day. Higher doses raised 25(OH)D substantially but did not improve bone strength; instead, the 4000 IU and 10,000 IU groups had statistically lower radial volumetric bone mineral density, and the 10,000 IU group also had lower tibial volumetric BMD. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2748796 "Effect of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation on Volumetric Bone Density and Bone Strength: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Osteoporosis | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

## Functional outcomes beyond bone

For **fractures and major disease-prevention outcomes in adults 50 to 74**, the [2024 supporting systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312) found no significant benefit of vitamin D supplementation for fracture risk, all-cause mortality, cancer, cardiovascular events, stroke, or myocardial infarction; nephrolithiasis showed a small increased risk signal. In adults 50 to 74 with 25(OH)D below a trial-defined threshold, the available evidence still did not show fracture, mortality, cancer, or cardiovascular benefit.

For **adults ≥75**, the same review found no significant reduction in fracture risk or in the person-level risk of having a fall, although fall incidence was modestly lower; there was also a very small reduction in all-cause mortality. Evidence in the subgroup of adults ≥75 with low 25(OH)D was very low certainty.

For **falls**, high-dose intermittent vitamin D is not a harmless “more is better” strategy. The [2024 systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312) found that intermittent high-dose vitamin D given at intervals greater than 12 weeks showed increased fall risk compared with lower-dose approaches.

For **respiratory infections**, the evidence is mixed and does not establish 30 ng/mL as a universal sufficiency threshold. A 2024 dose-response meta-analysis of 43 RCTs and 49,320 participants found no significant overall preventive effect against acute respiratory infections, although daily dosing, shorter trials, and winter/spring/autumn contexts showed modest subgroup signals; importantly, the authors reported no preventive effect in a study-level subgroup with baseline 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L, equivalent to 20 ng/mL, and cautioned that subgroup findings should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating. ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12937-024-00990-w "Optimal methods of vitamin D supplementation to prevent acute respiratory infections: a systematic review, dose-response and pairwise meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials | Nutrition Journal | Springer Nature Link"))

For **mood**, recent meta-analyses suggest vitamin D supplementation may reduce depressive symptoms in some contexts, but that does not validate a universal 25(OH)D sufficiency threshold of 30 ng/mL. A 2025 meta-analysis noted heterogeneous clinical evidence and differences by dose, duration, baseline status, and population; because of heterogeneity and trial-quality limitations, proposed threshold-based targets should be treated as hypothesis-generating rather than established. Its funding statement reported only article-processing support from Qilu Medical University and no commercial financial relationships. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1622796/full "Frontiers | Meta-analysis of the effect of vitamin D on depression"))

For **prediabetes**, vitamin D has a more credible disease-specific signal than many wellness claims. The [2024 supporting review](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312) found moderate-certainty evidence that vitamin D reduced progression to diabetes in adults with prediabetes, but this is a targeted high-risk population, not proof that all adults should chase 30, 40, or 60 ng/mL.

## Individual variation and the single-threshold problem

A single universal threshold is scientifically fragile because 25(OH)D status is affected by sun exposure, diet, supplements, season, age, adiposity, skin pigmentation, assay method, illness, and genetics. The National Academies report explicitly noted uncertainty from assay variability, sun exposure, intake-status nonlinearity, and limited dose-response evidence. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

Genetics also matters. A large genome-wide association study of 417,580 Europeans identified **143 independent loci** associated with 25(OH)D, implicating pathways beyond canonical vitamin D metabolism, including lipid metabolism, dermal tissue properties, and 25(OH)D conjugation pathways. Another large GWAS reported **69 independent loci**, **63 of which were previously unreported**, and partially shared heritability with socioeconomic traits, possibly mediated through time outdoors. ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-15421-7 "Genome-wide association study identifies 143 loci associated with 25 hydroxyvitamin D concentration | Nature Communications")) ([American Journal of Human Genetics](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajhg.2020.01.017 "Genome-wide Association Study for Vitamin D Levels Reveals 69 Independent Loci"))

This supports individual variation in vitamin D status, but it does not yet prove that each person has a measurable personalized “optimal” 25(OH)D target. The [2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305) specifically stated that thresholds are likely to vary by population and outcome, yet available trials were not designed to define those thresholds.

## Threshold abandonment does not eliminate thresholds

Removing endorsed thresholds does not remove real-world thresholds. It shifts them to laboratories, clinicians, insurers, local norms, and influencers.

The [2024 Endocrine Society communication](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae322) documents wide variation in laboratory reference ranges: some labs label 20 to 30 ng/mL as borderline, others define “optimal” as ≥30 ng/mL, and hospitals in one US state used minimum values of 20, 25, or 30 ng/mL. ([OUP](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1948/7685309 "Vitamin D Insufficiency and Epistemic Humility: An Endocrine Society Guideline Communication"))

A 2024 laboratory reference-interval study found that direct and indirect reference intervals for 25(OH)D differed, seasonal intervals differed, and regional factors mattered. The authors argued that local reference intervals may help adapt deficiency cutoffs, but reference intervals are not automatically clinical thresholds for treatment benefit or harm; without a strong clinical-outcome threshold, “normal” can become method-, region-, and institution-dependent. ([Biochemia Medica](https://www.biochemia-medica.com/en/journal/34/2/10.11613/BM.2024.020706/fullArticle "Direct and indirect reference intervals of 25-hydroxyvitamin D: it is not a real vitamin D deficiency pandemic - Biochemia Medica"))

Direct evidence that the **2024 Endocrine Society threshold withdrawal** has already changed clinical practice variation is limited. The guideline’s supporting systematic review found no trials evaluating a strategy of screening with 25(OH)D testing and supplementing only below a threshold in healthy adults, adults with dark complexion, or adults with obesity. ([2024 supporting systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

The [2011 Endocrine Society guideline](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2011-0385) stated that the task force received no corporate funding or remuneration. However, its disclosures included financial or business interests involving pharmaceutical companies, a diagnostic company, the UV Foundation, Mushroom Council, Dairy Management, and Pharmavite among some task-force members. That does not invalidate the guideline, but it is relevant because the guideline endorsed the higher ≥30 ng/mL sufficiency threshold.

The [2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305) reported that development funding came from the Endocrine Society and that no other entity provided financial support. It reported that 7 percent of panel members had relevant or potentially relevant conflicts, including disclosures involving pharmaceutical-product consultation or speaking engagements, with management steps documented.

The Hansen postmenopausal-women RCT was supported by the National Institute on Aging and Office of Dietary Supplements; the funder had no role in study conduct or publication decisions, and the only reported author disclosure was unrelated work as a local principal investigator for a Takeda trial. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2422066 "Treatment of Vitamin D Insufficiency in Postmenopausal Women: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Nutrition | JAMA Internal Medicine | JAMA Network"))

The Burt high-dose vitamin D RCT was funded by the Pure North S’Energy Foundation through an investigator-initiated grant, and the authors reported that the funder had no role in design, analysis, interpretation, manuscript preparation, or publication decisions. Several authors reported outside relationships with osteoporosis-drug companies or analysis software interests. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2748796 "Effect of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation on Volumetric Bone Density and Bone Strength: A Randomized Clinical Trial | Osteoporosis | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

## Quick reference values

- **<12 ng/mL:** deficiency risk relative to bone health in the National Academies framework. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

- **12 to 20 ng/mL:** possible inadequacy for some people. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

- **≥20 ng/mL:** sufficient for practically all people for bone-health needs in the National Academies framework. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))

- **≥30 ng/mL:** older Endocrine Society sufficiency target, no longer endorsed by the 2024 Endocrine Society guideline for generally healthy people. ([2011 Endocrine Society guideline](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2011-0385)) ([2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

- **40 to 60 ng/mL:** sometimes used in wellness or more aggressive clinical frameworks, but not established by RCTs as a general-population target for better outcomes. ([2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

- **>50 ng/mL:** level where the National Academies expressed concern about possible adverse effects. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf"))


## Practical interpretation

For generally healthy adults, **20 ng/mL has the stronger evidence base as a bone-health adequacy threshold**. It should not be oversold as an optimal level for every outcome, but the evidence does not support routine pursuit of 30, 40, or 60 ng/mL for disease prevention in most healthy adults. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf")) ([2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

The **30 ng/mL target is best understood as a historically influential clinical opinion based on surrogate markers and observational evidence**, not as a universally validated outcome threshold. It may still be used in selected clinical contexts, but the burden of proof is higher when it is applied to healthy people. ([2011 Endocrine Society guideline](https://doi.org/10.1210/jc.2011-0385)) ([2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

Threshold uncertainty does **not** mean severe deficiency is harmless. Very low 25(OH)D remains clinically important, especially for rickets, osteomalacia, malabsorption states, institutionalized older adults, and other high-risk situations; those clinical indications are different from routine wellness testing and optimization. ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56050/ "Summary - Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D - NCBI Bookshelf")) ([2024 Endocrine Society guideline](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

The best summary is: **20 ng/mL is defensible for population bone adequacy; 30 ng/mL is not proven as a universal minimum; higher targets should be justified by patient context, not assumed.**
