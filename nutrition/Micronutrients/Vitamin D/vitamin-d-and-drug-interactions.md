# Vitamin D and Drug Interactions

Several medication classes interact with vitamin D status, vitamin D response, calcium handling, or bone risk. The key distinction is that not all of them are true vitamin D-depleting drugs: enzyme-inducing antiseizure medications have the clearest vitamin D metabolism signal; orlistat and bile acid sequestrants mainly raise absorption concerns; thiazides raise hypercalcemia concerns; and proton pump inhibitors are better treated as a contested bone/fracture issue than as confirmed vitamin D-depleting agents. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40120-025-00805-y "Antiseizure Medications and Bone Health"))

## Glucocorticoids

Glucocorticoids are best framed as a bone-risk and calcium-handling interaction, not as a simple vitamin D-depletion interaction. In a [small controlled metabolic study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6969728/ "Effects of short term glucocorticoid administration on intestinal calcium absorption and circulating vitamin D metabolite concentrations in man"), prednisone 20 mg/day for 14 days reduced intestinal calcium absorption by 31 percent, while measured vitamin D metabolites did not fall in a way that explained the absorption change.

The evidence that systemic glucocorticoids consistently lower serum 25(OH)D is mixed. In [NHANES 2001-2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21956424/ "Association of Glucocorticoid Use and Low 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: Results from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey"), steroid users had a higher prevalence of severe 25(OH)D deficiency below 10 ng/mL, with an adjusted odds ratio of 2.21, but the study was cross-sectional and could not cleanly separate drug effect from underlying illness.

A [systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22188740/ "Clinical review: Do glucocorticosteroids alter vitamin D status? A systematic review with meta-analyses of observational studies") found that glucocorticoid users had only slightly lower 25(OH)D than healthy controls, by about 0.5 ng/mL, and did not differ from disease controls. That makes confounding by the underlying inflammatory or autoimmune condition a serious possibility.

Mechanistically, glucocorticoids can interact with CYP24A1/24-hydroxylase regulation, but the pathway is not clinically simple. Some experimental work shows glucocorticoid potentiation of vitamin D-induced 24-hydroxylase transcription, while other human osteoblast work found inhibitory effects on CYP24A1 expression. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20564225/ "Novel regulation of 25-hydroxyvitamin D3 24-hydroxylase transcription by glucocorticoids"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0303720719302278 "Effects of glucocorticoids on vitamin D3-metabolizing 24-hydroxylase in Saos-2 cells and primary human osteoblasts"))

The practical takeaway is that chronic systemic glucocorticoid users need fracture-risk assessment, adequate calcium intake, avoidance of vitamin D deficiency, and treatment according to glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis risk when indicated. The 2022 American College of Rheumatology guideline recommends fracture-risk assessment for adults receiving at least 2.5 mg/day prednisone-equivalent for more than 3 months, with pharmacologic therapy for patients at elevated fracture risk. ([ACR guideline summary](https://assets.contentstack.io/v3/assets/bltee37abb6b278ab2c/blt8c68fa62e5f70069/giop-guideline-summary-2022.pdf "2022 American College of Rheumatology Guideline Summary"))

## CYP-inducing antiseizure medications

The clearest vitamin D-depleting drug class is older enzyme-inducing antiseizure medication, especially phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine, and related inducers. Modern reviews describe these drugs as accelerating vitamin D catabolism, lowering active vitamin D signaling, increasing parathyroid hormone, and promoting bone resorption. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40120-025-00805-y "Antiseizure Medications and Bone Health"))

A [2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 68 studies](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1439279/full "Vitamin D content and prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in patients with epilepsy") found vitamin D deficiency was common in epilepsy both before antiseizure medication exposure and after antiseizure medication exposure, with pooled deficiency estimates around 50 percent in newly diagnosed epilepsy and 48 percent after antiseizure medication intervention. The authors concluded that antiseizure medication exposure can further reduce vitamin D content and that vitamin D monitoring should be part of routine epilepsy care.

The newer-drug picture is more nuanced. A [2021 systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0920121121000723 "The effects of antiepileptic drugs on bone health: A systematic review") found long-term antiepileptic therapy was the most important risk factor for bone mineral density loss, carbamazepine had the most frequent unfavorable bone findings, and lamotrigine appeared comparatively bone-protective. The same review noted that the evidence did not consistently prove enzyme-inducing drugs as a class were always worse than non-enzyme-inducing drugs.

Valproate is not a classic CYP-inducing antiseizure medication, but it should not be treated as bone-neutral. A recent review describes valproate as capable of reducing active vitamin D through mechanisms distinct from enzyme induction, while levetiracetam, lamotrigine, and lacosamide appear more favorable from a bone-health perspective. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40120-025-00805-y "Antiseizure Medications and Bone Health"))

## Orlistat and bile acid sequestrants

Orlistat inhibits fat absorption, so an interaction with vitamin D is biologically plausible because vitamin D is fat-soluble. Clinically, however, the signal is mixed because many trials used multivitamins, and obesity or weight loss can independently affect 25(OH)D. ([DailyMed](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/fda/fdaDrugXsl.cfm?setid=6240792b-9224-2d10-e053-2a91aa0a2c3e "XENICAL orlistat prescribing information"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "Effects of orlistat on fat-soluble vitamins in obese adolescents"))

A [small open-label adolescent study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "Effects of orlistat on fat-soluble vitamins in obese adolescents") found that mean vitamin D fell significantly after 1 month of orlistat despite multivitamin use. In contrast, a [larger randomized adolescent trial](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/201079 "Effect of Orlistat on Weight and Body Composition in Obese Adolescents") of 539 participants, with 357 assigned to orlistat and 182 assigned to placebo, found that 25(OH)D rose in both groups with multivitamin supplementation and did not differ significantly between groups; beta-carotene increased less with orlistat, while the authors reported no clinically relevant laboratory differences overall. That larger trial was funded by F. Hoffmann-La Roche, and the sponsor was involved in study design, conduct, data analysis, and interpretation, so the reassuring findings should be interpreted with that funding context in mind. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/201079 "Effect of Orlistat on Weight and Body Composition in Obese Adolescents"))

Bile acid sequestrants are mechanistically plausible vitamin D absorption inhibitors, but the direct clinical evidence is thinner than the mechanism suggests. An [older cholestyramine study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1552980/ "Effect of cholestyramine on the absorption of vitamin D3 and calcium") found reduced absorption of radiolabeled vitamin D3 in rats, likely through bile-salt binding and disruption of micelle formation, while a [later long-term cholestyramine study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1583392/ "Effects of long-term cholestyramine administration on vitamin D and parathormone levels in middle-aged men with hypercholesterolemia") did not find an apparent adverse effect on vitamin D metabolite availability.

The practical framing is “possible absorption impairment,” not guaranteed deficiency. The concern is most relevant when orlistat or bile acid sequestrants are used chronically, when baseline vitamin D intake is low, or when 25(OH)D fails to rise as expected despite supplementation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "Effects of orlistat on fat-soluble vitamins in obese adolescents"))

## Thiazide diuretics

Thiazide diuretics are not vitamin D-depleting agents. Their interaction is calcium-based: hydrochlorothiazide reduces urinary calcium excretion, so vitamin D supplementation can raise serum calcium more than it would otherwise. ([WashU Research Profiles](https://profiles.wustl.edu/en/publications/risk-of-hypercalcemia-in-blacks-taking-hydrochlorothiazide-and-vi/ "Risk of hypercalcemia in Blacks taking hydrochlorothiazide and vitamin D"))

In a [post hoc analysis of a randomized vitamin D dose-finding trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24657333/ "Risk of hypercalcemia in Blacks taking hydrochlorothiazide and vitamin D"), Black adults using hydrochlorothiazide who received vitamin D3 up to 4,000 IU/day for 3 months had serum calcium about 0.2 mg/dL higher than nonusers, but only one hydrochlorothiazide user developed hypercalcemia. This suggests the interaction is real but not usually dramatic at standard upper-limit dosing over short follow-up.

The concern becomes more clinically important when other hypercalcemia risks are present. In a [population-based study of thiazide-associated hypercalcemia](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4803175/ "Thiazide-Associated Hypercalcemia: Incidence and Association With Primary Hyperparathyroidism Over Two Decades"), most cases occurred in older women, severe hypercalcemia was not observed, and 71 percent remained hypercalcemic after stopping thiazides, suggesting that unrecognized primary hyperparathyroidism was common.

## Proton pump inhibitors

The proton pump inhibitor interaction is contested. A [meta-analysis of 11 observational studies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002934311001641 "Proton Pump Inhibitors and Risk of Fractures: A Meta-Analysis of 11 International Studies") found PPI use associated with modestly higher fracture risk, with relative risks of 1.30 for hip fracture, 1.56 for spine fracture, and 1.16 for any-site fracture, but the authors noted that residual confounding could not be excluded.

The vitamin D-specific pathway is weak. In a [prospective matched controlled study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23102518/ "Proton pump inhibitors have no measurable effect on calcium and bone metabolism in healthy young males") of healthy young men, 12 weeks of PPI therapy had no measurable effect on vitamin D, parathyroid hormone, ionized calcium, osteocalcin, or a bone resorption marker.

A [large older-adult cohort](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10579148/ "The association between proton pump inhibitors and hyperparathyroidism") found that PPI users taking calcium supplements had higher odds of hyperparathyroidism even after adjustment for serum vitamin D, which supports a calcium absorption/parathyroid hormone mechanism more than a direct vitamin D-depletion mechanism.

The practical interpretation is that long-term PPI users with fracture risk deserve medication review and bone-risk assessment, but PPIs should not be treated like enzyme-inducing antiseizure medications as proven vitamin D metabolism-depleting drugs, nor like orlistat as a direct fat-soluble-vitamin absorption concern. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002934311001641 "Proton Pump Inhibitors and Risk of Fractures: A Meta-Analysis of 11 International Studies"))

## Quick reference values

- **Highest concern for vitamin D metabolism:** phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine, and other enzyme-inducing antiseizure medications. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40120-025-00805-y "Antiseizure Medications and Bone Health"))

- **Bone-risk interaction, not simple vitamin D depletion:** chronic systemic glucocorticoids. ([ACR guideline summary](https://assets.contentstack.io/v3/assets/bltee37abb6b278ab2c/blt8c68fa62e5f70069/giop-guideline-summary-2022.pdf "2022 American College of Rheumatology Guideline Summary"))

- **Absorption concern:** orlistat and bile acid sequestrants, especially with chronic use or poor supplement response. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "Effects of orlistat on fat-soluble vitamins in obese adolescents"), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1552980/ "Effect of cholestyramine on the absorption of vitamin D3 and calcium"))

- **Hypercalcemia concern:** thiazide diuretics combined with high-dose vitamin D, high calcium intake, or unrecognized primary hyperparathyroidism. ([WashU Research Profiles](https://profiles.wustl.edu/en/publications/risk-of-hypercalcemia-in-blacks-taking-hydrochlorothiazide-and-vi/ "Risk of hypercalcemia in Blacks taking hydrochlorothiazide and vitamin D"))

- **Contested:** proton pump inhibitors; fracture associations exist, but direct vitamin D depletion is not well established. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002934311001641 "Proton Pump Inhibitors and Risk of Fractures: A Meta-Analysis of 11 International Studies"))


## Practical interpretation

- When 25(OH)D is unexpectedly low, check the medication list before assuming nonadherence or inadequate dose. Enzyme-inducing antiseizure medications and fat-malabsorption drugs are the most direct medication explanations. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40120-025-00805-y "Antiseizure Medications and Bone Health"))

- In chronic systemic glucocorticoid users, the main clinical issue is fracture risk and impaired calcium handling, not a reliably large drop in 25(OH)D. Bone-risk assessment and glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis management matter more than simply increasing vitamin D. ([ACR guideline summary](https://assets.contentstack.io/v3/assets/bltee37abb6b278ab2c/blt8c68fa62e5f70069/giop-guideline-summary-2022.pdf "2022 American College of Rheumatology Guideline Summary"))

- In thiazide users, vitamin D is not contraindicated, but stacking high-dose vitamin D with high calcium intake deserves serum calcium awareness, especially when hyperparathyroidism is possible. ([WashU Research Profiles](https://profiles.wustl.edu/en/publications/risk-of-hypercalcemia-in-blacks-taking-hydrochlorothiazide-and-vi/ "Risk of hypercalcemia in Blacks taking hydrochlorothiazide and vitamin D"))

- In long-term PPI users, it is reasonable to review fracture risk, calcium intake, and the need for continued PPI therapy, but the current evidence does not justify calling PPIs a proven vitamin D-depleting drug. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002934311001641 "Proton Pump Inhibitors and Risk of Fractures: A Meta-Analysis of 11 International Studies"))
