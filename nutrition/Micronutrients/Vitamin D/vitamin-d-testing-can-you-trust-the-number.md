# Vitamin D Testing: Can You Trust the Number?

A vitamin D result is usually useful as an approximate clinical estimate, but it should not be treated as a perfectly portable number. The same blood sample can produce meaningfully different 25-hydroxyvitamin D results depending on the assay, laboratory, calibration, and whether the method handles vitamin D2, vitamin D3, and related metabolites accurately. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/vitamin-d-standardization-program-vdsp-intralaboratory-study-assessment-25))

## What the test is trying to measure

Vitamin D status is usually assessed with serum total 25-hydroxyvitamin D, abbreviated 25(OH)D. This is the major circulating vitamin D metabolite and is generally treated as the best available marker of vitamin D exposure from sun, diet, and supplements. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/))

That does not mean the number is simple. Total 25(OH)D usually includes 25(OH)D3 and 25(OH)D2, and assays can differ in how well they detect each form. Some assays can also be affected by other vitamin D metabolites, which can push results higher or lower depending on the sample and method. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/), [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/vitamin-d-standardization-program-vdsp-intralaboratory-study-assessment-25))

Liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry, or LC-MS/MS, is generally considered more analytically specific than many automated immunoassays, but LC-MS/MS is not automatically accurate just because it is LC-MS/MS. Calibration, reference materials, operator method, and external quality assessment still matter. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/interlaboratory-comparison-25-hydroxyvitamin-d-assays-vitamin-d-standardization-program))

## How assay variability can change the answer

In a comparison of three commercial assays, the same group of samples produced mean 25(OH)D values of 85 nmol/L by HPLC-APCI-MS, 70 nmol/L by radioimmunoassay, and 60 nmol/L by chemiluminescent immunoassay. Using 50 nmol/L as the insufficiency cutoff, the estimated prevalence of insufficiency was 8 percent, 22 percent, or 43 percent depending on the assay. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0011555))

In another study, researchers selected a subset of 50 serum samples from an Ausimmune Study sample bank of 813 participants and compared results from three laboratories with a certified reference laboratory. Two laboratories read higher than the certified laboratory by about 12 to 13 nmol/L on average, while one read lower by about 11 nmol/L. The estimated prevalence of deficiency below 50 nmol/L was 24 percent at the certified laboratory, but 16 percent, 12 percent, and 41 percent at the three comparison laboratories. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478))

This is clinically important because a patient near a cutoff can move from "deficient" to "not deficient by that cutoff" without any biological change. The authors of the same study noted that an assay reading too low could lead to unnecessary supplementation, while an assay reading too high could lead to missed treatment in someone who is actually deficient. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478))

A clinical decision-making study of 494 patients compared two commonly used assays and found that, using 30 ng/mL as the "desirable" cutoff, 79 percent of patients were classified below the threshold by one assay versus 64 percent by the other. Depending on the agreement rule used, the two assays agreed on classification only 41 to 68 percent of the time. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/97/3/835/2536347))

## What standardization has improved

External quality programs have improved vitamin D measurement over time. The Vitamin D External Quality Assessment Scheme, or DEQAS, has monitored 25(OH)D testing since 1989, and reported that mean inter-laboratory imprecision improved from a coefficient of variation of 32.0 percent in 1994 to 15.3 percent in 2009. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20302938/))

The Vitamin D Standardization Program helped move the field toward accuracy-based targets rather than simple agreement with the average laboratory result. Since 2012, reference-measurement procedures have been used to assign target values to DEQAS materials, allowing laboratories to compare results against reference values rather than only against a trimmed participant mean. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jaoac/article-abstract/100/5/1277/5654287))

Modern DEQAS remains large and international, distributing human-serum samples four times per year to more than 1,000 participating laboratories. That kind of external assessment is important because DEQAS data involve many laboratories, instruments, and manufacturers. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34750644/))

In a VDSP intralaboratory comparison of 13 assay entries in a single laboratory, including immunoassays and one LC-MS/MS assay, all 13 met the VDSP imprecision criterion of coefficient of variation at or below 10 percent, and 9 of 13 met the mean-bias criterion of within plus or minus 5 percent. However, high-25(OH)D2 samples affected the response of ten immunoassays, showing that standardization progress does not eliminate method-specific problems. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34010687/), [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/vitamin-d-standardization-program-vdsp-intralaboratory-study-assessment-25))

## Why standardization is still incomplete

VDSP interlaboratory data show that real-world performance still lags behind the ideal. In one interlaboratory study, results from 15 LC-MS/MS assay sets from 14 laboratories were evaluated; only 53 percent met the VDSP criterion of mean bias within plus or minus 5 percent, and the assays that failed had mean biases of about 12 to 21 percent. ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/interlaboratory-comparison-25-hydroxyvitamin-d-assays-vitamin-d-standardization-program))

In the companion interlaboratory study of 32 ligand-binding assays across 17 laboratories, only 50 percent met the same VDSP mean-bias criterion. Among 13 unique ligand-binding assays, only 4 were consistently within plus or minus 5 percent, while another 4 were consistently outside that range. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34435207/))

A 2024 consensus statement also noted a limitation of the VDSP mean-bias criterion: an assay can have acceptable average bias while still showing substantial error for individual samples. In CDC-certified assays from November 2019, the mean proportion of individual samples passing a plus or minus 5 percent criterion was 30 percent for immunoassays and 61 percent for LC-MS/MS assays. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Testing less is not the same as testing never

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline recommends against routine 25(OH)D screening in generally healthy people without established indications. The panel emphasized that randomized trial evidence does not define outcome-specific vitamin D thresholds for disease prevention in the general population. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

That recommendation should not be read as "vitamin D testing is useless." The guideline explicitly applies to people without established indications, and the broader consensus literature still supports measuring 25(OH)D in selected at-risk patients when the result is likely to change management. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

The practical issue is that discouraging routine testing does not eliminate interpretation when testing is performed. A 2024 guideline communication noted that laboratory reference ranges vary across major health systems and commercial laboratories, which means interpretation can shift to lab defaults when expert guidance is absent. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1948/7685309))

## How to minimize measurement error

For longitudinal tracking, use the same laboratory and the same assay when possible. This is not because one lab is always right, but because switching methods can create an artificial rise or fall that looks biological when it is partly analytic. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478))

Interpret small changes cautiously. A 25(OH)D change is more meaningful when it exceeds the expected combination of analytic variation and biological variation, and consensus guidance notes that laboratories should understand method-specific reference-change values for serial interpretation. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Be especially cautious when the result is near a decision threshold. Assay differences of roughly 10 to 15 nmol/L are large enough to change classification around common cutoffs such as 50 nmol/L or 30 ng/mL. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0011555), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/97/3/835/2536347))

Consider method quality when the stakes are higher. A well-validated, standardized LC-MS/MS method may be preferable when results are unexpected, when vitamin D2 supplementation is being used, when assay interference is suspected, or when the clinical decision depends heavily on the exact value. Even then, the laboratory's calibration and participation in accuracy-based external quality assessment remain important. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [NIST](https://www.nist.gov/publications/interlaboratory-comparison-25-hydroxyvitamin-d-assays-vitamin-d-standardization-program), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Practical interpretation

- A 25(OH)D result is useful, but it is an estimate with method-dependent uncertainty. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478))

- Assay variability can misclassify patients, especially near common cutoffs. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0011555), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/97/3/835/2536347))

- Standardization has improved the field, but it has not fully solved individual-sample error or between-laboratory differences. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20302938/), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jaoac/article-abstract/100/5/1277/5654287), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

- The right response to assay noise is not measurement nihilism; it is better standardization, better interpretation, and more selective testing. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

- For routine wellness screening in generally healthy people, current guideline evidence does not support broad testing. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

- For clinically indicated testing, the result should be interpreted in context, preferably using the same lab and assay over time. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135478), [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))
