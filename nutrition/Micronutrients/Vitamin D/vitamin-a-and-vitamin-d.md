# Vitamin A and Vitamin D: Synergy, Antagonism, or Noise?

Vitamins A and D clearly interact at the molecular level. Retinoic acid signals mainly through retinoic acid receptors, vitamin D through the vitamin D receptor, and both receptor systems can use retinoid X receptor as a heterodimerization partner. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285)) ([Nucleic Acids Research](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/53/6/gkaf230/8102293))

That biochemical overlap is real. The supplement claim built on top of it is much weaker. The human trials and observational studies reviewed below have not demonstrated that adding preformed vitamin A to vitamin D improves immune health or bone density in non-deficient people. Human evidence also has not demonstrated that normal, RDA-range vitamin A intake meaningfully blocks vitamin D in daily life. The more defensible concern is narrower: high preformed retinol and retinyl ester intake has a mixed but plausible fracture-risk signal, while preformed vitamin A has clearer toxicity risks than vitamin D-adjacent "synergy" benefits. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667268524001219)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5615580/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16469975/))

## What the receptor crosstalk actually shows

Vitamin D receptor and retinoic acid receptor signaling can compete or interact because both use RXR-containing nuclear receptor complexes. In a live-cell receptor study, RAR and VDR co-expression with RXR produced competition for DNA binding; each receptor's own agonist increased its DNA-bound fraction, while the competing agonist reduced it. The same study found that RAR tended to dominate over VDR when no agonist or both agonists were present. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285))

Newer genomic work in differentiated human THP-1 cells found that ligand-activated RARα and VDR binding regions substantially overlapped, and that many jointly regulated genes showed cooperative regulation rather than simple one-way antagonism. That supports real pathway crosstalk, but it also shows why "vitamin A blocks vitamin D" is too simple. ([Nucleic Acids Research](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/53/6/gkaf230/8102293))

A 2025 study in primary human CD4+ T cells also found opposing immune effects under experimental conditions: active vitamin D increased IL-13 and suppressed IFN-γ, while 9-cis-retinoic acid showed opposite effects; combined treatment produced intermediate cytokine levels. The authors interpreted this as evidence that RXR binding to one receptor pathway can limit the other's activity. ([University of Copenhagen Research Portal](https://researchprofiles.ku.dk/en/publications/vitamin-dsub3subvdr-and-vitamin-a-rar-affect-il-13-and-ifn%CE%B3-secre/))

The practical point is that receptor crosstalk is not the same thing as a supplement recommendation. Cell systems can show competition, cooperation, or context-dependent effects depending on ligand dose, receptor abundance, cell type, and gene target. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285)) ([Nucleic Acids Research](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/53/6/gkaf230/8102293)) ([University of Copenhagen Research Portal](https://researchprofiles.ku.dk/en/publications/vitamin-dsub3subvdr-and-vitamin-a-rar-affect-il-13-and-ifn%CE%B3-secre/))

## Animal and acute human evidence for antagonism

Animal data support the possibility that high vitamin A exposure can antagonize vitamin D physiology. In rats, increasing retinyl acetate reduced bone ash, widened the epiphyseal plate, and eliminated vitamin D2's ability to raise serum calcium under a low-calcium, low-phosphorus diet condition. The authors concluded that retinyl acetate antagonized vitamin D action in intestine and bone in vivo. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623023544))

A small acute human study reported that a vitamin A dose comparable to one serving of liver antagonized the rapid intestinal calcium response to physiological vitamin D in nine healthy subjects. That is relevant mechanistic human evidence, but it is not a fracture trial, not a bone-density trial, and not evidence that ordinary mixed-diet vitamin A intake blocks vitamin D in daily life. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11585356/))

More recent animal work also complicates a simple antagonism story. In a 2024 swine model, vitamin D treatment increased bone mineral density, while vitamin A treatment did not meaningfully affect bone mineral density or bone mineral content. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/13/2037))

## Human RCT evidence for adding vitamin A to vitamin D

Human trials directly testing vitamin A plus vitamin D versus vitamin D alone are sparse and mostly disease-specific among the evidence reviewed here. They do not support the broad claim that vitamin A should be added to vitamin D to maximize immune or skeletal outcomes in non-deficient people. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667268524001219))

A 2 x 2 factorial randomized controlled trial in 800 adults treated for pulmonary tuberculosis compared standard therapy alone, vitamin A, vitamin D, and vitamin A plus D. Vitamin A was given at 2,000 IU/day and vitamin D at 400 IU/day during the intensive treatment phase. Neither vitamin A nor vitamin D significantly improved time to sputum smear conversion, and there was no significant vitamin A-vitamin D interaction. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c))

Post-hoc analyses from the same trial also did not show a protective effect of vitamin A or vitamin D against tuberculosis-drug-induced liver injury, and did not show glycemic-control benefits in participants with pulmonary tuberculosis and diabetes. The glycemic-control analysis reported public Chinese funding and declared no conflicts of interest. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0899900721000174)) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/adjunctive-vitamin-a-and-d-for-the-glycaemic-control-in-patients-with-concurrent-type-2-diabetes-and-tuberculosis-a-randomised-controlled-trial/E96B69BF2DBCB98D29EF524305F37F93))

A small 2025 randomized double-blind trial in pneumosepsis tested high-dose vitamin D, high-dose vitamin A, both, or standard care. The combination group showed some short-term differences in clinical and immune markers, but the trial was small, acute-care specific, and hypothesis-generating. It did not establish that vitamin A adds benefit to vitamin D for general immune prevention or long-term health. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667268524001219))

Across the randomized trials cited here, evidence has not demonstrated that adding preformed vitamin A to vitamin D supplementation improves bone mineral density, fracture risk, respiratory infection prevention, or general immune health in non-deficient adults. The fracture and bone-density evidence discussed below is observational, animal, or acute mechanistic rather than a direct vitamin D-alone versus vitamin A-plus-D skeletal-outcome trial in non-deficient adults. The absence of direct supportive evidence matters because the popular claim is not merely "A and D interact"; it is "extra A improves D's benefits," which has not been shown in the relevant human outcomes. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667268524001219)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523239404)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5615580/))

## Observational fracture evidence: mixed, and mostly about retinol

The fracture concern is mainly about preformed vitamin A, meaning retinol and retinyl esters, not beta-carotene. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/))

In the Nurses' Health Study, 72,337 postmenopausal women were followed for 18 years. Women with total vitamin A intake of at least 3,000 µg retinol equivalents/day had higher hip fracture risk than women consuming less than 1,250 µg/day. The association was stronger for retinol: intake of at least 2,000 µg/day was associated with higher hip fracture risk compared with less than 500 µg/day. Beta-carotene was not significantly associated with hip fracture risk. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/))

The Women's Health Initiative Observational Study found a different overall pattern. In 75,747 postmenopausal women, vitamin A and retinol intake were not significantly associated with hip or total fracture risk after adjustment. However, among women with lower vitamin D intake, the highest quintiles of vitamin A and retinol intake were associated with modestly higher total fracture risk. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523239404))

A Danish cohort of 2,016 perimenopausal women found no association between vitamin A intake and bone mineral density or fracture risk. The authors noted that retinol intake in that Danish population was lower than in Sweden and the United States, which may help explain differences across cohorts. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15034644/))

A cohort meta-analysis has evaluated total vitamin A, retinol, beta-carotene, blood vitamin A markers, and fracture outcomes, but the underlying evidence remains observational and heterogeneous. Read together with the mechanistic literature, the fracture literature supports caution with high retinol intake, not the stronger claim that normal vitamin A intake reliably antagonizes vitamin D in humans. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5615580/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11585356/)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285))

## Preformed retinol is not beta-carotene

Preformed vitamin A and provitamin A carotenoids should not be treated as interchangeable for risk.

Retinol and retinyl esters come from animal foods and supplements, and they can raise preformed vitamin A exposure more directly. Beta-carotene is a provitamin A carotenoid that must be converted to retinol, and fracture cohorts often separate it from retinol because the risk signal is not the same. In the Nurses' Health Study, retinol showed a stronger association with hip fracture risk, while beta-carotene did not show a significant association. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/))

This distinction matters for practical advice. Eating carotenoid-rich foods is not the same exposure as taking high-dose retinyl palmitate or retinyl acetate. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/))

## Safety: the asymmetry is real

The safety asymmetry is stronger than the synergy claim. Preformed vitamin A has a narrower usable range than most casual supplement advice implies. ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16469975/))

EFSA's 2024 scientific opinion retained an adult tolerable upper intake level for preformed vitamin A of 3,000 µg retinol equivalents/day. The opinion considered teratogenicity, hepatotoxicity, and bone-related endpoints when evaluating safety. ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))

A clinical review on vitamin A toxicity notes that chronic toxicity can occur after high intakes of preformed vitamin A over months to years, and that serum retinol is not a sensitive marker of excessive liver vitamin A stores. The same review highlights concern about preformed vitamin A exposure from diet and supplements in developed-country settings. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16469975/))

Vitamin A deficiency is still a real public-health issue in some groups. For example, analyses from India's Comprehensive National Nutrition Survey reported meaningful vitamin A deficiency prevalence among children. That supports targeted deficiency prevention and treatment; it does not, by itself, support blanket retinol co-supplementation for adults already consuming adequate vitamin A. ([Clinical Epidemiology and Global Health](https://cegh.net/article/S2213-3984%2821%2900072-5/fulltext)) ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))

## Funding and bias note

The main vitamin A plus D tuberculosis trial does not provide an obvious supplement-industry explanation for the null findings in the accessible article record, and the related glycemic-control analysis reported public funding from the National Natural Science Foundation of China and declared no conflicts of interest. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/adjunctive-vitamin-a-and-d-for-the-glycaemic-control-in-patients-with-concurrent-type-2-diabetes-and-tuberculosis-a-randomised-controlled-trial/E96B69BF2DBCB98D29EF524305F37F93))

The main conclusion here does not depend on supplement-manufacturer-funded evidence. The main limitation is not obvious industry sponsorship; it is that the most relevant human trial evidence is sparse, disease-specific, and not designed to prove broad "A plus D" benefits in replete healthy adults. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667268524001219))

## Quick reference values and findings

- RXR crosstalk: real at the molecular level; RAR and VDR can compete or cooperate depending on context. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285)) ([Nucleic Acids Research](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/53/6/gkaf230/8102293))

- Acute antagonism evidence: high preformed vitamin A exposure can antagonize vitamin D-related calcium physiology in rats and in a small acute human study. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316623023544)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11585356/))

- RCT evidence for A plus D immune benefit: limited and disease-specific; a large tuberculosis RCT found no significant improvement in sputum conversion and no significant A-D interaction. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c))

- RCT evidence for A plus D skeletal benefit: no direct vitamin D-alone versus vitamin A-plus-D skeletal-outcome RCT cited here demonstrates improved bone density or fracture outcomes in non-deficient adults. ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5615580/))

- Fracture cohorts: high preformed retinol is associated with higher hip fracture risk in some cohorts, but not in others after adjustment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523239404))

- Beta-carotene: should be analyzed separately from retinol; the fracture signal is mainly about preformed retinol. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11754708/)) ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/))

- Adult upper limit: EFSA retained 3,000 µg retinol equivalents/day as the tolerable upper intake level for preformed vitamin A. ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))


## Practical interpretation

- The claim that vitamin A and vitamin D interact is true. The claim that people should add preformed vitamin A to vitamin D for better immunity or bone density is not supported by human outcome evidence. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0021925823000285)) ([Nucleic Acids Research](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/53/6/gkaf230/8102293)) ([RSC Publishing](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2020/fo/c9fo02751c))

- The claim that vitamin A always antagonizes vitamin D is also too strong. Human observational fracture data are mixed, and mechanistic antagonism does not automatically translate into clinically meaningful harm at normal intakes. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11585356/)) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523239404)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15034644/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5615580/))

- The most defensible caution is about high preformed retinol intake, especially from supplements, liver, cod liver oil, multivitamins, or stacked formulas. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))

- People who are pregnant, trying to become pregnant, have liver disease, drink heavily, use retinoid medications, or already consume high-retinol foods should be especially cautious with preformed vitamin A. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16469975/))

- Correcting vitamin A deficiency is different from adding retinol to "optimize" vitamin D. Deficiency treatment should be status- and context-specific. ([Clinical Epidemiology and Global Health](https://cegh.net/article/S2213-3984%2821%2900072-5/fulltext)) ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/))

- For most non-deficient adults, the safer default is not to stack retinol with vitamin D unless there is a clear dietary, clinical, or laboratory reason. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))


## What would settle the question

The ideal evidence would be randomized controlled trials comparing vitamin D alone versus vitamin A plus D, stratified by baseline vitamin A and vitamin D status, using clinically meaningful endpoints such as fractures, bone mineral density, respiratory infections, vaccine responses, or autoimmune outcomes.

Those trials would also need to separate preformed retinol from beta-carotene, track retinol dose from foods and supplements, monitor liver and pregnancy-related safety risks, and test whether any benefit exists only in people who are vitamin A deficient. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminA-HealthProfessional/)) ([EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2024.8814))

Until then, the best summary is: **real molecular crosstalk, weak human evidence for synergy, mixed observational evidence for fracture harm at high retinol intakes, and a clearer safety reason to avoid casual preformed vitamin A stacking.**
