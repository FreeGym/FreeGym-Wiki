# Vitamin K2 and the Calcium Routing Hypothesis

The calcium routing hypothesis is mechanistically plausible, but the clinical claim often outruns the available randomized trial evidence. Vitamin K is required to activate proteins involved in mineral handling, including matrix Gla protein in blood vessels and osteocalcin in bone. The stronger claim that ordinary vitamin D supplementation without K2 causes arterial calcification is not established by clinical trials. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22516722/ "Vitamin K-dependent carboxylation of osteocalcin: friend or foe?"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37802169/ "Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Outcomes With Vitamin D Supplementation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

## What the claim gets right

Vitamin D's role in the claim is straightforward: vitamin D helps regulate calcium absorption from the intestine, especially through the active vitamin D metabolite 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D. That makes the "more vitamin D means more calcium absorption" part biologically plausible, although the size of the effect depends on baseline vitamin D status, calcium intake, dose, and physiology. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21664413/ "Vitamin D and intestinal calcium absorption"))

Vitamin K's role is also real. Matrix Gla protein requires vitamin K-dependent carboxylation to become fully active, and activated MGP inhibits soft-tissue and vascular calcification. Inactive dephospho-uncarboxylated MGP, usually abbreviated dp-ucMGP, is commonly used in research as a marker of poor vitamin K status in extrahepatic tissues. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"))

Osteocalcin is also vitamin K-dependent. It is produced by osteoblasts, and its vitamin K-dependent carboxylation affects its structure and relationship to bone mineral. The percentage of circulating osteocalcin that remains undercarboxylated is used as a biomarker of vitamin K status, but clinical trials do not uniformly support the idea that vitamin K supplementation broadly reduces bone loss or fracture risk in the general population. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22516722/ "Vitamin K-dependent carboxylation of osteocalcin: friend or foe?"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33030563/ "The effect of vitamin MK-7 on bone mineral density and microarchitecture in postmenopausal women with osteopenia, a 3-year randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial"))

The oversimplification is the word "directs." Vitamin K does not literally escort calcium from arteries into bones. A more precise statement is that vitamin K helps activate proteins that regulate mineralization in different tissues. That mechanism supports a hypothesis; it does not by itself prove that adding K2 to vitamin D prevents heart attacks, fractures, or vascular calcification. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22516722/ "Vitamin K-dependent carboxylation of osteocalcin: friend or foe?"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

## Observational evidence

The Rotterdam Study is one of the main sources behind the K2-cardiovascular claim. In 4,807 adults without prior myocardial infarction at baseline, higher dietary menaquinone intake was associated with lower coronary heart disease mortality, lower all-cause mortality, and lower odds of severe aortic calcification. Phylloquinone intake, or vitamin K1, was not related to those outcomes in that analysis. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15514282/ "Dietary intake of menaquinone is associated with a reduced risk of coronary heart disease: the Rotterdam Study"))

A later Danish Diet, Cancer, and Health cohort also found inverse associations between dietary vitamin K intake and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease hospitalizations. In 53,372 Danish participants followed for a median of 21 years, the highest vitamin K2 intake group had a lower risk of ASCVD hospitalization than the lowest intake group, though vitamin K1 showed a similar inverse association in that study. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34369182/ "Vitamin K Intake and Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease in the Danish Diet Cancer and Health Study"))

These observational findings are useful, but they are not proof that K2 supplements prevent calcification. Higher K2 intake can track with broader dietary patterns, food sources, health behaviors, and socioeconomic variables. The Rotterdam findings are especially hypothesis-generating because they align with the MGP mechanism, but the clinical question still requires randomized trials. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15514282/ "Dietary intake of menaquinone is associated with a reduced risk of coronary heart disease: the Rotterdam Study"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34369182/ "Vitamin K Intake and Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease in the Danish Diet Cancer and Health Study"))

## What randomized trials show for vascular outcomes

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 randomized controlled trials, including 1,533 participants, found that vitamin K supplementation significantly reduced dp-ucMGP and modestly slowed coronary artery calcium progression in pooled analysis. The coronary calcium result was small, and the authors emphasized that more rigorous RCTs are needed before translating the finding into practice. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

The same review shows why the evidence remains unsettled. In the pooled dp-ucMGP analysis, seven trials were included, and the combined result showed a statistically significant decline, but dp-ucMGP is a biomarker, not a validated clinical surrogate known to translate into fewer cardiovascular events. Eight trials measuring major arterial or valve calcification reported no appreciable benefit, and the authors described the role of vitamin K in coronary calcium as controversial. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

One frequently cited MK-7 trial randomized 244 healthy postmenopausal women to 180 µg/day MK-7 or placebo for 3 years. MK-7 reduced dp-ucMGP by about 50 percent compared with placebo and improved some measures of arterial stiffness, especially in women with higher baseline stiffness. This was not a direct vascular calcification trial; it measured non-invasive arterial stiffness rather than direct calcification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25694037/ "Menaquinone-7 supplementation improves arterial stiffness in healthy postmenopausal women"))

This trial also has a clear funding consideration. It was supported by NattoPharma, the company associated with the MK-7 ingredient used in the study. The results should not be dismissed outright, but the funding source matters when interpreting a positive surrogate-outcome supplement trial, and independent replication with hard endpoints remains limited. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25694037/ "Menaquinone-7 supplementation improves arterial stiffness in healthy postmenopausal women"), [Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

The strongest direct test of a K2-plus-D vascular calcification strategy is less supportive. In a randomized, double-blind trial of 365 older men with aortic valve calcification, 720 µg/day MK-7 plus 25 µg/day vitamin D for 24 months did not slow aortic valve calcification, coronary artery calcification, valve surgery, death, or cardiovascular events compared with placebo. It did significantly reduce dp-ucMGP. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

A related coronary calcium analysis randomized 389 men to 720 µg/day vitamin K2 plus 25 µg/day vitamin D or placebo. Among 304 participants without prior ischemic heart disease, mean coronary artery calcium progression over 2 years was not significantly reduced. A subgroup with CAC scores >=400 had lower CAC progression, but the authors described that result as hypothesis-generating because the primary endpoint was neutral. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772963X23006208 "Effects of Vitamin K2 and D Supplementation on Coronary Artery Disease in Men: A RCT"))

## What randomized trials do not show

A key gap is a large, adequately powered D versus D-plus-K2 trial using vascular calcification, myocardial infarction, stroke, fracture, and mortality endpoints. The available vascular trials mostly test K2 plus D versus placebo, or K2 versus placebo, and they often rely on biomarkers such as dp-ucMGP or surrogate measures such as arterial stiffness and calcium scores. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772963X23006208 "Effects of Vitamin K2 and D Supplementation on Coronary Artery Disease in Men: A RCT"))

That distinction matters. Lowering inactive MGP is exactly what vitamin K2 should do if the mechanism is working. But a biomarker moving in the right direction is not the same as proving fewer heart attacks, fewer fractures, or clinically meaningful regression of calcification. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

## Does vitamin D without K2 calcify arteries?

For typical vitamin D supplementation, the claim is not demonstrated. In the Women's Health Initiative coronary calcium substudy, women randomized to 1,000 mg/day calcium carbonate plus 400 IU/day vitamin D3 had similar coronary artery calcium scores to placebo after a mean of 7 years. Mean CAC was 91.6 in the calcium/D group and 100.5 in the placebo group, with no significant difference. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"))

Large vitamin D trials and meta-analyses also do not show a clear cardiovascular harm signal from vitamin D supplementation. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 36 trials with 493,389 participants found no significant difference between vitamin D and placebo for cardiovascular mortality, stroke or cerebrovascular events, myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular mortality, or arrhythmias. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37802169/ "Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Outcomes With Vitamin D Supplementation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

That does not mean high-dose vitamin D is risk-free. Vitamin D toxicity is characterized by hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria, and the risk is mainly tied to uncontrolled megadoses, vitamin D metabolites, or special conditions such as granulomatous disease, lymphoma, idiopathic infantile hypercalcemia, or use of active vitamin D analogs. ([Frontiers in Endocrinology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Vitamin D Toxicity-A Clinical Perspective"))

So the best-supported version is narrower: high-dose or poorly monitored vitamin D regimens can create calcium-related risks, especially when they produce hypercalcemia or are used in medically vulnerable contexts. The broad supplement-community warning that standard-dose vitamin D without K2 "calcifies arteries" is not supported by current RCT evidence. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37802169/ "Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Outcomes With Vitamin D Supplementation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

## Bone outcomes

The bone evidence is mixed. In the same 244-person postmenopausal women trial, 180 µg/day MK-7 for 3 years improved vitamin K status and reduced age-related decline in bone mineral content and bone mineral density at some sites, including lumbar spine and femoral neck, but not total hip. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23525894/ "Three-year low-dose menaquinone-7 supplementation helps decrease bone loss in healthy postmenopausal women"))

A separate 3-year randomized placebo-controlled trial in 142 postmenopausal women with osteopenia tested 375 µg/day MK-7 as an add-on to vitamin D3 and calcium. MK-7 strongly reduced undercarboxylated osteocalcin, showing that the supplement affected the target biomarker, but it did not improve bone mineral density, bone turnover markers, or bone microarchitecture over 3 years. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33030563/ "The effect of vitamin MK-7 on bone mineral density and microarchitecture in postmenopausal women with osteopenia, a 3-year randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial"))

A 2022 meta-analysis of RCTs in postmenopausal osteoporosis found that vitamin K2 appeared to improve lumbar spine BMD and reduce undercarboxylated osteocalcin, but the fracture evidence was uncertain. Six included studies reported fracture incidence, and the authors' conclusion about fracture reduction depended on sensitivity analyses and heterogeneous studies. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9403798/ "Efficacy of vitamin K2 in the prevention and treatment of postmenopausal osteoporosis: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis found that vitamin K2 improved bone turnover biomarkers, including osteocalcin and undercarboxylated osteocalcin, but it focused on biochemical markers rather than proving fracture reduction. The review highlighted differences in dose, co-supplementation, baseline vitamin K status, and study population as likely explanations for inconsistent clinical findings. ([Frontiers in Endocrinology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2025.1703116/full "The effect of vitamin K2 supplementation on bone turnover biochemical markers in postmenopausal osteoporosis patients: a systematic review and meta-analysis"))

## MK-4 versus MK-7

MK-4 and MK-7 should not be treated as interchangeable. MK-7 trials commonly use microgram doses, such as 180 µg/day, 375 µg/day, or 720 µg/day. The major vascular calcification trial used 720 µg/day MK-7 plus 25 µg/day vitamin D, while several bone trials used 180 to 375 µg/day MK-7. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23525894/ "Three-year low-dose menaquinone-7 supplementation helps decrease bone loss in healthy postmenopausal women"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33030563/ "The effect of vitamin MK-7 on bone mineral density and microarchitecture in postmenopausal women with osteopenia, a 3-year randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial"))

MK-4 studies often use milligram doses. In a dose-response study of postmenopausal women with osteoporotic fractures, 0.5 mg/day MK-4 reduced undercarboxylated osteocalcin, 5 mg/day reduced it further, and 45 mg/day did not add further benefit over 5 mg/day for that biomarker. This was a small, open-label, short-term study focused on osteocalcin carboxylation, not fracture prevention. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30816822/ "Maximal dose-response of vitamin-K2 (menaquinone-4) on undercarboxylated osteocalcin in women with osteoporosis"))

The practical implication is that "K2 works" is too broad. Effects may differ by vitamer, dose, population, baseline vitamin K status, kidney function, calcium intake, vitamin D intake, and endpoint. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"), [Frontiers in Endocrinology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2025.1703116/full "The effect of vitamin K2 supplementation on bone turnover biochemical markers in postmenopausal osteoporosis patients: a systematic review and meta-analysis"))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

The MK-7 arterial stiffness trial in healthy postmenopausal women was supported by NattoPharma, and that should be stated whenever the trial is used to support supplement claims. It is a positive RCT, but it used surrogate outcomes and did not directly measure vascular calcification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25694037/ "Menaquinone-7 supplementation improves arterial stiffness in healthy postmenopausal women"))

The 2023 vascular calcification meta-analysis declared no commercial or financial conflicts, but it pooled heterogeneous trials across different vitamin K forms, doses, populations, and endpoints. Its own conclusion still calls for more RCTs before clinical translation. ([Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

The most clinically relevant K2-plus-D calcification trial found a strong biomarker effect but no significant reduction in aortic valve or coronary artery calcification. That is an important counterweight to smaller positive surrogate-outcome studies. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

- MK-7 arterial stiffness trial: 180 µg/day for 3 years in 244 healthy postmenopausal women; improved dp-ucMGP and some arterial stiffness measures; funded by NattoPharma; no direct calcification measurement. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25694037/ "Menaquinone-7 supplementation improves arterial stiffness in healthy postmenopausal women"))

- MK-7 plus vitamin D aortic valve calcification trial: 720 µg/day MK-7 plus 25 µg/day vitamin D for 24 months in 365 older men; reduced dp-ucMGP but did not slow aortic valve calcification, coronary calcification, surgery, death, or cardiovascular events. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2772963X23006208 "Effects of Vitamin K2 and D Supplementation on Coronary Artery Disease in Men: A RCT"))

- MK-7 bone trial with calcium and D: 375 µg/day MK-7 for 3 years in 142 postmenopausal women with osteopenia; improved osteocalcin carboxylation but not BMD or microarchitecture. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33030563/ "The effect of vitamin MK-7 on bone mineral density and microarchitecture in postmenopausal women with osteopenia, a 3-year randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial"))

- WHI calcium plus vitamin D coronary calcium substudy: 1,000 mg/day calcium carbonate plus 400 IU/day vitamin D3 for about 7 years; no significant difference in CAC versus placebo. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"))


## Practical interpretation

The mechanistic foundation is real: vitamin K activates MGP and osteocalcin, and those proteins are relevant to vascular and skeletal mineral biology. The clinical extrapolation is the weak point. The evidence supports "K2 improves vitamin K-dependent biomarkers" more strongly than "K2 prevents arterial calcification or fractures." ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22516722/ "Vitamin K-dependent carboxylation of osteocalcin: friend or foe?"), [Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"))

The phrase "K2 directs calcium to bones instead of arteries" is acceptable as a loose metaphor, but it is not a precise clinical claim. A better phrasing is: vitamin K supports the activation of proteins that regulate mineralization in bone and soft tissues. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22516722/ "Vitamin K-dependent carboxylation of osteocalcin: friend or foe?"))

For ordinary vitamin D maintenance dosing, current RCT evidence does not show that vitamin D without K2 causes vascular calcification. The concern becomes more plausible with high-dose, poorly monitored vitamin D, hypercalcemia, active vitamin D analogs, kidney disease, granulomatous disease, or other conditions that disturb calcium-phosphate metabolism. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37802169/ "Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Outcomes With Vitamin D Supplementation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"), [Frontiers in Endocrinology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Vitamin D Toxicity-A Clinical Perspective"))

People using warfarin or other vitamin K antagonists should not add K2 casually. Vitamin K can have a serious interaction with warfarin and similar anticoagulants, and sudden changes in vitamin K intake from food or supplements can alter the anticoagulant effect. Supplement changes in that setting should be clinician-supervised. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminK-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin K - Health Professional Fact Sheet"))

## Bottom line

Vitamin K2 has a plausible mechanism and consistent effects on vitamin K-dependent biomarkers. Observational studies link higher K2 intake with lower vascular calcification and cardiovascular risk, but RCTs have not confirmed a clear reduction in hard cardiovascular outcomes. The strongest K2-plus-D calcification trial lowered inactive MGP but did not slow aortic valve or coronary calcification. ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6510326/ "Vitamin K-Dependent Matrix Gla Protein as Multifaceted Protector of Vascular and Tissue Integrity"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15514282/ "Dietary intake of menaquinone is associated with a reduced risk of coronary heart disease: the Rotterdam Study"), [Frontiers in Nutrition](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1115069/full "Vitamin K supplementation and vascular calcification: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))

The claim that "vitamin D without K2 calcifies arteries" is too strong for typical supplementation. A cautious, evidence-based version would be: vitamin K2 may be worth studying as a modifier of calcification risk, especially in people with poor vitamin K status or high calcification burden, but adding K2 to vitamin D has not yet been proven to prevent cardiovascular events, fractures, or clinically meaningful vascular calcification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20551849/ "Calcium/vitamin D supplementation and coronary artery calcification in the Women's Health Initiative"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37802169/ "Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Outcomes With Vitamin D Supplementation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35465686/ "Vitamin K2 and D in Patients With Aortic Valve Calcification: A Randomized Double-Blinded Clinical Trial"))
