# Blind Supplementation: The Unintended Consequence

The strongest version of the argument is not that every healthy adult needs routine vitamin D testing. It is that discouraging routine testing should not be interpreted as permission for long-term, unmeasured supplementation at doses far above ordinary replacement.

## What the testing guidelines actually say

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline suggests against routine 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] testing in the general population because clinical trials have not established outcome-specific blood-level thresholds for disease prevention. The same guideline also suggests against empiric vitamin D supplementation above current dietary reference intakes for healthy adults under 75 years old. It does not apply to people with established indications for vitamin D treatment or testing. ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"))

The guideline explicitly notes that testing may be clinically appropriate in special populations expected to need more than ordinary intake, including people with malabsorption, certain medications that increase vitamin D catabolism, or increased renal losses such as nephrotic syndrome. ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"))

A JAMA systematic review on screening found no studies directly evaluating the health benefits or harms of vitamin D screening itself. Among asymptomatic community-dwelling adults with low vitamin D levels, treatment was not significantly associated with mortality, fractures, falls, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, depression, or adverse events; kidney stone rates were similar between treatment and control groups. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force | Complementary and Alternative Medicine | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

So the guideline claim is narrow: routine population screening has not shown clinical benefit. That does not prove that long-term, high-dose, unmeasured supplementation is the safest default.

## Supplement use is rising

NHANES data show that daily supplemental vitamin D intake of at least 1,000 IU rose from 0.3 percent in 1999-2000 to 18.2 percent in 2013-2014. Daily intake of at least 4,000 IU rose from 0.2 percent in 2007-2008 to 3.2 percent in 2013-2014. The same JAMA research letter notes that 4,000 IU/day is the adult tolerable upper limit. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494 "Trends in Use of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplements Exceeding 1000 or 4000 International Units Daily, 1999-2014 | Nutrition | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

More recent NHANES data through August 2021-August 2023 show the other side of that trend: the proportion of the US population with 25(OH)D above 125 nmol/L, or above 50 ng/mL, increased from 2.2 percent in 2007-2008 to 7.6 percent in 2021-2023. Among people reporting one vitamin D-containing supplement, it increased from 2.0 percent to 11.4 percent; among people reporting two or more vitamin D-containing supplements, it increased from 4.7 percent to 30.6 percent. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002916525005994 "Trends in vitamin D status in the United States, 2007-2023: a cross-sectional analysis of data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey - ScienceDirect"))

A separate NHANES analysis found that mean 25(OH)D rose modestly between 2007-2010 and 2011-2014, but after adjustment for supplement dose, the increase was no longer statistically significant. Users of vitamin D-containing supplements at 1,000 IU/day or more had 25(OH)D concentrations about 31 nmol/L higher than nonusers. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316622002693 "Supplemental Vitamin D Increased Serum Total 25-Hydroxyvitamin D in the US Adult Population During 2007-2014"))

## High 25(OH)D is not the same as toxicity

A population-based Mayo Clinic Proceedings study found that 25(OH)D values above 50 ng/mL rose sharply in Olmsted County, Minnesota, from 2002 to 2011, but without a corresponding rise in acute clinical toxicity. In that dataset, 8.4 percent of measured people had values above 50 ng/mL, 0.6 percent had values at or above 80 ng/mL, and 0.2 percent had values at or above 100 ng/mL; only one clinical toxicity case was identified, at 364 ng/mL. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0025619615001858 "Changing Incidence of Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Values Above 50 ng/mL: A 10-Year Population-Based Study - ScienceDirect"))

That matters because values above 50 ng/mL should not be treated as poisoning. They are better interpreted as a possible overshoot signal, especially when they occur in the context of supplement use. The newer NHANES paper labels values above 125 nmol/L, or 50 ng/mL, as "may be of concern," not as toxicity. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002916525005994 "Trends in vitamin D status in the United States, 2007-2023: a cross-sectional analysis of data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey - ScienceDirect"))

## Standard doses and high-dose regimens are different risk categories

For many healthy adults, common supplement doses such as 1,000 to 2,000 IU/day appear to be low risk, with direct trial safety data at 2,000 IU/day. The VITAL trial used vitamin D3 at 2,000 IU/day and reported no significant treatment-associated increases in hypercalcemia, kidney stones, or gastrointestinal symptoms. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7089819/ "Principal Results of the VITamin D and OmegA-3 TriaL (VITAL)"))

Low risk does not mean clearly beneficial. The same screening evidence base found little evidence that treating asymptomatic community-dwelling adults with low vitamin D improves major outcomes such as mortality, fractures, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, or depression. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force | Complementary and Alternative Medicine | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

Higher doses are a different question. In a randomized clinical trial of healthy adults assigned to 400, 4,000, or 10,000 IU/day for three years, the 4,000 and 10,000 IU/day groups did not show improved bone strength compared with 400 IU/day, and higher-dose treatment was associated with lower radial bone mineral density; tibial bone mineral density was lower in the 10,000 IU/day group. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6714464/ "Effect of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation on Volumetric Bone Density and Bone Strength: A Randomized Clinical Trial"))

A safety analysis of the same high-dose trial reported broadly similar safety profiles across 400, 4,000, and 10,000 IU/day, but hypercalciuria was common and occurred more often at higher doses. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31746327/ "Safety of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation"))

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 48 randomized trials with 19,833 participants found that long-term vitamin D supplementation increased the risk of hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria, though it did not increase kidney stone risk. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522046299 "Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and kidney stones in long-term studies of vitamin D supplementation: a systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"))

Intermittent megadoses are also not interchangeable with daily replacement. In a randomized trial of 2,256 women aged 70 or older, annual oral vitamin D at 500,000 IU increased falls and fractures compared with placebo. Consistent with that concern, the 2024 Endocrine Society guideline suggests daily lower-dose vitamin D rather than intermittent high-dose vitamin D for nonpregnant adults over 50 when vitamin D supplementation is used. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/185854 "Annual High-Dose Oral Vitamin D and Falls and Fractures in Older Women: A Randomized Controlled Trial | Geriatrics | JAMA | JAMA Network")) ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"))

## Toxicity is rare but real

Vitamin D toxicity is usually driven by excessive intake, not sunlight or normal diet. A clinical review describes vitamin D toxicity as severe hypercalcemia related to excessive long-term intake, disordered vitamin D metabolism, or diseases that increase active vitamin D production. It identifies 25(OH)D above 150 ng/mL, or 375 nmol/L, as the hallmark of toxicity from vitamin D overdosing. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Frontiers | Vitamin D Toxicity - A Clinical Perspective"))

The same review lists common manifestations of toxicity as hypercalcemia-related symptoms, including confusion, apathy, recurrent vomiting, abdominal pain, polyuria, polydipsia, dehydration, nephrocalcinosis, and renal failure. It also states that high-dose regimens above upper limits require regular monitoring. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Frontiers | Vitamin D Toxicity - A Clinical Perspective"))

Case-series evidence supports that toxicity is uncommon but clinically meaningful. One adult case series reported 10 cases of hypercalcemia due to vitamin D intoxication, with vomiting, polyuria, polydipsia, encephalopathy, and renal dysfunction; the authors reported no conflict of interest and no funding. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3191699/ "Vitamin D Toxicity in Adults: A Case Series from an Area with Endemic Hypovitaminosis D"))

A 2022 cross-sectional study from Pakistan reviewed 105,398 people tested for 25(OH)D and found that 0.34 percent had values above 150 ng/mL. After exclusions, 186 cases were analyzed, and all were using vitamin D supplements; the authors concluded that supplementation was a leading cause of potentially toxic 25(OH)D levels and that careful use plus monitoring could prevent cases. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2049080121011778 "Practices of vitamin D supplementation leading to vitamin D toxicity: Experience from a Low-Middle Income Country - ScienceDirect"))

## Behavioral evidence is thinner

There is little direct evidence comparing "testing access" versus "no testing access" as a behavior-change intervention for supplement users. The JAMA screening review found no studies directly evaluating the benefits or harms of screening itself, so claims that testing reliably prevents toxicity are not strongly proven. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force | Complementary and Alternative Medicine | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

The best available behavioral evidence is indirect. In a study of direct-to-public dried blood spot vitamin D testing users, 372 users had 25(OH)D above 220 nmol/L; 361 were taking supplements, at reported doses from 1,000 to 120,000 IU/day, and two reported bolus doses of 300,000 and 900,000 IU. Only 6.4 percent of supplement users were medically supervised, and the internet was the main supplement source for 74 percent. ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0004563216662073 "Self-administration of vitamin D supplements in the general public may be associated with high 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentrations - Robyn L Shea, Jonathan D Berg, 2017"))

That study cannot estimate population prevalence because the users were self-selected people who had chosen direct-access testing. It does show that self-directed supplementation can reach very high doses and that testing may function as a detection point for people who otherwise might not be medically supervised. ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0004563216662073 "Self-administration of vitamin D supplements in the general public may be associated with high 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentrations - Robyn L Shea, Jonathan D Berg, 2017"))

## Harm-reduction interpretation

A "do not routinely screen everyone" policy is evidence-consistent. A "do not measure even when people are taking high doses for long periods" norm is not the same policy.

For a healthy adult taking ordinary doses below the adult upper limit, especially 1,000 to 2,000 IU/day, routine 25(OH)D testing is unlikely to add much value if it does not change management. This is consistent with the absence of direct screening benefit, the general safety of 2,000 IU/day in VITAL, and the guideline's recommendation against routine testing in generally healthy adults without established indications. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force | Complementary and Alternative Medicine | JAMA | JAMA Network")) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7089819/ "Principal Results of the VITamin D and OmegA-3 TriaL (VITAL)")) ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"))

For people taking 4,000 IU/day or more, 5,000 to 10,000 IU/day, repeated or prolonged 50,000 IU doses, injectable preparations, or intermittent boluses, monitoring becomes a different risk-management question. These regimens sit near or above the adult upper limit, can be associated with higher achieved 25(OH)D, and have evidence of calcium-related adverse effects or bone/fall concerns in some trials and toxicity reports. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494 "Trends in Use of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplements Exceeding 1000 or 4000 International Units Daily, 1999-2014 | Nutrition | JAMA | JAMA Network")) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316622002693 "Supplemental Vitamin D Increased Serum Total 25-Hydroxyvitamin D in the US Adult Population During 2007-2014")) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6714464/ "Effect of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation on Volumetric Bone Density and Bone Strength: A Randomized Clinical Trial")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31746327/ "Safety of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation")) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522046299 "Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and kidney stones in long-term studies of vitamin D supplementation: a systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect")) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/185854 "Annual High-Dose Oral Vitamin D and Falls and Fractures in Older Women: A Randomized Controlled Trial | Geriatrics | JAMA | JAMA Network")) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2049080121011778 "Practices of vitamin D supplementation leading to vitamin D toxicity: Experience from a Low-Middle Income Country - ScienceDirect"))

The most practical monitoring package for higher-risk supplementation is not just 25(OH)D. Because toxicity is mediated mainly through calcium dysregulation, serum calcium and kidney function are important, and urinary calcium may matter when hypercalciuria or kidney-stone risk is a concern. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Frontiers | Vitamin D Toxicity - A Clinical Perspective")) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522046299 "Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and kidney stones in long-term studies of vitamin D supplementation: a systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"))

## Quick reference values

- **1,000 to 2,000 IU/day:** commonly used, generally low risk in healthy adults, below the adult upper limit, and supported by direct trial safety data at 2,000 IU/day in VITAL. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494 "Trends in Use of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplements Exceeding 1000 or 4000 International Units Daily, 1999-2014 | Nutrition | JAMA | JAMA Network")) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7089819/ "Principal Results of the VITamin D and OmegA-3 TriaL (VITAL)"))

- **4,000 IU/day:** adult tolerable upper limit, not a recommended target; about 3.2 percent of US adults reported daily intake at or above this level in NHANES 2013-2014. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494 "Trends in Use of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplements Exceeding 1000 or 4000 International Units Daily, 1999-2014 | Nutrition | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

- **5,000 to 10,000 IU/day:** not automatically toxic, but no longer ordinary maintenance dosing; RCT evidence does not show bone-strength benefit and raises concern about hypercalciuria and bone density at higher doses. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6714464/ "Effect of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation on Volumetric Bone Density and Bone Strength: A Randomized Clinical Trial")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31746327/ "Safety of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplementation"))

- **25(OH)D >50 ng/mL:** not toxicity, but a "may be of concern" category in recent NHANES analysis and a useful overshoot signal when driven by supplements. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0002916525005994 "Trends in vitamin D status in the United States, 2007-2023: a cross-sectional analysis of data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey - ScienceDirect"))

- **25(OH)D >150 ng/mL:** hallmark biochemical level for vitamin D toxicity from overdosing, especially when accompanied by hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Frontiers | Vitamin D Toxicity - A Clinical Perspective"))

- **Annual/intermittent megadoses:** not equivalent to daily replacement; annual 500,000 IU increased falls and fractures in older women. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/185854 "Annual High-Dose Oral Vitamin D and Falls and Fractures in Older Women: A Randomized Controlled Trial | Geriatrics | JAMA | JAMA Network"))


## Practical interpretation

Routine vitamin D testing for all healthy adults is not supported by direct trial evidence. The better conclusion is targeted restraint: do not turn 25(OH)D into a default wellness screen, but do not let high-dose supplementation become blind either. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force | Complementary and Alternative Medicine | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

The cleanest harm-reduction position is dose- and risk-based. Low-dose supplementation can often proceed without testing in low-risk adults, but long-term high-dose supplementation, stacked supplement products, bolus regimens, symptoms of hypercalcemia, kidney disease, malabsorption, relevant medication use, granulomatous disease, lymphoma, hyperparathyroidism, or prior kidney stones are all situations where measurement is more defensible. ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00550/full "Frontiers | Vitamin D Toxicity - A Clinical Perspective")) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916522046299 "Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and kidney stones in long-term studies of vitamin D supplementation: a systematic review and meta-analysis - ScienceDirect"))

The major population-trend papers cited here were not supplement-company efficacy trials. Rooney et al. was supported by NIH/NHLBI and the Office of Dietary Supplements; one author reported personal fees from Siemens Healthcare Diagnostics, and funders had no role in the study. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494 "Trends in Use of High-Dose Vitamin D Supplements Exceeding 1000 or 4000 International Units Daily, 1999-2014 | Nutrition | JAMA | JAMA Network"))

Schleicher et al. reported support for data collection and laboratory analyses from the Office of Dietary Supplements, NIH, and reported no author conflicts of interest. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022316622002693 "Supplemental Vitamin D Increased Serum Total 25-Hydroxyvitamin D in the US Adult Population During 2007-2014"))

The Endocrine Society guideline was funded by the Endocrine Society, with no other financial support; 7 percent of panel members had relevant or potentially relevant conflicts of interest. ([Endocrine Society / JCEM](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"))

The Mayo Clinic Proceedings population study was supported by the Rochester Epidemiology Project, and one author was a consultant for Biomedical Systems. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0025619615001858 "Changing Incidence of Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Values Above 50 ng/mL: A 10-Year Population-Based Study - ScienceDirect"))

Shea and Berg should be interpreted with special caution because both authors ran a direct-to-public vitamin D testing service. Their study is useful for describing high-dose self-supplementation among selected testing users, not for estimating population prevalence. ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0004563216662073 "Self-administration of vitamin D supplements in the general public may be associated with high 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentrations - Robyn L Shea, Jonathan D Berg, 2017"))
