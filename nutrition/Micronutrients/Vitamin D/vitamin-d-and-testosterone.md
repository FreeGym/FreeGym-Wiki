# Vitamin D and Testosterone: Real Signal or Wishful Thinking?

The vitamin D-testosterone link is biologically plausible, observationally recurring, and easy to overstate. Low 25-hydroxyvitamin D often tracks with lower testosterone, but randomized supplementation trials do not consistently show that vitamin D raises testosterone, especially in healthy or already eugonadal men. The best current read is narrower: correcting deficiency is reasonable when clinically indicated, and vitamin D status may interact with testosterone biology in some deficient, metabolically unhealthy, or infertile subgroups, but vitamin D should not be framed as a dependable testosterone-raising therapy, even in men selected for low testosterone. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/), [2019 clinical-trial meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31332821/), [2024 meta-analytic review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11506788/), [Lerchbaum et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/))

## Why the claim sounds plausible

Vitamin D is not just a bone-health nutrient; [vitamin D receptor expression and vitamin D-metabolizing enzymes](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20172873/) have been demonstrated in human male reproductive tissues, including the testis and sperm cells.

A later mechanistic study found that human testicular tissue exposed to active vitamin D released more testosterone ex vivo, and the same paper reported associations between vitamin D status and markers related to Leydig-cell function. This supports biological plausibility, but it does not prove that oral vitamin D supplementation meaningfully raises serum testosterone in real-world men. ([Holt et al. 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31953167/))

## What the observational evidence shows

Observational studies generally report a positive association between 25(OH)D and testosterone, but this association is vulnerable to confounding. Men with higher vitamin D status may differ in adiposity, metabolic health, physical activity, and other lifestyle or body-composition variables, all of which can complicate causal interpretation. ([D'Andrea et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32880851/), [Damas-Fuentes et al. 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36120442/), [Książek et al. 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-99571-8))

A study in young men with obesity found that vitamin D-deficient men had lower total and free testosterone, but the association weakened after adjustment, with BMI acting as an important mediator and with a clearer signal mainly in morbid obesity. ([Damas-Fuentes et al. 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36120442/))

A study in healthy, active young men found no meaningful relationship between vitamin D status and total testosterone, free testosterone, bioavailable testosterone, LH, FSH, or SHBG after adjustment for lifestyle and body-composition variables. ([Książek et al. 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-99571-8))

## The early positive trial

The most cited positive trial is [Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/). It was a one-year randomized trial in overweight men participating in a weight-reduction program, with baseline 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L. Men received 3,332 IU/day of vitamin D or placebo.

In the vitamin D group, 25(OH)D rose substantially, and total testosterone increased from about 10.7 to 13.4 nmol/L, with increases also reported for bioactive and free testosterone. The placebo group did not show a significant testosterone change. ([Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/))

The limitation is that this was a small male subgroup, testosterone was not the original primary endpoint, and the population was not "healthy men optimizing testosterone." It was overweight men with vitamin D insufficiency in a weight-loss context. ([Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/))

## The replication problem

[Lerchbaum et al. 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28938446/) tested 20,000 IU/week of vitamin D for 12 weeks in healthy men with normal baseline testosterone and 25(OH)D below 75 nmol/L. Vitamin D increased 25(OH)D, but it did not significantly increase total testosterone.

That trial is important because it directly challenges the common "vitamin D raises testosterone" interpretation. Its participants were much more testosterone-replete at baseline than the participants in [Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/), and the authors noted that they could not exclude effects in men with hypogonadism or more severe vitamin D deficiency. ([Lerchbaum et al. 2017](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/102/11/4292/4096785))

[Lerchbaum et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/) then tested vitamin D in men with low total testosterone and 25(OH)D below 75 nmol/L. Weekly vitamin D for 12 weeks again produced no significant treatment effect on total testosterone, free testosterone, bioavailable testosterone, SHBG, LH, or FSH, including in the subgroup with baseline 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L.

Other trials also lean null. [Jorde et al. 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23686706/) found no testosterone increase after high-dose vitamin D supplementation in men without marked vitamin D deficiency, and the [EVITA trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29546692/) in men with advanced heart failure and low 25(OH)D found that long-term vitamin D did not prevent testosterone decline.

## What the meta-analyses say

A [2019 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31332821/) found no significant effect of vitamin D supplementation on total testosterone or SHBG. Subgroup analyses by intervention duration, vitamin D type, baseline vitamin D status, and baseline testosterone also did not show a clear testosterone-raising effect.

A newer [2024 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11506788/) found a small increase in total testosterone with vitamin D supplementation, but no significant effect on free testosterone, LH, FSH, SHBG, estradiol, or free androgen index. The authors also reported heterogeneity and risk-of-bias concerns, so the result is better read as a weak signal than a settled clinical effect.

A [2026 systematic review/meta-analysis focused on infertile men](https://peerj.com/articles/21002/) reported small improvements in semen parameters and serum testosterone, but did not find significant effects on clinical pregnancy or live birth. That evidence is relevant to infertile men, not to healthy replete men using vitamin D as a testosterone enhancer.

## Deficiency, BMI, and the "correcting a deficit" hypothesis

The most defensible hypothesis is not that vitamin D is a general testosterone booster. It is that correcting clinically relevant vitamin D deficiency might support testosterone biology in specific men whose low vitamin D is part of a broader metabolic or reproductive problem. ([Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/), [Damas-Fuentes et al. 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36120442/), [Holt et al. 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37555466/))

The strongest positive RCT signal came from overweight vitamin D-insufficient men, while null trials in healthier, less-deficient, or low-testosterone populations failed to reproduce a broad testosterone-raising effect. ([Pilz et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/), [Lerchbaum et al. 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28938446/), [Lerchbaum et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/))

A [2024 randomized trial in infertile men](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37555466/) found no overall effect of vitamin D plus calcium on serum sex steroids, LH, testosterone/LH ratio, or SHBG, but a predefined subgroup with 25(OH)D at or below 50 nmol/L showed a higher testosterone/LH ratio. That supports a possible deficiency-specific effect, but it is still subgroup evidence and included calcium co-supplementation.

## Funding and conflict-of-interest notes

The major vitamin D/testosterone trials and reviews cited here should still be read with funding and material-support disclosures in mind. The [2017 Graz trial](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/102/11/4292/4096785) disclosed support from the Austrian National Bank and study-medication/placebo delivery by Fresenius Kabi.

The [2019 Graz low-testosterone trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/) used Oleovit D3 and placebo delivered by Fresenius Kabi. That is not the same as a proprietary testosterone-boosting supplement sponsor, but it is still a relevant material support disclosure.

The [2024 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11506788/) reported no external funding, article-processing support from Alfaisal University, and no declared conflicts of interest.

The [2024 infertile-men RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37555466/) reported institutional and foundation funding rather than a vitamin D manufacturer as the main sponsor.

## Quick reference values and findings

- [Pilz 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21154195/): 3,332 IU/day vitamin D for one year in overweight men with 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L increased total, bioactive, and free testosterone versus placebo, but the testosterone analysis came from a small, specific subgroup.

- [Lerchbaum 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28938446/): 20,000 IU/week for 12 weeks in healthy eugonadal men increased vitamin D status but did not significantly increase total testosterone.

- [Lerchbaum 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/): 20,000 IU/week for 12 weeks in men with low total testosterone did not significantly affect total testosterone, free testosterone, bioavailable testosterone, SHBG, LH, or FSH.

- [2019 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31332821/): randomized trials showed no significant effect on total testosterone or SHBG.

- [2024 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11506788/): vitamin D showed a small total-testosterone increase, but no consistent effect on free testosterone or other reproductive hormones.

- [2026 infertile-men meta-analysis](https://peerj.com/articles/21002/): small improvements were reported for testosterone and semen parameters, but not for clinical pregnancy or live birth.


## Practical interpretation

- The vitamin D-testosterone association is real enough to study, but not clean enough to treat as causal from observational data alone. ([D'Andrea et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32880851/))

- Correcting vitamin D deficiency is reasonable for general health when clinically indicated, but the promise should be "correct deficiency," not "optimize testosterone." ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/), [2019 clinical-trial meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31332821/))

- In healthy men with normal testosterone, vitamin D supplementation has not shown reliable testosterone-raising effects. ([Lerchbaum et al. 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28938446/))

- In deficient men with obesity or infertility, there may be a modest signal, but it remains subgroup-dependent and not strong enough to frame vitamin D as a dependable testosterone therapy. ([Damas-Fuentes et al. 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36120442/), [Holt et al. 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37555466/), [2026 infertile-men meta-analysis](https://peerj.com/articles/21002/))

- In men selected for low testosterone, the best direct RCT evidence did not show a testosterone increase. ([Lerchbaum et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/))

- The cleanest future trial would enroll men with confirmed low testosterone, true vitamin D deficiency, BMI stratification, adequate duration, LC-MS/MS testosterone measurement, free testosterone, LH, SHBG, symptoms, body composition outcomes, and independent funding. ([Lerchbaum et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6842386/))
