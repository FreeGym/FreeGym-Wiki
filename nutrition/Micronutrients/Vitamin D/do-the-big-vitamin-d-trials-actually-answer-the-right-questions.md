# Do the Big Vitamin D Trials Actually Answer the Right Question?

The large vitamin D trials answer an important question, but a narrower one than they are often used to answer.

They show that routine vitamin D supplementation in mostly vitamin D-replete adults does not clearly prevent major chronic diseases such as cancer, cardiovascular disease, or type 2 diabetes over the trial follow-up periods. They do not fully answer whether identifying and correcting deficiency changes chronic-disease risk, especially in people with low baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D, severe deficiency, high physiologic need, or shorter-term functional outcomes. This caveat does not question the established need to prevent and treat severe vitamin D deficiency for bone-mineralization disorders such as rickets and osteomalacia. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1809944); [Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/); [Bouillon et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-021-00593-z))

Throughout, “deficient” should be read cautiously because thresholds vary by guideline and outcome. The Food and Nutrition Board framework treats levels below 12 ng/mL as increasing deficiency risk and levels of 20 ng/mL or more as sufficient for most people, but the optimal 25-hydroxyvitamin D level for disease prevention remains uncertain. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/); [Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease))

## The question VITAL and D2d mostly answered

VITAL tested vitamin D3 at 2000 IU/day in generally healthy middle-aged and older adults, with primary endpoints of invasive cancer and major cardiovascular events. The main trial found no significant reduction in either primary endpoint. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1809944))

D2d tested vitamin D3 at 4000 IU/day in adults with prediabetes, with new-onset type 2 diabetes as the primary endpoint. The main trial did not show a statistically significant reduction in diabetes incidence in the overall randomized comparison. ([Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906); [UT Southwestern record](https://utsouthwestern.elsevierpure.com/en/publications/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-prevention-of-type-2-diabetes))

These trials therefore support a relatively specific conclusion: adding vitamin D to broad populations that are not selected for deficiency does not reliably prevent these chronic disease endpoints over roughly 2.5 to 5.3 years. They are weaker evidence for the different question of whether correcting low vitamin D status in deficient people matters. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1809944); [Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906))

## VITAL was not a deficiency-enriched trial

VITAL’s baseline vitamin D status was generally adequate by common clinical cut-points. In the blood-sample subset, mean baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D was 30.8 ng/mL, and only 12.7 percent were below 20 ng/mL. Another 32.2 percent were between 20 and less than 30 ng/mL. ([Luttmann-Gibson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6875603/))

That matters because a nutrient trial in mostly replete participants is closer to a “more is better?” test than a “does correcting deficiency help?” test. If baseline intake or blood level is already sufficient for the relevant biology, the expected marginal benefit of supplementation is likely to be small, even if deficiency correction would be beneficial in a different population. ([Bouillon et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-021-00593-z); [Luttmann-Gibson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6875603/))

VITAL also used chronic disease endpoints. Cancer and cardiovascular events are plausible public-health endpoints, but they may not be the most sensitive endpoints for detecting the effects of correcting deficiency. A five-year RCT can test medium-term event reduction in the enrolled population; it cannot establish what lifelong prevention or early correction of deficiency would have done. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1809944))

## D2d was also mostly not deficiency-enriched

D2d enrolled adults with prediabetes, not adults selected for vitamin D deficiency. Mean baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D was about 28.0 ng/mL, and 78.3 percent of participants had baseline levels at or above 20 ng/mL. ([Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906); [D2d Study PDF](https://d2dstudy.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/2019-Pittas-NEJM-Vitamin-D-supplementation-and-prevention-of-type-2-diabetes.pdf))

During the trial, vitamin D supplementation substantially raised 25-hydroxyvitamin D, but the primary intention-to-treat comparison still did not reach statistical significance for diabetes prevention. The main result therefore argues against a large average diabetes-prevention effect in the enrolled prediabetes population, not against every possible benefit in deficient subgroups. ([Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906); [UT Southwestern record](https://utsouthwestern.elsevierpure.com/en/publications/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-prevention-of-type-2-diabetes))

Later pooled analyses complicate the simple “vitamin D does nothing” interpretation. An individual participant data meta-analysis of vitamin D trials in prediabetes estimated a 15 percent lower diabetes risk with vitamin D overall, and a larger effect among the smaller subgroup with very low baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D below 30 nmol/L, or about 12 ng/mL. ([Pittas et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.7326/M22-3018); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36745886/))

## Subgroup signals are useful, but not confirmatory

The strongest interpretation is not that VITAL and D2d “prove deficiency correction works.” It is that their null main results should not be stretched to rule out deficiency correction, because both trials included relatively few truly deficient participants. ([Luttmann-Gibson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6875603/); [Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906))

In VITAL, signals varied by subgroup. African American participants assigned to vitamin D had a suggestive reduction in total cancer incidence, but the interaction by race was not conventionally significant. That makes the finding hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. ([VITAL overview](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8299924/))

VITAL’s advanced cancer analysis found that vitamin D reduced metastatic or fatal cancer overall, with the strongest signal among participants with normal BMI. However, baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D did not clearly modify the advanced cancer result, including when comparing participants below versus above 20 ng/mL or below versus above the median. ([Chandler et al., 2020](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2773074))

That distinction is important. VITAL contains some subgroup signals, but they do not cleanly show that lower baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D was the key effect modifier for cancer or cardiovascular outcomes. ([Chandler et al., 2020](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2773074))

## Body size may be more informative than baseline level in VITAL

A later VITAL biomarker analysis found that baseline vitamin D biomarkers were lower at higher BMI, and that the biomarker response to supplementation was smaller in participants with higher BMI. The same paper noted that several VITAL secondary analyses showed stronger apparent benefits among normal-weight participants than among participants with overweight or obesity. ([Tobias et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2800490))

This does not prove that vitamin D prevents cancer, autoimmune disease, or other outcomes only in leaner people. It does suggest that the same oral dose does not produce the same achieved vitamin D biology in everyone. A fixed-dose trial may therefore obscure heterogeneity by body size, bioavailability, distribution, sequestration, metabolism, adherence, and baseline status. ([Tobias et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2800490))

## D2d reanalyses point toward achieved level, but remain exploratory

D2d follow-up analyses found that diabetes risk was lower among some participants who achieved higher intratrial 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels, including levels around or above 40 ng/mL. In one analysis, Black and White participants with intratrial mean 25-hydroxyvitamin D of at least 40 ng/mL had lower diabetes risk, with no statistically significant interaction by race or BMI. ([Chatterjee et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523463046))

A 2026 D2d genetic reanalysis reported that vitamin D receptor genotype may modify the relationship between supplementation, achieved 25-hydroxyvitamin D, and diabetes risk. The authors framed these findings as genetic-association evidence needing confirmation. ([Dawson-Hughes et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2848109); [PMC record](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13107228/))

These achieved-level analyses are informative, but they are not the same as the original randomized comparison. Achieved 25-hydroxyvitamin D is a post-randomization variable and can reflect adherence, body size, metabolism, baseline physiology, sun exposure, illness, and other factors. That makes these analyses useful for generating better trial designs, but not definitive proof that targeting higher achieved levels prevents diabetes. ([Chatterjee et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523463046); [Cochrane Handbook](https://www.cochrane.org/authors/handbooks-and-manuals/handbook/current/chapter-08))

## Meta-analyses support a narrower conclusion than “vitamin D is ineffective”

Recent reviews generally agree that vitamin D supplementation of mostly replete adults has not shown reliable prevention of major chronic diseases. A Nature Reviews Endocrinology review summarized the evidence as showing no clear prevention of cancer incidence, cardiovascular events, falls, or type 2 diabetes progression in vitamin D-replete adults, while also emphasizing that correcting severe deficiency remains important. ([Bouillon et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-021-00593-z))

For diabetes specifically, the evidence is more mixed than the D2d headline alone suggests. A review of diabetes-prevention trials reported that the three large trials not selected for deficiency each produced modest relative risk reductions, and that a D2d subgroup with baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D below 12 ng/mL had a larger apparent risk reduction. ([Pittas et al., 2020](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/105/12/3721/5897217); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32844212/))

A VITAL diabetes ancillary study found no significant diabetes-prevention effect in the overall VITAL population, and no clear difference by baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D. However, a meta-analysis of four vitamin D trials in people at risk for diabetes estimated a modest lower diabetes risk. ([Tobias et al., 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-58721-6))

So the best summary is not “vitamin D prevents diabetes” or “vitamin D does nothing.” It is that broad supplementation produces, at most, modest diabetes-prevention effects in at-risk populations, with unresolved questions about whether deficiency, achieved level, genotype, body size, or dosing strategy identifies responders. ([Pittas et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.7326/M22-3018); [Tobias et al., 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-58721-6); [Dawson-Hughes et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2848109))

## Functional outcomes are a different question

VITAL also tested several musculoskeletal outcomes. In participants not selected for vitamin D deficiency, low bone mass, osteoporosis, or high fall risk, vitamin D did not reduce fall risk, fractures, functional decline, or frailty. Mean baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D was about 30.7 ng/mL, and only 2.4 percent were below 12 ng/mL. ([Journal of Gerontology: Series A](https://academic.oup.com/biomedgerontology/article/78/Supplement_1/73/7199280); [Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/105/9/2929/5865851); [Orkaby et al., 2022](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2796088))

VITAL also found no reduction in fracture risk among generally healthy midlife and older adults. ([LeBoff et al., 2022](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2202106); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35939577/))

A VITAL physical-performance ancillary study similarly found that vitamin D supplementation did not improve grip strength, walking speed, short physical performance battery score, or timed-up-and-go performance over two years, and effects did not vary clearly by total or free 25-hydroxyvitamin D. ([Chou et al., 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/110/1/e44/7629844); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38488491/))

DO-HEALTH, another large trial in adults aged 70 or older, found that vitamin D3 at 2000 IU/day, omega-3 fatty acids, and a strength-training intervention did not significantly improve the primary endpoints, including nonvertebral fractures, physical performance, infection rate, cognition, or blood pressure. However, that trial also was not a clean severe-deficiency-correction trial. ([Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2020](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2772758))

STURDY came closer to a low-vitamin-D functional trial because it enrolled older adults with baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D between 10 and 29 ng/mL and elevated fall risk. Higher daily vitamin D doses did not prevent falls compared with 200 IU/day, and some adverse-fall analyses raised concern about possible harm at higher doses. ([Appel et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.7326/M20-3812); [PMC record](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8240534/))

For bone stress injury, the evidence is more specific but less clean for vitamin D alone. A randomized trial in female Navy recruits found that calcium plus vitamin D reduced stress fracture incidence by about 20 percent, but because the intervention combined calcium and vitamin D, it cannot isolate vitamin D’s independent effect. ([Lappe et al., 2008](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544))

## Immune outcomes show why dosing and baseline status matter

Respiratory infection evidence illustrates the same design issue, although the effect is now less secure than the 2017 result alone suggested. An individual participant data meta-analysis found that vitamin D reduced acute respiratory infection risk, with stronger effects in people with very low baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D and with daily or weekly dosing rather than large bolus dosing. ([Martineau et al., 2017](https://www.bmj.com/content/356/bmj.i6583); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28202713/))

That does not mean vitamin D is a broad immune panacea. A 2021 update found only a small protective effect, and a 2025 update found no statistically significant overall protective effect after adding newer trials. It means nutrient trials are sensitive to baseline status, dosing schedule, and outcome choice. A trial in replete people using chronic disease endpoints may miss effects that appear in deficient people, with daily dosing, on shorter-term functional endpoints. ([Jolliffe et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33798465/); [Jolliffe et al., 2025](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/landia/article/PIIS2213-8587%2824%2900348-6/fulltext); [PubMed record](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39993397/))

## Conflict of interest and funding bias

VITAL was primarily publicly funded, but the vitamin D study pills and matching placebo were donated by Pharmavite, and the omega-3 study agents were donated by Pronova BioPharma and BASF. That should be disclosed when interpreting the trial, although the main vitamin D results were largely null rather than strongly pro-supplement. ([Manson et al., 2019 VITAL principal results](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7089819/))

D2d was primarily supported by public and nonprofit funding, and the trial pills were purchased from an independent supplement manufacturer with no association with the D2d Research Group. That lowers, but does not eliminate, concern about supplement-industry influence. ([D2d design and conduct paper](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6993875/))

Industry funding is not the main weakness of VITAL or D2d for the deficiency question. The more important limitation for that question is participant selection: both trials enrolled many people who were already vitamin D sufficient or near-sufficient at baseline. ([Luttmann-Gibson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6875603/); [Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906))

## Practical interpretation

- VITAL and D2d are strong evidence against routine vitamin D supplementation as a broad chronic-disease-prevention strategy in mostly replete adults. ([Manson et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1809944); [Pittas et al., 2019](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1900906); [Bouillon et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-021-00593-z))

- They are weaker evidence against clinically indicated testing, diagnosing, and correcting true deficiency, because neither trial was designed around severe deficiency correction. This should not be read as proof that routine population screening is beneficial; the Endocrine Society suggests against routine 25-hydroxyvitamin D screening in generally healthy adults without established indications, and the USPSTF finds evidence insufficient for screening asymptomatic community-dwelling nonpregnant adults. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease); [USPSTF](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-d-deficiency-screening))

- Subgroup findings in VITAL and D2d are biologically interesting but should be treated as hypothesis-generating unless replicated in trials designed around baseline status, achieved level, body size, and dosing strategy. ([Chandler et al., 2020](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2773074); [Tobias et al., 2023](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2800490); [Dawson-Hughes et al., 2026](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2848109))

- The most defensible conclusion is narrower than either extreme: vitamin D is not a general preventive drug for already-replete adults, severe deficiency correction remains established for rickets and osteomalacia, and the chronic-disease-prevention question in deficient populations remains only partly answered. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/); [Bouillon et al., 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41574-021-00593-z))

- Better trials would enrich for low baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D, use treat-to-target designs, prespecify deficiency strata, measure achieved levels, and include functional outcomes such as falls, stress injury, muscle performance, infection risk, and recovery, not only long-latency chronic disease endpoints.
