# Should You Test Your Vitamin D Levels?

Routine vitamin D screening in generally healthy adults is not supported by direct trial evidence. But “routine screening” and “one strategic baseline measurement before meaningful supplementation” are not the same question.

The best-supported position is narrow: routine population screening has not been shown to improve health outcomes in average-risk adults. The weaker, but still reasonable, position is that targeted testing can be useful when the result would change a decision: diagnosing a likely deficiency, avoiding unnecessary high-dose supplementation, checking response in people at risk of persistent deficiency, or preventing overcorrection in people using higher-dose products. Direct evidence that one strategic baseline test improves patient-important outcomes remains limited.

## What vitamin D testing measures

Vitamin D status is usually assessed with serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D, or 25(OH)D. This is the accepted biomarker because it reflects vitamin D from sunlight, food, and supplements better than circulating active hormone levels. However, the “right” 25(OH)D level is still debated, partly because thresholds differ depending on whether the outcome is bone health, falls, fracture risk, immune outcomes, performance, or general disease prevention. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

That uncertainty matters because screening is only useful if a test result leads to an action that improves outcomes. A test can be analytically useful but still not proven as a population-screening tool. The 2024 international consensus statement explicitly distinguishes broad public-health screening from targeted clinical testing in higher-risk settings. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## What the major guidelines actually say

The [Endocrine Society’s 2024 guideline](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease) suggests against routine 25(OH)D testing in generally healthy adults without established indications. The guideline also says there is no clinical trial evidence supporting routine screening in the general population, generally healthy people with obesity, or generally healthy people with dark complexion, and that no outcome-specific 25(OH)D threshold has been established for broad screening. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

The [USPSTF](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-d-deficiency-screening) does not say that vitamin D testing is “pointless.” It gives an “I statement,” meaning the evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening asymptomatic, community-dwelling, nonpregnant adults. It found no direct evidence that screening itself improves outcomes, and no direct evidence on screening harms. ([USPSTF, 2021](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-d-deficiency-screening); [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778487))

A 2025 systematic review of 31 vitamin D guidelines found that no included guideline recommended screening the general population, but about two-thirds recommended screening or testing in at-risk groups. ([Zemp et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13690-025-01709-x))

## The evidence behind “do not routinely screen”

The main problem is not that vitamin D is irrelevant. The problem is that screening programs need direct evidence: test people, treat those below a threshold, and show fewer fractures, falls, hospitalizations, deaths, or other patient-important outcomes compared with not screening.

The USPSTF evidence review found no direct studies evaluating the benefits or harms of screening for vitamin D deficiency in asymptomatic adults. It also found that treating asymptomatic, community-dwelling adults with low vitamin D had no clear effect on mortality or the incidence of fractures, falls, depression, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, or adverse events, while evidence was inconclusive for physical functioning and infection. ([Kahwati et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488); [USPSTF, 2021](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-d-deficiency-screening))

The systematic review supporting the Endocrine Society guideline reached a similar conclusion: it identified trials of vitamin D supplementation, but did not identify trials testing the benefits and harms of screening with serum 25(OH)D. ([Shah et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1961/7686350))

This is why the strongest anti-screening argument is evidence-based: no randomized trial has shown that routine 25(OH)D screening improves health outcomes in average-risk adults. That is different from saying vitamin D status never matters.

## Why “routine screening” and “one baseline test” are different

Routine screening means testing a broad asymptomatic population, often repeatedly, regardless of whether the result is likely to change management. A strategic baseline test is narrower: it asks whether a specific person’s starting level should influence supplementation, dosing, follow-up, or evaluation for risk factors.

The 2024 consensus statement is consistent with this distinction. It says general-population screening is not recommended, but that 25(OH)D measurement may be appropriate in clinical contexts with risk factors for severe deficiency or metabolic bone disease. Examples include older adults prone to osteoporosis, people who are housebound or institutionalized, people with long indoor working hours, people with dark skin and low sun exposure, obesity, bariatric surgery, malabsorption, chronic kidney disease, liver disease, parathyroid disorders, and medications that increase vitamin D catabolism. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Dark skin or obesity alone should not be treated as automatic indications for routine screening in otherwise healthy adults. Testing is more defensible when risk factors combine with a clinical or supplementation decision that depends on the result. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305); [Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

That means the useful question is not “Should everyone test?” It is “Would this result change what I do?” If the answer is no, testing has little value. If the answer is yes, the test is no longer routine population screening; it is targeted testing.

## Supplementing without feedback

Discouraging routine testing does not necessarily stop supplementation. In the United States, use of vitamin D supplements at doses of at least 1,000 IU/day rose from 0.3 percent in 1999-2000 to 18.2 percent in 2013-2014. In the same analysis, about 3 percent of adults reported taking vitamin D at or above 4,000 IU/day, the tolerable upper intake level cited by the authors. ([Rooney et al., 2017](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494))

This does not prove that discouraging testing causes over- or under-dosing. It does show that many people supplement at meaningful doses independent of a formal screening program. Once people are already supplementing, the decision changes from “Should we screen everyone?” to “Is this person taking enough, too much, or a dose that does not match their risk?”

Vitamin D toxicity is rare, but it can be serious. The 2024 consensus statement describes toxicity as usually arising from unintentional overdosing or inappropriate high-dose products, with a biochemical pattern often involving very high 25(OH)D, hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and hyperphosphatemia. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

At the same time, routine monitoring is not necessary for everyone taking ordinary maintenance doses. The same consensus statement says routine monitoring is generally unnecessary for long-term maintenance doses up to at least 2,000 IU/day, but retesting after 8 to 12 weeks may be appropriate in people with poor adherence, symptoms, risk of persistent low 25(OH)D, obesity, bariatric surgery, malabsorption, institutionalization, subclinical primary hyperparathyroidism, or use of anticonvulsants or glucocorticoids. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Low sun exposure and high-load training

Chronically low sun exposure is a stronger testing rationale than “health optimization” in the abstract. Season, latitude, cultural clothing patterns, skin pigmentation, body mass index, age, sex, physical activity, fortification, supplement use, and genetics can all influence 25(OH)D levels. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Athletes are a useful example because high training load does not automatically justify testing, but indoor training, low sun exposure, stress-fracture risk, or recurrent musculoskeletal symptoms may. In a large study of German elite athletes, 55.5 percent had 25(OH)D below 30 ng/mL and 16 percent were below 20 ng/mL; indoor versus outdoor discipline was one factor associated with status. ([Hacker et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05699-6))

However, vitamin D testing should not be oversold as a performance tool. A 2023 meta-analysis of randomized trials in athletes found that supplementation increased serum 25(OH)D but did not produce a statistically significant improvement in muscle strength or power. The authors rated the evidence as limited and mixed. ([Sist et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full))

So the reasonable athlete-specific claim is modest: testing may be useful when low vitamin D is plausible and the result would change supplementation or medical evaluation. It is not proven as a general performance-screening strategy.

## Cost-effectiveness and behavioral evidence

The evidence base is thin for one-time baseline testing in high-risk subgroups. A 2022 Italian expert document noted that there were no randomized studies comparing screening with no screening, no evidence that general-population screening is cost-effective, and no clear data on the cost-effectiveness of baseline 25(OH)D testing in at-risk categories. ([Bertoldo et al., 2022](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/14/19/4148))

Existing cost-effectiveness work often depends heavily on assumptions about falls and fractures. One decision model in 65-year-old community-dwelling adults found that population screening and universal supplementation had similar cost-effectiveness profiles, but the model’s conclusions depended on the assumed benefit of supplementation for fall reduction. ([Lee et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23631393/))

There is also limited behavioral evidence. A 2025 Taiwanese cohort of people who voluntarily paid for vitamin D testing found that repeat testers had lower deficiency rates over time, but the study was observational, self-selected, and did not show that testing improved clinical outcomes. ([Yeh et al., 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/public-health-nutrition/article/voluntary-vitamin-d-testing-a-decadelong-study-of-utilisation-patterns-and-impact-on-deficiency-outcomes-in-taiwan/FFB05E6FC560283FA44DF045EF5DDB36))

Policy restrictions can reduce test volume, but they do not necessarily improve targeting. The 2024 consensus statement notes that an Australian criteria change halved vitamin D measurements but was associated with more unnecessary tests and fewer tests among high-risk individuals, with only a small improvement in deficiency detection. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Conflict of interest and funding notes

The Endocrine Society guideline was funded by the Endocrine Society and states that no other entity provided funding; 7 percent of the panel had relevant or potentially relevant conflicts of interest. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

The USPSTF evidence report reported no author conflicts of interest and was supported under an AHRQ contract; the report states that AHRQ had no role in study selection, quality assessment, or evidence synthesis. ([Kahwati et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488))

The 2024 international consensus statement reported support in part from the International Vitamin D Expert Association; the conference and editorial assistance were supported by an unrestricted educational grant from Abiogen Pharma, and the paper states that sponsors had no role in topic selection, speaker or author selection, preparation, or review. The article also reports multiple author disclosures. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

The NHANES supplementation-trend study was supported by NIH-related funding, and one author reported personal fees from Siemens Healthcare Diagnostics; the funders were reported to have no role in the study. This is relevant because Siemens is a diagnostics company, not a vitamin D supplement manufacturer. ([Rooney et al., 2017](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494))

The Taiwanese voluntary-testing cohort reported no external funding, institutional support, and no competing interests. ([Yeh et al., 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/public-health-nutrition/article/voluntary-vitamin-d-testing-a-decadelong-study-of-utilisation-patterns-and-impact-on-deficiency-outcomes-in-taiwan/FFB05E6FC560283FA44DF045EF5DDB36))

## Practical interpretation

- For generally healthy adults with no symptoms, no risk factors, and no plan to supplement beyond ordinary dietary-reference-level intake, routine 25(OH)D screening is not supported by direct trial evidence. ([USPSTF, 2021](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/vitamin-d-deficiency-screening); [Demay et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305))

- For people with established indications or higher-risk contexts, testing is different from routine screening. Risk contexts include osteoporosis or metabolic bone disease, hypocalcemia or hypercalcemia evaluation, malabsorption, bariatric surgery, chronic kidney disease, liver disease, parathyroid disorders, obesity with concern for persistent deficiency, housebound status, institutionalization, chronically low sun exposure, dark skin with low sun exposure, and medications that increase vitamin D breakdown. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

- For people already taking meaningful doses, especially multiple products or doses above ordinary maintenance intake, one baseline measurement is not proven to improve clinical outcomes, but it can be a rational feedback tool if the result would change the dose, stop unnecessary supplementation, or prompt evaluation for persistent deficiency or excess. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127); [Rooney et al., 2017](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2632494))

- For athletes, testing is more defensible when there is low sun exposure, indoor training, recurrent stress injury, symptoms, or planned supplementation; it is not proven as a general performance-optimization screen. ([Hacker et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05699-6); [Sist et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full))


## Bottom line

The “do not routinely screen” message is evidence-based when it means population-wide testing of average-risk, asymptomatic adults. It becomes oversimplified when translated as “vitamin D testing is pointless.”

A better rule is: test when the result would change a decision. Do not test just to collect a number. Do test when there is a plausible risk of deficiency, a clinical indication, a meaningful supplementation plan, or a need to avoid both under-treatment and over-treatment.
