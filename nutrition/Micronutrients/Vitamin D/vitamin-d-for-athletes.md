# Vitamin D for Athletes: Different Stakes, Different Evidence

Athletes are not simply “healthy adults who exercise.” They may be healthy, but in vitamin D evidence their risk question can be different: bone stress injury, missed training, seasonal exposure, indoor sport, high-latitude winters, and heavy loading may matter more than broad population outcomes like cancer or cardiovascular disease prevention. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

## What the athlete question is and is not

For athletes, the strongest practical question is not whether vitamin D improves health outcomes in average-risk adults. It is whether low vitamin D status is associated with risk for bone stress injury, impaired training availability, or reduced performance under high mechanical load. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society")) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2325967120966967 "Association of Serum Vitamin D Levels and Stress Fractures in Collegiate Athletes"))

Population guidelines often ask a different question. The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline focuses on vitamin D use for disease prevention in people without established indications for treatment or 25-hydroxyvitamin D testing; it suggests against routine testing in generally healthy adults under 50 because outcome-specific benefits have not been established in that population. That scope is important: it does not directly test athlete-specific outcomes such as stress fracture risk, sport performance, or missed training days. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society"))

So the athlete-specific interpretation should be narrower: population guidance and current athlete trials do not strongly support routine screening or supplementation for every athlete, but higher-risk athletes are a different evidence question from average-risk adults. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

## Why athletes can have different risk profiles

Vitamin D status varies with season, latitude, skin pigmentation, body size, supplement use, and factors that reduce UVB exposure, including cultural or clothing patterns that limit skin exposure. Winter declines in 25-hydroxyvitamin D are expected in many regions, especially where UVB exposure is low. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link"))

A systematic review and meta-analysis of elite athletes found vitamin D insufficiency, defined as 25-hydroxyvitamin D less than or equal to 50 nmol/L, in about 30 percent of adult elite athletes and 39 percent of adolescent elite athletes. The authors noted that these estimates were probably conservative because relatively few studies sampled athletes during winter or spring, when low status is more common. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link"))

Earlier athlete literature also found that risk was higher in winter or early spring, at higher latitudes, and in indoor sports. In pooled athlete data, Farrokhyar and colleagues reported that more than half of athletes had vitamin D inadequacy, with higher risk in winter/spring and at latitudes at or above 40°N. Prevalence estimates across reviews are not directly comparable because they used different cutoffs, athlete populations, sampling seasons, and study designs. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

Elite Para-athletes appear to be another high-risk group. A 2024 systematic review found that low vitamin D was common across the year, with insufficiency reported most often in winter compared with summer. The review also highlighted indoor training and wheelchair sport contexts as relevant risk factors. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00756-y "The Prevalence of Low Vitamin D in Elite Para-Athletes: A Systematic Review")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39230661/ "The Prevalence of Low Vitamin D in Elite Para-Athletes"))

## What the stress fracture evidence says

The most directly relevant randomized trial is the Navy recruit trial. In 5,201 female Navy recruits, daily calcium plus vitamin D during basic training reduced stress fracture incidence compared with placebo. The intervention provided 2,000 mg calcium plus 800 IU vitamin D per day, and the reported stress fracture incidence was 5.3 percent in the supplement group versus 6.6 percent in the control group. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits"))

That result matters because the outcome was not a surrogate marker. It was stress fracture incidence under repetitive high-load training. But the trial does not prove that vitamin D alone prevents stress fractures, because calcium and vitamin D were given together. It also studied military recruits rather than competitive athletes. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits"))

Athlete cohort data are suggestive but weaker than randomized evidence. In collegiate athletes, Millward and colleagues reported a lower observed stress fracture rate among athletes whose low vitamin D status improved to at least 40 ng/mL than among athletes who remained low. This supports correction of low status, but it remains observational, event counts were small, confounding was difficult to adjust for, and the 40 ng/mL threshold is not a universally established athlete target. ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2325967120966967 "Association of Serum Vitamin D Levels and Stress Fractures in Collegiate Athletes"))

More recent military data make the picture less simple. A prospective study in British Army recruits found that vitamin D metabolite patterns, especially 24,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, were associated with lower-body overuse musculoskeletal injury and bone stress injury risk, but 25-hydroxyvitamin D alone was not clearly predictive in isolation. The authors noted that causal effects had not been demonstrated and that randomized trials were still needed in young adult athletes and military populations. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article/38/10/1453/7610390 "Vitamin D Metabolites Are Associated With Musculoskeletal Injury in Young Adults"))

A 2026 randomized trial in men starting arduous military training corrected vitamin D status with oral vitamin D3 or simulated sunlight exposure, but it did not find a clear reduction in lower-body overuse musculoskeletal injury during training or later service. At baseline, only 29 percent were vitamin D sufficient; after four weeks, supplementation achieved sufficiency in 95 percent, yet injury hazard ratios did not show a meaningful protective effect. The outcome was broad lower-body overuse musculoskeletal injury, not stress fractures alone. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

A 2025 collegiate-athlete cohort also reported fewer bone stress injuries during a vitamin D3 supplementation period compared with a retrospective no-supplement period, but the reduction was not statistically significant and the design was not randomized. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40452696/ "Supplemental Vitamin D3 for the Prevention of Bone Stress Injuries in Collegiate Athletes")) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12126936/ "Supplemental Vitamin D3 for the Prevention of Bone Stress Injuries in Collegiate Athletes"))

Taken together, the injury evidence supports a cautious position: correcting documented low vitamin D status can be considered in high-risk athletes as part of bone-health management, but the evidence does not justify treating vitamin D as a standalone injury-prevention solution. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2325967120966967 "Association of Serum Vitamin D Levels and Stress Fractures in Collegiate Athletes")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

## What supplementation does for performance

Vitamin D supplementation can raise 25-hydroxyvitamin D in athletes, but raising the blood value does not reliably improve performance. A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials found that supplementation increased vitamin D status, yet physical performance outcomes did not significantly improve. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28577257/ "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Concentrations and Physical Performance in Athletes"))

Strength and power findings are similarly mixed. A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials found no statistically significant effect of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength or power in athletes. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full "Effects of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength and power in athletes"))

An updated 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis found a possible signal for quadriceps strength, but the overall pooled effect across upper- and lower-extremity strength outcomes was not statistically significant. The authors concluded that evidence remains limited and does not warrant broad claims of significant overall strength enhancement once athletes reach adequate vitamin D status. ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1381301/full "Effects of vitamin D3 supplementation on strength of lower and upper extremities in athletes"))

Individual randomized trials fit that pattern. In club-level athletes, weekly vitamin D increased serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D but did not significantly improve measured performance outcomes. In professional rugby players, vitamin D supplementation improved vitamin D status but had little effect on performance measures such as sprinting or strength. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23410885/ "The effects of vitamin D3 supplementation on serum total 25(OH)D concentration and physical performance: a randomised dose-response study")) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28771064/ "Vitamin D3 Supplementation Does Not Improve Sprint Performance in Professional Rugby Players"))

The practical implication is not “vitamin D boosts performance.” A better reading is: vitamin D supplementation may correct deficiency or insufficiency, but there is little evidence that pushing already-sufficient athletes higher improves sprinting, strength, or power. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28577257/ "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Concentrations and Physical Performance in Athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full "Effects of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength and power in athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1381301/full "Effects of vitamin D3 supplementation on strength of lower and upper extremities in athletes"))

## Why “don’t test routinely” does not settle the athlete question

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline recommends against routine 25-hydroxyvitamin D testing in generally healthy adults under 50 when there is no established indication. That recommendation is about population-level disease prevention and the lack of trial evidence for specific prevention outcomes in average-risk people. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society"))

Athletes with indoor training, winter seasons, high-latitude exposure, recurrent bone stress injury, low sun exposure, or Para-sport constraints are not the same practical group as average-risk adults. The evidence does not prove that all of them need routine testing, but it does support treating them as a separate risk group for clinical judgment rather than applying population screening guidance as if sport injury outcomes had already been answered. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00756-y "The Prevalence of Low Vitamin D in Elite Para-Athletes: A Systematic Review"))

## Quick reference values and findings

- Elite athletes: vitamin D insufficiency less than or equal to 50 nmol/L was estimated in about 30 percent of adult elite athletes and 39 percent of adolescent elite athletes. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link"))

- Risk periods and settings: winter/spring, high latitude, and indoor sport are recurring risk factors in athlete studies. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis"))

- Elite Para-athletes: low vitamin D status is common, with winter insufficiency reported more often than summer insufficiency. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00756-y "The Prevalence of Low Vitamin D in Elite Para-Athletes: A Systematic Review"))

- Navy recruit RCT: calcium plus vitamin D reduced stress fracture incidence in female recruits during basic training, but the intervention tested combined calcium and vitamin D, not vitamin D alone. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits"))

- Collegiate athlete cohort: athletes whose low vitamin D status improved had lower observed stress fracture risk than athletes who remained low, but the evidence was observational, event counts were small, and the 40 ng/mL threshold is not a universally established athlete target. ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2325967120966967 "Association of Serum Vitamin D Levels and Stress Fractures in Collegiate Athletes"))

- 2025 collegiate-athlete cohort: vitamin D3 supplementation was associated with fewer bone stress injuries, but the reduction was not statistically significant and the design was not randomized. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40452696/ "Supplemental Vitamin D3 for the Prevention of Bone Stress Injuries in Collegiate Athletes")) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12126936/ "Supplemental Vitamin D3 for the Prevention of Bone Stress Injuries in Collegiate Athletes"))

- 2026 male military RCT: vitamin D sufficiency improved substantially, but lower-body overuse injury risk did not clearly fall. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

- Performance trials: supplementation raises vitamin D status, but meta-analyses do not show consistent improvements in sprinting, strength, or power, especially when athletes are already sufficient. ## Practical interpretation

Vitamin D is best treated as a deficiency-correction and bone-health variable, not as a general performance enhancer. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28577257/ "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Concentrations and Physical Performance in Athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full "Effects of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength and power in athletes")) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits"))

For athletes with low baseline status or clear risk factors, clinician-directed testing and correction may be reasonable to consider, especially when the clinical concern is bone stress injury, recurrent overuse injury, low sun exposure, or winter indoor training. This is different from routine population screening of average-risk adults. ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease | Endocrine Society")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link")) ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/19417381251398501 "Metabolic Workup for Bone Stress Injury: A Practical Approach for Evaluating Bone Health in Athletes"))

For athletes already sufficient at baseline, supplementation has not consistently improved sprinting, strength, power, or broad performance outcomes. The evidence argues against “optimizing” vitamin D above sufficiency as a performance strategy. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28577257/ "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Concentrations and Physical Performance in Athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full "Effects of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength and power in athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1381301/full "Effects of vitamin D3 supplementation on strength of lower and upper extremities in athletes"))

For stress fracture prevention, the strongest RCT signal comes from calcium plus vitamin D in female Navy recruits, but later evidence is mixed and does not establish vitamin D alone as protective. Vitamin D should sit inside a wider bone-health plan that includes adequate energy availability, calcium intake, progressive loading, recovery, and medical evaluation when bone stress injury is suspected. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/19417381251398501 "Metabolic Workup for Bone Stress Injury: A Practical Approach for Evaluating Bone Health in Athletes"))

## Evidence still needed

Athlete-specific evidence is still thin where the practical question is most specific: bone stress injury endpoints, baseline vitamin D status, calcium intake, and sport-specific training exposure. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

Based on the gaps left by the existing evidence, the highest-value studies would be randomized trials in athletes with stress fracture or bone stress injury endpoints, stratified by baseline vitamin D status and calcium intake. Trials should separate vitamin D alone from calcium plus vitamin D, because the Navy recruit trial tested the combination. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core"))

Seasonal cohort studies are also needed across indoor and outdoor sports, high- and low-latitude settings, Para-sport groups, and adolescent versus adult athletes. These should track 25-hydroxyvitamin D, training load, injury diagnosis, missed training days, and return-to-play outcomes. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis | European Journal of Nutrition | Springer Nature Link")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0267-6 "Prevalence of Vitamin D Inadequacy in Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis")) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00756-y "The Prevalence of Low Vitamin D in Elite Para-Athletes: A Systematic Review"))

Performance trials should stratify athletes by baseline status. Combining deficient, insufficient, and already-sufficient athletes can obscure the main practical question: whether correction helps athletes who are low, not whether extra vitamin D improves performance in athletes who are already sufficient. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28577257/ "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Concentrations and Physical Performance in Athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2023.1163313/full "Effects of vitamin D supplementation on maximal strength and power in athletes")) ([Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2024.1381301/full "Effects of vitamin D3 supplementation on strength of lower and upper extremities in athletes"))

## Funding and conflict notes

The key injury evidence cited here includes military, collegiate, academic, and guideline-based evidence rather than only branded supplement evidence. The 2026 military trial, for example, was conducted in men beginning arduous military training and was published as an accepted manuscript in the British Journal of Nutrition; that gives it practical relevance but also limits generalization to competitive athletes in all sports. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial | British Journal of Nutrition | Cambridge Core")) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jbmr/article-abstract/23/5/741/7599544 "Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation Decreases Incidence of Stress Fractures in Female Navy Recruits")) ([Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2325967120966967 "Association of Serum Vitamin D Levels and Stress Fractures in Collegiate Athletes"))

Funding and conflict claims should stay modest. Because some abstract or landing pages do not display full funding and conflict-of-interest statements, this article should not state that the main studies are free of supplement-manufacturer conflicts unless the full papers are checked directly. The safer interpretation is that funding and conflict status should be read source by source.
