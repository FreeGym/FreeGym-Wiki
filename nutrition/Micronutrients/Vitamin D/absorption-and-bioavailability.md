# Vitamin D - Absorption and Bioavailability

Vitamin D status response is not a fixed “1000 IU raises 25(OH)D by X” formula. The same dose can produce different serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] responses depending on baseline status, meal context, formulation, body weight/adiposity, vitamin D form, and intestinal fat absorption. Randomized feeding studies, dose-response analyses, D2/D3 head-to-head trials, and malabsorption sources all support that variability. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/"); [OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259 "https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259"))

## What absorption and bioavailability mean

Vitamin D absorption usually refers to how much ingested vitamin D enters circulation after a dose. Some pharmacokinetic studies measure plasma vitamin D after a single dose, while longer trials usually measure serum 25(OH)D, the main clinical status marker. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

Bioavailability and achieved serum 25(OH)D response are not determined only by the IU number on the label. Meal fat, physical formulation, bile acids, and conditions or medications that interfere with fat digestion can affect intestinal absorption. Body size and adiposity also affect achieved serum response; the NIH Office of Dietary Supplements notes that obesity does not alter vitamin D absorption from the gut, so the lower 25(OH)D response in people with higher BMI should not be reduced to a simple gut-absorption problem. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/"); [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"))

## Fat with dosing: helpful, but formulation matters

Vitamin D is fat-soluble, and several human studies support taking it with food rather than treating it as a fasting supplement. In a randomized single-dose study in 50 older adults, 50,000 IU vitamin D3 taken with a fat-containing breakfast produced a 32 percent higher mean peak plasma vitamin D3 concentration than the same dose taken with a fat-free breakfast. The ratio of monounsaturated to polyunsaturated fat did not materially change absorption. This trial reported no potential conflicts of interest, but it was cofunded by a Pfizer investigator-initiated grant and a USDA/HNRCA pilot grant. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"))

A smaller randomized study also found higher 25(OH)D after 50,000 IU vitamin D3 when the dose was taken with a high-fat meal rather than a low-fat meal. A prospective clinical cohort found that patients taking vitamin D with their largest meal had about a 50 percent increase in serum 25(OH)D after changing the timing of the same supplement regimen. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22190928/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22190928/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/"))

The food effect is not universal. In an 88-person randomized open-label crossover study, a single 50,000 IU dose of vitamin D3 in an oily solution produced similar 25(OH)D responses whether taken fasting or with a standardized high-fat breakfast. This study was sponsored by Laboratoires SMB SA, and several authors were company employees, so its reassuring result for oily formulations should be interpreted with that funding context in mind. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"))

The practical interpretation is that vitamin D products not already solubilized in oil are generally better taken with a meal that contains fat, while an oil-based formulation may be less dependent on meal fat. The evidence does not prove that the “largest” or highest-fat meal is always necessary; it mainly shows that fat-free or fasting dosing can underperform in some settings. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"); [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/"))

## BMI, adiposity, and dose response

Adults with higher body weight or BMI tend to have a smaller rise in 25(OH)D for the same vitamin D dose. A systematic review found body weight to be an important predictor of serum 25(OH)D response to supplementation, and a large VITAL ancillary analysis found that vitamin D3 supplementation increased vitamin D biomarkers less strongly in participants with higher BMI. This should not be simplified as reduced gut absorption; the NIH Office of Dietary Supplements notes that obesity does not alter vitamin D absorption from the gut. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

One dose-response analysis estimated that, compared with normal-weight adults, overweight adults may need about 1.5 times as much vitamin D and adults with obesity may need about 2 to 3 times as much to achieve similar 25(OH)D concentrations. This is a population-level modeling estimate, not a substitute for measuring an individual’s response. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4220998/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4220998/"))

The common shorthand that “adipose tissue sequesters vitamin D” is probably incomplete. A head-to-head D2/D3 trial found that vitamin D3 produced greater storage than vitamin D2, but the authors reported no evidence that either form was simply being sequestered in fat at the studied dose range. The better-supported claim is that higher body weight and adiposity blunt serum 25(OH)D response; mechanisms remain debated and may include distribution, dilution, metabolism, and storage-related explanations rather than one single pathway. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

## D2 and D3 are not equivalent for correcting status

Vitamin D2 and vitamin D3 can both raise total 25(OH)D, but the evidence generally favors vitamin D3 for raising and maintaining vitamin D status. In a classic pharmacokinetic trial, vitamin D2 and D3 produced similar early rises in circulating parent vitamin D, but the 25(OH)D response was much larger and longer-lasting after vitamin D3; the 28-day 25(OH)D area under the curve was about 205 ng·days/mL for D3 versus about 60 ng·days/mL for D2. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259 "https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259"))

A randomized trial using 50,000 IU per week for 12 weeks found vitamin D3 to be about 87 percent more potent than vitamin D2 for raising 25(OH)D and to produce 2- to 3-fold greater vitamin D storage. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/"))

A 25-week double-blind placebo-controlled trial found that 1000 IU/day vitamin D3 was more effective than 1000 IU/day vitamin D2 at maintaining total 25(OH)D; vitamin D2 also caused a greater decline in the D3-derived metabolite 25(OH)D3. This trial reported university funding and no author conflicts of interest. ([Cambridge University Press & Assessment](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/longterm-vitamin-d3-supplementation-is-more-effective-than-vitamin-d2-in-maintaining-serum-25hydroxyvitamin-d-status-over-the-winter-months/A6CD4FE5C649E0FAC9BCBAFB23538707 "https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/longterm-vitamin-d3-supplementation-is-more-effective-than-vitamin-d2-in-maintaining-serum-25hydroxyvitamin-d-status-over-the-winter-months/A6CD4FE5C649E0FAC9BCBAFB23538707"))

Meta-analyses also favor D3 over D2 for increasing total 25(OH)D. An older randomized trial found daily D2 and D3 to be similarly effective at 1000 IU/day, but systematic reviews that pool broader head-to-head evidence generally conclude that D3 is more effective. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2266966/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2266966/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37865222/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37865222/"))

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found that vitamin D2 supplementation significantly reduced serum 25(OH)D3 compared with controls, with a weighted mean difference of about -18 nmol/L at end of trial and about -9 nmol/L for absolute change. This review was not supplement-industry funded, but one author disclosed roles and patents related to UVB-treated material and D3-related technology, so that conflict should be noted. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/advance-article/doi/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf166/8256613 "https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/advance-article/doi/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf166/8256613"))

The practical conclusion is that D2 is not useless, but D3 is usually the better choice when the goal is reliable correction or maintenance of vitamin D status. When high-dose D2 is used in prescription settings, that use should not be interpreted as biological equivalence to D3. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259 "https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/"))

## Malabsorption, medications, and “standard dose” failure

Vitamin D absorption depends on fat digestion and bile-related intestinal handling, so disorders or medications that impair fat absorption can make standard dosing unreliable. Direct evidence from older absorption studies found that cholestyramine reduced absorption of vitamin D3, and case-based evidence links cholestyramine-associated vitamin D malabsorption with osteomalacia risk in susceptible patients. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/5020877/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/5020877/"))

Orlistat can also complicate vitamin D status because it inhibits intestinal fat absorption. A small adolescent obesity study found that vitamin D concentrations fell after one month of orlistat despite multivitamin use. The evidence is less clean than for bile-acid sequestrants, but it is strong enough to justify monitoring rather than assuming a fixed standard dose will work. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

After Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, vitamin D absorption and status can remain impaired. A direct absorption study found peak cholecalciferol levels reduced by about 25 percent after gastric bypass, and a systematic review of 51 observational studies found that mean 25(OH)D often remained below 30 ng/mL after bariatric surgery despite supplementation regimens. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21339336/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21339336/"); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/"))

Inflammatory bowel disease is also associated with lower vitamin D status. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 studies found that people with IBD had higher odds of vitamin D deficiency than controls, with an odds ratio of 1.64. This does not prove that every case is caused by malabsorption, because inflammation, diet, sun exposure, medication use, surgery, and disease activity can all contribute. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/"))

Cystic fibrosis is a high-risk setting because fat malabsorption is common. Reviews report that vitamin D deficiency remains common in cystic fibrosis despite routine supplementation, with contributors including fat malabsorption, low intake, reduced sun exposure, impaired metabolism, and adherence problems. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/"))

## Limits and open debates

The fat-with-meal evidence supports avoiding fasting or fat-free dosing, but it does not prove that very high-fat meals are required for all formulations. The oily-solution trial is an important counterexample, although it was industry sponsored. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"); [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"))

The BMI evidence supports higher dose requirements in heavier adults, but “adipose sequestration” should not be treated as the only mechanism. Human dose-response data show blunted serum response with higher BMI, while at least one controlled trial did not find simple fat sequestration at the studied doses. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

The D2 versus D3 evidence is mostly based on biochemical outcomes such as 25(OH)D, not hard clinical outcomes. For correcting low 25(OH)D, the biomarker evidence consistently favors D3, but that does not automatically prove superiority for every downstream clinical endpoint. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/"))

In malabsorption states, the most defensible approach is not a universal dose multiplier. It is to dose, remeasure 25(OH)D, and adjust under clinical supervision, especially when bariatric surgery, cystic fibrosis, bile-acid sequestrants, or severe intestinal disease are present. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

## Quick reference values

- Fat-containing meal versus fat-free meal: 50,000 IU vitamin D3 produced a 32 percent higher mean peak plasma vitamin D3 concentration when taken with fat. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"))

- Largest-meal timing: a clinical cohort found about a 50 percent increase in serum 25(OH)D after patients switched vitamin D dosing to their largest meal. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200983/"))

- Oily vitamin D3 formulation: 50,000 IU vitamin D3 in an oily solution showed no meaningful fasting-versus-fed difference in one randomized crossover study; the trial was industry sponsored. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"))

- Higher body weight: dose-response modeling estimated about 1.5 times higher dose needs in overweight adults and about 2 to 3 times higher dose needs in adults with obesity. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4220998/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4220998/"))

- D3 versus D2 pharmacokinetics: one trial reported 28-day 25(OH)D area under the curve of about 205 ng·days/mL for D3 versus about 60 ng·days/mL for D2. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259 "https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259"))

- D3 potency: a 12-week trial found D3 about 87 percent more potent than D2 for raising 25(OH)D. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21177785/"))

- D2 effect on 25(OH)D3: a 2025 meta-analysis found D2 supplementation lowered serum 25(OH)D3 by about 18 nmol/L at end of trial compared with controls. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/advance-article/doi/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf166/8256613 "https://academic.oup.com/nutritionreviews/advance-article/doi/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf166/8256613"))

- Inflammatory bowel disease: meta-analysis found higher odds of vitamin D deficiency in IBD, with OR 1.64. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/"))

- Gastric bypass: direct absorption data suggest about a 25 percent reduction in peak cholecalciferol after Roux-en-Y gastric bypass. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21339336/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21339336/"))


## Practical interpretation

- Take vitamin D with a meal that contains fat rather than fasting or with a fat-free meal, especially for products not already solubilized in oil. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25441954/"); [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309 "https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/8/5/309"))

- Do not assume that a given IU dose produces the same 25(OH)D increase in people with different body weights; higher BMI is associated with a blunted biomarker response, not simply reduced gut absorption. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24292820/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36648947/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

- Prefer vitamin D3 over vitamin D2 when the goal is status correction or reliable maintenance, unless there is a specific reason to use D2. ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259 "https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/89/11/5387/2844259/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22552031/"))

- In bariatric surgery, cystic fibrosis, bile-acid sequestrant use, or significant intestinal disease, “standard dose” vitamin D may be inadequate; measured response is more reliable than dose assumptions. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4792683/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38233891/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/4318526/"); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26348447/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))

- Orlistat is a caution zone rather than a simple dose rule: it can lower fat-soluble vitamin exposure, but vitamin D findings vary, so monitoring is more defensible than guessing a fixed multiplier. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12126214/"); [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/"))
