# Population Guidelines vs Individual Decisions: The Decision Logic Gap

The deepest critique of current vitamin D guidance is not simply about vitamin D biology. It is about decision logic.

Population guidelines ask whether routine testing or supplementation improves average net benefit across broad groups. Athletes, coaches, clinicians, and self-experimenters may ask a different question: whether a cheap, actionable measurement can reduce uncertainty for a specific person whose downside risk is unusually costly.

## What population guidelines are optimizing

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline was explicitly written for people without established indications for vitamin D treatment or 25-hydroxyvitamin D testing. The panel prioritized randomized placebo-controlled trials, used GRADE methodology, and considered patient values, costs, resources, feasibility, acceptability, and equity. It also stated that the guideline process did not use a risk-assessment framework and was not intended to replace Dietary Reference Intakes. ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline")).

That matters because the guideline question was not "can any individual benefit from knowing vitamin D status?" The question was closer to "should broad groups without established indications be routinely tested or supplemented above standard intake targets to prevent disease?" For generally healthy adults younger than 75 within the populations it considered, the panel suggested against empiric vitamin D above the current DRI, and it found no clinical trial evidence supporting routine 25(OH)D screening in the general population, including people with obesity or dark complexion. ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline")).

The USPSTF evidence review similarly did not support routine screening, but as an insufficient-evidence conclusion rather than a recommendation against screening. In 46 studies including 16,205 participants, no study directly evaluated the health benefits or harms of screening adults for vitamin D deficiency. Among asymptomatic community-dwelling adults with low vitamin D levels, treatment was not associated with meaningful effects on mortality, fractures, falls, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, depression, or adverse events. ([Kahwati et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force")).

## "Do not screen routinely" is not the same as "testing is irrational"

A population conclusion that does not support routine screening means that the average case has not been shown to justify systematic testing. It does not prove that no subgroup, athlete, coach, clinician, or informed individual can rationally choose testing under a different utility function.

The Endocrine Society guideline itself incorporates cost, access, feasibility, and health equity into its reasoning. In several contexts, the practical concern is that testing can add cost, require medical visits, reduce feasibility, and make intervention less equitable than simple empiric intake advice. ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline")).

That is a different argument from "the number is useless." It is closer to: "for population-level prevention, routine testing has not been proven to improve outcomes enough to justify its cost, complexity, and downstream consequences."

For an individual, this becomes a decision analysis rather than a direct guideline claim: the case for testing becomes more defensible if the pretest probability is higher, the result would change behavior, the intervention is low-risk when properly dosed, dosing stays within standard safety limits or is clinician-supervised, and the downside of missing a correctable contributor is unusually large. The NIH Office of Dietary Supplements is cited here for serum 25(OH)D as a status marker, intake and safety-limit context, and toxicity cautions, not for the entire decision framework. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin D - Fact Sheet for Health Professionals")).

## What decision models show

A vitamin D screening decision model in community-dwelling adults aged 65 to 80 compared population screening with universal supplementation for fall prevention. The model simulated 36 months of follow-up and found both strategies cost-effective under the model assumptions, with screening becoming more favorable at older ages. The authors also noted that the model was sensitive to fall-risk reduction and the cost of testing. ([Lee et al., 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3656128/ "Cost-Effectiveness of Vitamin D Screening Compared to Universal Supplementation to Prevent Falls Among Community-Dwelling Older Adults")).

That model should not be treated as proof that athletes should be screened. It was built around older adults, falls, older fall-prevention assumptions, and evidence available at the time. It is useful because it shows the right structure: testing decisions depend on baseline risk, test cost, intervention effectiveness, event cost, and the probability that test results change management.

A 2024 consensus statement on vitamin D status assessment made the same tension explicit. It argued that general-population screening should be avoided because it is not informative enough and carries substantial economic burden, while also acknowledging that some characteristics and conditions can place people at risk for more severe deficits. It warned that if every "reasonable" risk group is listed, most people can be included, which leads back to overtesting. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127 "Consensus Statement on Vitamin D Status Assessment and Supplementation: Whys, Whens, and Hows")).

## Athletic setting: different stakes, still imperfect evidence

Vitamin D insufficiency is common in elite athletes under common research cutoffs. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 51 studies and 5,456 elite athletes defined insufficiency as 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L and estimated prevalence at 30 percent in adults and 39 percent in adolescents, with moderate evidence quality. The authors also noted that the estimates may be conservative because relatively few included studies were conducted in winter and spring. Clinical thresholds vary, so this cutoff should not be treated as a universal disease threshold requiring treatment. ([Harju et al., 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis")) ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin D - Fact Sheet for Health Professionals")).

Bone stress injuries are a meaningful athletic outcome, not a minor lab abnormality. In NCAA injury surveillance data, bone stress injuries were described as a major source of functional impairment, and prior collegiate stress-fracture literature summarized in the article identified risk factors including repetitive-impact sport training, body mass index below 19, insufficient calcium and vitamin D levels, and female sex. ([Bratsman et al., 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23259671211014496 "Epidemiology of NCAA Bone Stress Injuries: A Comparison of Athletes in Divisions I, II, and III")).

The strongest trial evidence in high-load young adults is mixed and does not cleanly support broad vitamin D testing. An older randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in female Navy recruits found that calcium plus vitamin D supplementation reduced stress fractures, but the intervention combined 2,000 mg calcium with 800 IU vitamin D per day, so it does not isolate vitamin D testing or vitamin D alone. ([Lappe et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18433305/ "Calcium and vitamin D supplementation decreases incidence of stress fractures in female Navy recruits")).

A newer 2026 randomized placebo-controlled trial in 250 young men entering arduous winter military training found that oral vitamin D3 or simulated sunlight achieved vitamin D sufficiency in most participants, but there was no clear evidence that vitamin D reduced lower-body overuse musculoskeletal injury during 6 months of training or 3 years of follow-up. ([Carswell et al., 2026](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial")).

So the athlete case is not "vitamin D testing prevents injuries." The more defensible claim is narrower: low vitamin D is common in athletes, bone stress injuries are costly, and risk-stratified testing may be rational as a decision tool when the result would change a concrete prevention plan. That last step is an inference from the evidence above, not a direct outcome finding from an athlete-specific screening trial.

## Cost of injury changes the utility calculation

Sports injuries carry direct healthcare costs, indirect time-loss costs, and organizational costs. A 2024 scoping review of 31 cost studies found that sports-injury cost estimation is growing but methodologically inconsistent; studies varied in whether they counted direct healthcare costs, indirect costs, or both, and only about one-third used the perspective of a sporting organization. ([Turnbull et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1440244024000781 "Estimating the cost of sports injuries: A scoping review")).

A collegiate injury-prevention cohort study illustrates why teams care about tail risk. In NCAA Division I athletics, teams using a surveillance and prevention program had reductions in clinic visits, healthcare encounters, and observed healthcare costs, while nonuser teams saw increases. This was not a vitamin D study, and it used a commercial force-plate system, but it shows how athletic departments may evaluate prevention through avoided injury costs rather than average population screening logic. ([Parisien et al., 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23259671211029898 "Implementation of an Injury Prevention Program in NCAA Division I Athletics Reduces Injury-Related Health Care Costs")).

This creates a practical prevention tension for coaches. A guideline can reasonably say "do not routinely screen healthy adults," while a coach can still face a different question: "given this athlete's sport, season, prior injury history, diet, skin exposure, and consequences of missing training, is a low-cost test justified because it will change management?"

## N-of-1 logic is legitimate, but vitamin D is partly a measurement problem

N-of-1 trials are a real scientific framework for individualized decisions. A 2023 systematic review of nutrition-related N-of-1 studies stated that N-of-1 trials can provide high-quality evidence for deciding which treatment works best for an individual, but it found only 7 nutrition-related N-of-1 studies with 83 total participants, so the nutrition evidence base remains small. ([Allman-Farinelli et al., 2023](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/7/1756 "Nutrition-Related N-of-1 Studies Warrant Further Research to Provide Evidence for Dietitians to Practice Personalized (Precision) Medical Nutrition Therapy: A Systematic Review")).

Vitamin D differs from many classic N-of-1 cases because the key status variable, serum 25(OH)D, is directly measurable. A blinded crossover may be useful for subjective outcomes, but for vitamin D status itself the more practical individualized design is usually measurement, intervention, repeat measurement, and predefined decision. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin D - Fact Sheet for Health Professionals")).

That design should still be disciplined. The individual should specify the decision rule before testing: what result counts as low, what intervention will be used, how long repletion will continue, when retesting will occur, and which outcomes matter. Otherwise testing can become vague reassurance-seeking rather than a decision tool.

## Guideline messaging can create behavior gaps

Vitamin D messaging is hard because it sits between sun exposure, skin-cancer prevention, diet, supplementation, bone health, and laboratory testing. In a qualitative focus-group study of 52 adults in Australia, researchers found low vitamin D knowledge, confusion around balanced sun exposure messages, and barriers including work, weather, concern about skin cancer, and limited ability to go outside. ([Bonevski et al., 2013](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/5/3/915 "The ABC of Vitamin D: A Qualitative Study of the Knowledge and Attitudes Regarding Vitamin D Deficiency amongst Selected Population Groups")).

That matters for guideline translation. A public message of "do not test routinely" should not be shortened to "vitamin D status does not matter." A supplement message should not be shortened to "everyone should take high-dose vitamin D." A better message separates population screening from individual risk assessment.

## A better decision rule

For generally healthy adults under 75 with no symptoms, no relevant medical conditions, no major risk factors, and no decision that depends on the result, current evidence supports not making 25(OH)D testing routine. The major guidelines and evidence reviews have not found direct screening evidence showing improved health outcomes in that setting. ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1907/7685305 "Vitamin D for the Prevention of Disease: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline"); [Kahwati et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force")).

For higher-risk athletes, testing becomes more defensible when several conditions are present: winter or high latitude, mostly indoor training, dark complexion or otherwise low UVB exposure, covering clothing, low dietary vitamin D intake, low energy availability, low BMI, prior bone stress injury, recurrent injuries, malabsorption risk, medications affecting vitamin D metabolism, or a sport where missed training has unusually high cost. These are not proof that testing prevents injury. Some factors increase pretest probability; others increase the utility of information by making the result more likely to change management. ([Harju et al., 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-022-02967-z "Prevalence and novel risk factors for vitamin D insufficiency in elite athletes: systematic review and meta-analysis"); [Bratsman et al., 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23259671211014496 "Epidemiology of NCAA Bone Stress Injuries: A Comparison of Athletes in Divisions I, II, and III"); [Giustina et al., 2024](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127 "Consensus Statement on Vitamin D Status Assessment and Supplementation: Whys, Whens, and Hows")).

For coaches, a cautious practical position is not "test everyone" or "never test." It is to define a risk-stratified protocol: who gets tested, why the result would change action, what dosing stays within standard safety limits or is clinician-supervised, when retesting happens, and when a clinician should be involved.

## Practical interpretation

- The strongest critique of vitamin D guidelines is not that the guidelines are "wrong." It is that population guidelines and individual decisions often answer different questions.

- A population screening program can be unjustified even when a cheap test is defensible for a specific high-risk person.

- For low-risk adults, routine testing is hard to justify because direct screening evidence is absent and treatment trials in asymptomatic low-vitamin-D adults have not shown broad outcome benefits. ([Kahwati et al., 2021](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2778488 "Screening for Vitamin D Deficiency in Adults: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force")).

- For athletes, the case for testing is strongest when the result will change a defined action plan and when the costs of a missed deficiency are high.

- For coaches, structured risk assessment, documentation, and referral when appropriate are more defensible than blanket testing or blanket dismissal.

- An important evidence gap is a proper athlete-specific decision analysis: test cost, prevalence by risk group, supplementation response, injury reduction, adverse effects, retesting costs, time-loss costs, and downstream medicalization.


## Conflict-of-interest and funding-bias notes

The central guideline and evidence-review claims above come chiefly from systematic reviews, clinical guidelines, and peer-reviewed evidence reports. This article has not performed a full funding-source or conflict-of-interest audit of every underlying trial, so the evidence should still be read with that limitation.

The athlete evidence includes observational studies and military-training trials, so it should not be overstated as proof of routine screening benefit.

The Parisien NCAA cost study used a commercially available force-plate system, and the article disclosed orthopedic-industry payments or grant support for some authors. It is useful for illustrating injury-cost logic, but it should not be treated as independent proof that any specific screening tool reduces costs. ([Parisien et al., 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23259671211029898 "Implementation of an Injury Prevention Program in NCAA Division I Athletics Reduces Injury-Related Health Care Costs")).

The Lappe trial tested calcium plus vitamin D together in female Navy recruits, so it cannot isolate vitamin D alone or vitamin D-status-guided screening. The newer Carswell trial is more directly relevant to vitamin D exposure and musculoskeletal injury risk in arduous training, and it did not show a clear injury-risk reduction despite improving vitamin D sufficiency. ([Lappe et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18433305/ "Calcium and vitamin D supplementation decreases incidence of stress fractures in female Navy recruits"); [Carswell et al., 2026](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/vitamin-d-supplementation-and-musculoskeletal-injury-risk-in-young-men-undergoing-arduous-training-a-randomised-placebocontrolled-trial/40018EAAFC8F9E7FB9208F1BF3CF0D1D "Vitamin D supplementation and musculoskeletal injury risk in young men undergoing arduous training: a randomised placebo-controlled trial")).
