# Vitamin D in High-Prevalence Regions: When “Don’t Test” Meets Lived Reality

The case against routine vitamin D testing is strongest when the question is population-wide disease prevention in generally healthy adults. It becomes less straightforward in regions where vitamin D deficiency is common, high-dose supplementation is widely used, and a single baseline value may change dosing, follow-up, and safety decisions.

## What the 2024 guideline actually says

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline focuses on vitamin D use for disease prevention in people who do not already have an established indication for vitamin D treatment or 25-hydroxyvitamin D testing. Its recommendations should not be read as a general diagnostic or management guideline for patients with established indications for vitamin D treatment or testing, or for patients with conditions that substantially alter vitamin D physiology. Examples include suspected rickets, osteomalacia, hypocalcemia, malabsorption-related deficiency, chronic kidney disease-mineral bone disorder, unexplained bone pain, or suspected vitamin D toxicity. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae290)) ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)) ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Within that disease-prevention frame, the guideline suggests against routine 25(OH)D screening in generally healthy adults, including adults with obesity and adults with dark complexion, because clinical trials have not shown that a screening strategy improves the prioritized health outcomes. It also states that outcome-specific 25(OH)D thresholds for disease prevention have not been established. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae290)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/109/8/1907/7685305))

The “dark complexion” recommendation is therefore best read as: there is no trial evidence that routine screening of generally healthy adults with dark complexion improves disease-prevention outcomes. It should not be read as: dark-skinned populations have low deficiency rates, or deficiency is clinically irrelevant in high-prevalence regions. The guideline itself notes that race is a problematic proxy, that no trials directly tested screening by skin complexion, and that consequences in resource-poor settings and dark-complexioned populations are not well studied. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae290)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article-abstract/109/8/1907/7685305))

## Why high prevalence changes the testing argument

This section is a clinical-reasoning argument based on prevalence and actionability rather than a direct screening-trial result. Testing may become more informative when the prior probability of deficiency is high and the result is actionable. In a low-prevalence population, many tests may identify people who are already sufficient or only mildly low, and testing may not change management. In a high-prevalence population, the same test is more likely, on prevalence grounds, to confirm biochemical deficiency, quantify severity, and distinguish someone who may need structured repletion from someone who may only need routine maintenance.

That is a Bayesian argument about information yield, not proof that universal screening improves fractures, falls, infections, diabetes, cardiovascular outcomes, or mortality. The strongest evidence gap is still the same: there are very few trials comparing “test-and-treat” strategies against empiric supplementation strategies in high-prevalence South Asian settings. The 2024 systematic review supporting the Endocrine Society guideline found no eligible screening-strategy trials in healthy adults, adults with obesity, or adults with dark complexion. ([Shah et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1961/7686350))

## What Indian and South Asian prevalence data show

A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis of South Asian adults included 65 studies and 44,717 participants. Using a deficiency cutoff of 25(OH)D below 20 ng/mL, the pooled prevalence of vitamin D deficiency was 68 percent, with country estimates of 73 percent in Pakistan, 67 percent in Bangladesh, 67 percent in India, 57 percent in Nepal, and 48 percent in Sri Lanka. The weighted mean 25(OH)D concentration was 19.15 ng/mL. The authors reported substantial heterogeneity, so the pooled value should be treated as a broad regional estimate rather than a precise nationally representative number for every district, season, sex, caste, diet pattern, or socioeconomic group. ([Siddiqee et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-021-11888-1)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12889-021-11888-1))

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis in South Asian children and adolescents included 41 studies and 18,233 participants. Using a 25(OH)D cutoff below 20 ng/mL for hypovitaminosis D, the pooled prevalence was 61 percent overall, with country estimates of 96.2 percent in Afghanistan, 94 percent in Pakistan, 64 percent in India, 35.5 percent in Bangladesh, 35 percent in Nepal, and 25 percent in Sri Lanka. Neonates had the highest pooled estimate at 85 percent. ([Siddiqee et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1186/s41043-022-00287-w)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41043-022-00287-w))

Indian reviews often report very wide prevalence ranges because studies differ by region, age, season, clothing, sun exposure, diet, assay, and cutoff. One review of selected Indian studies reported hypovitaminosis D ranges of 84.9 to 100 percent in schoolchildren, 42 to 74 percent in pregnant women, 44.3 to 66.7 percent in infants, 70 to 81.1 percent in lactating mothers, and 30 to 91.2 percent in adults. The same review emphasized that different cutoffs and assays make direct comparisons difficult. ([Kamboj et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.4103/ijmr.IJMR_1807_18)) ([Indian Journal of Medical Research](https://ijmr.org.in/prevalence-of-hypovitaminosis-d-in-india-way-forward/))

More nationally representative data can look less dramatic when a stricter deficiency cutoff is used. In India’s Comprehensive National Nutrition Survey, vitamin D deficiency was defined as 25(OH)D below 12 ng/mL. Using that threshold, deficiency prevalence was 13.7 percent in children aged 1 to 4 years, 18.2 percent in children aged 5 to 9 years, and 23.9 percent in adolescents aged 10 to 19 years. That does not necessarily contradict the higher estimates; it illustrates that prevalence changes sharply depending on whether “deficiency” means below 12 ng/mL, below 20 ng/mL, below 30 ng/mL, or below another threshold. ([Rana et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13312-023-2835-1)) ([Indian Pediatrics](https://www.indianpediatrics.net/epub012023/RP-00479.pdf))

A multicentre Indian study in children and adolescents measured dried blood spot 25(OH)D3 using LC-MS/MS. LC-MS/MS methods are generally more consistent and accurate than many immunoassays when standardized and quality-controlled. Using 50 nmol/L as the sufficiency cutoff, about 58 percent were below sufficiency: 20 percent deficient and 38 percent insufficient. If 75 nmol/L was used as the cutoff, only about 10 percent were classified as sufficient. ([Khadilkar et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-022-21279-0)) ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-21279-0)) ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Testing yield is not the same as outcome proof

High prevalence supports the clinical-reasoning claim that a baseline test is more likely to be informative when it would change management. It does not automatically prove that routine testing improves clinical outcomes compared with empiric supplementation, food fortification, safe sunlight interventions, or targeted testing of high-risk groups.

This distinction matters because guidelines usually require outcome evidence, not just biochemical prevalence evidence. The Endocrine Society’s supporting systematic review found no trials that directly evaluated 25(OH)D screening strategies in healthy adults, adults with dark complexion, or adults with obesity. It also noted that most vitamin D trials enrolled people whose baseline vitamin D levels were not severely low, which limits certainty about whether people with very low baseline levels benefit more. ([Shah et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1961/7686350))

So the more accurate position is: in India and similar high-prevalence regions, a baseline 25(OH)D test has stronger practical plausibility than it does in low-prevalence settings, especially before high-dose treatment. But the hard-outcome evidence for population-wide screening remains incomplete.

## Supplementation practices: why measurement can have harm-reduction value

The Indian supplement market includes common high-dose cholecalciferol preparations such as 60,000 IU sachets, capsules, tablets, and granules. A review of vitamin D products in the Indian market reported that 60,000 IU cholecalciferol sachets were commonly administered weekly, and described a common regimen of 60,000 IU weekly for about eight weeks followed by monthly maintenance. The same paper warned that many available products contained calcitriol or alfacalcidol, which are not preferred for uncomplicated nutritional vitamin D deficiency and can increase hypercalcemia risk if used inappropriately. This is useful evidence of product availability and described practice, not a current market-share estimate. ([Lhamo et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.4103/0250-474X.180248))

A small open-label randomized trial in vitamin D-deficient adults compared 60,000 IU weekly with 1,000 IU daily for 10 weeks. The weekly high-dose regimen raised 25(OH)D more than the daily low-dose regimen over the short follow-up period. This is evidence that high-dose repletion can correct biochemistry faster, not evidence that everyone in a high-prevalence region should receive high-dose therapy without measurement. The paper reported no financial support and no author conflicts, but the vitamin D and calcium tablets were provided by Lupin Limited and Sun Pharmaceutical Industries, so product support should be kept in mind. ([Singh et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.4103/jfmpc.jfmpc_272_19)) ([PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6618212/))

A 2025 survey of 74 Indian clinicians reported that 55 percent prescribed 60,000 IU weekly for eight weeks and 64 percent used monthly maintenance dosing. This should be treated as low-level practice-pattern evidence, not high-quality outcome evidence. The survey was supported by Abbott India, and the manuscript states that the authors received support for manuscript development from Abbott India; the findings should be interpreted with that funding relationship in mind. ([Modi and Reddy, 2025](https://www.ijmscr.com/asset/images/uploads/17650884316043.pdf)) ([IJMSCR](https://www.ijmscr.com/asset/images/uploads/17650884316043.pdf))

In this context, testing may have harm-reduction value in both directions. A baseline 25(OH)D value can identify severe deficiency that may need structured repletion and follow-up. It can also identify people who are already sufficient or only mildly low, for whom repeated high-dose boluses may offer little benefit and could increase risk if layered on top of calcium, calcitriol, fortified foods, injections, or unsupervised supplement use. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

## Harm reduction cuts both ways

Vitamin D toxicity is uncommon, but it is clinically important when it occurs because it can cause hypercalcemia and kidney complications. A 2024 international consensus statement notes that toxicity is rare, usually involves very high 25(OH)D levels, and is often linked to unintentional overdosing or inappropriate pharmaceutical use. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Routine repeat testing is not usually necessary for people taking ordinary maintenance doses such as up to 2,000 IU/day, but retesting after about 8 to 12 weeks may be appropriate when adherence is uncertain, symptoms suggest persistent deficiency, risk factors remain strong, or high-dose treatment has been used. The same consensus statement notes that monitoring treatment can be important for efficacy and safety, while acknowledging that evidence on the best monitoring interval is limited. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Intermittent high-dose dosing deserves special caution. The Endocrine Society’s supporting systematic review found no significant benefit of higher-dose intermittent vitamin D for several outcomes and reported that intermittent dosing at intervals longer than 12 weeks was associated with increased falls in subgroup analysis. A prior meta-analysis in older adults also found that high-dose intermittent vitamin D did not reduce falls or fractures. ([Shah et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312); [Zheng et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0115850)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1961/7686350)) ([PLOS One](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0115850))

This does not mean every weekly repletion regimen is unsafe. It means that “more” and “less frequent” are not automatically better, and that repeated bolus dosing without knowing baseline status, response, serum calcium status, kidney function, formulation, cumulative dose, or concurrent calcitriol/calcium use can be a poor harm-reduction strategy.

## Cutoffs and assays need localization

Serum total 25(OH)D is the accepted biomarker for vitamin D status, but interpretation is limited by assay variability, cutoff disagreement, and uncertainty about what level is optimal for which outcome. A 2024 consensus statement emphasized that assay standardization remains a major challenge and that desirable 25(OH)D levels may vary by clinical context. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

LC-MS/MS methods are generally more consistent and accurate than many immunoassays when standardized and quality-controlled, and laboratories should report method, units, and reference values clearly. This matters in India and South Asia because prevalence estimates can shift substantially when studies use different assays or define deficiency at different thresholds. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

Localization should not mean simply choosing a higher or lower cutoff because it feels appropriate. As a research and implementation goal, it should mean validating thresholds against relevant outcomes in the local population: rickets, osteomalacia, bone mineral outcomes, parathyroid hormone response, calcium and phosphate status, pregnancy outcomes, dietary calcium intake, sun exposure, season, skin pigmentation, air pollution, clothing, and assay method.

## Practical interpretation

- The 2024 “don’t test routinely” recommendation is about disease-prevention screening in people without established indications. It is not a claim that vitamin D deficiency is rare in India or South Asia. ([Demay et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae290)) ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease))

- In high-prevalence regions, one baseline 25(OH)D test is more likely to change management than it would in a low-prevalence setting, especially before high-dose loading or prolonged intermittent supplementation. That conclusion is based on prevalence and clinical reasoning; direct test-versus-empiric outcome trials remain limited. ([Siddiqee et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-021-11888-1); [Shah et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgae312)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12889-021-11888-1)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/109/8/1961/7686350))

- Empiric low-dose supplementation, food fortification, and targeted testing are not mutually exclusive strategies. In high-prevalence settings, testing is most defensible when symptoms, skeletal disease, malabsorption, obesity, kidney/liver/parathyroid disease, interacting medications, pregnancy-related concerns, planned high-dose therapy, or possible toxicity would make the result actionable. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))

- Local assay validation and outcome-linked cutoffs matter. Applying Western thresholds without considering assay method, dietary calcium intake, sun exposure, season, skin pigmentation, and local skeletal outcomes can make testing noisier than it needs to be. ([Giustina et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1210/endrev/bnae009)) ([OUP Academic](https://academic.oup.com/edrv/article/45/5/625/7659127))
