# Vitamin D for Skin and Hair: The Wellness Loop

Vitamin D is real skin and hair biology, but it is not a general "fix your D, fix your skin and hair" intervention. The strongest evidence separates three different things that wellness content often collapses: rare vitamin D receptor disorders that can cause hereditary alopecia ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0303720711003005 "The role of vitamin D receptor mutations in the development of alopecia")); topical vitamin D analogues used directly on psoriatic skin ([Cochrane](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD005028_skin-treatments-chronic-plaque-psoriasis "Skin treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis")); and oral vitamin D supplementation, which has much weaker evidence for common hair-loss and inflammatory skin conditions ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33690970/ "Vitamin D status and efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in acne patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39683522/ "Vitamin D Supplementation for Treating Atopic Dermatitis in Children and Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis - PubMed")).

## What the vitamin D claim gets right

Vitamin D signaling matters in skin. The vitamin D receptor is involved in hair-follicle regulation, and mutations affecting vitamin D receptor function can produce hereditary vitamin D-resistant rickets with alopecia. That is a genuine biological link, but it is a rare genetic phenotype, not evidence that oral vitamin D regrows common hair loss in otherwise replete people. ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0303720711003005 "The role of vitamin D receptor mutations in the development of alopecia"))

Topical vitamin D analogues are also legitimate dermatologic drugs. Calcipotriol, calcitriol, and related analogues are evidence-based topical treatments for plaque psoriasis, especially when used with topical corticosteroids. That is not the same exposure as raising serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D with an oral supplement. ([Cochrane](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD005028_skin-treatments-chronic-plaque-psoriasis "Skin treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"))

## Hair loss: low vitamin D is associated, but causality is not established

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of non-scarring alopecia found lower mean serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D in people with androgenetic alopecia, female pattern hair loss, alopecia areata, or telogen effluvium compared with controls. It included 23 studies with 3,374 patients and 7,296 controls, and reported lower 25-hydroxyvitamin D and higher odds of vitamin D deficiency in pooled alopecia analyses; subtype differences and high heterogeneity limit causal interpretation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

That pattern supports "low vitamin D is common in people with hair-loss diagnoses," not "vitamin D deficiency is the cause of the hair loss." Most included studies were observational, and the review reported substantial heterogeneity; that limits causal interpretation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

For telogen effluvium, the intervention evidence is much thinner than the association evidence. One open-label study of 40 women with telogen effluvium reported lower baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D than controls and improvement after three months of oral vitamin D, but it did not include a placebo group; the authors framed the findings as promising and in need of larger-scale evidence. The study reported no conflicts of interest. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39170090/ "Oral Vitamin D Treatment in Patients with Telogen Effluvium: Clinical and Dermoscopic Evaluation - PubMed"))

Another study treated 40 women with diffuse hair loss/telogen effluvium using repeated high-dose oral vitamin D3 and reported improvement, but it also lacked a placebo-control arm. Without a blinded control group, improvement can reflect natural recovery, regression to the mean, concurrent care, seasonal change, or correction of a true deficiency rather than a general regrowth effect of vitamin D. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33642467/ "Efficacy of Oral Vitamin D3 Therapy in Patients Suffering from Diffuse Hair Loss (Telogen Effluvium) - PubMed"))

For female pattern hair loss and androgenetic alopecia, the evidence is not strong enough to treat oral vitamin D as a stand-alone regrowth therapy. Small comparative studies exist, including trials combining oral vitamin D with minoxidil, but they do not establish that vitamin D alone regrows hair in replete or mildly insufficient patients. ([Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jocd.14743 "Treatment with oral vitamin D alone, topical minoxidil, or combination of both in patients with female pattern hair loss"))

## Alopecia areata and the VDR link

Alopecia areata has a stronger immune and vitamin D association than many other hair-loss claims, but the same caution applies. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses report lower serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D and higher prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in alopecia areata patients compared with controls. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29633370/ "Increased prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in patients with alopecia areata"))

That does not mean oral vitamin D supplementation has been shown to reverse alopecia areata. A protocol paper on vitamin D supplementation for alopecia areata noted that conclusive evidence supporting supplementation was lacking, which is a good summary of the state of the oral-supplement evidence: biologically plausible, clinically underproven. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36281137/ "Vitamin D supplementation for patients with alopecia areata"))

Topical vitamin D analogues have been studied in alopecia areata, including calcipotriol trials and pilot studies, but this again points to local pharmacologic treatment rather than a broad "take vitamin D for hair growth" rule. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31236672/ "Topical calcipotriol vs narrowband ultraviolet B in treatment of alopecia areata"))

## Psoriasis: topical vitamin D works; oral vitamin D is a different claim

Psoriasis is the clearest example of the wellness conflation. Topical vitamin D analogues are evidence-based psoriasis treatments, and Cochrane-reviewed topical therapy evidence supports vitamin D analogues, especially in combination with topical corticosteroids, for chronic plaque psoriasis. ([Cochrane](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD005028_skin-treatments-chronic-plaque-psoriasis "Skin treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"))

But oral vitamin D supplementation has not shown the same kind of benefit. In a randomized clinical trial of adults with active plaque psoriasis, generally low baseline severity, and 25-hydroxyvitamin D below 24 ng/mL, vitamin D3 supplementation with a loading dose followed by 20,000 IU weekly for four months did not produce a measurable improvement in psoriasis severity, physician global assessment, or quality of life compared with placebo. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"))

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of four randomized trials, including 333 psoriasis patients, likewise found no robust overall improvement in PASI, quality-of-life measures, or inflammatory markers with vitamin D supplementation. The review reported no specific funding and no competing interests. ([PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

The practical distinction is simple: topical vitamin D analogues are psoriasis drugs; oral vitamin D is not proven to be a psoriasis treatment just because psoriasis skin responds to local vitamin D analogue medication. ([Cochrane Library](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005028.pub3/full "Topical treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

## Acne, hidradenitis suppurativa, and atopic dermatitis

Acne studies show an association between lower vitamin D status and acne, but the treatment evidence is still narrow. A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis found lower serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D in acne patients and included both observational studies and supplementation trials; the review listed Beijing Natural Science Foundation support, not supplement-industry funding. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33690970/ "Vitamin D status and efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in acne patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

A more recent double-blind randomized trial tested weekly vitamin D2 as an adjunct to topical benzoyl peroxide in mild-to-moderate acne and reported reduced relapse of inflammatory lesions. That is a useful signal, but it is adjunctive acne care, not evidence that vitamin D is a primary acne treatment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38957412/ "Role of vitamin D supplement adjunct to topical benzoyl peroxide in acne"))

For hidradenitis suppurativa, the evidence remains mostly observational. A 2024 systematic review included eight studies from 2014 to 2024, covering case-control, cross-sectional, and cohort designs; vitamin D deficiency was common in HS cohorts, and some studies linked lower vitamin D with severity, but the review still called for further research on supplementation, dosing, and outcomes. The review declared no competing interests. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"))

Atopic dermatitis has mixed, modest supplementation evidence, but it is still not a universal "vitamin D fixes eczema" story. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 11 randomized trials with 686 participants found a modest reduction in atopic dermatitis severity with vitamin D supplementation, with moderate heterogeneity and a call for larger, longer trials. Some review authors disclosed pharmaceutical relationships, mostly with dermatology and allergy companies rather than vitamin D manufacturers, so the findings should be read with that disclosure in mind. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39683522/ "Vitamin D Supplementation for Treating Atopic Dermatitis in Children and Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"))

A 2024 randomized controlled trial in 101 children found that weekly vitamin D3 improved vitamin D status but did not improve atopic dermatitis severity or type 2 inflammation biomarkers compared with placebo. That result tempers the meta-analysis and supports a cautious interpretation: correcting deficiency may be reasonable, but vitamin D should not be framed as a stand-alone eczema treatment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38483248/ "Effect of weekly vitamin D supplementation on the severity of atopic dermatitis and type 2 immunity biomarkers in children"))

## Sun exposure: vitamin D synthesis is not a reason to abandon photoprotection

The "stop avoiding the sun, your skin needs vitamin D" claim mixes two different outcomes: vitamin D synthesis and UV-induced skin damage. A systematic review found that experimental sunscreen use can block vitamin D production under artificial UV conditions, but field trials and observational studies generally did not show that real-world sunscreen use lowers 25-hydroxyvitamin D. The authors concluded that vitamin D concerns should not undermine skin-cancer prevention advice, while noting that high-SPF evidence was limited. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30945275/ "The effect of sunscreen on vitamin D: a review - PubMed"))

A randomized sun-holiday trial found that SPF 15 sunscreens used under very high UV conditions prevented sunburn while still allowing vitamin D synthesis. That trial included authors affiliated with Walgreens Boots Alliance, so its formulation-specific findings should be interpreted with that potential commercial context in mind. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31069787/ "Optimal sunscreen use, during a sun holiday with a very high ultraviolet index, allows vitamin D synthesis without sunburn - PubMed"))

Photoprotection has direct skin-outcome evidence. In a randomized trial of 903 adults followed for 4.5 years, daily sunscreen use was associated with less skin aging than discretionary sunscreen use, with the trial funded by the Australian National Health and Medical Research Council. Earlier randomized sunscreen trials also evaluated prevention of keratinocyte cancers under regular sunscreen use. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-158-11-201306040-00002 "Sunscreen and prevention of skin aging: A randomized trial"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10475183/ "Daily sunscreen application and betacarotene supplementation in prevention of basal-cell and squamous-cell carcinomas of the skin: a randomized controlled trial"))

## Quick reference values

- Non-scarring alopecia: a 2024 meta-analysis found lower 25-hydroxyvitamin D and pooled higher odds of deficiency in alopecia groups, but subtype differences, high heterogeneity, and observational designs limit causal claims. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

- Telogen effluvium: small oral vitamin D studies report improvement, but the key studies are open-label or uncontrolled rather than blinded placebo-controlled regrowth trials. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39170090/ "Oral Vitamin D Treatment in Patients with Telogen Effluvium: Clinical and Dermoscopic Evaluation - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33642467/ "Efficacy of Oral Vitamin D3 Therapy in Patients Suffering from Diffuse Hair Loss (Telogen Effluvium) - PubMed"))

- Alopecia areata: lower vitamin D status is repeatedly observed, but oral supplementation as a regrowth treatment remains unproven. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29633370/ "Increased prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in patients with alopecia areata"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36281137/ "Vitamin D supplementation for patients with alopecia areata"))

- Psoriasis: topical vitamin D analogues are evidence-based; oral vitamin D supplementation has been mostly null or clinically unconvincing in randomized psoriasis trials and meta-analysis. ([Cochrane Library](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005028.pub3/full "Topical treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

- Acne and HS: evidence is mostly association plus small or adjunctive supplementation studies, not strong evidence for vitamin D as a primary treatment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33690970/ "Vitamin D status and efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in acne patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38957412/ "Role of vitamin D supplement adjunct to topical benzoyl peroxide in acne"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"))

- Atopic dermatitis: RCT meta-analysis suggests a modest average benefit, but heterogeneity and a newer null pediatric RCT argue against overclaiming. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39683522/ "Vitamin D Supplementation for Treating Atopic Dermatitis in Children and Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38483248/ "Effect of weekly vitamin D supplementation on the severity of atopic dermatitis and type 2 immunity biomarkers in children"))

- Sun exposure: real-world sunscreen use generally does not appear to meaningfully compromise vitamin D status, and sunscreen has direct evidence for reducing sunburn and photoaging outcomes. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30945275/ "The effect of sunscreen on vitamin D: a review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31069787/ "Optimal sunscreen use, during a sun holiday with a very high ultraviolet index, allows vitamin D synthesis without sunburn - PubMed"), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-158-11-201306040-00002 "Sunscreen and prevention of skin aging: A randomized trial"))


## Practical interpretation

- Low vitamin D can coexist with hair loss, acne, HS, eczema, or psoriasis, but coexistence is not proof that low vitamin D caused the condition. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33690970/ "Vitamin D status and efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in acne patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39683522/ "Vitamin D Supplementation for Treating Atopic Dermatitis in Children and Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

- Correcting a documented deficiency is different from using vitamin D as a disease-specific skin or hair treatment. The evidence is strongest for deficiency correction as general medical care and weakest for "extra vitamin D" in people who are already replete. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin D - Health Professional Fact Sheet"))

- High-dose vitamin D regimens used in small studies should not be copied as wellness protocols without medical guidance; vitamin D excess can be harmful, and testing or dosing decisions depend on clinical context. ([NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/ "Vitamin D - Health Professional Fact Sheet"))

- Psoriasis is the main place where the vitamin D story is strongest, but mainly through topical analogues, not oral supplementation. ([Cochrane Library](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005028.pub3/full "Topical treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"))

- For hair loss, vitamin D testing may be part of a broader workup when deficiency risk is plausible, but oral vitamin D should not be sold as a proven regrowth treatment for telogen effluvium, female pattern hair loss, androgenetic alopecia, or alopecia areata. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39170090/ "Oral Vitamin D Treatment in Patients with Telogen Effluvium: Clinical and Dermoscopic Evaluation - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33642467/ "Efficacy of Oral Vitamin D3 Therapy in Patients Suffering from Diffuse Hair Loss (Telogen Effluvium) - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29633370/ "Increased prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in patients with alopecia areata"), [Wiley Online Library](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jocd.14743 "Treatment with oral vitamin D alone, topical minoxidil, or combination of both in patients with female pattern hair loss"))

- For acne, HS, and atopic dermatitis, vitamin D is best framed as a possible adjunct or deficiency-correction issue, not a replacement for standard dermatologic treatment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38957412/ "Role of vitamin D supplement adjunct to topical benzoyl peroxide in acne"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39683522/ "Vitamin D Supplementation for Treating Atopic Dermatitis in Children and Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38483248/ "Effect of weekly vitamin D supplementation on the severity of atopic dermatitis and type 2 immunity biomarkers in children"))

- Sun protection and vitamin D status should not be treated as opposites. The available field-trial evidence does not support abandoning sunscreen for vitamin D. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30945275/ "The effect of sunscreen on vitamin D: a review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31069787/ "Optimal sunscreen use, during a sun holiday with a very high ultraviolet index, allows vitamin D synthesis without sunburn - PubMed"))


## Evidence gaps

The most useful next studies would be placebo-controlled oral vitamin D trials for telogen effluvium and androgenetic alopecia stratified by baseline 25-hydroxyvitamin D; dose-response trials in alopecia areata; head-to-head comparisons of oral supplementation versus topical vitamin D analogues in inflammatory dermatoses; prospective acne and HS trials that test whether correcting deficiency changes disease trajectory independent of standard therapy; and UV dosimetry studies that measure vitamin D synthesis alongside erythema, DNA damage, photoaging markers, and skin-cancer-relevant endpoints. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38010941/ "Serum 25 hydroxyvitamin D in non-scarring alopecia: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29633370/ "Increased prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in patients with alopecia areata"), [Cochrane Library](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD005028.pub3/full "Topical treatments for chronic plaque psoriasis"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamadermatology/fullarticle/2802780 "Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Psoriasis Severity in Patients With Lower-Range Serum 25-Hydroxyvitamin D Levels: A Randomized Clinical Trial"), [PLOS](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0294239 "Efficacy and safety of vitamin D supplementation on psoriasis: A systematic review and meta-analysis"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33690970/ "Vitamin D status and efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in acne patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39549121/ "Relationship of vitamin D to pathogenesis and outcomes of hidradenitis suppurativa: a systematic review - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30945275/ "The effect of sunscreen on vitamin D: a review - PubMed"), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-158-11-201306040-00002 "Sunscreen and prevention of skin aging: A randomized trial"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10475183/ "Daily sunscreen application and betacarotene supplementation in prevention of basal-cell and squamous-cell carcinomas of the skin: a randomized controlled trial"))
