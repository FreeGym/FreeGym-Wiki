# Micronutrients

The most debated questions about vitamins, minerals, and micronutrient supplementation.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Do You Need a Multivitamin?

Multivitamins are the most popular supplement in the world. Doctors are split. Some call them insurance, others call them expensive urine.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people have dietary gaps, and a multivitamin is cheap insurance against deficiency
2. Large RCTs (like the Physicians' Health Study II) show minimal benefit for disease prevention in well-nourished populations
3. Multivitamins may cause harm by providing nutrients in forms or doses the body doesn't handle well (e.g., folic acid vs folate, beta-carotene in smokers)
4. If your diet is reasonable, you don't need one; if your diet is terrible, a pill won't fix it

**Evidence needed:** Large-scale multivitamin RCTs, nutrient status surveys across populations, bioavailability comparisons of supplemental vs dietary forms.

## Are RDAs Too Low?

The Recommended Dietary Allowance is set to prevent deficiency in 97.5% of healthy people. But preventing deficiency and achieving optimal health may not be the same thing.

**Competing claims:**

1. RDAs are sufficient for health and higher intakes offer no additional benefit
2. RDAs prevent clinical deficiency but don't reflect optimal levels, especially for vitamin D, magnesium, and omega-3s
3. "Optimal" is poorly defined and often used to sell supplements at megadoses
4. Individual variation in genetics, gut absorption, and lifestyle means a single RDA can't apply to everyone

**Evidence needed:** Studies comparing RDA-level intake vs higher intake on functional outcomes, biomarker optimization research, genetic variation in nutrient metabolism.

## Vitamin D: How Much Is Enough?

Vitamin D might be the single most debated micronutrient. Deficiency is widespread. Supplement recommendations range from 600 IU to 10,000 IU daily. Blood level targets vary by organization.

For the full breakdown of the vitamin D debate - testing, thresholds, trial design problems, athlete-specific considerations, and regional context - see [Vitamin D Deep Dive](vitamin-d.md).

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people are deficient and should supplement with 2,000-5,000 IU daily to reach 40-60 ng/mL
2. The "deficiency epidemic" is overblown due to arbitrary cutoffs; 20 ng/mL is sufficient for most people
3. Vitamin D supplementation improves bone health, immunity, and mood
4. Large RCTs (VITAL) show that supplementation doesn't reduce cancer, heart disease, or fracture risk in people who aren't severely deficient

**Evidence needed:** VITAL trial data, dose-response studies, meta-analyses on vitamin D and health outcomes, optimal blood level threshold research.

## Do Athletes Need More Micronutrients?

Training increases metabolic demand, sweat losses, and oxidative stress. But does this translate to higher micronutrient requirements?

**Competing claims:**

1. Athletes have significantly higher needs for iron, zinc, magnesium, and B vitamins due to increased turnover and losses
2. Athletes generally eat more food, so they get more micronutrients without supplementation
3. Female athletes, those in caloric restriction, and endurance athletes are at genuine risk of deficiency
4. Supplementing beyond dietary intake doesn't enhance performance unless you're correcting a deficiency

**Evidence needed:** Micronutrient status studies in athletic populations, supplementation and performance trials, iron and ferritin research in endurance athletes.

## Is Iron Supplementation Overprescribed or Underprescribed?

Iron deficiency is the most common nutrient deficiency worldwide. But iron overload is also harmful. Testing and treatment thresholds vary widely.

**Competing claims:**

1. Iron deficiency is underdiagnosed, especially in women, athletes, and vegetarians, and more people should supplement
2. Routine iron supplementation without testing is dangerous because excess iron is pro-oxidant and pro-inflammatory
3. Ferritin cutoffs for "deficiency" are too low in standard practice; functional deficiency begins well above clinical cutoffs
4. Heme iron from meat is the safest way to maintain status; supplemental iron causes GI issues and may feed pathogenic gut bacteria

**Evidence needed:** Ferritin threshold studies, iron supplementation trials in athletes and women, heme vs non-heme iron absorption data, iron and gut microbiome research.

## Magnesium: Is Everyone Deficient?

Magnesium is involved in 300+ enzymatic reactions. Supplement advocates claim widespread deficiency. Skeptics say the data is overstated.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people don't meet the RDA for magnesium through diet, and supplementation improves sleep, stress, and muscle function
2. Dietary surveys overstate deficiency because they don't account for magnesium in water and underreport food intake
3. Serum magnesium (the standard test) is a poor marker of total body status, so we don't actually know how many people are deficient
4. Magnesium supplementation benefits are largely placebo or confounded by other lifestyle changes

**Evidence needed:** Magnesium intake surveys, serum vs intracellular magnesium measurement studies, supplementation RCTs for sleep, cramps, and performance.

## Antioxidant Supplements: Helpful or Harmful?

Vitamins C, E, and other antioxidants were once promoted as disease preventers. Then trials showed they might cause harm, especially in high doses.

**Competing claims:**

1. Antioxidant-rich diets reduce disease risk, so antioxidant supplements should too
2. Supplemental antioxidants at high doses increase mortality risk (vitamin E, beta-carotene trials)
3. Antioxidant supplements blunt the adaptive response to exercise, reducing training benefits
4. The issue is dose and form: food-based antioxidants behave differently than isolated megadoses

**Evidence needed:** Antioxidant supplementation RCTs, exercise adaptation studies (Ristow et al.), vitamin C/E and training response data.

## Zinc: Immune Booster or Overhyped?

Zinc lozenges for colds. Zinc for testosterone. Zinc for immune function. The claims are broad. The evidence is mixed.

**Competing claims:**

1. Zinc supplementation reduces the duration and severity of colds
2. Zinc only helps if you're already deficient, and most people in developed countries get enough
3. Zinc supplementation boosts testosterone in deficient men but has no effect on men with adequate levels
4. High-dose zinc supplementation depletes copper and causes its own set of problems

**Evidence needed:** Zinc and common cold meta-analyses, zinc and testosterone studies, zinc-copper interaction data, population-level zinc status research.

## Should You Get Micronutrients From Food or Supplements?

"Food first" is standard advice. But supplements are more convenient and allow precise dosing. Does it actually matter where your nutrients come from?

**Competing claims:**

1. Nutrients from whole foods are better absorbed and come with synergistic co-factors
2. Supplemental forms of most nutrients are equally bioavailable and more practical
3. Some nutrients are difficult to get in sufficient amounts from food alone (vitamin D, omega-3s if you don't eat fish)
4. The food matrix matters: nutrients behave differently in isolation than in a food context

**Evidence needed:** Bioavailability comparisons of food vs supplemental nutrients, food matrix effect studies, nutrient interaction research.

## Electrolytes: Do You Need to Supplement?

Sodium, potassium, and magnesium are critical for performance and health. Sports drinks, salt tablets, and electrolyte mixes are everywhere. But most people just need food and water.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people get enough electrolytes from a normal diet and don't need supplements
2. Athletes training in heat or for long durations need deliberate sodium and potassium replacement
3. Low-carb and ketogenic dieters need extra electrolytes because insulin reduction increases sodium excretion
4. The sodium fear is overblown; most active people can and should consume more salt than guidelines recommend

**Evidence needed:** Electrolyte loss studies during exercise, sodium and performance research, hyponatremia case data, low-carb electrolyte depletion studies.

## Vitamin K2: The Missing Vitamin?

Vitamin K1 (from leafy greens) handles blood clotting. Vitamin K2 (from fermented foods and animal products) may play a completely different role - directing calcium into bones and out of arteries. Yet most guidelines don't distinguish between the two.

**Competing claims:**

1. K2 supplementation reduces cardiovascular calcification and improves bone density independently of K1
2. The evidence for K2 is mostly observational and from small trials; large RCTs are lacking
3. Vitamin D supplementation without adequate K2 may cause calcium to deposit in arteries rather than bones
4. Most people get enough K2 from diet (cheese, eggs, meat) and supplementation is unnecessary

**Evidence needed:** K2 and arterial calcification RCTs, D3+K2 synergy studies, K1 vs K2 functional comparisons, MK-4 vs MK-7 subform research.

## Vitamin B12: Who's Actually Deficient?

B12 deficiency can cause irreversible nerve damage, yet it develops slowly and is often missed. Vegans are the obvious at-risk group, but elderly adults, people on acid-suppressing medications, and those with gut issues may also be deficient.

**Competing claims:**

1. B12 deficiency is common and underdiagnosed, especially in vegans, vegetarians, and the elderly
2. Most omnivores get plenty of B12 and don't need to worry about it
3. Serum B12 is a poor marker; functional markers like methylmalonic acid and homocysteine are needed to detect subclinical deficiency
4. The form matters: methylcobalamin and hydroxocobalamin are superior to cyanocobalamin, which requires conversion and contains a cyanide molecule

**Evidence needed:** B12 status surveys across dietary patterns, functional marker vs serum B12 comparisons, cyanocobalamin vs methylcobalamin bioavailability studies, B12 absorption decline with age data.

## Vitamin A: Can You Get Enough From Plants?

Vitamin A is essential for vision, immunity, and skin. But there are two forms: preformed retinol (from animal foods) and beta-carotene (from plants), which the body must convert. That conversion is highly variable.

**Competing claims:**

1. Beta-carotene from a varied plant diet provides sufficient vitamin A for most people
2. Genetic polymorphisms in the BCMO1 gene reduce beta-carotene conversion by up to 70% in some individuals, making plant sources inadequate
3. Preformed vitamin A (retinol) is more reliable but carries toxicity risk at high doses, especially during pregnancy
4. Vitamin A deficiency is primarily a developing-world problem; most people in developed countries get enough from either source

**Evidence needed:** BCMO1 polymorphism prevalence and conversion efficiency studies, beta-carotene vs retinol status comparisons in vegetarian populations, vitamin A toxicity dose thresholds, population-level status data.

## Folate (B9): Folic Acid vs Methylfolate

Folic acid fortification of grain products was a public health triumph - neural tube defects dropped dramatically. But folic acid is a synthetic form that requires enzymatic conversion, and not everyone converts it efficiently.

**Competing claims:**

1. Folic acid fortification is safe and effective, and the synthetic form is fine for the vast majority of people
2. MTHFR gene variants (carried by up to 40% of some populations) impair folic acid metabolism, causing unmetabolized folic acid to accumulate with unknown long-term effects
3. Methylfolate (5-MTHF) supplements bypass the MTHFR conversion step and are safer for everyone, regardless of genotype
4. High folic acid intake can mask vitamin B12 deficiency, allowing neurological damage to progress undetected

**Evidence needed:** MTHFR variant prevalence and clinical significance research, unmetabolized folic acid studies, methylfolate vs folic acid supplementation trials, B12 masking case data.

## Calcium: Do You Need Dairy?

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the body. Dairy has been positioned as the primary source for decades. But plant-based advocates say you can get enough without it, and some research questions whether calcium supplements do more harm than good.

**Competing claims:**

1. Dairy is the most bioavailable and practical source of calcium, and populations that consume more dairy have stronger bones
2. Countries with the highest dairy intake (Scandinavia, US) also have the highest osteoporosis rates, suggesting dairy isn't the answer
3. Calcium supplements increase cardiovascular risk by depositing calcium in arterial walls rather than bones
4. Plant sources (kale, broccoli, fortified foods, tofu) provide adequate calcium if planned carefully, but absorption rates vary widely

**Evidence needed:** Calcium supplement and cardiovascular event meta-analyses (Bolland et al.), dairy intake and fracture risk epidemiology, calcium absorption comparisons across food sources, vitamin D and K2 cofactor interaction data.

## Iodine: A Quiet Deficiency Making a Comeback?

Iodine deficiency was largely solved by salt iodization in the 20th century. But as people switch to sea salt, Himalayan salt, and low-sodium diets, intake may be dropping again - and most people don't realize these salts contain little to no iodine.

**Competing claims:**

1. Iodine deficiency is re-emerging in developed countries due to the shift away from iodized salt and declining dairy intake
2. Most people still get enough iodine through diet without thinking about it
3. Kelp and seaweed supplements provide iodine but risk excessive intake, which can be as harmful as deficiency (thyroid dysfunction, autoimmune flares)
4. Subclinical iodine insufficiency impairs thyroid function and metabolism more than commonly recognized

**Evidence needed:** Population iodine status trends (NHANES, urinary iodine data), thyroid function studies at varying iodine intakes, kelp supplement iodine variability testing, impact of non-iodized salt adoption on population status.

## Selenium: Essential in Micrograms, Toxic in Milligrams

Selenium has one of the narrowest safe intake ranges of any nutrient. It's essential for thyroid function and antioxidant defense, but the margin between enough and too much is small. The SELECT trial also killed the idea that selenium prevents cancer.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people in developed countries get adequate selenium from diet and don't need supplements
2. Soil selenium varies enormously by region, so dietary intake depends heavily on where your food is grown (e.g., much of Europe and parts of China have selenium-poor soils)
3. Selenium supplementation prevents cancer - a claim once supported by the NPC trial but contradicted by the larger SELECT trial, which showed no benefit and a possible increase in diabetes risk
4. Brazil nuts are a natural and sufficient source, but selenium content per nut varies so wildly (from 10 to 90+ mcg each) that dosing is unreliable

**Evidence needed:** SELECT and NPC trial data, regional soil selenium and population status mapping, Brazil nut selenium variability analyses, selenium and thyroid function dose-response studies.
