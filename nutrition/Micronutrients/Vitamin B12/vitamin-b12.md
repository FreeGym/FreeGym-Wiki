# Vitamin B12: Deep Dive

B12 is the only vitamin not produced by plants, the only one with a complex multi-step absorption pathway, and one of the few where deficiency causes irreversible neurological damage. It is also one of the easiest deficiencies to miss - serum B12 is a poor marker, symptoms develop slowly, and the most at-risk populations (elderly, vegans, people on PPIs and metformin) are not routinely screened.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Who Is Actually Deficient?

B12 deficiency is concentrated in specific populations, but standard clinical practice often misses subclinical cases by relying on serum B12 alone in inappropriate populations.

**Competing claims:**

1. Vegans and long-term vegetarians without supplementation are at high risk - dietary B12 is essentially absent in plant foods, and stores last 3-5 years before symptoms appear
2. Adults over 60 have a 10-30% prevalence of subclinical B12 deficiency primarily due to atrophic gastritis and reduced acid secretion - this group is severely underdiagnosed
3. Long-term users of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), H2 blockers, and metformin have measurably lower B12 status - the drug interaction is real but rarely flagged at the prescription level
4. Normal-omnivore non-elderly populations are at low risk in absolute terms, but subclinical functional deficiency at "normal serum B12" is more common than headline numbers suggest

**Evidence needed:** B12 deficiency prevalence stratified by age, diet, and medication use; PPI/metformin/B12 longitudinal cohort data; subclinical deficiency outcome studies; population screening cost-effectiveness analyses.

## Serum B12 vs Functional Markers

Serum B12 is the standard test and the wrong test for ruling out functional deficiency. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine elevate before serum B12 falls below standard cutoffs - making them more sensitive markers.

**Competing claims:**

1. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) is the most sensitive marker of functional B12 deficiency - elevated MMA with "normal" serum B12 indicates intracellular deficiency that the standard test misses
2. Holotranscobalamin (active B12) measures the bioavailable fraction and may improve diagnostic specificity, though it is rarely available in standard labs
3. Homocysteine elevation is a non-specific marker (also raised in folate, B6 deficiency, and renal dysfunction) but useful as a screening signal in combination with MMA
4. The persistent reliance on serum B12 alone in primary care is a major source of missed diagnoses, particularly in older adults presenting with fatigue, cognitive complaints, or neuropathy

**Evidence needed:** MMA vs serum B12 diagnostic performance studies, holotranscobalamin clinical utility data, multi-marker B12 screening algorithms, primary care diagnostic accuracy audits.

## Form Matters: Cyanocobalamin vs Methylcobalamin vs Hydroxocobalamin

The form of B12 in supplements differs in stability, absorption, and metabolic processing. The market has shifted toward methylcobalamin partly on biological reasoning and partly on marketing.

**Competing claims:**

1. Cyanocobalamin is the cheapest and most stable form, but requires intracellular conversion to active forms - producing a small cyanide molecule that is normally cleared without harm in healthy people
2. Methylcobalamin and adenosylcobalamin are biologically active forms that bypass the conversion step, theoretically benefiting people with metabolic or detoxification limitations
3. In healthy adults, cyanocobalamin and methylcobalamin produce similar functional outcomes in B12 status correction - the practical advantage of methylcobalamin is modest
4. Hydroxocobalamin (used in injection therapy) has the longest serum half-life and is preferred for rapid correction of severe deficiency; oral hydroxocobalamin is rare commercially

**Evidence needed:** Form-specific B12 absorption and retention RCTs, comparative status correction trials, MMA and homocysteine response by form, head-to-head trials in subgroups (MTHFR variants, smokers, kidney disease).

## Oral High-Dose vs Injection vs Sublingual

Conventional wisdom says severe B12 deficiency requires injections. Evidence for oral high-dose (1000-2000mcg/day) shows it can match injection therapy in most cases, even when intrinsic factor is impaired.

**Competing claims:**

1. Oral B12 at 1000-2000mcg/day produces equivalent status correction to monthly injections in most patients with pernicious anemia, via passive absorption that does not require intrinsic factor
2. Injections remain the standard for severe neurological deficiency where rapid correction matters - the pace of repletion differs even if the final endpoint is similar
3. Sublingual B12 is marketed as superior to oral, but most evidence shows it produces similar absorption to swallowed tablets - the buccal mucosa contribution is minimal
4. Despite oral therapy evidence, monthly injection regimens persist in clinical practice partly due to patient adherence concerns and partly due to billing structures

**Evidence needed:** Oral vs injection B12 RCTs in pernicious anemia, sublingual vs oral pharmacokinetic studies, neurological recovery comparisons by route, clinical practice adoption studies of oral high-dose protocols.

## B12 in Aging: An Underrecognized Lever

Atrophic gastritis affects 20-30% of older adults, reducing intrinsic factor and acid secretion. Combined with PPIs and chronic disease, this makes B12 a high-yield screen in the elderly that is rarely performed.

**Competing claims:**

1. Routine B12 + MMA screening in adults over 65 would detect substantial subclinical deficiency that contributes to cognitive complaints, balance issues, and peripheral neuropathy
2. B12 status correction in deficient elderly produces measurable cognitive and neurological improvement, though severe long-standing deficiency may be partially irreversible
3. The "normal aging" frame absorbs symptoms (fatigue, brain fog, neuropathy) that are sometimes B12 deficiency in disguise - reframing this could shift screening practice
4. Universal screening is not cost-effective by current modeling, but targeted screening of those on PPIs, metformin, with diabetes, or with vegetarian patterns is - this is a hierarchy current guidelines do not consistently promote

**Evidence needed:** B12 supplementation in elderly cognitive outcome trials, atrophic gastritis prevalence studies, targeted screening cost-effectiveness analyses, neurological recovery as a function of deficiency duration.

## Vegan B12: Supplementation Is Non-Negotiable

The plant-foods-only B12 question is unusually settled scientifically and unusually contested culturally. The evidence is clear; supplementation messaging within vegan communities is uneven.

**Competing claims:**

1. Plant foods do not reliably contain B12 - claims about spirulina, nori, fermented foods, and unwashed produce providing adequate B12 are not supported by absorbable-form analyses
2. Vegan and long-term vegetarian populations consistently show lower B12 status without supplementation, with measurable functional consequences (elevated MMA, homocysteine)
3. Affordable supplementation (cyanocobalamin tablets or fortified foods) reliably maintains B12 status in vegans - this is one of the simplest, highest-leverage public health interventions in plant-based populations
4. Despite the clear evidence, B12 messaging within vegan communities has historically been uneven, with some content downplaying the risk and others advocating tests-and-supplements consistently

**Evidence needed:** B12 status studies in long-term vegan cohorts, fortified food vs supplement comparison data, plant-claimed B12 source absorbable-form analyses, vegan community B12 messaging audits.

## Toxicity and Upper Limits

B12 is among the safest vitamins in oral use - no UL is set, allergic reactions are rare, and the body excretes excess. A few emerging signals deserve framing without overclaiming.

**Competing claims:**

1. B12 has no tolerable upper intake level - oral high-dose supplementation (1000-2000mcg/day or higher) has minimal documented toxicity in healthy adults
2. Very high serum B12 levels (>1000 pg/mL) without supplementation have been associated with certain malignancies (liver, hematologic) - but the relationship is almost certainly markers of underlying disease, not B12 causing harm
3. Cyanocobalamin produces a small cyanide molecule on conversion to active forms - clinically irrelevant in healthy people but a theoretical concern in heavy smokers, kidney disease, and Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy where methylcobalamin or hydroxocobalamin is preferred
4. Rare allergic reactions (mostly to injection vehicles or preservatives, not B12 itself) are documented but uncommon - overall B12 ranks among the safest vitamins for routine supplementation

**Evidence needed:** High serum B12 and cancer risk reverse-causation studies, cyanide load from cyanocobalamin in kidney disease research, allergic reaction case data and component analyses, ultra-high-dose B12 long-term safety.

## Interactions

B12 has clinically meaningful drug interactions across multiple medication classes - most well-documented but inconsistently flagged at prescription.

**Competing claims:**

1. Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) and H2 blockers reduce B12 absorption by impairing acid release of food-bound B12 - sustained users often develop subclinical deficiency, with the effect compounding in older adults already low on intrinsic factor
2. Metformin reduces B12 absorption through effects on calcium-dependent ileal absorption - long-term metformin users have measurably lower B12 status, with the deficiency rate increasing with duration and dose
3. Nitrous oxide (N2O) inactivates B12 by oxidizing the cobalt center - recreational and chronic anesthesia exposure can produce acute neurological deficiency in B12-borderline patients, often missed due to "normal" serum B12 with elevated MMA
4. Colchicine, neomycin, and chronic alcohol use also impair B12 absorption to varying degrees - making B12 a high-yield screen in patients on these agents

**Evidence needed:** PPI-B12 deficiency longitudinal cohort studies, metformin-B12 dose-duration effect quantification, nitrous oxide neurological toxicity case series and B12 status data, multi-drug B12 interaction prevalence research.
