# Diet Approaches

The most debated questions about named diets, dietary patterns, and the "which diet is best" question. ## Does the "Best Diet" Exist?

Every few years, a new diet claims superiority. Low-fat. Low-carb. Paleo. Keto. Mediterranean. Carnivore. Vegan. The research consistently points in one direction: long-term adherence matters more than the specific diet. But that answer is unsatisfying to most people.

**Competing claims:**

1. Head-to-head RCTs (DIETFITS, A TO Z, POUNDS LOST) consistently show that named diets produce similar weight loss when calories are matched or when adherence is accounted for - the best diet is the one you can stick to
2. The "all diets work equally" conclusion is misleading because it masks individual variation - some people do genuinely better on low-carb, others on low-fat, and the average hides meaningful responder differences
3. The Mediterranean diet has the strongest overall evidence base for long-term health outcomes (PREDIMED trial), even if it's not always the best for rapid weight loss - "best diet" depends on what outcome you're optimizing
4. "The best diet is the one you can stick to" is a cop-out that avoids the harder question: what makes some diets easier to stick to, and can we design dietary approaches that optimize both health outcomes and adherence?

**Evidence needed:** DIETFITS trial data and individual response analyses, PREDIMED reanalysis, meta-analyses of diet comparison trials, adherence predictors across dietary patterns, individual variability research (insulin sensitivity, genetics, microbiome as predictors of diet response).

## Flexible Dieting (IIFYM) vs Clean Eating

"If It Fits Your Macros" says you can eat anything as long as you hit your protein, carb, fat, and calorie targets. Clean eating says food quality matters beyond macros. Both camps have vocal advocates and legitimate points.

**Competing claims:**

1. For body composition, macros and calories are what matter - you can eat pop-tarts and protein powder and get the same physique results as someone eating chicken and rice, as long as targets are matched
2. Food quality affects health outcomes, satiety, micronutrient status, gut health, and long-term disease risk in ways that macros alone don't capture - hitting your macros from ultra-processed sources is not equivalent to hitting them from whole foods
3. Rigid clean eating creates an all-or-nothing mentality that increases the risk of binge-restrict cycles - flexible dieting allows dietary freedom that improves psychological relationship with food
4. The practical sweet spot is "mostly whole foods with flexible macro tracking" - an 80/20 approach where most intake comes from nutrient-dense sources but no food is categorically off-limits

**Evidence needed:** Body composition outcomes with matched macros from different food sources, dietary flexibility and eating disorder risk studies, micronutrient adequacy at varying food quality levels within the same macro targets, psychological outcomes of rigid vs flexible dietary approaches.

## Is the Carnivore Diet Safe?

The all-meat carnivore diet has grown from a fringe experiment to a visible online movement. Advocates report dramatic improvements in autoimmune conditions, energy, and mental clarity. Critics point out the complete absence of fiber, phytonutrients, and long-term safety data.

**Competing claims:**

1. Anecdotal reports of autoimmune symptom resolution, weight loss, and improved mental health on carnivore are numerous and consistent enough to warrant serious investigation - elimination of plant antigens and lectins may explain the autoimmune benefit
2. There are zero long-term RCTs on the carnivore diet - all evidence is anecdotal, case reports, or short-term - and survivorship bias is extreme (people who feel worse on carnivore don't post about it)
3. Complete elimination of fiber, vitamin C (beyond trace amounts in organ meats), and plant phytonutrients contradicts virtually all epidemiological evidence linking plant intake to reduced disease risk
4. Carnivore may work as a short-term elimination diet to identify food sensitivities, but there's no evidence base for it as a long-term dietary pattern - and the saturated fat and processed meat intake raises cardiovascular and cancer risk questions

**Evidence needed:** Any controlled trials on carnivore diet and health outcomes (few or none exist), nutrient adequacy analyses of all-meat diets, autoimmune case series with proper documentation, long-term cardiovascular and cancer risk data in high-meat populations.

## Can You Build Muscle on a Vegan Diet?

The vegan bodybuilding question has shifted from "is it possible?" (yes, clearly) to "is it optimal?" and "what are the practical challenges?"

**Competing claims:**

1. Meta-analyses show no significant difference in muscle mass or strength gains between plant-based and omnivorous diets when total protein and leucine intake are matched - vegan athletes can build muscle as effectively as omnivores
2. Matching protein and leucine on a vegan diet requires deliberate planning - plant proteins are generally less leucine-dense and less digestible (lower DIAAS scores), so total protein intake may need to be ~10-20% higher to achieve the same MPS stimulus
3. The practical challenges are real but solvable - combining protein sources, supplementing B12, creatine, and potentially iron, omega-3 (algae-based), and vitamin D covers the main nutritional gaps
4. The environmental and ethical motivations for veganism are separate from the performance question - you don't have to argue that vegan diets are nutritionally superior to justify choosing one; "adequate with planning" is a sufficient answer

**Evidence needed:** Vegan vs omnivore resistance training and hypertrophy RCTs (Hevia-Larrain et al., others), DIAAS scores and protein quality comparisons, vegan athlete nutrient adequacy surveys, creatine supplementation benefit in vegan populations.

## Is Paleo Based on Real Science?

The Paleolithic diet argues that humans should eat what our ancestors ate before agriculture: meat, fish, vegetables, fruits, nuts, and seeds - no grains, legumes, dairy, or processed food. The evolutionary logic is appealing. The science behind it is contested.

**Competing claims:**

1. Paleo produces weight loss and metabolic improvements in short-term trials - reduced waist circumference, improved insulin sensitivity, and lower inflammatory markers compared to conventional dietary advice
2. The evolutionary premise is flawed - Paleolithic diets varied enormously by geography and season, many modern foods didn't exist in their current form, and human genetics have continued evolving (lactase persistence, amylase copy number) since the agricultural revolution
3. Eliminating grains, legumes, and dairy removes foods with strong evidence for health benefits - whole grains and legumes are consistently associated with reduced disease risk in large cohort studies, and dairy provides calcium, protein, and fermentation benefits
4. Paleo works for weight loss not because of evolutionary logic but because it eliminates processed food, sugar, and alcohol by default - it's an elimination diet that happens to produce a calorie deficit and better food quality

**Evidence needed:** Paleo diet intervention RCTs with adequate controls and duration, evolutionary biology critique of the "ancestral diet" premise, grain and legume elimination studies (health outcomes), comparison of Paleo outcomes vs other whole-food diets at matched food quality.

## Do Elimination Diets Work for Non-Allergy Conditions?

Beyond diagnosed food allergies and celiac disease, elimination diets are widely used to identify food "sensitivities" or "intolerances" for conditions like IBS, acne, joint pain, brain fog, and fatigue. The evidence ranges from strong (FODMAP for IBS) to weak (most other applications).

**Competing claims:**

1. The low-FODMAP diet has strong RCT evidence for reducing IBS symptoms in the majority of patients - it's a legitimate, evidence-based elimination protocol with a structured reintroduction phase
2. Broader elimination diets (removing gluten, dairy, eggs, soy, etc. simultaneously) may identify triggers but are prone to placebo/nocebo effects, confirmation bias, and unnecessary long-term restriction if reintroduction is done poorly
3. Non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) is a real but likely overdiagnosed condition - blinded rechallenge studies show that many self-diagnosed "gluten sensitive" individuals don't react to gluten when they don't know they're eating it
4. Elimination diets risk nutrient deficiency and disordered eating when maintained long-term without clinical supervision - removing entire food groups requires careful replacement planning

**Evidence needed:** Low-FODMAP IBS RCTs and meta-analyses, NCGS blinded rechallenge studies (Biesiekierski et al., Skodje et al.), elimination diet and nutrient adequacy studies, long-term outcomes of sustained food group elimination.

## Is "Intuitive Eating" Evidence-Based?

Intuitive eating rejects diet culture, calorie counting, and food rules in favor of hunger/fullness cues, body respect, and unconditional food permission. It's gained massive cultural influence. The evidence base is growing but has significant limitations.

**Competing claims:**

1. Intuitive eating is associated with better psychological well-being, lower rates of disordered eating, improved body image, and healthier eating behaviors in observational studies
2. The evidence is almost entirely cross-sectional and observational - people who eat intuitively may be psychologically healthier to begin with, and few RCTs test whether teaching intuitive eating produces these outcomes
3. Intuitive eating is not designed for weight loss and proponents are explicit about this - but it's often presented to people seeking weight loss without clarifying that body composition goals require different tools
4. For people recovering from restrictive eating disorders or chronic dieting, intuitive eating can be genuinely healing - but for people with no history of restriction who simply want to improve body composition, it may lack the structure needed to create change

**Evidence needed:** Intuitive eating RCTs (not just cross-sectional), intuitive eating and body weight outcomes, comparison of intuitive eating vs flexible tracking for psychological and physical outcomes, intuitive eating in clinical eating disorder recovery contexts.

## Should You "Eat for Your Body Type"?

Somatotype-based nutrition (eat for your ectomorph/mesomorph/endomorph body type) persists in fitness culture despite having no scientific support. The broader question is whether body type, genetics, or metabolic phenotype should influence dietary recommendations.

**Competing claims:**

1. The ectomorph/mesomorph/endomorph classification was developed in the 1940s for psychological profiling, not nutrition - it has no validated relationship to metabolic rate, macronutrient needs, or diet response
2. While somatotypes are pseudoscience, the underlying intuition has some truth - people do vary in insulin sensitivity, fat distribution, appetite regulation, and metabolic flexibility, and these differences may affect optimal macro ratios
3. Genetic testing for diet optimization (nutrigenomics) is emerging but still too early for practical recommendations - most genetic variants explain only tiny fractions of dietary response, and the commercial tests oversell their precision
4. The most reliable personalization comes from tracking your own response (body composition, performance, energy, bloodwork) to dietary changes - n-of-1 data beats any classification system

**Evidence needed:** Somatotype and diet response studies (lack thereof), insulin sensitivity and optimal carb intake research, nutrigenomic testing accuracy and clinical utility studies, personalized nutrition trial data (PREDICT study and similar).

## Is Counting Macros Necessary or Just Obsessive?

Macro tracking has become the default approach in fitness nutrition. But not everyone needs it, and for some people it creates more problems than it solves.

**Competing claims:**

1. Macro tracking is the most precise and effective tool for achieving specific body composition goals - it removes guesswork and provides objective feedback that hand-portion or intuitive methods can't match
2. Most people can achieve good results with simpler approaches - hand-portion methods (like Precision Nutrition's system), plate-based guidelines, or just prioritizing protein at each meal and eating mostly whole foods
3. Macro tracking can become compulsive and trigger or worsen disordered eating patterns - especially in people with perfectionist tendencies, anxiety, or a history of restriction
4. The right tool depends on the goal and the person - competitive physique athletes may need precise tracking for contest prep, while recreational fitness enthusiasts often do better with simpler systems that don't dominate their relationship with food

**Evidence needed:** Macro tracking vs simplified approaches for body composition outcomes, psychological impact of food tracking (disordered eating risk studies), precision vs accuracy of different dietary monitoring methods, population-specific recommendations (who benefits from tracking vs who doesn't).
