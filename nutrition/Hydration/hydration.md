# Hydration

The most debated questions about water, electrolytes, and fluid intake for health and performance.

**Status:** Seeking contributors. Each section frames a controversy. Evidence needed.

---

## Do You Really Need 8 Glasses of Water a Day?

The "8x8" rule (eight 8-ounce glasses, roughly 2 liters) is one of the most repeated health recommendations in history. It has no clear scientific origin. Yet it persists everywhere.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people don't drink enough water, and a simple rule like 8 glasses provides a useful minimum target
2. The 8x8 rule has no basis in evidence - a 2002 review found no studies supporting it, and thirst is a reliable guide for most healthy adults
3. Water needs vary enormously by body size, climate, activity level, and diet composition (high-water foods like fruit and vegetables count toward intake)
4. Chronic mild dehydration is real and underrecognized - many people function below optimal hydration without obvious symptoms

**Evidence needed:** Valtin (2002) review of the 8x8 origin, fluid intake surveys vs health outcomes, thirst sensitivity studies across age groups, chronic mild dehydration prevalence data.

## Is Thirst a Reliable Guide?

"Drink before you're thirsty" was exercise science dogma for decades. Then the pendulum swung: "drink to thirst" became the evidence-based recommendation. But thirst may not work equally well for everyone.

**Competing claims:**

1. Thirst is a well-evolved, reliable mechanism and drinking to thirst produces optimal hydration in most contexts
2. Thirst lags behind actual fluid needs during intense exercise, especially in heat - by the time you're thirsty, performance may already be declining
3. Thirst sensitivity decreases with age, making it unreliable for older adults
4. "Drink before you're thirsty" was driven partly by sports drink industry marketing, not purely by science

**Evidence needed:** Thirst sensitivity studies across age groups and exercise intensities, ad libitum vs prescribed drinking and performance outcomes, historical analysis of hydration guideline development and industry influence.

## How Much Does Dehydration Actually Hurt Performance?

The classic claim is that losing just 2% of body weight in sweat impairs performance. This number drives most hydration guidelines. But recent research questions whether it's that simple.

**Competing claims:**

1. 2% body mass loss consistently impairs endurance performance and cognitive function in controlled lab studies
2. Lab studies use forced dehydration protocols that don't reflect real-world drinking behavior - athletes who drink to thirst often exceed 2% loss without measurable performance decrement
3. The 2% threshold was established in hot conditions and may not apply to temperate climates or shorter-duration activities
4. Individual variation is large - some athletes tolerate 3-4% loss with minimal impact while others are affected at 1-2%

**Evidence needed:** Forced vs voluntary dehydration performance comparisons, field studies vs lab studies, individual variability in dehydration tolerance, dose-response data across different exercise types and durations.

## Can You Drink Too Much Water?

Overhydration and exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) - dangerously low blood sodium from excessive fluid intake - have caused deaths in marathons and military training. Yet most public messaging still focuses exclusively on "drink more."

**Competing claims:**

1. Hyponatremia is rare and only a concern for ultra-endurance athletes or people drinking extreme volumes - most people's risk is underhydration, not overhydration
2. EAH is more common than widely recognized, and "drink as much as possible" messaging from race organizers and military training programs has directly caused preventable deaths
3. Slower, smaller athletes (especially women) are at highest risk because they drink at the same rate as larger athletes but sweat less and take longer to finish
4. The fix is simple: drink to thirst, don't force fluids, and use sodium when exercising beyond 2-3 hours

**Evidence needed:** EAH incidence data in endurance events, case fatality reports, risk factor analyses (pace, body size, fluid intake rate), comparison of drink-to-thirst vs prescribed volume protocols on EAH rates.

## Do You Need Electrolytes?

Electrolyte supplements, salt tablets, and drinks with added sodium and potassium are marketed heavily to athletes and increasingly to everyday consumers. But most people eating a normal diet get plenty of electrolytes from food.

**Competing claims:**

1. Most people get adequate electrolytes from food and don't need supplements - electrolyte products are mostly marketing
2. Athletes training over 60-90 minutes in heat lose significant sodium through sweat, and replacement improves performance and prevents cramping
3. Low-carb and ketogenic dieters have genuinely higher electrolyte needs because lower insulin increases sodium excretion through the kidneys
4. Individual sweat sodium concentration varies 3-4x between people, so blanket recommendations are unreliable - some heavy salty sweaters genuinely need more, while others waste money on supplements they don't need

**Evidence needed:** Sweat sodium variability studies, electrolyte supplementation and exercise performance RCTs, electrolyte balance on ketogenic diets, population-level electrolyte intake vs requirements data.

## Are Sports Drinks Better Than Water?

Gatorade was developed in 1965 for the Florida Gators football team. Since then, sports drinks have become a multi-billion dollar industry. The question is whether most people who drink them actually need them.

**Competing claims:**

1. Sports drinks provide carbohydrates and electrolytes that improve performance during prolonged exercise (>60-90 minutes) better than water alone
2. For exercise under 60 minutes, sports drinks offer no performance benefit over water and add unnecessary sugar and calories
3. The sports drink industry has heavily funded and influenced exercise hydration research, creating guidelines that overstate the need for their products
4. Newer formulations (low-calorie electrolyte mixes, coconut water, oral rehydration solutions) may work as well or better than traditional sugar-heavy sports drinks for most use cases

**Evidence needed:** Sports drink vs water performance trials by exercise duration and intensity, industry funding disclosure analysis in hydration research (BMJ investigation), comparison of different fluid types for rehydration efficacy.

## Does Coffee Dehydrate You?

"Coffee doesn't count toward your water intake because caffeine is a diuretic." This claim is repeated constantly. The evidence says otherwise.

**Competing claims:**

1. Caffeine has a mild diuretic effect, but coffee and tea still provide a net positive fluid contribution - habitual caffeine users develop tolerance to the diuretic effect
2. At high doses (>300mg), caffeine can produce meaningful diuresis in non-habitual users, so the context matters
3. Coffee "counts" as fluid intake for the purpose of daily hydration in habitual drinkers - multiple studies show no difference in hydration status between coffee drinkers and water drinkers at equivalent volumes
4. The "coffee dehydrates you" myth likely originated from a single 1928 study and has been perpetuated without updating

**Evidence needed:** Caffeine dose-response diuresis data, habitual vs non-habitual caffeine user comparisons, hydration biomarker studies in coffee drinkers, historical origin tracing of the dehydration claim.

## Does Water Quality Matter?

Tap water, filtered water, mineral water, alkaline water, hydrogen water - the options keep multiplying. Most claims about special water types are marketing. But water quality and mineral content do vary meaningfully.

**Competing claims:**

1. Tap water in most developed countries is safe, regulated, and perfectly adequate - bottled and specialty water is largely unnecessary
2. Mineral water provides meaningful amounts of calcium, magnesium, and bicarbonate that contribute to daily mineral intake, especially in populations with low dairy consumption
3. Alkaline water claims (better hydration, acid-base balance, disease prevention) have no credible evidence behind them
4. Water contamination concerns (lead pipes, PFAS, microplastics) are legitimate in some areas and justify filtration, but that's a safety issue, not a hydration optimization issue

**Evidence needed:** Mineral water contribution to dietary mineral intake studies, alkaline water RCTs, municipal water quality surveillance data, PFAS and microplastic exposure assessments.

## Does Dehydration Cause Muscle Cramps?

"Drink more water and take salt to prevent cramps" is standard advice in every gym and on every sports field. But the evidence increasingly suggests that exercise-associated muscle cramps (EAMC) may have little to do with dehydration or electrolytes and more to do with neuromuscular fatigue.

**Competing claims:**

1. Dehydration and electrolyte depletion (especially sodium, potassium, and magnesium) cause muscle cramps - replacing fluids and electrolytes prevents them
2. The neuromuscular fatigue theory is better supported: cramps occur when fatigued muscles develop altered spinal reflex activity, and dehydration/electrolyte levels don't reliably differ between crampers and non-crampers in the same event
3. Pickle juice and mustard stop cramps within seconds - far too fast for electrolyte absorption, suggesting a neurological mechanism (TRP channel activation in the mouth/esophagus) rather than a fluid/electrolyte one
4. Both mechanisms may contribute depending on context: whole-body cramps during prolonged heat exposure may involve sodium loss, while localized cramps during intense effort are more likely neuromuscular

**Evidence needed:** Electrolyte status comparisons between crampers and non-crampers in endurance events, pickle juice mechanism studies, neuromuscular fatigue and cramp induction research, sodium supplementation and cramp prevention RCTs.

## How Do You Know If You're Hydrated?

Urine color charts are posted in every gym bathroom. Body weight changes before and after exercise are used by sports teams. But measuring hydration status is harder than it looks, and most practical methods have real limitations.

**Competing claims:**

1. Urine color is a simple, free, and good-enough indicator for most people - pale yellow means hydrated, dark yellow means drink more
2. Urine color is easily confounded by supplements (B vitamins turn urine bright yellow), food, medications, and time of day - it's directional at best and misleading at worst
3. Pre-to-post exercise body weight change is the most practical method for athletes to estimate acute fluid losses and guide rehydration
4. The only truly reliable markers are lab-based (plasma osmolality, urine specific gravity, urine osmolality) - but these are impractical for daily use, so practical methods with known limitations are still the best option for most people

**Evidence needed:** Urine color validation studies against plasma osmolality, confounding variable analyses (supplements, diet), body weight change accuracy for estimating sweat loss, comparison of field-practical vs lab-based hydration assessments.
