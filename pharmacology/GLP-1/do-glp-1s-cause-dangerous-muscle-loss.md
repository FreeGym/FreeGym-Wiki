# Do GLP-1s Cause Dangerous Muscle Loss?

GLP-1-based medicines cause some absolute loss of lean tissue during substantial weight loss. However, the best current evidence does not establish that they routinely cause a uniquely disproportionate loss of skeletal muscle or clinically dangerous weakness. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Wachiraphansakul et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209204/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

Fat mass accounts for most of the weight lost, but pooled reviews disagree about whether the proportion attributed to lean or muscle-related tissue is greater than with nonpharmacologic weight loss. Adequately weight-matched comparisons are still lacking. The major unresolved question is not whether lean mass decreases - it often does - but whether this translates into loss of strength, mobility, or independence, particularly in frail adults and people over 65. Those outcomes have rarely been measured. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Busk-Cirera et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42324178/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

Semaglutide and liraglutide are GLP-1 receptor agonists. Tirzepatide activates both GIP and GLP-1 receptors. For brevity, this article uses “GLP-1s” as a broad shorthand that includes tirzepatide while recognizing its dual mechanism. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Look et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39996356/))

## What “lean mass” means - and what it does not

Lean mass is not synonymous with skeletal muscle. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/))

Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) divides body mass into fat mass, bone mineral, and lean soft tissue. Lean soft tissue includes skeletal muscle but also body water, organs, connective tissue, glycogen-associated water, skin, and other nonfat tissues. DXA does not directly isolate contractile skeletal muscle. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/))

Bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA) is even more indirect. It estimates fat-free or lean mass from electrical impedance and model-based assumptions rather than directly measuring skeletal muscle. A change in the estimate should therefore not be interpreted as an equivalent change in muscle protein. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/))

Computed tomography and magnetic resonance imaging can measure muscle cross-sectional area, volume, and fat infiltration more specifically. Even these measurements do not tell us by themselves whether a person has become weaker or less mobile. Strength and physical-performance tests remain separate outcomes. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/))

This distinction changes how the numbers should be interpreted. If 25 percent of a 20 kg weight loss is classified as lean tissue, that means approximately 5 kg of the lost weight fell within a lean-tissue compartment. It does **not** mean the person lost 25 percent of all their skeletal muscle.

The claim that “most lean mass loss is only water, glycogen, or organ mass” goes beyond the available evidence. Some of the measured change is unquestionably nonmuscle tissue, but most GLP-1 studies have not partitioned lean loss precisely enough to determine how much was water, organ tissue, connective tissue, or contractile skeletal muscle. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

## What the systematic reviews show

A 2026 systematic review in _Annals of Internal Medicine_ included 35 primary studies of liraglutide, semaglutide, tirzepatide, or dulaglutide with body composition measured by BIA, DXA, CT, or MRI. A correction published on June 9, 2026 revised the study count and several summary estimates; the corrected values are used here. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [correction](https://doi.org/10.7326/ANNALS-26-02085))

Across incretin-treatment groups, the median proportion of total weight loss attributed to muscle-related or lean-tissue indices was 28.3 percent, with an interquartile range of 15.9 to 39.9 percent. In BIA or DXA studies, the median was approximately 29.0 percent. Among CT or MRI studies, the median proportion of total weight loss attributed to muscle-related indices was 25.3 percent. Results varied considerably across studies and measurement methods. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

The review compared these results with prespecified reference points of approximately 25 percent for BIA/DXA lean loss and 15 percent for CT/MRI muscle loss. These were contextual benchmarks chosen by the reviewers, not validated thresholds above which loss becomes dangerous. About two thirds of incretin interventions exceeded their respective benchmark, but 38 percent of the lifestyle or placebo comparator groups that lost weight also exceeded one. The comparator groups lost only a median of 2.5 percent of body weight, making them poorly matched to the much larger losses in the medication groups. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

A separate 2026 meta-analysis attempted to compare lean-mass changes with incretin and lifestyle interventions. However, the published report contains unresolved internal inconsistencies in body-composition sample sizes, intervention labels, the number of liraglutide trials, and absolute pooled estimates. Its precise agent-specific percentages and overall comparison should therefore not be treated as dependable estimates without a formal correction or independent reanalysis. ([Eisa and Barood, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877354/))

Even apart from those reporting problems, that meta-analysis is not equivalent to directly randomizing people to a GLP-1 drug or diet while matching both groups for the amount and rate of weight loss. Its estimates came from different trials with different participants, durations, interventions, and amounts of weight loss. ([Eisa and Barood, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877354/))

A June 2026 meta-analysis found a different pattern. It included 12 diet-and-exercise trials, five incretin trials, and four bariatric-surgery trials in which participants lost at least 10 percent of body weight. The pooled proportion attributed to fat-free mass was 33.3 percent with incretin therapy, 22.3 percent with diet without exercise, and 7.7 percent with diet plus exercise. ([Busk-Cirera et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42324178/))

These pooled groups came from different trials; they were not weight-matched arms in a direct drug-versus-diet randomization. The analysis strengthens the case for monitoring fat-free mass and including exercise, but it does not by itself establish a drug-specific excess after the amount and rate of weight loss are matched. ([Busk-Cirera et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42324178/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

A May 2026 network meta-analysis of 43 randomized trials involving 3,379 participants and 17 interventions or doses found no statistically significant between-group differences in change in total lean tissue across the comparative network, although some specific regimens showed significant reductions from baseline. The regimen-specific findings are consistent with variation by agent and dose rather than one universal GLP-1 lean-loss percentage. ([Wachiraphansakul et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209204/))

## Semaglutide body-composition findings

In an exploratory DXA analysis of STEP 1 reported as a journal-supplement abstract, 140 participants were assessed over 68 weeks. With semaglutide 2.4 mg, body weight decreased by 15.0 percent, total fat mass by 19.3 percent, visceral fat by 27.4 percent, and lean body mass by 9.7 percent. Because fat mass fell more rapidly, lean mass as a proportion of total body weight increased by approximately 3 percentage points. ([Wilding et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/jendso/bvab048.030))

The 9.7 percent lean-mass reduction should not be divided directly by the 15.0 percent body-weight reduction to calculate the fraction of weight lost as lean. The percentages use different baseline denominators: initial lean mass and initial total body weight, respectively. ([Wilding et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/jendso/bvab048.030))

The substudy shows that lean tissue fell in absolute terms while overall body composition shifted toward a lower proportion of fat. It does not show whether participants lost clinically meaningful muscle strength because strength and mobility were not measured in this analysis. ([Wilding et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1210/jendso/bvab048.030))

**Funding note:** STEP 1 was funded by Novo Nordisk. The body-composition report included several Novo Nordisk employees as authors, so these findings should be interpreted alongside independent syntheses rather than in isolation. ([Wilding et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2032183))

## Tirzepatide body-composition findings

In the SURMOUNT-1 DXA substudy, 160 participants had scans at baseline and week 72. Among those assigned to tirzepatide, body weight decreased by 21.3 percent, fat mass by 33.9 percent, and lean mass by 10.9 percent. Corresponding placebo changes were 5.3, 8.2, and 2.6 percent. Approximately 75 percent of the kilograms lost were classified as fat and 25 percent as lean in both the tirzepatide and placebo groups. This was a descriptive similarity in proportional partitioning, not a weight-matched equivalence test, and the placebo group lost far less total weight. ([Look et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39996356/))

Post hoc analyses divided participants into age groups below 50, 50 to under 65, and 65 or older. The fat-to-lean proportions were broadly similar across most subgroups, but the older subgroup was small and the analysis was not designed to establish safety in frail older adults. Strength and physical performance were not reported. ([Look et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39996356/))

A later correction clarified that the y-axis of Figure 4 showed change from baseline in kilograms, not percent. Figure 4 should therefore not be read as a graph of percentage change. ([correction](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.70050))

**Funding note:** This study was funded by Eli Lilly. The company was involved in study design, data collection, analysis, interpretation, and manuscript preparation, and several authors were Lilly employees. The approximately 75-to-25 result is therefore important but still needs independent replication in adequately matched studies. ([Look et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39996356/))

## What muscle-specific MRI shows

A post hoc analysis of the SURPASS-3 MRI substudy provides more muscle-specific evidence than DXA. Among 246 adults with type 2 diabetes, pooled tirzepatide treatment over 52 weeks reduced thigh muscle volume by 0.64 litres and reduced the muscle-volume Z score by 0.22. At the same time, muscle fat infiltration decreased by 0.36 percentage points. ([Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/))

The observed muscle-volume loss was broadly consistent with the muscle-volume change predicted from weight loss in a UK Biobank comparison model. The reduction in muscle fat infiltration was greater than predicted, suggesting that muscle volume decreased while one measure of muscle composition or “quality” improved. ([Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/))

These findings argue against both extreme interpretations. Tirzepatide did not preserve every litre of skeletal muscle, but the comparison model did not identify a decline greater than predicted from weight loss. The trial did not include a weight-matched caloric-restriction group, so it cannot determine whether any part of the observed change was drug-specific. ([Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

**Funding note:** SURPASS-3 MRI was funded by Eli Lilly. Three authors were Lilly employees and shareholders, while other authors reported consulting or financial relationships with Lilly and additional pharmaceutical companies. ([Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/))

## Are GLP-1s worse than matched diet-induced weight loss?

A direct 14-week randomized trial compared liraglutide 1.8 mg with a prescribed energy deficit of approximately 390 kcal per day and with sitagliptin. At least 5 percent weight loss was achieved by 44 percent of the caloric-restriction group, 22 percent of the liraglutide group, and 5 percent of the sitagliptin group. The caloric-restriction group had the largest improvement in its fat-to-lean ratio. ([Silver et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37188932/))

This trial does not demonstrate that liraglutide causes disproportionate muscle loss. The caloric-restriction group lost more weight, treatment lasted only 14 weeks, and the liraglutide dose was lower than the 3.0 mg obesity dose. The achieved calorie deficits and weight losses were therefore not matched. ([Silver et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37188932/))

Among the studies included in the current systematic reviews, none was a large human trial that directly compared semaglutide or tirzepatide with caloric restriction while matching the groups for total weight loss, rate of loss, protein intake, physical activity, baseline muscle reserve, and follow-up duration, and then measured both muscle-specific imaging and physical function. This missing experiment is why a small drug-specific catabolic effect cannot be ruled in or ruled out confidently. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Wachiraphansakul et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209204/))

## Do people become weaker?

Body-composition trials have measured kilograms far more often than function. In the corrected 2026 systematic review, none of the 35 included studies reported an objective physical-function outcome such as gait speed, chair-rise performance, or stair climbing. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

A separate randomized trial, the S-LiTE study, provides some functional evidence. After 193 adults aged 18 to 65 completed an eight-week low-calorie diet and lost an average of approximately 13.1 kg, they were assigned to exercise, liraglutide 3.0 mg, exercise plus liraglutide, or placebo for one year. Outcomes included stair-climbing time, cardiorespiratory fitness, and isometric knee-extension strength. The exercise program combined cycling, circuit training, and individual moderate-to-vigorous sessions; it was not a trial of resistance training alone. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/))

Absolute knee-extension strength was preserved across groups, with no statistically significant between-group differences. Relative strength improved in the active-treatment groups because body weight was lower while absolute strength was maintained. Liraglutide alone did not improve overall physical fitness, but it did not produce a detectable decline in absolute knee-extension strength either. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/))

Exercise was the factor that improved performance. Compared with liraglutide alone, exercise plus liraglutide shortened the stair test by 1.2 seconds, or 8.6 percent, and increased peak oxygen consumption by 3.0 mL/min/kg of fat-free mass. Exercise alone produced similar functional benefits. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/))

**Funding note:** The S-LiTE program received Novo Nordisk Foundation and academic support, and Novo Nordisk supplied liraglutide and placebo. Several investigators disclosed research, advisory, speaking, or other financial relationships with Novo Nordisk or other manufacturers. These conflicts do not invalidate the randomized findings, but they should be considered when interpreting them. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/))

A prospective cohort called SEMALEAN enrolled 115 people with severe obesity receiving semaglutide 2.4 mg; 106 completed the 12-month follow-up. At 12 months, average body weight had fallen by approximately 13 percent and fat mass by approximately 18 to 19 percent. Lean mass declined by about 3 kg during the first seven months and then appeared to stabilize, while handgrip strength increased by approximately 4 to 4.5 kg. ([Alissou et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41068996/))

This is reassuring but not definitive. SEMALEAN had no untreated or weight-matched comparison group, was conducted at a single clinical centre, and the 12-month analysis reflects participants who completed follow-up. The design cannot isolate semaglutide's effect or determine why grip strength increased. ([Alissou et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41068996/))

**Funding note:** The investigators reported no study funding, although one author was employed by Novo Nordisk. That conflict should be noted, but the study was not a manufacturer-funded randomized trial. ([Alissou et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41068996/))

## Lean-mass loss is not automatically sarcopenia

Under the 2019 EWGSOP2 European consensus definition, probable sarcopenia is identified primarily by low muscle strength. Low muscle quantity or quality is used to confirm the diagnosis, while poor physical performance indicates severe sarcopenia. Under this definition, a decrease in DXA lean mass without weakness or impaired performance is not, by itself, a diagnosis of sarcopenia. ([Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/))

This matters because a person starting with high body mass and above-average absolute lean mass may lose some lean tissue without crossing a clinically important threshold. The same numerical loss could be much more consequential in someone who begins with low strength, frailty, recent illness, inadequate food intake, or limited mobility. That risk depends on baseline reserve and functional change, not solely on the percentage shown by a DXA or BIA report. ([Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

**Funding note:** Development of the EWGSOP2 consensus received operational support from Abbott through the European Geriatric Medicine Society. Abbott had observers at meetings but, according to the paper, did not select members or participate in manuscript preparation or approval. ([Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/))

## Older adults and higher-risk groups

Evidence in older adults is inadequate. In the corrected 2026 systematic review, mean participant ages across the included studies ranged from 20 to only 63.7 years, the review included no studies focused specifically on adults aged 65 or older, and no included study reported an objective physical-function outcome. Frail adults, people with established sarcopenia, and adults dependent on assistance for daily activities remain insufficiently represented in the broader evidence base. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

The Eisa and Barood meta-analysis reported no statistically significant difference in proportional lean loss between trials with average ages below and above 65. However, only a small number of studies fell into the older category, the comparison used trial-level rather than individual-participant age data, and the review has the internal reporting inconsistencies described above. The age analysis therefore cannot establish safety in adults with frailty or low baseline muscle reserve. ([Eisa and Barood, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877354/))

A July 2026 multidisciplinary consensus reached a similar position: population-level clinical harm has not been established, but evidence on muscle function, long-term outcomes, and high-risk groups remains insufficient. It recommends interpreting body composition alongside nutritional status, strength, mobility, physical performance, and ability to perform daily activities. ([Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

The most defensible interpretation is therefore not that GLP-1 treatment has been shown to cause sarcopenia in older adults, nor that it has been proven harmless. Older and frail adults remain a biologically and clinically plausible higher-risk population for whom direct evidence is insufficient. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

## Funding and conflict-of-interest context

The _Annals of Internal Medicine_ review reported no primary funding. The Eisa and Barood meta-analysis reported no funding or author conflicts, but the internal inconsistencies described above limit confidence in its quantitative results. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Eisa and Barood, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877354/))

Several of the main agent-specific, long-duration body-composition estimates come from manufacturer-sponsored trial programs:

- STEP 1 was funded by Novo Nordisk and included company employees as authors. ([Wilding et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2032183))

- SURMOUNT-1 was funded by Eli Lilly, which participated in the study and publication process. ([Look et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39996356/))

- SURPASS-3 MRI was funded by Eli Lilly and included Lilly employees and shareholders as authors. ([Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/))

- S-LiTE received Novo Nordisk Foundation support and drug supply from Novo Nordisk, with multiple investigator disclosures. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/))

Industry funding does not make these measurements false, and the independent syntheses cited here generally report that fat loss predominates. Nevertheless, estimates of lean loss vary, one recent meta-analysis has unresolved reporting problems, and independent, weight-matched replication with prespecified muscle and functional outcomes remains limited. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Eisa and Barood, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877354/); [Wachiraphansakul et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209204/))

## Limits and open debates

The proportion of weight loss classified as lean or muscle-related tissue is a useful population-level description, not a biological rule. Individual estimates vary widely with participant characteristics, the amount and pace of weight loss, dietary intake, physical activity, and measurement method. The corrected systematic review's interquartile range extended from 15.9 to 39.9 percent. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

There is no validated percentage of lean-tissue loss at which treatment becomes clinically dangerous. Clinical concern should be tied more directly to declining strength, mobility, physical performance, nutritional status, or ability to perform daily activities. ([Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

Most available data come from DXA or BIA, which cannot determine precisely how much of the change is contractile skeletal muscle. MRI evidence shows that actual muscle volume can decrease, but also suggests that muscle fat infiltration may improve. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Sattar et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40318682/))

Direct evidence on falls, fractures attributable to weakness, gait deterioration, disability, hospitalization, or loss of independence is particularly sparse. The fact that these outcomes have not been demonstrated in current trials is not proof that they never occur; the trials were generally not designed or powered to detect them. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

## Practical interpretation

- **Claim 1: GLP-1 RAs cause disproportionate lean-mass loss compared with diet-induced weight loss.** This has not been established. Pooled estimates conflict, and adequately weight-matched human comparisons are lacking. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Busk-Cirera et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42324178/); [Wachiraphansakul et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42209204/))

- **Claim 2: Approximately 25 to 35 percent of weight loss can be classified as lean tissue in some studies.** This range overlaps with many study estimates, but it is not a validated biological rule or an established expectation for a weight-matched caloric deficit. The corrected Batsis review found a median of 28.3 percent with an interquartile range of 15.9 to 39.9 percent across incretin studies, while the available drug-versus-diet comparisons are not sufficiently matched to establish equivalence. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/))

- **Claim 3: GLP-1-associated muscle loss is clinically dangerous.** Clinically important weakness has not been demonstrated as a general effect in trial populations. Nevertheless, the absence of strong functional data, especially in frail older adults, means danger cannot be excluded for vulnerable individuals. ([Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/); [Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

- **Claim 4: Lean mass includes water, glycogen, and organs rather than only skeletal muscle.** Correct. The stronger claim that _most_ of the observed loss is nonmuscle tissue is currently unproven. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/))

- Structured exercise has direct support for improving fitness and preserving measured strength during pharmacological weight management. Progressive resistance training is biologically reasonable and is recommended as a countermeasure to lean-tissue loss, but the available GLP-1-specific functional trial did not isolate resistance training from other exercise modes. The evidence supports evaluating strength and performance, not merely body weight or a BIA estimate, particularly in older adults or anyone beginning treatment with low muscle reserve. ([Tinsley and Heymsfield, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39372917/); [Jensen et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41579235/); [Cruz-Jentoft et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30312372/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))

The most accurate conclusion is that GLP-1-based weight loss is **not fat-only**, but neither is it currently shown to produce uniquely severe or routinely dangerous muscle wasting. The concern is most credible in people with limited baseline muscle reserve, and that is precisely the population for which better long-term, function-focused trials are still needed. ([Batsis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41996180/); [Dobbie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42419343/))
