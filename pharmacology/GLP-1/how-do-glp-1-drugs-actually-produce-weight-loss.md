# How Do GLP-1-Based Drugs Actually Produce Weight Loss?

GLP-1-based drugs do not appear to produce large, sustained weight loss mainly by mechanically keeping food in the stomach. The clearest long-term human finding is that people continue to consume less energy during standardized ad libitum meal tests. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255); [Friedrichsen et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14280); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6))

The best current explanation is a distributed gut-brain mechanism. GLP-1-based pharmacotherapy alters neural systems involved in hunger, satiation, meal size, food preoccupation, and responses to food cues, while also slowing gastric emptying. Gastric slowing is real, often appears stronger early in treatment and with short-acting drugs, and can attenuate with continued exposure. Reward-pathway modulation is plausible, but human neuroimaging remains too small and heterogeneous to establish it as the dominant mechanism. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255); [Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019))

## First, tirzepatide is not a pure GLP-1 receptor agonist

Semaglutide and liraglutide are GLP-1 receptor agonists. Tirzepatide activates both the glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor, or GIP receptor, and the GLP-1 receptor. ([DailyMed: Wegovy](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=ee06186f-2aa3-4990-a760-757579d8f77b); [DailyMed: Saxenda](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=3946d389-0926-4f77-a708-0acb8153b143); [DailyMed: Zepbound](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=487cd7e7-434c-4925-99fa-aa80b1cc776b))

Therefore, appetite, gastric, and neuroimaging findings from tirzepatide cannot be attributed exclusively to GLP-1 signaling. In the principal 2025 mechanistic tirzepatide trial, the authors explicitly noted that they could not separate the neurobiological contributions of GIP and GLP-1 receptor activation because selective GIP agonists and antagonists were not available for human comparison. ([Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

## The strongest human finding: people eat less

A 60-week double-blind randomized controlled trial randomized 120 adults with overweight or obesity to semaglutide 2.4 mg or placebo. At standardized ad libitum laboratory lunches, the adjusted between-group differences in change from baseline favored semaglutide by approximately 295 kilocalories at week 20, 250 kilocalories at week 40, and 238 kilocalories at week 60. Thus, lower energy intake at a controlled meal persisted for more than a year. These values do not measure total daily or free-living energy intake. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403))

Interestingly, semaglutide improved post-meal appetite suppression, past-week hunger, and food preoccupation at week 20. The estimated between-group differences were smaller and no longer statistically significant at weeks 40 or 60. Responsiveness to food remained lower through week 40 but not week 60. Objective energy intake at the laboratory lunch therefore remained lower even as several questionnaire-based differences became smaller or less statistically certain. This could reflect adaptation, limitations of subjective measurements, differential attrition, or a change in which mechanisms contribute most over time; it does not establish that neural effects disappeared. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403))

By week 60, 84.7 percent of participants assigned to semaglutide and 54.2 percent assigned to placebo completed the assessment. Missing outcomes were addressed with jump-to-reference multiple imputation and sensitivity analyses. Both groups also received structured lifestyle counseling. Gastrointestinal adverse events were common, including nausea and vomiting, and the trial did not formally determine how much those symptoms contributed to reduced meal intake. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403))

**Funding note:** This semaglutide trial was supported in part by a Novo Nordisk investigator-sponsored research grant to the University of Pennsylvania. The authors reported that Novo Nordisk had no role in the study's conception, conduct, analysis, or reporting, although several authors disclosed research funding or other relationships with Novo Nordisk and other obesity-drug manufacturers. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403))

A separate six-week phase 1 trial randomized 114 adults with overweight or obesity to tirzepatide, placebo, or open-label liraglutide. At week 3, the prespecified analysis found that the change from baseline in ad libitum lunch intake was 534.1 kilocalories lower with tirzepatide than with placebo, with a 95 percent confidence interval from -668.2 to -400.0 kilocalories. Tirzepatide also reduced questionnaire measures of hunger, cravings, disinhibition, and susceptibility to the surrounding food environment. The meal result was an early, single-meal endpoint, not a measurement of long-term free-living intake, and many secondary endpoints were not adjusted for multiple comparisons. ([Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

**Funding note:** The tirzepatide trial was funded by Eli Lilly, tirzepatide's manufacturer. The sponsor participated in the design, data collection, analysis, interpretation, manuscript writing, and decision to publish; numerous authors were Lilly employees and shareholders. The prespecified meal-intake result was large and statistically precise, while the detailed mechanistic and imaging findings require independent replication. ([Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

These trials provide direct evidence that reduced meal energy intake is a major proximal behavioral effect of treatment and a plausible mediator of weight loss. They did not formally quantify how much of the weight loss was mediated by reduced intake, nor did they determine whether the reduction originated primarily from direct brain receptor activation, peripheral gut-brain signaling, gastric distension, nausea, reward modulation, or some combination. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

## Central appetite and satiation signaling

The strongest human receptor-specific evidence comes from an acute randomized crossover experiment involving 48 adults who were lean, had obesity without diabetes, or had obesity and type 2 diabetes. Intravenous exenatide reduced food intake and reduced food-cue responses in the insula, amygdala, putamen, and orbitofrontal cortex in the groups with obesity. Pretreatment with the GLP-1 receptor antagonist exendin(9-39) largely blocked these effects. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849))

That experiment demonstrates that the intake and brain-response effects were mediated through the GLP-1 receptor. It does **not** show where the decisive receptors were located. Exenatide could have acted at brain sites accessible from the circulation, at peripheral receptors whose signals were relayed to the brain, or through both routes. The study was also acute and used intravenous infusions during a somatostatin clamp, so it does not reproduce months of treatment with long-acting semaglutide. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849))

**Funding note:** This experiment was supported partly by a Novo Nordisk grant, and the investigators reported additional institutional pharmaceutical relationships. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849))

Causal dissection is more advanced in animals. In mice, reducing GLP-1 receptor expression in a Nestin-Cre neural-lineage model markedly attenuated liraglutide's reduction of food intake and body weight while leaving much of its glucose-lowering action intact. This supports an important role for neural-lineage GLP-1 receptors in liraglutide's anorectic effects, although the model was not a deletion restricted only to mature neurons. ([Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434))

However, pharmacologic GLP-1 agonists do not simply reproduce the normal endogenous brain GLP-1 system. Another mouse study found that preproglucagon neurons in the nucleus tractus solitarius responded to satiation-related signals but were not necessary for liraglutide or semaglutide to suppress eating. Activating these neurons produced an additional reduction in intake during semaglutide treatment, suggesting that endogenous central GLP-1 neurons and pharmacologic GLP-1 agonists can engage partly independent, additive circuits. ([Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4))

The animal evidence therefore supports an important role for neuronal signaling but not a single "appetite center." The relevant network appears to include brainstem, hypothalamic, and higher-order regions and may be reached through both circulating drug and peripheral sensory pathways. Causal evidence for particular receptor populations is strong in rodent models, but the dominant receptor populations have not been localized in treated humans. ([Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4); [Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434); [van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019))

## What neuroimaging actually shows

A 2025 systematic review identified only eight eligible human studies of GLP-1 signaling and functional brain connectivity through April 2024. Reported effects involved the default-mode, salience, visuospatial, and frontoparietal networks. Some studies found lower connectivity or activity in the hypothalamus, lateral orbitofrontal cortex, or amygdala, whereas others found increased connectivity in the hypothalamus, nucleus tractus solitarius, thalamus, or hippocampus. The direction and location of the effects were therefore not uniform across drugs, populations, tasks, and imaging methods. This count applies to functional-connectivity studies, not to every form of human GLP-1 neuroimaging. ([Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019))

The more recent tirzepatide trial illustrates the uncertainty. Its aggregated endpoint for brain responses to highly palatable food pictures did not differ significantly from placebo. In secondary category-specific analyses, tirzepatide reduced responses to high-fat, high-sugar food pictures in the medial frontal and cingulate gyri, orbitofrontal cortex, and hippocampus at week 3. No positive food-versus-nonfood voxels were detected in the ventral striatum, and the regional comparisons were not adjusted for multiplicity. The absence of positive ventral-striatal voxels under that contrast is not an equivalence test and does not establish that the ventral striatum was unaffected. The authors appropriately described the regional results as requiring cautious interpretation and replication. ([Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

Functional MRI uses blood-oxygen-level-dependent, or BOLD, signals associated with neural activity. It does not directly measure dopamine release, subjective reward, compulsion, or the causal importance of a brain region. A lower food-cue BOLD response could reflect reduced incentive value, greater satiation, less attention to the pictures, learning or habituation, weight loss itself, nausea, changes in metabolic state or cerebral blood flow, or other physiological changes. ([Logothetis et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1038/35084005); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

The imaging literature therefore supports the claim that GLP-1-based treatment changes brain responses related to appetite and food cues. It does not yet establish that a specific mesolimbic "reward switch" is the primary cause of weight loss. ([Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849))

**Conflict note:** The functional-connectivity systematic review reported in its funding statement that the article was not funded by any entity, but its senior author disclosed consultation or speaking relationships with numerous pharmaceutical companies, including Novo Nordisk. Most of the review's other authors reported no relevant conflicts. ([Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019))

## Delayed gastric emptying is real

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis of prospective studies included ten studies and 300 participants. The analysis pooled standardized mean differences and then back-transformed the pooled result into minutes, producing an estimate corresponding to approximately 74 minutes of prolonged gastric-emptying half-time. Between-study subgroup estimates were larger with short-acting agents, at approximately 116 minutes, and in studies lasting less than ten weeks, at approximately 82 minutes. These subgroup comparisons were not randomized head-to-head tests of drug class or treatment duration. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6))

These estimates should not be interpreted as a universal 74-minute delay for every patient. Study populations, meals, agents, doses, treatment durations, and measurement techniques differed substantially. Statistical heterogeneity was extreme, with I² of approximately 97.7 percent. Only one included study evaluated semaglutide, and the certainty of the estimated effect size was rated **very low**. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6))

**Funding note:** The gastric-emptying meta-analysis used internal departmental funding from Toronto Western Hospital rather than GLP-1-manufacturer funding. Two senior investigators disclosed consulting or other support from pharmaceutical companies. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6))

The most informative direct time-course study randomized 40 adults with obesity to liraglutide 3 mg or placebo. Relative to baseline, the median within-group change in gastric-emptying half-time was a 70-minute delay with liraglutide and a four-minute delay with placebo at week 5. At week 16, the corresponding changes were a 30.5-minute delay and a one-minute acceleration. Thus, the estimated gastric effect was smaller at week 16 but remained statistically detectable. ([Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6))

That trial also found a post hoc correlation between the gastric-emptying delay at week 5 and weight loss at week 16. Correlation does not establish that the gastric delay caused the weight loss: both could reflect higher effective drug exposure or individual sensitivity to liraglutide. The study was small and explicitly described as a pilot trial. It was funded by the US National Institutes of Health rather than the manufacturer. ([Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6))

A Novo Nordisk-funded 20-week semaglutide randomized controlled trial found no detectable delay on its week-20 paracetamol-absorption test, despite substantially lower appetite and energy intake at a standardized meal. Paracetamol absorption is an indirect measure and is less direct than scintigraphic measurement of solid-food emptying, so the null result does not prove that semaglutide had no gastric effect. It nevertheless shows that marked appetite and test-meal intake effects can coexist with no detectable delay on that particular late-treatment test. ([Friedrichsen et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14280))

## Does delayed gastric emptying cause the appetite suppression?

A 2026 secondary analysis examined the relationship between gastric emptying and appetite during liraglutide treatment. Although gastric emptying showed statistically detectable relationships with some appetite and satiation measures when data were pooled, those relationships explained only approximately 4 to 6 percent of the variance in specific measured endpoints. Within the liraglutide group, gastric-emptying changes were not significantly correlated with appetite suppression, and appetite outcomes did not differ meaningfully among participants with normal, transiently delayed, or persistently delayed emptying. ([Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))

This supports the interpretation that measured gastric-emptying half-time was not the principal direct cause of liraglutide-induced appetite suppression in that study. It is not proof that gastric effects are irrelevant. The study was a secondary association analysis, not a randomized intervention on the proposed mediator or a formal causal mediation analysis. It was limited to liraglutide and to the appetite and satiation tests used in the parent trial. Gastric-emptying half-time also does not capture every possible gastrointestinal contribution, such as gastric accommodation, antral contractions, pyloric tone, distension, nausea, visceral interoception, or intestinal feedback. ([Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))

The most defensible interpretation is that delayed gastric emptying contributes to the overall experience, particularly early in treatment and with short-acting agents, but is unlikely to be the principal sustained mechanism behind appetite suppression from long-acting semaglutide or liraglutide. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255); [Friedrichsen et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14280))

## "Food noise" and reward signaling

"Food noise" is an emerging patient-reported psychological construct describing persistent, intrusive thoughts about food that disrupt daily life and make healthy behaviors difficult. A Food Noise Questionnaire was developed and psychometrically validated in 2025, although its clinical boundaries and utility require further study. The questionnaire measures self-reported experiences; it does not directly measure dopamine signaling or establish a formal diagnosis. ([Diktas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.24216))

Clinical trials do show improvement in several components that people may describe as food noise. Semaglutide reduced food preoccupation and responsiveness to food early in treatment, while tirzepatide reduced cravings, hunger, disinhibition, and susceptibility to the surrounding food environment. Those trials measured related components, not the Food Noise Questionnaire itself. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Diktas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.24216))

However, in the 60-week semaglutide trial, the estimated advantage in food preoccupation was smaller and no longer statistically significant at weeks 40 or 60, even though participants continued to consume less at the standardized laboratory lunch. Meanwhile, the tirzepatide imaging study did not find a significant effect on its aggregated highly palatable food endpoint, and the planned ventral-striatal interpretation was limited because no positive food-versus-nonfood voxels were detected for that contrast. These findings do not support the simple claim that the drugs permanently "switch off" the reward system. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

A more precise conclusion is that GLP-1-based drugs can reduce cue-driven wanting, cravings, or the salience of food in at least some people. These changes may contribute to eating less, but current human evidence cannot determine how much of the effect is specifically due to reward pathways versus reduced hunger, earlier satiation, altered interoception, learned responses to smaller meals, or other neural processes. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019); [van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849))

Evidence concerning diagnosed binge-eating disorder is also different from evidence concerning compulsive-eating symptoms, questionnaire-defined food-addiction constructs, or ordinary food preoccupation. Binge-eating disorder is a formal eating-disorder diagnosis. Measures such as the Yale Food Addiction Scale operationalize a distinct research construct and should not be treated as interchangeable with that diagnosis. ([American Psychiatric Association](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/eating-disorders/what-are-eating-disorders); [Gearhardt et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1037/adb0000136))

## Central versus peripheral effects

The distinction between "central" and "peripheral" is not as clean as it sounds. A drug does not need to penetrate every part of the brain freely to influence eating. It may act at neural structures that are relatively accessible from the circulation, alter vagal or other peripheral sensory signals, change gastric and intestinal feedback, or activate brainstem pathways that subsequently influence hypothalamic and cortical networks. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4); [Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434))

Human receptor-blockade and imaging studies demonstrate GLP-1-receptor-dependent changes in food intake and brain activity, but they cannot localize the decisive receptors. Mouse knockout studies show that neural-lineage GLP-1 receptors are important for the full anorectic action of liraglutide, while the Brierley experiments show that the endogenous nucleus-tractus-solitarius GLP-1 system is not simply the required relay for semaglutide and liraglutide. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434); [Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4))

Thus, altered neural control of eating is strongly supported. The narrower claim that long-term weight loss is produced mainly by direct activation of one hypothalamic or brainstem receptor population has not been demonstrated in humans. ([van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434); [Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4); [Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019))

## How the mechanism changes over time

The claim that gastric effects often weaken over time is supported, but the strength of the evidence varies. The direct liraglutide time-course study found a smaller estimated delay at week 16 than at week 5. The gastric-emptying meta-analysis also found larger estimates in shorter studies, but that result came from a between-study subgroup comparison rather than a randomized within-person test of duration. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6))

The claim that central effects simply persist unchanged is not supported. In the 60-week semaglutide randomized controlled trial, lower laboratory-lunch intake persisted, but several subjective appetite and food-preoccupation differences became smaller or less statistically certain. In the tirzepatide imaging trial, regional food-cue differences were found at week 3 but not consistently at week 6, although repeated exposure to the same imaging task could have produced habituation. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

A plausible working model is **mechanistic reweighting**, not a binary switch. Early treatment may combine gastric slowing, gastrointestinal symptoms, strong changes in hunger and food preoccupation, and neural responses to food cues. With continued treatment, gastric and some consciously perceived appetite effects may attenuate, while lower meal size and energy intake persist through a combination of residual satiation signals, altered eating patterns, neural adaptation, and continued pharmacologic signaling. The cited studies have not longitudinally measured all of these candidate mediators together or formally established how their causal contributions change over time. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))

## Ranking the competing claims

1. **"The primary driver is central appetite suppression through hypothalamic and brainstem GLP-1 receptors." - Broadly supported, but too anatomically specific.** Sustained lower energy intake at standardized meals is well demonstrated, and causal animal evidence supports an important role for neural-lineage GLP-1 receptors. Human studies do not yet identify one dominant receptor population or cleanly separate direct central action from peripheral-to-central signaling. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Sisley et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI72434); [Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4))

2. **"Delayed gastric emptying is a major contributor, especially early." - Partly supported.** Gastric emptying is measurably delayed, and the effect often appears larger early and with short-acting agents. Calling it a major cause of sustained appetite suppression is not supported by the recent liraglutide analysis. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))

3. **"GLP-1 drugs reduce food noise and compulsive eating by modifying reward pathways." - Plausible, but lower-certainty.** Cravings and food preoccupation can improve, and some imaging studies show altered responses in reward-related regions. Imaging results are mixed, generally short-term, and cannot establish a specific dopamine or compulsion mechanism. "Food noise" has a validated patient-reported questionnaire, but the questionnaire is not a direct neural measurement. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Au et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2024.11.019); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Diktas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.24216))

4. **"The mechanism shifts over time: gastric effects diminish while central effects persist." - Directionally reasonable, but oversimplified.** Gastric effects often attenuate, while lower laboratory-meal intake can persist. Some subjective appetite and imaging effects also become smaller or less certain, so the evidence supports the possibility of changing contributions over time rather than an unchanged central mechanism replacing a disappearing gastric one. Formal causal reweighting has not yet been demonstrated. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))

## Practical interpretation

- The most defensible description is that GLP-1-based drugs produce weight loss principally by lowering energy intake through altered gut-brain control of eating. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [van Bloemendaal et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.2337/db14-0849); [Brierley et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-021-00344-4))

- Delayed gastric emptying is a genuine pharmacologic effect, not a myth, but it is agent-dependent, time-dependent, and unlikely to explain most sustained appetite suppression from long-acting drugs. ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255); [Friedrichsen et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.14280))

- Improvements in cravings and food preoccupation may be clinically meaningful, but "food noise" should not be presented as a proven dopamine disorder or a directly measured reward-circuit endpoint. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Diktas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.24216); [Logothetis et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1038/35084005))

- Tirzepatide findings represent combined GIP and GLP-1 receptor activation and cannot isolate a specifically GLP-1-mediated mechanism. ([DailyMed: Zepbound](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=487cd7e7-434c-4925-99fa-aa80b1cc776b); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9))

- The mechanism may change with dose escalation and treatment duration, but current evidence does not support a simple sequence in which gastric action ends and one unchanging central mechanism takes over. ([Tronieri et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ajcnut.2026.101403); [Martin et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-03774-9); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12630-026-03114-6); [Halawi et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253%2817%2930285-6); [Friedman et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1002/oby.70255))
