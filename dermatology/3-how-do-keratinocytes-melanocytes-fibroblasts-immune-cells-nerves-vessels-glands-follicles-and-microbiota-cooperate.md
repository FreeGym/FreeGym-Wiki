# How Do Keratinocytes, Melanocytes, Fibroblasts, Immune Cells, Nerves, Vessels, Glands, Follicles, and Microbiota Cooperate?

Skin works because its cells divide the labor without working in isolation. Keratinocytes build and renew the epidermal barrier. Melanocytes produce pigment and transfer it to keratinocytes. Fibroblasts maintain the dermal matrix and provide signals to nearby cells. Immune cells monitor the tissue, tolerate harmless residents, respond to danger, and help direct repair. Nerves detect environmental changes while also influencing inflammation, blood flow, glands, and healing. Blood vessels transport cells, nutrients, heat, and signals, while lymphatic vessels drain interstitial fluid and route immune cells and antigens. Hair follicles and glands function as regenerative and biochemical units. Microorganisms occupy the surface and surface-connected appendages, especially follicular canals, where they interact with host cells and each other. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))

A useful first rule: **do not assign one job to one cell.** A keratinocyte is both a structural cell and an immune sensor. A fibroblast is both a matrix-producing cell and a signaling cell. A sensory neuron is both a detector and a local regulator. A hair follicle is both a hair-producing structure and a stem-cell niche. Skin function emerges from the conversation among these components, not from any one component acting alone. ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/))

## The practical orientation map

The skin’s cooperative network is organized by physical location. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/))

**The epidermis** is the outer cellular layer. It is made mainly of keratinocytes, with melanocytes, Langerhans cells, Merkel cells, occasional lymphocytes, and sensory nerve endings occupying particular epidermal neighborhoods. The epidermis contains no blood vessels, so its living cells depend on diffusion and communication across the dermal-epidermal junction. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/))

**The dermis** contains fibroblasts, extracellular matrix, blood vessels, lymphatic vessels, immune cells, sensory and autonomic nerves, hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and other supporting structures. This makes the dermis more than structural padding. It is the supply network and signaling environment beneath the epidermis. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/))

**Skin appendages** include hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands. These structures extend from the surface through the dermis and, depending on the structure and body site, may reach the superficial subcutis. They create specialized epithelial compartments with their own stem cells and local relationships with nerves, immune cells, and vessels. Surface-connected compartments, particularly pilosebaceous follicles, can also harbor microbial communities distinct from those at the surrounding surface. They are connected to the surface but have local biology that differs from the surrounding interfollicular epidermis. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24492848/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

**The surface ecosystem** consists of corneocytes, epidermal lipids, sweat, sebum, salts, proteins, microbial products, and resident organisms. The surface environment is continually shaped by cells beneath it, while molecules and organisms at the surface signal back to keratinocytes and immune cells. ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

These regions are separated anatomically, but not biologically. Soluble signals cross short distances. Cells touch one another through junctions and membrane receptors. Matrix fibers transmit mechanical forces. Nerves and vessels connect local skin events to larger body systems. Microbial metabolites and host lipids diffuse across the surface environment. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/)) ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/))

## Keratinocytes: builders, sensors, and broadcasters

Keratinocytes make up most of the epidermis. Basal keratinocytes proliferate, and their descendants move outward while changing their structure and gene expression. They produce keratins, junctional proteins, lipids, natural moisturizing components, and the specialized envelope that eventually allows terminally differentiated cells to become part of the stratum corneum. This renewal program maintains the physical barrier despite continuous shedding and environmental damage. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/))

But keratinocytes do not simply become barrier material. They detect microbial molecules, tissue damage, ultraviolet radiation, physical stress, allergens, irritants, and cytokines produced by other cells. They can respond by producing antimicrobial peptides, cytokines, chemokines, lipids, growth factors, and alarm signals. These outputs can recruit immune cells, alter nerve sensitivity, influence fibroblast activity, stimulate or restrict vascular growth, and change the behavior of neighboring keratinocytes. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/))

Keratinocytes also help control the type of immune response that develops. Different epidermal layers express different combinations of receptors and defensive molecules. Signals from keratinocytes can favor antiviral, antibacterial, antifungal, allergic, or tissue-repair responses depending on the stimulus and surrounding cytokine environment. This does not mean keratinocytes independently choose the correct response. It means they are active participants in a decision made by the tissue as a whole. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/)) ([24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12685724/))

A useful analogy is that keratinocytes are both the bricks of the wall and the alarm system built into the bricks.

## Melanocytes: pigment producers in an epidermal partnership

Melanocytes are found mainly in the basal epidermis and in hair follicles. They synthesize melanin inside organelles called melanosomes. The pigment is then transported through melanocyte extensions and transferred to surrounding keratinocytes. Effective epidermal pigmentation therefore depends on production by melanocytes, transfer between cells, uptake by keratinocytes, and processing or persistence within keratinocytes. ([5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37511054/))

This transfer is essential because much of melanin’s photoprotective function is carried out after the pigment has entered keratinocytes. Transferred pigment, particularly eumelanin-rich pigment, can form caps around keratinocyte nuclei, absorb and dissipate ultraviolet radiation, and reduce ultraviolet-associated DNA damage. Pheomelanin generally provides less photochemical protection than eumelanin and can contribute to oxidative chemistry under some conditions. Pigmentation is therefore not paint applied by melanocytes. It is a cooperative protective arrangement between melanocytes and the cells they supply. ([5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37511054/)) ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([25](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2671032/))

Communication moves in both directions. Keratinocytes exposed to ultraviolet radiation or inflammation release signals that can alter melanocyte survival, dendrite formation, melanin synthesis, and melanosome transfer. Dermal fibroblasts also release factors that influence melanocyte behavior, helping explain why pigmentation can be affected by changes occurring beneath the epidermis. Inflammatory mediators from immune cells and neuroendocrine signals can also influence pigmentary responses, although the relative contribution of individual pathways varies by context. ([5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37511054/)) ([6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28711237/)) ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/)) ([26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35306737/))

These interactions can contribute to post-inflammatory pigment alteration after a visible rash has settled, including post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. The form, visibility, and persistence of the change vary with the underlying condition, the depth and intensity of inflammation, ultraviolet exposure, and skin tone. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is often more prominent and persistent in darker skin tones. ([6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28711237/)) ([26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35306737/)) ([27](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39075672/))

## Fibroblasts: matrix engineers with local memory

Fibroblasts are major cellular residents of the dermis. They produce and reorganize collagen, elastic-fiber components, proteoglycans, glycosaminoglycans, and other extracellular matrix molecules. This matrix gives the dermis strength and flexibility, but it also stores growth factors, transmits mechanical forces, and provides biochemical cues that influence cell adhesion, movement, differentiation, and survival. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/))

Fibroblasts are not one uniform cell population. Cutaneous fibroblasts from superficial and deeper dermis and from sites such as the scalp, face, and palms can retain different patterns of gene expression and behavior. Related site-specific programs are also found in fibroblasts from noncutaneous tissues such as oral mucosa. These differences are sometimes described as positional identity or positional memory. The word “fibroblast” therefore identifies a broad family of related cells rather than one interchangeable cell type. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/))

Fibroblasts communicate upward with the epidermis and sideways with vessels, nerves, follicles, fat cells, and immune cells. Their secreted factors can influence keratinocyte growth, epidermal differentiation, melanocyte activity, hair cycling, inflammation, and angiogenesis. In return, fibroblasts respond to cytokines, mechanical tension, oxygen availability, microbial products, ultraviolet-induced signals, and molecules released from injured keratinocytes. ([6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28711237/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12685724/))

During repair, some fibroblasts become more contractile and matrix-producing. These activated states help close wounds and rebuild tissue, but prolonged or excessive activation can contribute to hypertrophic scars, keloids, and other forms of fibrosis. A fibroblast response can therefore be necessary for healing while still becoming harmful when its timing, magnitude, or resolution is abnormal. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

## Immune cells: surveillance, tolerance, defense, and repair

The skin immune system includes both tissue-resident populations and cells that arrive from the circulation. Langerhans cells occupy the epidermis. Dendritic cells, macrophages, mast cells, innate lymphoid cells, regulatory T cells, resident memory T cells, and other populations occupy different dermal or appendage-associated niches. Neutrophils, monocytes, lymphocytes, and additional cells can be recruited when injury or infection changes local signals. ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12685724/))

Their job is not simply to attack. Healthy skin must tolerate normal microbes, ordinary environmental contact, and harmless tissue turnover while remaining capable of responding quickly to genuine danger. Dendritic cells and Langerhans cells sample antigens and help shape adaptive immune responses. Macrophages remove debris and participate in repair. Mast cells respond to tissue and neural signals. Regulatory and resident T-cell populations contribute to immune restraint, local memory, barrier maintenance, and appendage homeostasis. ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38492673/)) ([17](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38711955/))

Immune cells also support cells that are not traditionally labeled “immune.” Skin-resident myeloid cells can provide growth and survival signals to keratinocytes, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and appendage-associated stem cells. Immune cells help coordinate the transition from inflammation to repair after injury. If this transition fails, inflammation may persist, microbes may remain uncontrolled, or repair may become fibrotic. ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([22](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/13/7/624)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

The immune system is therefore both a security service and a maintenance crew.

## Nerves: sensory cables that also signal outward

Cutaneous sensory nerves transduce mechanical, thermal, pruritogenic, and potentially damaging stimuli. The resulting signals travel toward the spinal cord and brain, where they contribute to perceptions such as touch, temperature, itch, and pain. Communication is not one-way. Sensory nerve endings can release neuropeptides and other mediators into the skin, where they influence immune cells, keratinocytes, blood vessels, and fibroblasts. ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38653601/))

Immune cells and skin cells signal back to nerves. Cytokines, growth factors, microbial products, tissue metabolites, and keratinocyte-derived alarm molecules can increase or reduce neuronal sensitivity. This bidirectional signaling helps explain why inflammation can cause itch, burning, tenderness, or pain even when the visible surface change appears modest. ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38653601/)) ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/))

Some dendritic cells and Langerhans cells lie close to sensory fibers and express receptors for neuronal mediators. Neurons can alter dendritic-cell activation, migration, and antigen presentation, while dendritic-cell products can change sensory signaling. Much of the detailed pathway work comes from animal and laboratory models, but the anatomical and functional evidence supports a genuine neuroimmune network within skin. ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38653601/)) ([11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38492673/))

Neural input also helps regulate sweat secretion, vascular tone, piloerection, and local stress responses. Skin should therefore be understood as a sensory and neuroendocrine organ, not merely as tissue containing passive nerve endings. ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/))

## Blood vessels and lymphatics: supply lines that also send instructions

Blood vessels in the dermis provide oxygen and nutrients, remove metabolic products, regulate heat exchange, and create the route through which circulating immune cells reach inflamed or injured skin. Endothelial cells lining these vessels respond to inflammatory mediators and can alter vessel diameter, permeability, adhesion-molecule expression, and immune-cell entry. They are active participants in tissue responses, not inert plumbing. ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

Vascular relationships are particularly visible around appendages. Blood-vessel networks remodel during hair cycling, and hair-follicle cells can release factors that affect nearby vessels. Endothelial signals can also influence hair-follicle stem-cell behavior. Much of the detailed causal evidence comes from mouse models, so the exact importance of individual signals in normal human hair cycling remains less certain. ([12](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6684267/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/))

Lymphatic vessels collect excess interstitial fluid and participate in the movement of antigens and immune cells toward draining lymph nodes. In mouse models, lymphatic vessels interact with hair-follicle stem-cell niches during regeneration; whether they directly regulate epidermal stem cells remains under investigation. Research increasingly treats lymphatics as signaling partners in skin repair, although this field is less mature than the study of blood vessels. ([13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38487264/))

As a simplified orientation, blood vessels mainly bring and exchange, while lymphatics mainly drain and route. Both influence immunity and tissue organization. ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38487264/))

## Glands: factories for surface chemistry

Eccrine sweat glands release water, electrolytes, metabolites, and proteins onto the skin surface. Evaporation supports cooling, while sweat composition contributes transiently to surface hydration and also affects acidity, salt concentration, and antimicrobial defense. Sweat therefore changes the environment encountered by keratinocytes and microbes as well as regulating body temperature. ([15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24492848/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/))

Sebaceous glands release sebum into follicular canals. Sebum contains lipids that help coat the hair and skin surface, influence surface chemistry, and provide substrates or constraints for microorganisms. Sebum-derived and keratinocyte-derived lipids can also act as signaling molecules that affect inflammation, barrier activity, and microbial ecology. ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

Glands are themselves multicellular structures. Eccrine and apocrine secretory units contain secretory epithelial cells and myoepithelial cells connected to ducts, while sebaceous glands consist of sebocyte lobules whose cells release lipid-rich contents into ducts, usually associated with hair follicles. Nerves, vessels, immune cells, and local progenitors help regulate these structures and their secretions. Some gland-associated cells can contribute to local repair after injury, although their contribution depends on the type of gland, body site, and depth of damage. ([15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24492848/)) ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

This is why sweat and sebum should not be dismissed as waste products or cosmetic inconveniences. They are outputs of living appendages that help construct the skin’s surface environment. ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24492848/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

## Hair follicles: regenerative and signaling hubs

A hair follicle is a cycling mini-organ. It contains several epithelial compartments, hair-follicle stem cells, matrix cells that produce the hair shaft, dermal papilla and sheath cells, melanocytes, melanocyte stem cells, immune cells, nerve endings, vessels, and relationships with the sebaceous gland and arrector pili muscle. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/))

Hair-follicle stem cells alternate between relative quiescence and activation. Their behavior is influenced by signals from dermal papilla cells, fibroblasts, immune cells, nerves, fat cells, vessels, lymphatics, and other follicular cells. Hair-follicle stem cells also send signals outward, helping remodel their own niche. The follicle is therefore not simply controlled by its surroundings. It participates in organizing them. ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/)) ([17](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38711955/)) ([12](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6684267/))

Follicular epithelial cells can also contribute to re-epithelialization after skin injury. Their importance depends on wound depth, appendage survival, species, and anatomical site. In deep injuries that destroy follicles and glands, the repaired surface may close but lack normal appendages, contributing to the structural and functional differences between scar tissue and uninjured skin. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

Immune cells are part of the follicular niche as well. Macrophages and T-cell populations can influence stem-cell quiescence, activation, and cycling. Follicular cells can produce signals that attract, retain, or regulate those immune cells. Much of this mechanistic work remains preclinical, but it has replaced the older idea that hair growth is controlled only by hormones and follicular epithelial cells. ([17](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38711955/)) ([24](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12685724/))

## Microbiota: residents inside the network

Healthy skin is occupied by bacteria, fungi, viruses, and mites. These organisms are found on the surface and within protected, surface-connected sites such as follicular canals. Their distribution varies with moisture, oil, temperature, pH, oxygen, body site, host biology, hygiene practices, climate, and environmental exposure. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

Resident organisms can compete with potential pathogens, consume available nutrients, produce inhibitory compounds, alter local metabolites, and stimulate controlled immune responses. Keratinocytes and immune cells detect microbial molecules, while host-derived lipids, sweat, peptides, and immune mediators determine which organisms can persist. This produces a bidirectional relationship: the host shapes the microbial community, and the microbial community modifies the host environment. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/)) ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/))

The labels “good bacteria” and “bad bacteria” are often too simple. The effect of an organism can depend on its strain, abundance, body site, neighboring microbes, barrier condition, and the host’s immune state. A normally tolerated resident may contribute to disease when it enters deeper tissue or encounters a damaged barrier. Conversely, an organism associated with disease may not be the original cause of the disorder. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/))

Microbiome studies therefore require careful interpretation. A microbial difference between healthy and diseased skin may be a cause, a consequence, a marker, or part of a self-reinforcing loop. Sequencing can identify associations, but it does not automatically establish what initiated the disease or whether changing the organisms will improve clinical outcomes. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

## The main communication channels

Skin cells cooperate through several overlapping communication systems. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))

**Direct contact** allows adjacent cells to communicate through junctions, adhesion systems, and membrane-bound receptor-ligand interactions. Melanocyte-to-keratinocyte pigment transfer requires close cellular apposition and may occur through more than one transfer mechanism. Interactions between immune cells and nerves are another example of communication requiring very close proximity. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37511054/)) ([11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38492673/)) ([28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-30755-0))

**Paracrine signaling** occurs when one cell releases a molecule that acts on nearby cells. Cytokines, chemokines, growth factors, neuropeptides, lipid mediators, and antimicrobial molecules frequently work this way. Much of skin biology consists of short-range paracrine conversations between the epidermis, dermis, appendages, nerves, vessels, and immune cells. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36317800/))

**Autocrine signaling** occurs when a cell responds to a molecule it has released itself. This can reinforce or restrain a cellular state. During inflammation, autocrine and paracrine signals often operate together, making it difficult to attribute a tissue response to only one cell type. ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/))

**Extracellular-matrix signaling** allows the dermal scaffold and basement membrane to influence cell attachment, polarity, migration, proliferation, and differentiation. Cells continuously build, sense, pull on, and remodel this matrix, so mechanical information becomes part of biochemical communication. ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/))

**Vascular and lymphatic communication** moves cells and signals over greater distances. A local skin event can recruit circulating immune cells or send antigens and activated dendritic cells toward lymph nodes. Blood vessels deliver circulating nutrients, immune cells, and soluble mediators to skin, while lymphatic vessels primarily drain interstitial fluid and route antigens and immune cells toward lymph nodes. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38487264/))

**Microbial communication** includes microbial metabolites, cell-wall components, enzymes, antimicrobial substances, and competition between organisms. These signals are interpreted by host receptors and modified by sweat, sebum, epidermal lipids, pH, and immune activity. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/))

## The network in action after a small injury

A small cut demonstrates how rapidly the system changes from homeostasis to coordinated repair. Damaged vessels constrict, platelets adhere and aggregate, and the coagulation cascade helps stabilize the hemostatic plug. Platelets and injured cells release signals. Keratinocytes at the wound edge detect the breach. Sensory nociceptors transduce injury-related stimuli and generate signals that may be experienced as pain. Their peripheral endings can also release mediators that influence nearby immune and vascular responses. ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/))

Resident immune cells respond first, followed by recruited neutrophils, monocytes, and other circulating cells. These cells remove microbes, damaged tissue, and debris while releasing signals that affect keratinocytes, fibroblasts, vessels, and nerves. Inflammation is not an optional disturbance added to healing. A controlled inflammatory response is one component of normal repair. ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/)) ([22](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/13/7/624))

Keratinocytes migrate and proliferate to restore an epidermal surface. Surviving follicular and glandular epithelial cells may contribute additional cells. Fibroblasts enter activated states, produce provisional matrix, and help contract and strengthen the wound. Endothelial cells generate or remodel vessels to support metabolically active repair tissue. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

As closure progresses, the inflammatory response should resolve. Matrix is reorganized, unnecessary vessels regress, epidermal differentiation resumes, and the surface microbial environment is reassembled. Neural sensitivity and pigmentation can also change during or after repair, but the mechanisms and clinical importance vary by wound type, body site, species, and patient context. The tissue does not necessarily return to its exact pre-injury state. Disruption of particular repair processes may contribute to infection, chronic inflammation, delayed closure, or excessive fibrosis; some injuries can also leave persistent sensory or pigmentary changes. ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/)) ([22](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/13/7/624)) ([26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35306737/)) ([27](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39075672/))

No single “healing cell” performs this work. Repair succeeds when multiple cell populations enter the appropriate states in the appropriate sequence and later stop those responses. ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/)) ([22](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-4409/13/7/624))

## When cooperation becomes a feedback loop

Many skin disorders are not failures of one isolated cell type. They can involve self-reinforcing communication loops among barrier, immune, neural, stromal, and microbial systems. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/))

Inflammatory cytokines can change keratinocyte differentiation and weaken barrier function. Barrier disruption can expose immune cells to more environmental and microbial signals. Keratinocyte and immune mediators can sensitize sensory neurons, producing itch. Scratching then causes additional physical damage, generating more inflammatory signals. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38653601/))

Persistent immune activation can keep fibroblasts in matrix-producing states. Prolonged fibroblast activation can change tissue stiffness and extracellular signaling, which may then alter immune and epithelial behavior. Inflammation can also alter melanocyte activity and melanin handling, contributing to post-inflammatory pigment changes that may outlast visible inflammation. These changes can occur across skin tones but may be more prominent and persistent in darker skin tones. ([6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28711237/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38528155/)) ([26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35306737/)) ([27](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39075672/))

Microbial communities may change because inflammation alters nutrients, lipids, pH, moisture, barrier integrity, and immune pressure. The changed microbial community may then provide a different set of signals to keratinocytes and immune cells. This is biologically plausible and supported in several conditions, but the direction of causation differs across diseases and is not always established. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/))

The practical lesson is important: **a visible skin change may be the surface result of communication among epithelial, immune, neural, vascular, stromal, appendage, and microbial systems.** ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))

## Where the evidence is strongest, and where it is still developing

The basic organization of the epidermis, pigmentary unit, dermis, immune system, nerves, vessels, appendages, and microbiota is well established. It is also clear that these systems communicate through direct contact, soluble mediators, extracellular matrix, neural signaling, vascular transport, and microbial products. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))

The exact contribution of individual signaling pathways is often less certain. Many detailed findings about hair-follicle niches, neuroimmune circuits, vascular signals, and stem-cell behavior come from genetically modified mice, cell culture, organoids, or ex vivo skin. These methods can demonstrate mechanisms, but the size, timing, and clinical importance of the same pathway in normal human skin may differ. ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([12](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6684267/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/))

Single-cell and spatial studies can identify cell populations and predict which cells may communicate through matching ligands and receptors. That prediction is valuable, but it does not by itself prove that a signal is active, necessary, sufficient, or clinically important. Predictions can vary substantially with the computational method and interaction resource used, so functional experiments and human validation are still required. ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([28](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-30755-0))

Microbiome evidence needs similar discipline. A change in microbial abundance is not automatically proof of disease causation, and a product that changes microbial composition is not automatically proven to improve symptoms or long-term skin health. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/)) ([20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32791112/))

## Working vocabulary

**Homeostasis** is the active maintenance of a stable tissue state. It does not mean nothing is happening. Cells are continually renewing, signaling, monitoring, and adapting. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/))

**A niche** is the local cellular, structural, chemical, neural, vascular, and immune environment that regulates a cell or stem-cell population. ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/))

**Paracrine signaling** occurs when a cell releases a signal that acts on nearby cells. ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/))

**Autocrine signaling** occurs when a cell responds to a signal that it has produced itself. ([4](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7605526/))

**The extracellular matrix** is the organized network of proteins, sugars, and associated molecules surrounding cells. It provides structure while also carrying mechanical and biochemical information. ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/))

**Microbiota** refers to the organisms occupying an environment. **Microbiome** is often used more broadly to include those organisms, their genes, activities, products, and ecological context. ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/)) ([19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29332945/))

## The takeaway

Skin is a cooperative tissue network. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/))

Keratinocytes build the barrier and broadcast danger signals. Melanocytes supply pigment to keratinocytes. Fibroblasts build the dermal matrix and provide local instructions. Immune cells balance tolerance, defense, and repair. Nerves sense the environment while modifying inflammation and tissue behavior. Blood vessels move nutrients, heat, immune cells, and signals, while lymphatic vessels drain fluid and route immune cells and antigens. Glands manufacture the surface chemistry in which the barrier and microbiota operate. Hair follicles act as cycling mini-organs and regenerative niches. Microorganisms occupy the system and influence it without being separate from it. ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39115524/)) ([5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37511054/)) ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39062158/)) ([8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31013709/)) ([9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41686991/)) ([13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38487264/)) ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38897295/)) ([16](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8167365/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))

Normal skin function depends on interactions among these components, although the participating compartments and their relative importance vary by body site and physiological state. **Skin is not a stack of independent cell types. It is an organized conversation that happens to form an organ.** ([1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8977401/)) ([2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6098245/)) ([18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39895627/))
