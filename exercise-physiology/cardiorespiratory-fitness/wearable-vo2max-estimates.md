# Wearable VO₂ Max Estimates

Wearable VO₂ max is a modeled estimate of cardiorespiratory fitness, not a direct gas-exchange measurement. Consumer devices derive it from signals such as heart rate, motion, pace or GPS, and user characteristics, then process those signals with proprietary algorithms. That means the estimate can contain useful information without being interchangeable with laboratory VO₂ max. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0323741); [Düking et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.351)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12404996/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12404996/"))

## What wearable VO₂ max is and is not

A cardiopulmonary exercise test (CPET) measures oxygen uptake during exercise with gas analysis. A smartwatch does not. The current evidence supports a middle position: consumer wearables are not accurate enough to replace CPET for individual clinical decisions, but they are also not just random noise. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Lambe et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0323741)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))

Because the algorithms are proprietary and devices are updated frequently, validation is model-, firmware-, and protocol-specific. An estimate validated on one watch, one software version, and one use protocol should not be assumed to perform the same way everywhere else. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x); [Düking et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.351)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12404996/ "A guide to consumer-grade wearables in cardiovascular clinical care and population health for non-experts - PMC"))

## What validation studies show against CPET

The best pooled evidence comes from the 2022 INTERLIVE systematic review/meta-analysis of 14 validation studies. It found that exercise-based wearable estimates had a near-zero mean bias against criterion testing (−0.09 mL/kg/min), but the 95% limits of agreement were still wide (−9.92 to 9.74 mL/kg/min). Resting-condition algorithms performed worse, with mean overestimation of 2.17 mL/kg/min and even wider limits of agreement (−13.07 to 17.41 mL/kg/min). That review was partly funded by Huawei Technologies, a wearable manufacturer, so its pooled findings are useful but should be interpreted with that in mind and checked against independent validations. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))

Recent Apple Watch validation studies have been less flattering. In one Apple Watch Series 7 study, the watch underestimated laboratory VO₂ max by 4.51 mL/kg/min and showed poor reliability (ICC 0.47), with underestimation worsening at higher fitness levels; however, that study compared an Apple Watch estimate generated from outdoor running with a laboratory criterion obtained on a cycle ergometer, so it was not a pure like-for-like validation. In a later study that enrolled 30 participants and analyzed 28, mean underestimation was 6.07 mL/kg/min, mean absolute percentage error was 13.31 percent, and mean absolute error was 6.92 mL/kg/min. That later study used devices updated to watchOS 10 or later, so the finding was not confined to older software versions. ([Caserman et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2196/59459); [Lambe et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0323741)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11325102/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11325102/"))

Some Garmin studies have looked better in specific protocols and populations. In runners using a Forerunner 245 after three outdoor runs, overall MAPE was 5.7 percent. In a small sample of apparently healthy active and sedentary young adults using a Garmin fēnix 6, MAPE was 7.05 percent and concordance was 0.73 against 30-second averaged laboratory values. But accuracy was not uniform: in highly trained endurance athletes, a Forerunner 245 underestimated laboratory VO₂ max by 6.3 mL/kg/min, while moderately trained athletes had substantially lower error. ([Düking et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-1686-9068); [Carrier et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/s25010275); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9286863/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9286863/"))

## Systematic bias and subgroup effects

A clearly supported subgroup pattern is by fitness level. In the Apple Watch Series 7 validation study, the watch overestimated the lowest-fitness group but underestimated the good- and excellent-fitness groups, with the largest underestimation in the excellent group. In Garmin studies, accuracy was clearly better in moderately trained athletes than in highly trained athletes. ([Caserman et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.2196/59459); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x); [Düking et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-1686-9068)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11325102/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11325102/"))

Direct VO₂ max validation by skin tone is still limited, and many existing validation studies have mainly enrolled young healthy adults. The best evidence on skin tone is indirect: a systematic review of wrist-based heart-rate accuracy across skin tones found mixed and ultimately inconclusive results, while a 2025 exercise study found larger heart-rate error at higher intensities in darker skin tones. Because heart rate is a core input to most wearable VO₂ max algorithms, that creates a plausible pathway for subgroup bias, but the size of that bias in commercial VO₂ max estimates is still not well quantified. ([Koerber et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40615-022-01446-9); [Hung et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0318724); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9662769/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9662769/"))

Evidence specifically isolating running economy is also limited, but the repeated underestimation seen in highly trained runners is consistent with generic submaximal heart-rate–speed models missing individual physiology. That is a reasonable inference, not a settled mechanism. ([Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x); [Düking et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-1686-9068); [Düking et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.351)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x.pdf "https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x.pdf"))

## Are they useful for trends over time?

The direct longitudinal evidence is still limited, but it is not empty. In adults with complex congenital heart disease, smartwatch-reported peak VO₂ correlated well with repeated CPET measurements at both time points, and changes over time also correlated well; for each 1 mL/kg/min change on CPET, the smartwatch changed by about 0.896 mL/kg/min on average. The smartwatch values still ran high by about 3.15 mL/kg/min, so trend-tracking looked better than absolute calibration. ([Tran et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/heartjnl-2023-322989)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37827554/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37827554/"))

Repeated standardized use may also help. In elite youth soccer players using a Forerunner 245 on software 13.00, MAPE fell from 5.58 percent after one standardized run to 1.06 percent after a second run. Even so, recent review authors argue that month-to-month and year-to-year algorithm stability is still under-studied, and that longitudinal studies are needed because both models and software keep changing. ([Düking et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.351); [Železnik Mežan, 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1707991); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11149075/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11149075/"))

## Are they meaningful for population risk?

At the population level, estimated cardiorespiratory fitness is clearly meaningful. A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis covering 42 studies, 35 cohorts, and 3.8 million observations found that each 1-MET higher fitness level was associated with lower all-cause mortality (RR 0.86) and lower cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.84). It also found no statistically significant difference between objectively measured CRF and exercise- or non-exercise-estimated CRF in their dose-response relationships with mortality. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/"))

That does not mean every commercial smartwatch VO₂ max algorithm is equally calibrated for risk prediction. The strongest outcome data are for estimated CRF as a category, not for one specific watch model or firmware. Still, the broader mortality literature makes it hard to argue that estimated fitness is meaningless at the population level, even though extending that inference to any one commercial smartwatch algorithm requires caution. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/"))

## Limits that matter in clinical practice

Even when wearable VO₂ max tracks general fitness reasonably well, CPET still provides information a smartwatch does not, including variables such as anaerobic threshold and oxygen uptake efficiency slope. That matters when the question is clinical, not just recreational or epidemiologic. ([Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12404996/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12404996/"))

So the practical conclusion is narrower than “wearables are fine” and softer than “mostly noise.” The evidence supports using them for rough ranking, especially with exercise-based algorithms. The same evidence does not support using them as a standalone basis for diagnosis, elite-sport precision work, or other threshold-based clinical decisions where a 5 to 10 mL/kg/min individual error could matter. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y); [Lambe et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0323741); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))

- Pooled exercise-based wearable estimates: mean bias about −0.09 mL/kg/min, but 95% limits of agreement about −9.92 to 9.74 mL/kg/min. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))
    
- Pooled resting-condition estimates: mean overestimation about 2.17 mL/kg/min, with 95% limits of agreement about −13.07 to 17.41 mL/kg/min. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))

- Garmin validations in runners and small young-adult samples: MAPE around 5.7 to 7.05 percent in some settings, but much worse performance in highly trained athletes. ([Düking et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-1686-9068); [Carrier et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/s25010275); [Engel et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9286863/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9286863/"))

([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/"))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Wearable VO₂ max is not accurate enough to replace CPET for individual clinical decisions. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y); [Jamieson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44325-025-00082-6)) ([Syddansk Universitet](https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf "https://findresearcher.sdu.dk/ws/files/203839161/Molina_Garcia2022_Article_ValidityOfEstimatingTheMaximal.pdf"))
    
- It can be useful for coarse ranking and sometimes for trends, especially when the same device is used repeatedly under similar conditions. ([Tran et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/heartjnl-2023-322989); [Düking et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.351)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37827554/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37827554/"))
    
- The strongest population-level evidence says estimated fitness is prognostically meaningful, but device-specific calibration and subgroup bias still need more study. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Železnik Mežan, 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1707991)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/"))

**Note:** Most cited validation studies are model- and software-specific and often lag current retail hardware. Several Garmin results discussed here come from older devices (for example, Forerunner 245 and fēnix 6), while newer Apple evidence includes Series 9/Ultra 2 devices running watchOS 10 or later. Accuracy on newer watches or after major firmware changes should not be assumed without direct validation.
