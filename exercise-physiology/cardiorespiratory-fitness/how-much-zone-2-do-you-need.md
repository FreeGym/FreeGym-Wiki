# How Much Zone 2 Do You Need?

The answer depends on the goal. For health and longevity, the strongest evidence does not come from trials prescribing exact weekly "Zone 2 hours." It mostly comes from dose-response meta-analyses and large cohorts studying moderate-to-vigorous or total physical activity. Those data consistently show that modest weekly volumes already provide large benefits, with diminishing returns as volume rises. For improving fitness in previously sedentary adults, randomized trials show that even sub-guideline to guideline-like moderate training volumes can improve cardiorespiratory fitness, while higher doses usually improve it more. For trained endurance athletes, larger low-intensity volumes and training-intensity distribution matter more, but that is a different question from the minimum dose needed for general health. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554); [Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z))

## What Zone 2 is and is not

"Zone 2" is not a standardized public-health category, and the label changes across zone models. In recent expert work focused on the popular five-zone concept, Zone 2 is placed immediately below the first lactate or ventilatory threshold (LT1/VT1). But in many three-zone training-intensity-distribution papers, "Zone 2" means the band between the first and second thresholds. That is one reason public-health "moderate intensity" and popular "Zone 2" are not interchangeable terms. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40010355/); [Casado et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35418513/))

A second problem is measurement. Recent physiology papers show substantial intra- and interindividual variability in common Zone 2 anchors, and fixed proxies such as percentages of HRmax or VO2max can miss where LT1 actually sits. In practical terms, two people training at the same nominal "Zone 2" heart-rate percentage may be working in different physiological domains. ([Meixner et al., 2025](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1155/tsm2/2008291); [Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40088270/))

Recent expert commentary also argues that many of the expected adaptations from Zone 2 are not clearly unique to that narrow band and may also be induced slightly below or above it. So Zone 2 is a useful anchor, but the current literature does not support treating it as a uniquely magical intensity. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://cris.maastrichtuniversity.nl/en/publications/what-is-zone-2-training-experts-viewpoint-on-definition-training-/))

## What the strongest health evidence actually measures

A 2023 systematic review and cohort-level dose-response meta-analysis included 196 articles from 94 cohorts with more than 30 million participants. It found inverse nonlinear associations between non-occupational physical activity and mortality outcomes; at 8.75 mMET-hours/week, all-cause mortality risk was 31% lower and cardiovascular mortality risk 29% lower than in inactive adults. This is strong evidence for "some is much better than none," and for diminishing returns rather than an all-or-nothing threshold. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/))

A 2024 pooled analysis of 4 multinational megacohorts, including 2,011,186 adults followed for a median of 11.5 years, found the same basic shape. Compared with no activity, 7.5 MET-hours/week was associated with 14% lower mortality risk, 15.0 MET-hours/week with 22% lower risk, 22.5 MET-hours/week with 25% lower risk, and 30.0 to 37.5 MET-hours/week with about 26% lower risk. Even half the recommendation, 3.75 MET-hours/week, was associated with significantly lower mortality risk. ([Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554))

That pattern does not directly show that general health requires 3 to 10 hours per week of bona fide Zone 2, because these studies measured activity volume rather than lab-defined time just below LT1/VT1. For mortality and chronic disease risk, much of the benefit appears at modest weekly volumes, while larger volumes add smaller incremental benefit. Long-term cohort data also suggest that the near-maximal association with lower mortality is approached around 300 to 600 minutes per week of moderate activity or 150 to 300 minutes per week of vigorous activity, with no clear further lowering beyond those levels. ([Lee et al., 2022](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.058162); [Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554))

In older adults, the dose-response curve remains favorable and may shift upward somewhat. An umbrella review found that 7.5 to 15.0 MET-hours/week was associated with about 19% to 30% lower all-cause mortality and 25% to 34% lower cardiovascular mortality, while 15.0 to 22.5 MET-hours/week was associated with larger reductions. A large steps meta-analysis also found age-specific plateaus: mortality risk decreased up to roughly 6,000 to 8,000 steps/day in adults 60 years and older, and up to 8,000 to 10,000 steps/day in younger adults. After accounting for total steps, the independent role of stepping rate was attenuated, although some peak-cadence metrics remained associated, again pointing to activity volume mattering a lot for health. ([Fukushima et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1525861023008356); [Paluch et al., 2022](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/article/PIIS2468-2667%2821%2900302-9/fulltext))

## What randomized trials say about fitness gains

For improving fitness rather than just lowering risk, randomized trials still do not support a universal "hours, not minutes" requirement in novices. In the DREW trial, sedentary overweight or obese postmenopausal women trained at about 50% of peak oxygen uptake for 6 months. The three exercise doses averaged about 72, 136, and 192 minutes/week, and all three improved fitness versus control, with a graded response of about 4.2%, 6.0%, and 8.2% increases in peak oxygen uptake. The lowest dose helped; the higher doses helped more. ([Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370))

Older walking trials tell a similar story. In sedentary postmenopausal women, 24 weeks of walking at 45% to 55% of VO2max with a weekly energy expenditure of about 1,000 to 1,500 kcal improved VO2max and body composition, and the authors described that dose as apparently close to a minimum effective dose. More recently, a 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis concluded that low-intensity endurance training improves aerobic fitness in healthy working-age adults, with moderate-to-large effects on VO2max/VO2peak and moderate effects on VT1. ([Asikainen et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12055113/); [Nuuttila et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41543030/))

At the same time, once people have already completed a moderate-training block, intensity progression can matter for further cardiorespiratory gains. A recent trial found that switching to energy-matched higher-intensity training produced further improvements in cardiorespiratory fitness compared with continuing moderate-intensity training, and related work from the same group found a higher VO2max responder rate with increased intensity despite equal energy expenditure. Earlier work from Ross and colleagues also found that increasing either the amount or the intensity of exercise reduced the rate of cardiorespiratory-fitness nonresponse. ([Reuter et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1298877/full); [Reuter et al., 2023](https://sportsmedicine-open.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s40798-023-00579-3); [Ross et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26455890/))

## Volume versus intensity distribution

For health outcomes, the best current evidence suggests that total physical activity volume is very important, but it does not directly test whether carving out a larger share of the week as exact Zone 2 is more important than simply being more active overall. A 2024 harmonized meta-analysis concluded that total physical activity volume was associated with lower mortality to a greater extent than the contribution of moderate-to-vigorous activity to total volume. A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis likewise found that, after accounting for total physical activity, vigorous activity did not clearly show a larger all-cause mortality reduction than moderate activity. ([Tarp et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39089430/); [Rey Lopez et al., 2020](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/6/1/e000775))

For endurance performance, the conclusion is different. Athlete-focused reviews and meta-analyses suggest that training-intensity distribution matters, and polarized plans can improve VO2peak more than some competing distributions, especially in shorter interventions and in more competitive athletes. But that is evidence about performance optimization, not evidence that the average person needs several weekly hours of Zone 2 for health. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Casado et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35418513/))

## Limits and open debates

The biggest gap is that direct, long-term randomized trials comparing several weekly doses of lab-defined Zone 2 are scarce. Much of the health literature measures moderate activity, total activity, or steps, not precise time just below LT1/VT1. And the measurement papers show that common surrogate anchors can misclassify where LT1 actually is. So anyone claiming a universally precise weekly Zone 2 requirement is overstating what the literature can currently support. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://cris.maastrichtuniversity.nl/en/publications/what-is-zone-2-training-experts-viewpoint-on-definition-training-/); [Meixner et al., 2025](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1155/tsm2/2008291); [Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40088270/); [Nuuttila et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41543030/))

## Quick reference values

- For general health, large cohort analyses support meaningful benefit from quite modest weekly volumes, with larger benefits up to guideline-like levels and diminishing returns beyond them. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554))
    
- For older adults, 7.5 to 15.0 MET-hours/week is clearly beneficial, and 15.0 to 22.5 MET-hours/week may provide additional mortality reduction. ([Fukushima et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1525861023008356))
    
- For previously sedentary adults trying to improve cardiorespiratory fitness, roughly 70 to 140 minutes/week of moderate training can work, and higher volumes tend to work better. ([Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370))
    
- For trained endurance athletes, low-intensity volume and training-intensity distribution are relevant, but that is a performance-planning problem, not the minimum required for health. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z); [Casado et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35418513/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Claim 1 is too strong for health. High weekly volumes may be useful for endurance-performance goals, but they are not required to capture large health benefits. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554); [Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370))
    
- Claim 2 is the best supported. For mortality and chronic disease risk, modest volumes capture a large share of the benefit, and more is mostly diminishing returns rather than a sharp threshold. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554); [Lee et al., 2022](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.058162))
    
- Claim 3 is supported. Age and baseline fitness matter: older adults may continue to gain at somewhat higher volumes, while lower-fit adults can improve fitness at relatively modest doses. ([Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554); [Fukushima et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1525861023008356); [Paluch et al., 2022](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpub/article/PIIS2468-2667%2821%2900302-9/fulltext); [Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370))
    
- Claim 4 is partly supported. For health, total activity volume and consistency appear very important, while current evidence does not directly establish that the precise share of time spent in Zone 2 matters more; for performance, intensity distribution becomes more important. ([Tarp et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39089430/); [Rey Lopez et al., 2020](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/6/1/e000775); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Reuter et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1298877/full))
    

A practical summary is this: if the goal is health, you do not need to chase elite-style Zone 2 hours. If the goal is building fitness from a low baseline, a few moderate sessions per week are enough to start producing measurable gains. If the goal is maximizing endurance performance, low-intensity volume becomes more important, but it still works as part of a broader distribution rather than as a standalone magic dose. ([Garcia et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10423495/); [Martinez-Gomez et al., 2024](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2826554); [Church et al., 2007](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108370); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40010355/))
