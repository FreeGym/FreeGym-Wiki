# Zone 2 for Older Adults: Safe Foundation or Too Simplistic?

Zone 2 is not the term typically used in the reviews cited here. In that literature, a close analogue is moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT), usually prescribed with heart-rate, workload, or VO₂-based targets rather than coaching-zone language. In this article, "Zone 2" is therefore used as a practical label for that kind of moderate continuous aerobic work, and the evidence discussed below comes mainly from MICT-HIIT comparisons plus broader trials of aerobic, resistance, balance, and concurrent training. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Stern et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-023-00551-1); [Marriott et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-021-00344-4))

## What the "safe foundation" claim gets right

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 29 trials including 1,227 older adults found that both HIIT and MICT significantly improved VO₂max, peak workload, body-fat percentage, glycemia, and blood pressure, with broadly similar effects across most outcomes. That supports the idea that moderate continuous aerobic training is a legitimate aerobic base in older adults: it reliably improves cardiorespiratory fitness and several cardiometabolic markers even when it is not clearly superior to harder formats. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274))

Safety and adherence data support the feasibility of supervised moderate and higher-intensity aerobic training in older adults, but not the idea that older adults must avoid all vigorous work. A 2021 scoping review of 69 studies reported that HIIT protocols in older adults were generally well tolerated, adherence was often high, and adverse events were usually few and, where directly compared in supervised studies, often comparable with moderate continuous training. The same review also noted that many studies were small and short, with an average duration of about 7.9 weeks, so the literature is stronger on short-term feasibility than on long-term injury comparisons. ([Marriott et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-021-00344-4))

## Where Zone 2 becomes too simplistic

The stronger claim that moderate continuous work alone is enough for maximal improvement is not well supported. In the 2024 meta-analysis, HIIT showed slight advantages over MICT for VO₂max and some waist and blood markers, especially in higher-quality controlled trials. But a 2023 meta-analysis focused on functional movement found that HIIT improved function versus no-exercise control while not being significantly better than MICT in direct comparisons. On outcomes such as Timed Up and Go, sit-to-stand, and walking capacity, higher intensity did not clearly outperform moderate training when older adults were already exercising. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Stern et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-023-00551-1))

So claim 2 is too absolute. Moderate continuous training can improve VO₂max and mobility-relevant outcomes, and occasional higher intensity may add some physiologic upside, but the current evidence does not show that every older adult must include HIIT to improve function meaningfully. It also matters that the reviews cited here place more emphasis on VO₂peak, six-minute walk, Timed Up and Go, sit-to-stand, and composite physical-performance measures than on direct ADL endpoints. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Stern et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-023-00551-1); [Marriott et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-021-00344-4))

## Why strength, power, and balance often matter more for falls and physical function

For fall prevention specifically, the evidence tilts away from aerobic-only thinking. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of falls-prevention interventions in community-dwelling older adults found the clearest benefits from exercise programs centered on balance/resistance and from group tai chi. These approaches had the most fall-related outcomes with at least low-certainty benefit, whereas none of the interventions with moderate-certainty benefit were walking-focused. Serious harms appeared small to none overall, although some exercise nodes showed small increases in minor adverse events. ([Pillay et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13643-024-02681-3))

Resistance and power training also target several fall-relevant functional outcomes directly. A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis found that resistance training improved gait-related outcomes compared with passive control, although most comparisons with other active exercise were similar. A 2023 systematic review found that high-velocity power training had broadly similar effects to traditional resistance training, with modest advantages for SPPB and Timed Up and Go but substantial uncertainty for many other outcomes. Together, these findings support claim 3 in spirit, but not in its strongest form: strength and power matter a great deal for gait and fall-relevant function, yet they do not make aerobic work obsolete. ([Jochum et al., 2026](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11357-026-02156-4); [Morrison et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S183695532300053X))

That balance is visible in broader head-to-head comparisons. A 2024 meta-analysis in middle-aged to older adults found that aerobic training improved VO₂max/peak and six-minute walk distance more than resistance training, while resistance training improved lean body mass more than aerobic training. In practice, aerobic work and resistance work solve different problems: aerobic training is better for cardiorespiratory capacity and walking endurance, whereas resistance training better covers muscle-related outcomes. ([An et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324002061))

## Why the concurrent model fits the evidence best

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of 49 studies in middle-aged to older adults found that concurrent training improved cardiorespiratory fitness compared with resistance training alone and improved strength compared with aerobic training alone, without showing a clear penalty versus single-modality programs. Put simply, combining aerobic and resistance training appears to preserve much of the main gains of each rather than forcing a major tradeoff. ([Khalafi et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0031938422001949))

That does not mean every multicomponent program automatically beats every well-designed single-component program for every endpoint. A 2025 systematic review comparing multicomponent with single-component fall-prevention programs found only six RCTs and concluded that the evidence was limited and heterogeneous; multicomponent programs improved several functional measures, but Tai Ji Quan, a single-component balance-focused approach, produced comparable or even greater reductions in actual falls in the available data. So the best reading is not "more components are always better," but rather that older adults usually benefit from covering more than one physical capacity, with balance-specific work remaining especially important for falls. ([Kasicki et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2025.1636439/full); [Pillay et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13643-024-02681-3))

## Practical interpretation

- Zone-2-like MICT is a sensible aerobic anchor for older adults because it reliably improves cardiorespiratory fitness and cardiometabolic markers and is supported by decent short-term adherence and safety data. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Marriott et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-021-00344-4))

- But it is too simplistic as a complete plan for fitness, function, and fall-related outcomes. For falls in particular, the strongest support in the cited reviews is for balance/resistance work and tai chi rather than walking-focused aerobic programs alone; gait-related function is also addressed by resistance and power training. ([Pillay et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13643-024-02681-3); [Jochum et al., 2026](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11357-026-02156-4); [Morrison et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S183695532300053X))

- Higher-intensity work can be useful, especially for pushing VO₂max, but it is an optional enhancer rather than a universal requirement; direct functional superiority over MICT is inconsistent. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Stern et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-023-00551-1))

- A well-supported default within this evidence base is to combine regular moderate aerobic work with progressive resistance training, while adding explicit balance practice for fall prevention. Brief higher-intensity work can be added when tolerated and appropriately supervised. ([Khalafi et al., 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0031938422001949); [Pillay et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13643-024-02681-3); [Kasicki et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2025.1636439/full); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167494324001274); [Marriott et al., 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-021-00344-4))

- For falls specifically, balance/resistance work and tai chi have stronger support than walking-focused aerobic programs. ([Pillay et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13643-024-02681-3); [Kasicki et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/public-health/articles/10.3389/fpubh.2025.1636439/full))
