# VO₂max and Strength Training: Do They Interfere?

Concurrent training combines endurance training and resistance training in the same program. The interference question is not whether this combination works at all, but whether gains are smaller than they would have been with a matched single-mode program. The newer literature suggests that the interference effect is real but narrower than the popular version of the claim: for most recreationally trained adults, average attenuation is clearest for explosive strength, not for maximal strength or whole-muscle hypertrophy, while aerobic development is generally preserved overall in concurrent programs. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"))

## What the interference effect is

The practical comparison is between concurrent training and a matched resistance-only or endurance-only program. In the newest umbrella review, Held et al. (2026) summarized 17 meta-analyses covering 144 studies and 1,492 healthy participants. Compared with endurance training alone, concurrent training produced similar aerobic-capacity gains and greater strength gains. Compared with resistance training alone, concurrent training improved aerobic capacity, while strength, power, and hypertrophy outcomes were broadly comparable on average. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/"))

## What newer meta-analyses show overall

Schumann et al. (2022) pooled 43 studies comparing concurrent training with matched strength-only programs. The pooled effect was essentially null for maximal strength (SMD -0.06) and muscle hypertrophy (SMD -0.01), but negative for explosive strength (SMD -0.28). Their conclusion was that concurrent training does not clearly compromise maximal strength or muscle hypertrophy on average, but may attenuate explosive strength gains. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"))

That broader picture is consistent with the 2026 umbrella review: concurrent training appears to preserve most of the major adaptations people care about while adding the benefits of the other mode. The main unresolved area is how well these conclusions generalize to highly trained and elite athletes, because that subgroup remains under-represented. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37847373/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37847373/"))

## Where interference is most likely

Huiberts et al. (2024) found a small interference effect for lower-body strength in males, but not in females. The same review found slightly blunted VO₂max improvements in untrained endurance participants, but not in trained or highly trained endurance athletes. At the same time, the review emphasized that highly trained athletes are still sparsely represented in this literature, so certainty is lower at the sharp end of sport. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10933151/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10933151/"))

Protocol details still matter. Wilson et al. (2012) found that higher endurance frequency and duration were negatively related to hypertrophy, strength, and power, and that running was more problematic than cycling in that older dataset. Newer work softens, but does not completely erase, that concern: Schumann et al. (2022) did not find significant overall moderator effects for cycling versus running or for higher versus lower weekly frequency, while Lundberg et al. (2022) still found a small negative effect on muscle-fiber hypertrophy overall and preliminary evidence that running may be less compatible than cycling for type I fiber hypertrophy. The fairest reading is that interference is context-dependent rather than a large, uniform penalty. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22002517/ "Concurrent training: a meta-analysis examining interference of aerobic and resistance exercises - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/"))

Measurement choice also changes the answer. Monserdà-Vilaró et al. (2023) found no clear difference in whole-muscle hypertrophy between resistance training and concurrent training, yet resistance training produced greater type I and type II fiber hypertrophy when concurrent programs included HIIT, either alone or mixed with continuous endurance work. That helps explain why whole-muscle imaging studies can look reassuring while fiber-level biopsy studies remain more cautious. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36508686/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36508686/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/"))

## Timing and session order

On the same day, order matters most for the quality of the lifting session. Markov et al. (2022) found that doing aerobic work before strength work caused a moderate acute drop in subsequent strength performance (SMD 0.79). The decline appeared larger after sessions lasting more than 30 minutes and after moderate-to-high-intensity endurance work, although subgroup differences for duration and intensity were not clearly significant in that review abstract; cycling showed a larger decrement than running. Muscle-power measures did not show the same clear acute decrement in that review. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34878640/ "Acute Effects of Aerobic Exercise on Muscle Strength and Power in Trained Male Individuals: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

Over weeks of training, the sequencing effect appears modest. Gao and Yu (2023) found no overall difference in VO₂max between strength-then-endurance and endurance-then-strength sequences, but the strength-first order produced slightly greater lower-body strength gains. That advantage was more apparent in older adults, women, interventions longer than 8 weeks, and twice-weekly programs. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36776981/ "Effects of concurrent training sequence on VO2max and lower limb strength performance: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))

Spacing likely helps, especially if power is a priority. In Schumann et al. (2022), explosive-strength attenuation was more pronounced when both modes were performed in the same session than when sessions were separated by at least 3 hours. In a 9-week trial, Lee et al. (2020) separated HIIT and lifting by about 3 hours and found similar gains in leg-press 1RM, lean mass, and aerobic fitness across concurrent-training orders, even though some countermovement-jump variables favored resistance-only training. In older men, Cadore et al. (2012) likewise found no difference in VO₂peak between intra-session orders, but greater muscle-quality improvement when strength preceded endurance. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32407361/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32407361/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22178632/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22178632/"))

## Can strength training improve endurance?

Strength training can improve endurance performance, but usually not by raising VO₂max itself. Ramos-Campo et al. (2025) found moderate-to-large improvements in running economy across the included reviews, while four meta-analyses found no significant change in VO₂max. Llanos-Lagos et al. (2024) similarly found that high-load and combined strength methods improved running performance, but none of the examined methods improved VO₂max, velocity at VO₂max, maximum metabolic steady state, or sprint capacity. In highly trained runners, Balsalobre-Fernández et al. (2016) reported a large beneficial effect on running economy, with effective programs typically using 2 to 3 strength sessions per week for 8 to 12 weeks. The umbrella-review evidence here is directionally supportive, though the methodological quality of included reviews was not uniformly high. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40153564/ "The Effect of Strength Training on Endurance Performance Determinants in Middle- and Long-Distance Endurance Athletes: An Umbrella Review of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38627351/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38627351/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26694507/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26694507/"))

That pattern points more toward neuromuscular and economy-related adaptations than toward a large central-aerobic change. A randomized trial in elite cyclists by Aagaard et al. (2011) found that adding strength training increased maximal force and rate of force development, left VO₂max unchanged, improved 45-minute endurance capacity by 8%, and shifted fiber composition toward more type IIA and less type IIX fibers. That suggests strength work can improve long-effort performance through better force production and efficiency even when VO₂max itself barely moves. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21362056/ "Effects of resistance training on endurance capacity and muscle fiber composition in young top-level cyclists - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40153564/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40153564/"))

## Older adults

Older adults are one of the clearest cases where doing both is usually better than picking one if the goal is to improve both fitness domains rather than maximize only one. Khalafi et al. (2022) found that, in adults over 50, concurrent training improved VO₂max/peak more than resistance training alone and improved upper- and lower-body strength more than aerobic training alone, with no significant decrement versus the matched single-mode comparator on the mode-specific outcome. Markov et al. (2023) also found moderate positive effects of concurrent training on strength (SMD 0.74) and power (SMD 0.50), plus a small positive effect on cardiorespiratory endurance (SMD 0.48), in healthy adults aged 50 to 73 versus passive controls. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35728627/ "Impact of concurrent training versus aerobic or resistance training on cardiorespiratory fitness and muscular strength in middle-aged to older adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36222981/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36222981/"))

The programming details in older adults look goal-specific rather than universally antagonistic. In Markov et al. (2023), the largest strength effects appeared with 12 weeks of training, 3 sessions per week, 30 to 60 minutes per session, higher endurance intensities, and strength before endurance within the same session. Cardiorespiratory-endurance gains were larger with 21 weeks, 4 sessions per week, 60 to 90 minutes per session, and separate endurance and strength sessions. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36222981/ "Effects of Concurrent Strength and Endurance Training on Measures of Physical Fitness in Healthy Middle-Aged and Older Adults: A Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis - PubMed"))

## Muscle fiber adaptations and why the literature can look mixed

One reason the literature feels contradictory is that whole-muscle size, single-fiber size, maximal strength, jump power, and VO₂max are not the same outcome. Schumann et al. (2022) found little average penalty for maximal strength or whole-muscle hypertrophy, Lundberg et al. (2022) found a small negative effect on fiber hypertrophy, and Monserdà-Vilaró et al. (2023) suggested that HIIT-containing concurrent programs may be the most likely to show that fiber-level cost. So the interference question is best answered only after deciding which adaptation matters most. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35476184/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36508686/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36508686/"))

## Longevity interpretation

The claim that concurrent training is best for longevity is reasonable, but the evidence here is observational rather than intervention-based. Momma et al. (2022) found that muscle-strengthening activity was associated with roughly 10 to 20 percent lower risk across several outcomes, and that combining muscle-strengthening with aerobic activity was associated with lower all-cause, cardiovascular, and total-cancer mortality than doing neither. In a large US cohort, Zhao et al. (2020) reported hazard ratios of 0.89 for recommended muscle-strengthening activity alone, 0.71 for aerobic activity alone, and 0.60 for doing both. Among older adults, Shailendra et al. (2024) found the greatest mortality reduction in those who combined aerobic exercise with weight training. These findings support a combined-program interpretation for long-term health, but they do not by themselves prove that concurrent training is superior in a causal intervention sense. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35228201/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35228201/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32611588/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32611588/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38831478/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38831478/"))

## Quick reference points

- Overall concurrent training effect versus resistance-only training: little average decrement in maximal strength or whole-muscle hypertrophy, but a small decrement in explosive strength is more consistent. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"))
    
- Training sequence: no clear overall difference in VO₂max by order, but strength-before-endurance tends to produce slightly better lower-body strength gains. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36776981/ "Effects of concurrent training sequence on VO2max and lower limb strength performance: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"))
    
- Same-day issue: aerobic work before lifting acutely reduces strength performance, especially when the endurance bout is longer and harder, though the clearest significant subgroup difference reported in the review abstract was a larger decrement after cycling than after running. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34878640/ "Acute Effects of Aerobic Exercise on Muscle Strength and Power in Trained Male Individuals: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))
    
- Endurance upside from strength work: better running economy and better endurance performance can occur without a consistent increase in VO₂max. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40153564/ "The Effect of Strength Training on Endurance Performance Determinants in Middle- and Long-Distance Endurance Athletes: An Umbrella Review of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"))
    
- Older adults: concurrent training reliably improves both strength and cardiorespiratory fitness and is usually at least as effective as single-mode training for the outcome each mode targets best, while also improving the other domain. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35728627/ "Impact of concurrent training versus aerobic or resistance training on cardiorespiratory fitness and muscular strength in middle-aged to older adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36222981/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36222981/"))
    
- Elite athletes: evidence is thinner, so sport-specific programming deserves more caution than broad public-health advice. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41762427/"))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If maximal strength or hypertrophy is the main goal, concurrent training is still workable, but it helps to keep endurance volume reasonable, place lifting before endurance when both must occur on the same day, and separate sessions by at least a few hours when possible. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22002517/ "Concurrent training: a meta-analysis examining interference of aerobic and resistance exercises - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36776981/ "Effects of concurrent training sequence on VO2max and lower limb strength performance: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"))
    
- If explosive strength is the priority, be more conservative. That is the outcome most consistently blunted in the meta-analyses. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34757594/"))
    
- If endurance performance is the priority, adding 2 to 3 weekly strength sessions can improve running economy, and endurance performance can improve even when VO₂max does not change much. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26694507/ "Effects of Strength Training on Running Economy in Highly Trained Runners: A Systematic Review With Meta-Analysis of Controlled Trials - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40153564/ "The Effect of Strength Training on Endurance Performance Determinants in Middle- and Long-Distance Endurance Athletes: An Umbrella Review of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21362056/ "Effects of resistance training on endurance capacity and muscle fiber composition in young top-level cyclists - PubMed"))
    
- If the goal is broad health or aging well, concurrent training is probably the strongest overall package because it develops cardiorespiratory fitness and strength at the same time, though the longevity case is based on cohort data rather than direct intervention trials. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35728627/ "Impact of concurrent training versus aerobic or resistance training on cardiorespiratory fitness and muscular strength in middle-aged to older adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis - PubMed"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35228201/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35228201/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32611588/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32611588/"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38831478/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38831478/"))
