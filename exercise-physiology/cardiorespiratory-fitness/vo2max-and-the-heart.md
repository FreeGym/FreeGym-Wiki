# VO₂ Max and the Heart: Beneficial Remodeling or Risk?

The key distinction is between **fitness** and **lifetime exposure**. Higher VO₂ max, or more precisely higher cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF), is one of the strongest predictors of lower mortality and lower cardiovascular risk overall. But the literature raising concern about endurance sport and arrhythmias usually tracks **years of training, competition history, and cardiac remodeling**, not just a single VO₂ max number. That is why both “endurance training is purely protective” and “extreme exercise damages the heart” are too simple. ([Singh et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/), [Newman et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/), [Andersen et al. 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23756332/), [Flannery et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40561495/))

## What endurance remodeling is and is not

Endurance training produces a recognizable “athlete’s heart”: larger ventricular chambers, larger atria, and higher stroke volume. A CMR meta-analysis in competitive male athletes found that training volume had a clear effect on chamber size, especially the right ventricle, without showing a corresponding reduction in biventricular function. A meta-analysis of left atrial function likewise found that elite athletes have larger left atria than controls, with altered atrial phasic function during active training. In other words, remodeling is real and often physiologic, but the atria are also where the benign-adaptive and potentially pro-arrhythmic boundaries become less clear. ([D’Ascenzi et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30553678/), [Cuspidi et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30907013/))

## Why the overall cardiovascular picture is still favorable

The broad CRF literature is strongly favorable. In a 2025 meta-analysis of 42 studies representing 3.8 million observations, each 1-MET higher CRF (about 3.5 mL/kg/min) was associated with lower all-cause mortality (RR 0.86) and lower cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.84). In a 122,007-patient clinical cohort, the lowest mortality was seen in the highest-fitness group, with no upper limit of benefit observed for all-cause mortality. ([Singh et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/), [Mandsager et al. 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/))

Athlete cohorts also look favorable on the outcomes that matter most. A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis found that athletes lived longer than the general population and had lower cardiovascular mortality overall. In the large [Vasaloppet skiing cohort](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/), skiers of either sex had lower stroke incidence than nonskiers, even though the highest-dose male skiers had more atrial fibrillation. That is a good summary of the whole debate: **better global cardiovascular outcomes can coexist with a narrower arrhythmia trade-off.** ([Runacres et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33368029/), [Svedberg et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/))

## Why atrial fibrillation is the main exception

The most consistent long-term risk signal is **atrial fibrillation**. A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis estimated that athletes had higher odds of atrial fibrillation than non-athlete controls (OR 2.46). In a cohort of 52,755 long-distance cross-country skiers, those with the highest race exposure had higher risks of atrial fibrillation and bradyarrhythmias, while supraventricular tachycardia and ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation/cardiac arrest were not clearly associated with greater race exposure. ([Newman et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/), [Andersen et al. 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23756332/))

More recent athlete-specific data point in the same direction. In former world-class rowers, atrial fibrillation prevalence was 21.5% versus 3.2% in matched community controls, and incident atrial fibrillation over 4 years was also higher (HR 2.8). The rowers had favorable conventional cardiovascular risk profiles, although stroke prevalence was higher, which suggests that exercise-induced remodeling and individual susceptibility, including genetic susceptibility, may both matter. ([Flannery et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40561495/))

Sex also matters. In the [Vasaloppet cohort](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/), female skiers had _lower_ atrial fibrillation incidence than female nonskiers, whereas male skiers had similar overall atrial fibrillation incidence to male nonskiers but the highest rates were concentrated in the men with the fastest times and the greatest number of races. That makes the signal look dose-dependent and male-skewed, not universal. ([Svedberg et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/))

## Why dose matters more than slogans

A 2024 dose-response meta-analysis of 103 prospective studies found that leisure-time physical activity reduced cardiovascular disease, coronary heart disease, and stroke risk up to roughly 20 to 25 MET-hours per week, with little extra benefit beyond that range. For atrial fibrillation, the curve was different: it was **U-shaped**, with the greatest risk reduction around 10 MET-hours per week and less benefit at higher levels. ([Kazemi et al. 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38659024/))

The CRF literature shows a similar tension. In [UK Biobank](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30796106/), each higher MET of fitness was associated with lower all-cause mortality and lower circulatory disease incidence, but very high fitness levels above 10 METs were associated with a greater incidence of atrial fibrillation compared with average fitness. So the most defensible interpretation is not that high fitness becomes “bad,” but that at the far right tail, **arrhythmia risk can rise even while overall prognosis stays favorable.** ([Steell et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30796106/))

## What imaging studies do and do not show

Imaging confirms that endurance training remodels the heart, but it does not support a simple “scarred heart” story in all athletes. A 2025 cross-sectional CMR study of master endurance athletes found only a limited median left atrial fibrotic burden and no clear association between fibrosis burden and training duration or atrial size/function, although fibrotic burden was higher in those who competed in long-distance mountain-bike races. That argues against a straightforward model in which more years of training automatically mean more atrial scar, but it does not settle the question. ([Hopman et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40397349/))

Coronary CT findings are also mixed. The [Master@Heart study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36881712/) found more coronary plaques, including more non-calcified proximal plaques, in lifelong male endurance athletes than in similarly low-risk non-athletes. But a 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis concluded that endurance athletes were more likely to have coronary atherosclerosis overall **without** being more likely to have high-risk or obstructive plaques. Those findings are not trivial, but they also do not prove that high VO₂ max or endurance sport causes worse clinical outcomes in a broad sense. ([De Bosscher et al. 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36881712/), [Guarnieri et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40097033/))

## Limits and open debates

Most of the evidence behind the arrhythmia debate is observational, male-dominant, and built on selected cohorts of veteran endurance athletes. That makes the evidence useful, but not definitive for causation. The pattern across studies suggests that atrial fibrillation risk is shaped by some combination of **lifetime exposure, chamber remodeling, sex, and genetic susceptibility**, rather than by a single “too high VO₂ max” threshold. That is why the “extreme exercise harms the heart” narrative is probably overstated as a general claim, even though the atrial fibrillation signal at very high lifelong volumes looks real. ([Cuspidi et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30907013/), [Newman et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/), [Svedberg et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/), [Flannery et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40561495/))

## Practical interpretation

- For most people, higher VO₂ max / CRF is strongly associated with lower mortality and lower cardiovascular risk. ([Singh et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/), [Mandsager et al. 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/))
    
- The clearest long-term trade-off of very high lifelong endurance exposure is a higher risk of atrial fibrillation, especially in men. ([Newman et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/), [Svedberg et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31446766/), [Flannery et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40561495/))
    
- Dose matters: benefits climb quickly from low to moderate-to-high activity, then flatten, while atrial fibrillation risk may rise at the far right tail. ([Kazemi et al. 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38659024/), [Steell et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30796106/))
    
- The most accurate summary is: **endurance training usually produces healthy remodeling, and higher CRF is associated with lower cardiovascular risk overall, but in some lifelong high-volume athletes it may also create an atrial substrate that raises atrial fibrillation risk.** ([D’Ascenzi et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30553678/), [Cuspidi et al. 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30907013/), [Singh et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/), [Mandsager et al. 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/), [Runacres et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33368029/), [Newman et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34253538/), [Flannery et al. 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40561495/))
