# Does Increasing VO₂ Max Cause Health Benefits, or Is It Just a Marker?

VO₂max is best treated as both a risk marker and a modifiable phenotype. The current evidence is difficult to reconcile with a pure-marker explanation, but it also falls short of proving that VO₂max itself, separated from the training and biologic changes that raise it, is the sole causal agent. The best-supported interpretation is that cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF) is both a marker of underlying health and plausibly part of the causal pathway to better outcomes. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/))

## What VO₂ max is and is not

VO₂max, or VO₂peak when a true maximum is not demonstrated, is a direct way to quantify CRF during exercise testing. In the long-term outcomes literature, however, many studies use CRF expressed in METs from maximal treadmill tests or estimated CRF rather than laboratory gas-analysis VO₂max. A 2025 meta-analysis of 42 studies found similar dose-response associations with all-cause and cardiovascular mortality whether CRF was objectively measured or estimated. That makes the broader CRF literature relevant to the VO₂max question, though estimated CRF should not be treated as interchangeable with laboratory VO₂max for causal inference. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))

That also means the practical causal question is not whether a number has mystical protective power. It is whether the physiologic capacity captured by CRF or VO₂max sits on the pathway to better health. Baseline associations alone cannot settle that, so the most relevant evidence here comes from dose-response cohorts, repeated-measures cohorts, randomized exercise trials, and mechanistic trials. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25281334/))

## The observational association is large

A 2024 overview of 26 systematic reviews representing more than 20.9 million observations found that high versus low CRF was associated with an all-cause mortality hazard ratio of 0.47, and each 1-MET higher CRF was associated with roughly 11 to 17 percent lower all-cause mortality. The same overview rated the certainty of the evidence from very low to moderate, which is an important reminder that most mortality data are still observational. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))

A 2022 dose-response meta-analysis of 34 cohort studies reached a very similar conclusion. Per 1-MET higher CRF, pooled relative risks were 0.88 for all-cause mortality, 0.87 for cardiovascular mortality, and 0.93 for cancer mortality. Compared with the lowest CRF category, the highest CRF category had pooled relative risks of 0.47 for all-cause mortality and 0.49 for cardiovascular mortality. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/))

## Why the pure-marker explanation is incomplete

If CRF were only a passive marker of preexisting health, then changes in fitness over time should add little once baseline health and weight are accounted for. But that is not what repeated-measures cohorts show. In the Aerobics Center Longitudinal Study, every 1-MET improvement in fitness over time was associated with 15 percent lower all-cause mortality and 19 percent lower cardiovascular mortality, and BMI change itself was not associated with mortality after adjustment for confounders and fitness change. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/))

That pattern also appears when fitness change follows training. In the Ball State cohort, adults completed two maximal cardiopulmonary exercise tests, the second after exercise training. After multivariable adjustment, each 1-MET improvement in CRF was associated with 20 percent lower mortality risk in men and 38 percent lower mortality risk in women, and follow-up CRF predicted mortality better than baseline CRF. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31303425/))

More recent repeated-measures data point in the same direction. In a 2024 cohort of 148,825 healthy young Asian men, a 1 percent annual slowing of 2.4 km run time over 5 to 9 years was associated with a 13 percent higher hazard of first major cardiovascular events and a 6 percent higher hazard of all-cause mortality; the association with cardiovascular events persisted in sensitivity analyses adjusted for baseline BMI. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39286322/))

## How much seems to be mediated by adiposity and classic risk factors

Part of the apparent benefit of higher fitness clearly runs through weight-related and cardiometabolic pathways. In the CARDIA cohort, low fitness in young adults predicted later diabetes, hypertension, and metabolic syndrome; adjustment for baseline BMI substantially weakened those associations, and the apparent benefit of improved fitness over 7 years was reduced after accounting for weight change. That is evidence for partial mediation, not for a purely independent fitness effect. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14679272/))

A modeling analysis of 19 exercise-only cardiac rehabilitation trials reached a similar conclusion from another angle. Exercise training reduced pooled cardiac mortality by 28 percent, and the model estimated that about 58 percent of that benefit could be attributed to changes in smoking, cholesterol, and systolic blood pressure. In other words, in that secondary-prevention setting, a large share of benefit seems to ride through familiar risk factors. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16926666/))

But “partly mediated” is not the same as “fully explained.” A 2025 systematic review of the joint association of CRF and BMI found that overweight-fit and obese-fit groups were not statistically different from the normal-weight-fit reference group in pooled mortality estimates, whereas normal-weight-unfit, overweight-unfit, and obese-unfit groups had roughly 2- to 3-fold higher mortality risk. That does not mean adiposity is irrelevant; it means CRF materially modifies risk and is not just a proxy for fatness. The absence of a statistically significant difference from the normal-weight-fit reference group should not be overread as proof of equivalence. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39537313/))

## What randomized trials can and cannot show

In adults without cardiovascular disease, randomized trials mostly stop at intermediate end points rather than lifespan. In a 2015 meta-analysis of 160 randomized controlled trials involving 7,487 participants, exercise training significantly raised CRF and improved several cardiometabolic biomarkers, including triglycerides, HDL-related measures, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, and HbA1c. The same paper noted that long-term randomized trials in healthy populations with cardiovascular disease incidence end points are lacking, so this evidence supports causal effects on biology more than direct proof about lifespan. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/))

For hard clinical end points, the strongest randomized evidence comes from secondary prevention. A 2023 meta-analysis of 85 randomized trials in coronary heart disease found that exercise-based cardiac rehabilitation reduced cardiovascular mortality, hospitalizations, and myocardial infarction. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/)) The 2021 Cochrane update reached a similar conclusion, finding reduced all-cause hospitalizations up to 12 months and possible reductions in cardiovascular mortality and myocardial infarction over longer follow-up. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34741536/)) Neither review, however, found clear evidence of reduced all-cause mortality at medium- or long-term follow-up. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34741536/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/))

Randomized data in disease populations also show that improving fitness can matter even when resting cardiac measurements barely move. In heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, a meta-analysis of six randomized trials found that exercise training improved peak oxygen uptake by 2.72 mL/kg/min and improved quality of life, without significant changes in resting systolic or diastolic function. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25399909/))

Still, these trials randomize exercise training, not isolated lifelong increments in VO₂max. So they support causality of interventions that raise CRF, but they do not prove that VO₂max by itself, abstracted from the training that produces it, is the only operative mechanism. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/))

## Mechanistic evidence

Flow-mediated dilation, or FMD, is an ultrasound measure of endothelial-dependent vasodilation. In a meta-analysis of randomized trials, aerobic, resistance, and combined training all improved FMD, and a 2-MET increase in aerobic training intensity was associated with about a 1 percentage-point greater FMD improvement. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25281334/)) In coronary artery disease patients specifically, a 2022 meta-analysis found that cardiac rehabilitation improved FMD by 1.04 while not significantly changing nitroglycerin-mediated dilation, which points toward endothelial-dependent adaptation rather than a general vasodilator artifact, though heterogeneity in that meta-analysis was high. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35468652/))

Mechanistic trials also argue against a pure relabeling effect. In older adults, six weeks of high-intensity interval training increased insulin sensitivity and VO₂max while lowering LDL cholesterol, visceral fat, and percent body fat, and increased muscle GLUT4, glycogen synthase, and hexokinase II. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29197155/)) In sedentary adults, interval training increased VO₂max alongside maximal cardiac output and skeletal-muscle mitochondrial oxidative capacity, and those gains tracked together. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18417645/)) Another randomized trial found that sprint-interval training, rather than the HIIT comparison arm, increased maximal mitochondrial respiration and proteins linked to mitochondrial remodeling, including PGC-1α and p53. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26572168/)) These findings make biological sense if improving CRF is part of the pathway to better health, not merely a passive sign of it. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29197155/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18417645/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26572168/))

## Limits and open debates

The remaining uncertainty is mostly about attribution, not about whether fitter phenotypes do better. The strongest observational overview still graded the long-term evidence from very low to moderate certainty, and even good repeated-measures cohorts cannot fully eliminate residual confounding or reverse causation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))

Genetic evidence is also mixed: one Mendelian-randomization analysis supported a causal association between genetically predicted fitness and lower type 2 diabetes risk independent of adiposity, ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37400433/)) whereas another did not find evidence that genetically predicted VO₂max improved type 2 diabetes or longevity. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38864459/))

It is also too strong to claim that VO₂max alone has been proven to extend lifespan in isolation. Exercise changes body composition, insulin sensitivity, lipids, blood pressure, endothelial function, and other cardiometabolic traits all at once. The best evidence therefore supports a multicausal model in which CRF is one integrated phenotype sitting amid several overlapping pathways. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25281334/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29197155/))

But the opposite claim, that fitness is only a marker of healthier people, is also too strong. Fitness change over time predicts later outcomes after adjustment for baseline fitness and BMI; randomized exercise trials improve clinical events in coronary disease; and mechanistic trials show training-induced changes in endothelial function, insulin sensitivity, cardiac output, and mitochondrial respiration. The cleanest summary is that CRF is both a marker and part of the causal chain, but the causal contribution of VO₂max itself, abstracted from the adaptations that raise it, remains incompletely resolved. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31303425/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25281334/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29197155/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18417645/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26572168/))

## Quick reference values

- High versus low CRF in meta-analytic overview: all-cause mortality HR 0.47. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))
    
- Per 1-MET higher CRF in cohort meta-analysis: RR 0.88 for all-cause mortality and 0.87 for cardiovascular mortality. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/))
    
- Repeated-measures cohort in men: each 1-MET improvement over time linked to 15 percent lower all-cause mortality and 19 percent lower cardiovascular mortality, independent of BMI change. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/))
    
- Post-training cohort: each 1-MET improvement linked to 20 percent lower mortality in men and 38 percent lower mortality in women. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31303425/))
    
- Exercise-based cardiac rehabilitation RCT meta-analysis: RR 0.74 for cardiovascular mortality, 0.77 for hospitalization, and 0.82 for myocardial infarction, but not a statistically significant reduction in overall mortality (RR 0.96). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/))
    
- Cardiac rehabilitation mediation model: about 58 percent of mortality benefit attributable to smoking, cholesterol, and systolic blood pressure changes. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16926666/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- VO₂max or CRF is an excellent prognostic marker. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))
    
- Raising CRF through training probably does confer real health benefits, but those benefits appear to come through multiple linked mechanisms rather than a stand-alone “VO₂max effect,” and long-term all-cause mortality reduction in randomized trials remains unproven. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34741536/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/))
    
- The evidence is strongest against the pure-marker view and weaker for the claim that VO₂max alone, independent of all accompanying adaptations, is the single causal driver of longer life. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22144631/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31303425/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25281334/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29197155/))
