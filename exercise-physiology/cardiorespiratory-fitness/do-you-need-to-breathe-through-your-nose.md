# Do You Need to Breathe Through Your Nose?

You do not need to breathe through your nose in the strong way online breathing trends often imply. The evidence supports a narrower conclusion: nasal breathing has real physiological functions and can be workable or useful at rest and during lower-intensity exercise, but controlled trials do not show a robust performance advantage from forcing exclusive nasal breathing, and at higher intensities it often becomes a ventilatory bottleneck. Mouth taping has limited, condition-specific evidence and real safety caveats. Claims about “CO₂ tolerance” are more speculative than the social-media versions suggest. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12094774/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31220170/))

## What nasal breathing is and is not

Nasal breathing is not a magic performance switch. The nose warms and humidifies inspired air, and it also delivers nitric oxide generated in the upper airways into the lungs. Those are genuine physiological functions. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8599744/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8971255/))

The strongest practical question is not whether the nose does anything useful. It does. The practical question is whether deliberately forcing nasal-only breathing improves outcomes such as power, endurance, ventilatory efficiency, or perceived effort during exercise. That is where the evidence becomes much less impressive. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10858538/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5466403/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))

## What controlled trials show in healthy people

In a 2023 randomized crossover trial, 19 physically healthy adults performed 60 minutes of low-intensity cycling with either nasal-only or unrestricted oronasal breathing. Nasal breathing reduced minute ventilation, breathing frequency, oxygen uptake, carbon dioxide output, and late-session lactate, but it did not significantly change training intensity distribution by power output or heart rate, and perceived effort was not significantly different. In other words, nasal breathing changed the breathing pattern, but it did not clearly improve performance. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/))

A 2024 study in 107 physically active adults compared nose-only, nose-in/mouth-out, and mouth-only breathing during bench press sets to failure. Repetitions to failure did not differ significantly by breathing route. Men had slightly lower postexercise heart rate with nose-only breathing, but there were no meaningful differences in oxygen saturation or perceived exertion. This is another example where changing the route of breathing did not translate into a meaningful performance gain. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10858538/))

A 2017 counterbalanced Wingate study found no difference in anaerobic performance between nasal-only and oral-only breathing. The nasal condition showed less evidence of hyperventilation, but heart rate was higher late in the test, suggesting that any respiratory pattern advantage did not produce better sprint output. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5466403/))

## The low-intensity versus high-intensity split

The most defensible summary is that nasal breathing may be workable at low intensity, but it becomes increasingly limiting as exercise intensity rises. That pattern has appeared in both older physiology work and newer controlled exercise studies. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7444224/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/), [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/22/5/718))

A classic exercise-physiology study found that 20 of 30 healthy adults naturally switched from nasal to oronasal breathing during submaximal exercise at about 105 watts and a ventilation of about 35 L/min. The transition point varied across people, but the broader point is that moving away from pure nasal breathing as ventilatory demand rises appears to be a normal physiological response rather than a failure of discipline. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7444224/))

The strongest recent maximal-exercise evidence points in the same direction. In a 2025 BreathWISE study, 12 healthy adults completed maximal cardiopulmonary exercise tests under different breathing conditions. Exclusive nasal breathing produced lower peak oxygen uptake, lower peak ventilation, lower respiratory rate, lower workload, and greater discomfort than standard or oral conditions. Differences were small at rest and around the anaerobic threshold, but at peak exercise nasal-only breathing impaired capacity, apparently because ventilation could not rise enough to match demand. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))

A 2025 within-subject treadmill study in healthy women also found that nasal breathing had little effect at lower speeds, but at 10 to 11 km/h it became less effective at sustaining ventilation than oral or oronasal breathing. At the highest tested speed, ventilatory efficiency markers were not better with nasal breathing. ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/22/5/718))

An older maximal-exercise study reached a similar mechanical conclusion: nasal breathing reduced maximal ventilation much more than it reduced maximal oxygen uptake, meaning people could still exercise, but the breathing route itself constrained how much ventilation they could generate. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8599744/))

## Mouth breathing is not a defect at higher intensity

Online discussions sometimes treat mouth breathing during exercise as a sign that something has gone wrong. The direct literature does not support that. As exercise gets harder, the body often shifts toward oronasal breathing because total airflow demand increases and nasal resistance becomes more limiting. That makes mouth or combined mouth-and-nose breathing a normal response at higher workloads, not obviously a problem to be “fixed.” ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7444224/))

That does not mean nasal breathing is useless. It means the claim has to be intensity-specific. At low intensities, nasal breathing can be comfortable and may reduce ventilation for a given workload. At high intensities, insisting on exclusive nasal breathing is more likely to cap ventilation than to improve performance. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))

## Nasal breathing and ventilatory efficiency

Ventilatory efficiency claims are partly true, but context matters. In the 2023 low-intensity cycling crossover trial, nasal-only breathing lowered ventilation and breathing frequency while maintaining similar external intensity. That can be described as a different, and in some respects more economical, ventilatory pattern during easy exercise. But this did not clearly produce superior training output or lower perceived effort. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/))

There is also clinical evidence that nasal breathing can improve ventilatory efficiency metrics during submaximal exercise in patients with heart failure or chronic coronary syndromes. In a 2024 randomized comparison, nasal breathing reduced VE/VCO2, ventilation, and breathing frequency and raised end-tidal CO2 during exercise at 50 percent of peak power. That is interesting and potentially clinically relevant, but it was a submaximal protocol in cardiac patients, not a demonstration that healthy people perform better when they force nasal-only breathing during hard training. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39165283/))

In healthy people at higher intensity, the picture becomes less favorable to exclusive nasal breathing. The 2025 maximal CPET study found lower peak ventilation and lower peak work with nasal-only breathing, and the treadmill study in women found that nasal breathing became less effective as speed increased. So the “efficiency” argument should not be stretched into a blanket performance claim. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/), [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/22/5/718))

## Nitric oxide: plausible mechanism, limited performance proof

The nitric-oxide part of the story is real. Studies from the 1990s showed that the paranasal sinuses are a major source of nitric oxide and that nasal breathing increases delivery of that nitric oxide to the lungs. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8971255/)) Exhaled nitric oxide is also higher during nasal than oral breathing. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9504133/)) Some experiments also showed modest improvements in oxygenation during nasal breathing under specific conditions. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8971255/))

But the leap from “nasal breathing increases nitric oxide delivery” to “therefore nasal breathing improves athletic performance” is not well supported. Yasuda and colleagues found higher exhaled nitric oxide with nasal breathing during submaximal cycling, but no significant difference in the measured cardiorespiratory variables. That is exactly the kind of result that should make people cautious about overstating the mechanism. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9504133/))

There is also reason to think the nitric-oxide advantage matters less as exercise intensity rises. During exercise, the nasal contribution to total exhaled nitric oxide falls and inhaled nasopharyngeal nitric-oxide concentration is diluted as minute ventilation rises, ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8806888/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10872130/)) and nasal nitric oxide in the cavity itself drops markedly during maximal exercise. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9124070/)) Mechanistically, that makes it less plausible that nasal nitric oxide is a major determinant of high-intensity performance.

## Mechanical nasal aids have not shown much

If the basic claim were that “more nasal airflow improves performance,” then devices that mechanically open the nose should show clear benefits. They largely have not. A systematic review and meta-analysis of external nasal dilator strips found no statistically significant improvement in VO2max, heart rate, or perceived exertion in healthy individuals. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32683573/))

A 2021 randomized crossover trial of a nasal “turbine” breathing device likewise found no significant improvement in peak oxygen uptake, peak work rate, dyspnea, ventilation, or work of breathing compared with sham. That does not prove nasal airflow never matters, but it argues against large, generalizable performance effects from nasal “hacks.” ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32520874/))

## Mouth taping is not a general performance tool

Mouth taping is the part of the trend where the safety discussion matters most. A 2025 systematic review identified only 10 studies with 213 total patients. Just two studies found significant improvement in sleep-apnea metrics; several others found no significant differences, and the review explicitly warned about the risk of harm, including asphyxiation when nasal obstruction is present. Many included studies excluded people with nasal blockage or other pathology, which means the practice has often been studied only in carefully selected people. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12094774/))

A separate 2025 scoping review reached a similarly cautious conclusion: the literature was heterogeneous, consensus was limited, and better studies are needed before nocturnal mouth taping can be treated as a broadly supported intervention. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39662104/))

There is one small preliminary study often cited by enthusiasts. In 20 selected mouth-breathers with mild obstructive sleep apnea who could tolerate mouth sealing, mouth taping reduced the apnea-hypopnea index and snoring index over one week. But this was a before-and-after study without a control group, in a narrow population, and it does not justify generalizing the practice to healthy people or treating it as a performance enhancer. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36141367/))

There is also direct counterevidence outside that narrow sleep-apnea context. A randomized crossover trial in symptomatic asthma found that mouth taping at night did not improve asthma control, morning peak flow, or symptoms. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19285849/))

## “CO₂ tolerance” is real, but the training claims are ahead of the evidence

“CO₂ tolerance” refers, roughly, to how breathing is regulated and how strongly rising carbon dioxide drives ventilatory discomfort and respiratory effort. That is a real physiological domain. But the popular claim that simple breathing drills substantially improve exercise performance by raising CO₂ tolerance is ahead of the controlled evidence. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31220170/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12354527/))

A 2019 paced-breathing training study found that seven days of practice changed carbon-dioxide handling during the breathing task itself: participants showed less drop in end-tidal CO2 while pacing their breathing, and the task felt more pleasant and relaxing. But the study did not show that this translated into better sports performance. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31220170/))

A 2025 review of breath-holding as a performance strategy was also cautious. It suggested there may be some potential for breath-holding as a priming method, but emphasized that placebo-controlled studies, practical dosing, and long-term effects remain uncertain. That is a long way from the stronger claims often made online. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12354527/))

## Quick reference

- **Nitric oxide claim:** Mechanistically real. Nasal breathing increases delivery of upper-airway nitric oxide to the lungs, but direct performance benefits are not consistently shown in controlled trials. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8971255/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9504133/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))
    
- **Low-intensity exercise:** Nasal breathing can reduce ventilation and breathing frequency without clearly changing external workload or perceived effort. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/))
    
- **High-intensity exercise:** Exclusive nasal breathing often limits peak ventilation and can reduce peak exercise capacity. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))
    
- **Mouth breathing:** Normal and often useful as intensity rises; the shift toward oronasal breathing is a common physiological response. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7444224/))
    
- **Nasal devices:** Meta-analytic and randomized evidence does not show a clear generic performance benefit. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32683573/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32520874/))
    
- **Mouth taping:** Limited, selective evidence in snoring or mild sleep-apnea contexts; not well supported as a general wellness or performance practice, and there are safety concerns if nasal obstruction is present. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12094774/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39662104/))
    
- **CO₂ tolerance training:** Physiologically plausible, but direct performance evidence remains limited and much weaker than popular claims suggest. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31220170/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12354527/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- You do not need to force nasal-only breathing to get good conditioning or good performance. The strongest evidence does not show a broad performance advantage from doing so. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10858538/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5466403/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))
    
- Nasal breathing is most reasonable as an option at rest, during easy exercise, or as a comfort/preference strategy, not as a universal rule. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/))
    
- Once exercise intensity rises enough that ventilation becomes limiting, switching to mouth or oronasal breathing is usually a normal solution, not a mistake. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7444224/))
    
- The “breathing hacks” story appears smaller than the internet version. That is an inference from the current trial pattern: effects are inconsistent, often small, and frequently disappear when the outcome is actual performance rather than a mechanistic marker. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156973/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10858538/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5466403/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12266398/))
    
- Mouth taping should be treated much more cautiously than it is on social media, especially in anyone with possible nasal obstruction, sleep-disordered breathing, or other respiratory issues. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12094774/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39662104/))
