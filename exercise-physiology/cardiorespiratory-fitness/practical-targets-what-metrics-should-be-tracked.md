# Practical Targets: What Metrics Should You Track?

A useful metric should do at least one of three jobs well: predict meaningful outcomes, detect real change, or make the behavior easier to repeat. No single metric is best across all three. Cardiorespiratory fitness measures such as VO₂ max are powerful for risk stratification, but simple behavior metrics, repeatable field-performance tests, and basic function tests are often better default tools because they are easier to collect, easier to understand, and easier to act on consistently. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Momma et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-105061); [Ferguson et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500\(22\)00111-X); [Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [Marín-Jiménez et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-109173)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S209525462400142X))

## What makes a metric useful

In practice, a metric is most useful when it has three properties: it links to outcomes people actually care about, it is reproducible enough that real change is larger than normal noise, and it is simple enough that people will keep measuring it. That is why the best "health" dashboard is not necessarily the same as the best "training" dashboard. Among commonly used options, outcome prediction is often strongest for cardiorespiratory fitness, steps, gait speed, and grip strength; sensitivity to training is often strongest for standardized performance tests; adherence support is often strongest for simple daily or weekly counts such as steps, minutes, and completed sessions. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Ferguson et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500\(22\)00111-X); [Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [López-Bueno et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S209525462400142X))

## VO₂ max and thresholds: high signal, lower practicality

Cardiorespiratory fitness is one of the strongest predictors in this whole area. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis covering 42 studies, 35 cohorts, and 3.8 million observations found that each 1-MET higher cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with a 14% lower all-cause mortality risk and a 16% lower cardiovascular mortality risk. Importantly, objectively measured fitness, exercise-estimated fitness, and non-exercise-estimated fitness performed similarly for mortality prediction. That means the signal is strong, but it also means the lab is not the only way to get useful information. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S209525462400142X))

Ventilatory thresholds add different information. VT1 and VT2 are exercise-intensity markers used in cardiopulmonary exercise testing, and threshold determination can be reasonably reliable when standardized methods are used. There is also prognostic evidence for oxygen uptake at ventilatory threshold, but that evidence base is much smaller than the broader cardiorespiratory-fitness literature and comes from more specific cohorts. ([Kunutsor et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijcard.2017.01.156); [Gaskill et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200111000-00007)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167527316321581))

The practical implication is that VO₂ max and VT1/VT2 are useful tools for structured endurance training, clinical exercise testing, or periodic calibration. They are less compelling as the main weekly dashboard for most people, because simpler metrics capture more of the day-to-day behavior that actually changes health. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Gaskill et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200111000-00007); [Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Ferguson et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500\(22\)00111-X)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S209525462400142X))

## Minutes, steps, and strength sessions: a practical default dashboard

The strongest case for a default tracking set is still simple behavior. A 2025 systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis of device-measured daily steps included 57 studies from 35 cohorts and found inverse associations between steps and multiple outcomes. Compared with 2000 steps per day, 7000 steps per day was associated with a 47% lower risk of all-cause mortality, a 25% lower risk of cardiovascular disease incidence, a 47% lower risk of cardiovascular mortality, a 38% lower risk of dementia, a 22% lower risk of depressive symptoms, and a 28% lower risk of falls. Certainty was moderate for several outcomes, but lower for some endpoints such as cardiovascular mortality and falls. A 2022 meta-analysis of 15 cohorts also found progressively lower mortality with higher step counts, with much of the benefit appearing by roughly 6000-8000 steps per day in older adults and 8000-10000 in younger adults. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Paluch et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(21\)00302-9)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2468266725001641))

Weekly minutes matter too, but recent evidence suggests the base layer is total movement rather than intensity alone. A harmonized meta-analysis of accelerometer-based cohort studies found total activity volume had the stronger association with lower mortality, with only a small added benefit from doing the same volume at higher intensity. Large dose-response meta-analyses still support the familiar 150-300 minutes per week range as meaningful, but the deeper message is that accumulating more movement is the foundation. ([Tarp et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amepre.2024.07.022); [Garcia et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2022-105669)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0749379724002629))

Strength sessions deserve to sit beside steps and weekly minutes. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of cohort studies found muscle-strengthening activities were associated with roughly 10-17% lower risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, total cancer, diabetes, and lung cancer. The dose-response pattern did not support an unlimited-more-is-better model; the most defensible practical floor in current cohort evidence was about 1-2 sessions per week. ([Momma et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-105061); [Coleman et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2022-105519)). ([Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Muscle-strengthening-activities-are-associated-with-Momma-Kawakami/477307b9054d05230426757878a5c92d72cad0e9))

These simple metrics also do better on adherence. A 2022 umbrella review of wearable-tracker interventions found activity trackers increased physical activity with standardized effects in the small-to-moderate range, translating roughly to about 1800 extra steps per day, about 40 more minutes of walking per day, and modest improvements in fitness and body weight. The same review judged the gains clinically meaningful and sustained over time. That makes steps, minutes, and session counts useful not just for prediction, but for behavior change. ([Ferguson et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500\(22\)00111-X)). ([The Lancet](https://www.thelancet.com/pdfs/journals/landig/PIIS2589-7500%2822%2900111-X.pdf))

## Performance metrics: often best for training response

If the goal is getting fitter or faster, standardized performance metrics are often more useful than VO₂ max as a routine tracking tool. Pace or power on a fixed route, a short repeatable time trial, or pace or power at a fixed heart rate under standardized conditions can pick up changes that a lab test may miss between formal assessments. Review-level reliability data show that many power-based performance tests have low typical error, often around 1-2%, while time trials are generally more reliable than time-to-exhaustion tests, which commonly show much larger variability. ([Hopkins et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200131030-00005); [Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Laursen et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1249/mss.0b013e31806010f5); [Shushan et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2024.04.006); [Shushan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00564-w)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11286357/))

Submaximal heart-rate metrics fit the same logic. The review literature in athletic settings suggests submaximal exercise heart rate can serve as a valid and useful proxy for tracking cardiorespiratory-fitness changes, especially when the test, terrain, and conditions are standardized. The clearest support here comes from team-sport and football settings rather than the general population. That is the rationale behind "pace at fixed heart rate" or "power at fixed heart rate" tracking. These are better thought of as training-response metrics than as broad health-risk markers. ([Shushan et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2024.04.006); [Shushan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00564-w)). ([Western Sydney University](https://researchers.westernsydney.edu.au/en/publications/assessing-criterion-and-longitudinal-validity-of-submaximal-heart/))

The practical implication is straightforward: if someone is serious about endurance training, the first advanced metric to add is usually a repeatable field-performance test, not a constant chase for a new VO₂ max number. VO₂ max and thresholds can still be useful periodic checkpoints, but they do not need to be the headline metric. ([Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)). ([University of Birmingham](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/validity-reliability-and-sensitivity-of-measures-of-sporting-perf))

## Function metrics: especially useful for aging, independence, and clinical relevance

Function metrics become especially compelling when the goal is independence, frailty prevention, or clinical relevance. Gait speed has one of the strongest outcome records in this space. The classic pooled analysis in older adults found gait speed was strongly associated with survival, and later meta-analytic work linked slow gait speed with premature mortality and cardiovascular outcomes. A 2024 systematic review also found usual and fast gait-speed tests to be reliable and valid in community-dwelling older adults, with intraclass correlation coefficients generally in the 0.72-0.98 range. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [Veronese et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jamda.2018.06.007); [Mehdipour et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1093/ageing/afae055)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))

Grip strength is similarly strong. A 2022 dose-response meta-analysis including 48 studies and more than 3.1 million participants found a clear inverse association between handgrip strength and all-cause, cancer, and cardiovascular mortality. Umbrella-review evidence also supports grip strength as a marker for mortality and disability, with the strongest umbrella-level support being highly suggestive rather than universally conclusive across all outcomes. Reliability is generally excellent, but agreement between devices is imperfect, which means longitudinal tracking should use the same dynamometer and the same protocol rather than switching devices and comparing raw numbers. ([López-Bueno et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778); [Soysal et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2020.06.009); [Bobos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2019.10.183); [Benton et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0270132)). ([Working Environment Research Center](https://nfa.elsevierpure.com/en/publications/thresholds-of-handgrip-strength-for-all-cause-cancer-and-cardiova/))

Chair-rise tests have a strong practical case as well. The Five Times Sit-to-Stand Test has high reliability in systematic-review and meta-analytic data, and the 1-minute sit-to-stand has supportive evidence for validity, test-retest reliability, and responsiveness. More recently, a 2026 systematic review included 155 studies overall, with 94 cohort studies in the meta-analysis, and concluded that higher handgrip strength and better chair-stand performance were associated with lower risk of multiple long-term health conditions across adult populations. That is especially relevant here because these tests require very little equipment. ([Muñoz-Bermejo et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3390/biology10060510); [Bohannon and Crouch, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1097/HCR.0000000000000336); [Marín-Jiménez et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-109173)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34207604/))

Stair tests are reasonable, but they are a step behind the three metrics above in standardization. Cohort evidence suggests more habitual stair climbing is associated with lower all-cause mortality, and stair-climb power tests can show excellent reliability in some settings. Still, the strongest and cleanest function literature I found was for gait speed, grip strength, and chair-stand measures rather than generic stair tests. ([Sanchez-Lastra et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.12679); [Ni et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ptj/pzx039)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33543604/))

## Reproducibility and measurement error

Reproducibility matters as much as sophistication. A metric that moves around because of device error, protocol drift, or context changes is much less useful than a simpler metric measured the same way every time. This is one reason simple field metrics often outperform more variable consumer estimates. ([Hopkins et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200131030-00005); [Mehdipour et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1093/ageing/afae055); [Bobos et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2019.10.183); [Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Doherty et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02077-2)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11286357/))

Wearables illustrate the problem well. A 2020 systematic review of commercial devices found reasonably good step-count accuracy in lab settings and more variable heart-rate accuracy, but no brand was accurate for energy expenditure. That review was funded by a public Canada Research Chair rather than a device company, but one author became employed by Garmin during the publication process, so that detail is worth flagging. A 2024 living umbrella review, which reported public Irish research funding and no conflicts of interest, reached a similar broad conclusion: heart rate is often acceptable, step counts are imperfect but usable, and wearable-estimated VO₂ max can substantially overestimate aerobic capacity. ([Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Doherty et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02077-2)). ([JMIR mHealth and uHealth](https://mhealth.jmir.org/2020/9/e18694/))

The practical lesson is to standardize the process before interpreting change. Use the same device, the same route, the same test instructions, and similar timing and conditions. That matters more than adding a more complicated metric. ([Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Fuller et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.2196/18694); [Mehdipour et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1093/ageing/afae055)). ([University of Birmingham](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/validity-reliability-and-sensitivity-of-measures-of-sporting-perf))

## A practical tracking hierarchy

For most adults, a strong default dashboard is:

- daily steps
    
- weekly total activity or exercise minutes
    
- strength sessions per week
    
- one simple performance or function check matched to the goal ([Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Tarp et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amepre.2024.07.022); [Momma et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-105061); [Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2468266725001641))
    

For endurance-oriented people, useful add-ons are:

- a standardized field-performance test such as a repeatable time trial
    
- pace or power at a fixed heart rate under consistent conditions
    
- optional periodic VO₂ max or threshold testing for calibration ([Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Shushan et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2024.04.006); [Shushan et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00564-w); [Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Gaskill et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200111000-00007)). ([University of Birmingham](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/validity-reliability-and-sensitivity-of-measures-of-sporting-perf))
    

For healthy aging, rehabilitation, or function-first goals, useful add-ons are:

- gait speed
    
- handgrip strength
    
- five-times sit-to-stand or 1-minute sit-to-stand
    
- stair-climb performance only if stairs are the most practical option available ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [López-Bueno et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778); [Muñoz-Bermejo et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3390/biology10060510); [Bohannon and Crouch, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1097/HCR.0000000000000336); [Marín-Jiménez et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-109173); [Ni et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ptj/pzx039)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))
    

## Quick reference

- Strongest single physiology marker for prognosis: cardiorespiratory fitness, usually expressed as VO₂ max or peak VO₂. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S209525462400142X))
    
- Strong default behavior metrics: steps, weekly movement volume, and strength sessions. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Tarp et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amepre.2024.07.022); [Momma et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-105061)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2468266725001641))
    
- Strong training-response metrics: repeatable field-performance tests. ([Hopkins et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200131030-00005); [Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Laursen et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1249/mss.0b013e31806010f5)). ([University of Birmingham](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/validity-reliability-and-sensitivity-of-measures-of-sporting-perf))
    
- Strong function metrics: gait speed, grip strength, and chair-stand performance. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [López-Bueno et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778); [Muñoz-Bermejo et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3390/biology10060510); [Bohannon and Crouch, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1097/HCR.0000000000000336); [Marín-Jiménez et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-109173)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))
    
- Strong adherence metric: something visible every day, especially steps or completed sessions. ([Ferguson et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2589-7500\(22\)00111-X)). ([The Lancet](https://www.thelancet.com/pdfs/journals/landig/PIIS2589-7500%2822%2900111-X.pdf))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If someone only tracks three things, steps, weekly activity, and strength sessions are a strong starting set. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667\(25\)00164-1); [Tarp et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amepre.2024.07.022); [Momma et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-105061)). ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2468266725001641))
    
- If someone trains seriously, add one repeatable performance test before adding more physiology metrics. ([Hopkins et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200131030-00005); [Currell and Jeukendrup, 2008](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200838040-00003); [Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986)). ([University of Birmingham](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/validity-reliability-and-sensitivity-of-measures-of-sporting-perf))
    
- If someone cares most about aging well, add gait speed, grip strength, or sit-to-stand. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2010.1923); [López-Bueno et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778); [Marín-Jiménez et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2024-109173)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))
    
- Lab VO₂ max and threshold testing are useful when access and goals justify them, but wearable VO₂ max should not be treated as a gold standard. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2024.100986); [Gaskill et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1097/00005768-200111000-00007); [Doherty et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02077-2)). ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02077-2))
