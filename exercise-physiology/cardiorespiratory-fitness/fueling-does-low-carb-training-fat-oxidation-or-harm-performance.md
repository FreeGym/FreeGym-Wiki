# Fueling: Does Low-Carb Training Improve Fat Oxidation or Harm Performance?

Low carbohydrate availability can mean several different things: selectively starting some sessions with low glycogen, restricting carbohydrate during recovery, or following a chronic low-carbohydrate/high-fat diet. Those approaches overlap metabolically, but they are not the same intervention, and they should not be judged as if they were interchangeable. The real question is not whether low carbohydrate availability changes metabolism; it does. The harder question is whether those changes improve performance more than they compromise training quality. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Gawelczyk et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41829910/); [Cao et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8400555/))

## What the debate is and is not

“Train low, compete high” is not the same as “low-carb always.” Periodized carbohydrate restriction usually means exposing selected sessions or recovery windows to lower carbohydrate availability while keeping carbohydrate available for key work and competition. Chronic ketogenic or low-carbohydrate/high-fat diets ask a different question: whether athletes can adapt so thoroughly to fat-based fueling that performance is preserved or improved across training and racing. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Gawelczyk et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41829910/))

The literature also measures different endpoints. Some studies look at signaling markers after exercise, some at substrate use during submaximal work, and some at real performance outcomes like time trials or race results. Those are related, but they are not interchangeable. A strategy can raise fat oxidation or alter gene expression without making an athlete faster. ([Diaz-Lara et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39352665/); [Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/))

## What low carbohydrate availability clearly does

Low carbohydrate availability does appear to amplify some molecular responses associated with fuel handling and training adaptation, but the effect is selective rather than universal. In a 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 19 randomized studies, low-carbohydrate conditions increased post-exercise PDK4, GLUT4, and UCP3 mRNA, but did not significantly increase PGC-1α or TFAM overall. PGC-1α rose mainly in subgroups where the glycogen difference between conditions was very large. In other words, “train low” can strengthen some signals, but the canonical mitochondrial-biogenesis story is less consistent than the popular narrative suggests. ([Diaz-Lara et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39352665/))

Low-carbohydrate and ketogenic interventions also very reliably increase fat oxidation. A 2026 systematic review/meta-analysis in trained athletes reported that all 30 aerobic-focused studies it identified as measuring fat oxidation showed increases under low-carbohydrate or ketogenic conditions, often with marked shifts away from carbohydrate use. A 2021 meta-analysis similarly found lower respiratory exchange ratio, consistent with greater fat use, but no significant overall improvement in VO₂max, time to exhaustion, maximal heart rate, or perceived exertion. ([Gawelczyk et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41829910/); [Cao et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8400555/))

## Why the performance story is different from the signaling story

The strongest performance-level synthesis does not show that periodized carbohydrate restriction outperforms normal/high carbohydrate availability. In a 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis of trained endurance athletes, periodized carbohydrate restriction showed no overall endurance-performance advantage versus control training with normal or high carbohydrate availability. The same review noted that restricting carbohydrate can compromise training intensity and may therefore be detrimental in endurance sports that include decisive high-intensity periods. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/))

The clearest evidence for harm comes from elite race-walking studies. In 2017, Burke and colleagues showed that a low-carbohydrate/high-fat diet greatly increased fat oxidation, but also increased the oxygen cost of race-pace walking. The high-carbohydrate and periodized-carbohydrate groups improved 10 km performance by 6.6% and 5.3%, while the low-carbohydrate/high-fat group did not improve. In a later 2021 elite study, just 5–6 days of low-carbohydrate/high-fat feeding increased exercise fat oxidation and raised oxygen cost by about 5–8% at race-related speeds; all high-carbohydrate athletes improved, whereas 6 of 7 low-carbohydrate/high-fat athletes were slower. Acute carbohydrate restoration did not fully normalize substrate use at race speeds. These studies are important because they measured athletes near the intensities where exercise economy and rapid carbohydrate flux matter most. ([Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Burke et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7891450/))

A 2026 meta-analysis suggests an important nuance, but it should be interpreted cautiously. Across broader trained-athlete samples, low-carbohydrate and ketogenic diets often preserved VO₂max, but performance responses remained heterogeneous. Very short interventions were more likely to coincide with impaired exercise economy, while longer adaptations more often showed maintained aerobic-capacity measures; however, that pattern should not be read as proof that adaptation beyond one week reliably rescues sport-specific performance. Fat oxidation rose in both acute and chronic settings. So it is possible for aerobic capacity to look “maintained” on lab tests while sport-specific performance still suffers in events where economy, surges, and high rates of carbohydrate oxidation are decisive. ([Gawelczyk et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41829910/); [Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Burke et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7891450/))

## Periodized carbohydrate availability: plausible, sometimes helpful, not clearly superior overall

Some of the short-term evidence for a periodized approach comes from “sleep-low” studies rather than chronic low-carb diets. In a one-week trial of trained cyclists, Marquet and colleagues used the same daily carbohydrate intake in both groups but distributed it differently so that one group completed selected training with lower carbohydrate availability. The sleep-low group improved 20 km time-trial performance by 3.2% versus control, without significant changes in the metabolic markers they measured. That makes periodization more plausible than “low-carb always,” because it attempts to preserve carbohydrate for the sessions where quality matters most. ([Marquet et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5188410/))

But the broader literature does not justify saying that periodized carbohydrate restriction is proven optimal. The overall meta-analysis in trained athletes still found no general performance advantage over normal/high carbohydrate availability. A later five-week trial in 17 well-trained cyclists likewise found no superiority of a periodized carbohydrate diet over an energy-matched high-carbohydrate diet for maximal lactate steady state, time to exhaustion, substrate oxidation, or body composition. The most defensible reading is narrower: selective low-carbohydrate exposure may be useful in some contexts, but it is a tool to be placed carefully inside a program, not a universally superior default. That is an inference from mixed performance data, not a settled meta-analytic conclusion. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Marquet et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5188410/); [Prieto-Bellver et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38276556/))

## Elite vs recreational athletes

Athlete level matters. In elite endurance competition, small losses in economy can outweigh large gains in fat oxidation, because races are often decided at intensities where oxygen cost and rapid carbohydrate-supported work rate are critical. That is why the Burke race-walker studies carry so much weight: they tested real elite athletes in event-specific contexts, not just recreational trainees in laboratory protocols. ([Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Burke et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7891450/))

In recreational athletes, the evidence is more mixed and usually much smaller. A 2019 crossover study in seven competitive recreational runners found much higher fat oxidation on a low-carbohydrate/high-fat diet and no clear difference in later 5 km time trials after adaptation, although the initial time trial was slower under the low-carbohydrate condition. That study is worth noting, but it was very small. It also deserves extra caution because, although the authors declared no conflict and the study was internally funded by Grove City College, one coauthor was listed as founder of [The Noakes Foundation](https://thenoakesfoundation.org/). ([Prins et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6873122/))

More recently, an eight-week 2026 trial in recreationally active males compared continuous low-carbohydrate/high-fat, continuous high-carbohydrate, and a periodized carbohydrate approach. The low-carbohydrate and periodized groups increased fat oxidation early on, and carbohydrate reintroduction in the periodized group restored carbohydrate oxidation patterns. But there were no clear between-group performance differences. The authors themselves cautioned that their findings were mainly metabolic and physiological, not proof of better real-world competition performance. ([Kripp et al., 2026](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12851979/))

## For general health, the stronger evidence is for being active

For general health rather than race optimization, the evidence base is much stronger for sustained physical activity than for choosing a specific carbohydrate ideology. A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis of 85 prospective studies found that people who were consistently or increasingly active had about 20–40% lower all-cause mortality and about 30–40% lower cardiovascular mortality than those who remained inactive. Benefits were seen even below formal guideline levels. ([Yu et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12418563/))

Outside sports performance, low-carbohydrate and low-fat patterns both can work, and the differences are mostly trade-offs rather than a clean winner. In a 2020 meta-analysis of 38 randomized trials, low-carbohydrate diets produced slightly greater short-term weight loss and better HDL/triglyceride changes, while low-fat diets were better for LDL and total cholesterol. In a 2022 meta-analysis of 33 randomized trials in overweight/obese adults, low-carbohydrate diets again had modest advantages for some markers over 6–23 months, but by 24 months there was no clear difference in weight loss or metabolic improvement overall. These are not athlete-performance trials, but for general health they suggest that sustainability and adherence matter more than identifying with a “low-carb” or “high-carb” label. ([Chawla et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33317019/); [Lei et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36017224/))

## Quick reference findings

- Low glycogen or low-carbohydrate availability reliably increases fat oxidation and some metabolic gene responses, but the evidence for stronger mitochondrial-biogenesis signaling is selective rather than universal. ([Diaz-Lara et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39352665/); [Gawelczyk et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41829910/))
    
- Periodized carbohydrate restriction has not shown a clear overall endurance-performance advantage over normal/high carbohydrate availability in trained athletes. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/))
    
- Chronic or short low-carbohydrate/high-fat exposure can markedly increase fat oxidation yet still worsen exercise economy and race outcomes in elite athletes. ([Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Burke et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7891450/))
    
- Small trained-athlete “sleep-low” studies suggest that periodized carbohydrate timing may help in some settings, but that is not the same as proving “train low” is broadly superior. ([Marquet et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5188410/); [Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Prieto-Bellver et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38276556/))
    
- For general health, regular physical activity has much stronger outcome evidence than any claim that one endurance-fueling ideology is uniquely best. ([Yu et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12418563/); [Chawla et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33317019/); [Lei et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36017224/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the goal is maximizing race performance, especially in events with sustained high intensity or repeated surges, the best-supported default is still high carbohydrate availability for competition and key quality sessions. That conclusion is strongest in elite data. ([Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Burke et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7891450/))
    
- Selective low-carbohydrate sessions may be reasonable during lower-intensity phases or on carefully chosen days, but they should be viewed as an optional programming tool, not as a proven superior philosophy. That is an inference from mixed evidence, not a settled fact. ([Marquet et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5188410/); [Gejl and Nybo, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8127206/); [Prieto-Bellver et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38276556/))
    
- “Low-carb always” is the hardest strategy to defend for elite endurance performance. In recreational athletes, it may be workable for some individuals, but higher fat oxidation should not be confused with better performance. ([Burke et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407976/); [Kripp et al., 2026](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12851979/); [Prins et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6873122/))
    
- For general health, the most robust advice is to stay consistently active and choose a sustainable diet pattern rather than chase a macronutrient identity. ([Yu et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12418563/); [Chawla et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33317019/); [Lei et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36017224/))
