# Sex Differences: Should VO₂ Max Be Interpreted Differently?

Women often show lower VO₂ max values than men, especially when VO₂ max is expressed in absolute terms or per kilogram of body mass. The best-supported answer is that VO₂ max should usually be interpreted differently by sex for reference standards and risk thresholds, while physiological comparisons should be more nuanced than a simple male-female body-mass comparison. Much of the average gap is explained by oxygen-delivery factors such as hemoglobin, cardiac size/output, and body composition, and lean-mass scaling often narrows the gap substantially. But lean-mass scaling does not always remove the difference completely. ([Santisteban et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9105160/); [Kaminsky et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34809986/); [Skattebo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40974561/))

## What the difference does and does not mean

VO₂ max is determined mainly by maximal oxygen delivery and oxygen extraction. Reviews of sex differences in endurance physiology consistently point to smaller average heart and lung size, lower hemoglobin mass or concentration, and a higher fat mass fraction in women as major reasons body-mass–normalized VO₂ max is lower on average. That does not automatically mean women have inferior skeletal-muscle aerobic machinery. ([Santisteban et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9105160/))

## Why women often show lower VO₂ max values

The strongest current mechanistic evidence suggests that the main difference is upstream oxygen delivery. In highly trained cyclists and triathletes, maximal cardiac output was lower in women, but that difference disappeared after normalizing to lean body mass. Even then, women had about 10 percent lower hemoglobin concentration and arterial oxygen content, which led to 11 to 14 percent lower lean-mass–normalized oxygen delivery and lower lean-mass–normalized VO₂ max, despite similar leg oxygen extraction and similar mitochondrial and capillary density. That pattern supports claim 1 in a qualified way: the sex gap is explained largely by hemoglobin, heart size/output, and body composition, but not necessarily entirely by body composition alone. ([Skattebo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40974561/))

## What happens when VO₂ max is scaled to lean mass

Lean-mass scaling clearly reduces the male-female gap. In amateur endurance athletes, absolute and body-mass–adjusted VO₂ max were higher in men, but lower-limb lean-mass–adjusted VO₂ max and lower-limb lean-mass–adjusted submaximal values at ventilatory thresholds were not significantly different between sexes. In another athletic cohort, hemoglobin mass tracked lean mass more closely than body mass, and the male-female difference in hemoglobin mass shrank from about 20 percent to about 7.5 percent after lean-mass normalization. ([Martins et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10218102/); [Goodrich et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32881623/))

But claim 2 is still too strong if stated as “normalize by lean mass and the gap disappears.” The answer depends on what is being normalized—lower-limb lean mass, total lean mass, or fat-free mass—and on the population being studied. In some cohorts the gap nearly vanishes; in others a residual difference remains because oxygen-carrying capacity is still lower after lean-mass adjustment. ([Martins et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10218102/); [Skattebo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40974561/))

## Normative data and why sex-specific references matter

Large CPET reference datasets already separate men and women. The updated FRIEND standards used 22,379 tests in apparently healthy adults aged 20 to 89 years and generated sex-stratified, decade-specific percentiles for treadmill and cycle testing. Median values were higher in men across all age groups and declined with age in both sexes. In the original FRIEND treadmill dataset, the 50th percentile peak VO₂ was 48.0 mL/kg/min for men and 37.6 for women at ages 20 to 29, falling to 24.4 and 18.3 respectively at ages 70 to 79. ([Kaminsky et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34809986/); [Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26455884/))

This is not just a presentation choice. In healthy 50-year-old Swedish adults, commonly used reference values underestimated women’s peak VO₂ more than men’s. In cycle ergometry, a sex-specific prediction equation reduced women’s estimation error from −14.7 percent with a generic modified ACSM equation to −1.7 percent with the women-specific equation. That directly supports claim 4, although the better-supported wording is “sex-unspecific or poorly female-calibrated norms misclassify women” rather than simply “male norms” in every case. ([Genberg et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27312352/); [de Souza e Silva and Araújo, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4633002/))

## Risk thresholds

The same issue appears in prognosis. In a post hoc analysis of the international METS study before major non-cardiac surgery, women had lower mean peak VO₂ than men, but the optimal threshold for postoperative complications was 12.4 mL/kg/min in women and 22.3 mL/kg/min in men. The sex-specific model predicted complications better than the commonly used non-sex-specific 14 mL/kg/min threshold. In other words, a single cutoff would have overstated risk in many women and understated risk in many men. ([Alfitian et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10556582/))

## Training response and programming

The evidence for claim 3 is mixed, and much weaker than the evidence for sex-specific interpretation of norms. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of HIIT and sprint interval training found similar VO₂ max improvements in women and men, with no significant sex differences in the primary analyses. A separate meta-analysis of concurrent strength and endurance training also found no significant sex difference in VO₂ max adaptation. These data do not support a general rule that women require fundamentally different VO₂ max programming, and they do not directly resolve the broader question of optimal intensity distribution. ([Lock et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37676620/); [Huiberts et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10933151/))

At the same time, an earlier meta-analysis restricted to healthy untrained adults found larger VO₂ max gains in men given the same endurance-training dose. So the most defensible conclusion is not that sex never matters for programming, but that current evidence does not justify a blanket claim that women need fundamentally different VO₂ max programming. Training status, study duration, and the specific outcome being measured likely matter a great deal. ([Diaz-Canestro and Montero, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31494865/); [Lock et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37676620/))

## Hemoglobin, iron status, and intervention data

Because oxygen-carrying capacity matters, iron status is an important part of interpretation rather than a side issue. In athletic cohorts, iron deficiency is more common in women and is associated with lower lean-mass–normalized hemoglobin mass. ([Goodrich et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32881623/))

Intervention evidence also points in the same direction. A systematic review/meta-analysis in women of reproductive age found that daily oral iron supplementation improved relative VO₂ max by 2.35 mL/kg/min and absolute VO₂ max by 0.11 L/min on average, although only 3 of 24 studies were at overall low risk of bias. A more recent systematic review in female athletes found that iron deficiency impaired endurance performance and that maximal aerobic capacity can improve after supplementation in iron-deficient athletes, but many of the included studies were small. This supports considering iron status as one possible contributor when interpreting unexpectedly low VO₂ max in women, especially athletes, but it does not by itself establish a screening recommendation or support overconfident conclusions from a thin literature. ([Pasricha et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24717371/); [Pengelly et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39536912/))

## Limits and open debates

The strongest conclusion here is about interpretation, not about one perfect normalization method. Sex-specific norms and thresholds are well supported. By contrast, the best physiologic scaling variable—body mass, fat-free mass, lower-limb lean mass, or allometric scaling—depends on the question being asked. Evidence for sex-specific training programming is much thinner and less consistent than evidence for sex-specific normative interpretation, and women remain underrepresented in parts of the performance literature, especially at the highest competitive levels. ([Kaminsky et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34809986/); [Santisteban et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9105160/); [Lock et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37676620/))

## Practical interpretation

- Claim 1 is mostly supported: average sex differences in VO₂ max are explained largely by hemoglobin, cardiac size/output, and body composition. ([Santisteban et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9105160/); [Skattebo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40974561/))
    
- Claim 2 is partly supported: lean-mass scaling often reduces the gap substantially, but does not always eliminate it. ([Martins et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10218102/); [Skattebo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40974561/))
    
- Claim 3 is not consistently supported: current meta-analyses do not justify a universal rule that women need fundamentally different VO₂ max programming, and they do not directly resolve whether women need a different intensity distribution. ([Lock et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37676620/); [Huiberts et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10933151/); [Diaz-Canestro and Montero, 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31494865/))
    
- Claim 4 is strongly supported in practice: sex-specific norms, prediction equations, and risk thresholds reduce misclassification. ([Kaminsky et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34809986/); [Genberg et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27312352/); [de Souza e Silva and Araújo, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4633002/); [Alfitian et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10556582/))
