# Does Body Weight “Penalize” VO₂ Max?

VO₂ max is often expressed as mL/kg/min, but that expression is not neutral. Dividing by total body mass can make heavier people look less fit and lighter people look more fit, because the relationship between oxygen uptake and body size is usually not directly proportional. That said, this does not make relative VO₂ useless. Absolute VO₂peak is the raw oxygen uptake achieved on the test, body-composition and allometric methods are often better for size-independent comparisons, and mL/kg/min can still carry useful functional and prognostic information in some settings. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Lolli et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/); [Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))

## What the common expressions mean

Absolute VO₂peak (L/min or mL/min) is the unscaled oxygen uptake reached at peak exercise. Relative VO₂peak (mL/kg/min) divides that value by total body mass. Body-composition scaling uses fat-free mass or lean mass instead of total mass, and allometric scaling uses body size raised to an exponent rather than dividing by body mass linearly. These alternatives exist because simple per-kilogram scaling assumes proportionality that often does not hold. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))

## Why mL/kg/min can disadvantage heavier people

In large scaling papers, the authors note that conventional mL/kg/min can penalize larger subjects and favor lighter subjects. A study of healthy obese and nonobese adults illustrates why: absolute VO₂peak was essentially the same in the two groups (2.39 vs 2.35 L/min), but the obese group looked much worse when VO₂peak was expressed per kilogram (22 vs 34 mL/kg/min). In a Fontan cohort, ratio-scaling to body mass also failed to create a size-independent expression; heavier patients’ scores were systematically suppressed and lighter patients’ scores inflated. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Lorenzo et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3318951/); [Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))

## Absolute VO₂peak is physiologically cleaner as the raw numerator, but not automatically the best comparison

Physiologically, the unscaled numerator is the measured peak oxygen uptake itself; in Fick terms, peak VO₂ reflects cardiac output multiplied by arteriovenous oxygen difference. That is why absolute VO₂peak is often the cleaner way to describe raw oxygen transport/use capacity. Supporting that, [Goran et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10918530/) found that after accounting for fat-free mass, fat mass did not independently influence VO₂max, and [Lorenzo et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3318951/) found similar absolute VO₂peak in obese and nonobese adults. But absolute VO₂peak is not size-independent either. In [Eriksson et al.’s](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/) very large cohort using estimated VO₂max from a submaximal cycle test, absolute L/min was a weaker predictor of CVD and all-cause mortality than conventional mL/kg/min. And in bariatric CPET work, some exercise-tolerance or recovery indices can improve even while unadjusted peak VO₂ falls, which shows that absolute VO₂ does not capture the whole functional picture by itself. ([Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/); [Goran et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10918530/); [Lorenzo et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3318951/); [Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Remigio et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30112600/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/))

## Does allometric scaling solve the problem?

Allometric scaling is meant to reduce size artifacts by using exponents smaller than 1 instead of a simple per-kilogram ratio. In a systematic review/meta-analysis of 36 studies and 6514 participants, the pooled exponent was 0.70 for total body mass and 0.90 for fat-free mass. The body-mass exponent also differed substantially by sex: 0.52 in females and 0.76 in males. That is a strong argument against treating kg^0.75, or simple per-kg scaling, as a universal answer. A newer FRIEND-registry modeling study likewise favored exponents around 2/3 and found that body mass plus body-fat percentage predicted VO₂max better than fat-free mass alone. Still, this newer paper is a modeling study, not an outcome study, so it sharpens the method question more than it settles the prognosis question. ([Lolli et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/); [Nevill et al., 2026](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12983784/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/))

## Which expression predicts outcomes best?

There is no single winner across all settings. In [Eriksson et al.’s](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/) cohort of 316,116 adults, all scaling models were associated with lower CVD risk and all-cause mortality, and the differences between conventional ratio scaling and most allometric models were small overall; absolute L/min and a height-only model were weaker, and adding waist circumference sometimes improved associations. In apparently healthy adults, however, VO₂peak normalized to fat-free mass predicted all-cause mortality more strongly than VO₂peak normalized to total body weight. In chronic heart failure, lean-tissue-adjusted peak VO₂ outperformed body-weight adjustment in some cohorts, especially in obese patients and milder disease, although another heart-failure study found only a slight advantage of body-mass adjustment over unadjusted peak VO₂. In a recent Fontan follow-up study, body-mass-ratio scaling was not significantly associated with Fontan failure, whereas fat-free-mass and lean-mass scaling were. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Imboden et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31985577/); [Osman et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11127451/); [Cicoira et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15470653/); [Ingle et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3627270/); [Wadey et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39673345/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))

## Weight loss can inflate relative VO₂ max without improving maximal oxygen transport/use

This is where the “penalty” issue becomes especially obvious. A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis of bariatric surgery found that surgery reduced absolute VO₂max/peak in the short term while increasing VO₂max/peak relative to body weight. A prospective CPET cohort similarly found that six months after surgery, VO₂peak relative to body weight rose because body weight fell, while absolute VO₂peak and lean-mass-adjusted VO₂peak did not improve. An older pediatric weight-loss study reported the same pattern: mL/kg/min rose, but L/min did not. One of the cleaner randomized pieces of evidence here is the CUT-IT trial: aerobic interval training increased VO₂peak scaled to fat-free-mass^0.67, whereas a low-energy diet producing 10.6% weight loss did not. By contrast, exercise training after bariatric surgery improves both absolute and relative VO₂max/peak, which is what you would expect when the numerator truly rises as well. ([Ibacache-Saavedra et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/); [Zhou et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9021623/); [Sothern et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11094863/); [Pedersen et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25082954/); [Carretero-Ruiz et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33860904/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/))

## Limits and open debates

Most of the scaling literature is observational, and some of the most methodologically informative studies come from specific clinical populations such as Fontan physiology or heart failure. Some cited papers report directly measured VO₂peak/VO₂max, whereas the largest prognosis paper used estimated VO₂max from a submaximal cycle test rather than directly measured maximal CPET. So the field has not identified one universal denominator that is best for every population, every intervention, and every outcome. The strongest conclusion is narrower: simple per-kilogram scaling is often imperfect, but the replacement has to match the question being asked. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/); [Nevill et al., 2026](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12983784/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))

## Quick reference points

- Systematic review/meta-analysis: pooled allometric exponent 0.70 for total body mass and 0.90 for fat-free mass. ([Lolli et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/))
    
- Large adult cohort: all models predicted lower CVD/all-cause mortality with higher fitness, but absolute L/min was weaker than mL/kg/min and overall differences between ratio and allometric models were small. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))
    
- Obese vs nonobese adults: absolute VO₂peak was similar (2.39 vs 2.35 L/min), but mL/kg/min was much lower in the obese group (22 vs 34). ([Lorenzo et al., 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3318951/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21940772/))
    
- Bariatric surgery meta-analysis: absolute VO₂max/peak fell short term (ES −0.539), while relative VO₂max/peak rose (ES 0.658). ([Ibacache-Saavedra et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/))
    
- Exercise after bariatric surgery meta-analysis: both absolute (ES 0.317) and relative (ES 0.673) VO₂max/peak improved with training. ([Carretero-Ruiz et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33860904/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33860904/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the question is “how much oxygen can this person transport and use at peak?”, report absolute VO₂peak and, if possible, a body-composition-aware or allometric expression. ([Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/); [Goran et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10918530/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/))
    
- If the question is “how hard is it to move this body mass during a task?”, mL/kg/min can still be informative. That is partly an inference from the repeated finding that weight loss can improve relative VO₂ and some exercise-tolerance markers even when absolute VO₂ does not improve. ([Remigio et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30112600/); [Zhou et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9021623/); [Sothern et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11094863/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30112600/))
    
- If you are comparing people with very different adiposity, do not rely on mL/kg/min alone. Fat-free mass, lean mass, or allometric scaling usually reduce that problem. ([Lolli et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/); [Wadey et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9798799/); [Nevill et al., 2026](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12983784/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/))
    
- In weight-loss studies, reporting only mL/kg/min is incomplete. At minimum, report absolute VO₂peak too; ideally add fat-free-mass or allometrically scaled values. ([Ibacache-Saavedra et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/); [Pedersen et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25082954/); [Zhou et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9021623/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34927337/))
    

## Bottom line

Body weight can indeed “penalize” VO₂ max when fitness is expressed as mL/kg/min, especially when extra weight is adipose tissue. But that does not mean mL/kg/min is meaningless. It captures something real for some functional and prognostic questions. The better conclusion is that no single expression is universally best: absolute VO₂peak is cleaner for raw physiology, fat-free-mass and allometric expressions are usually fairer for cross-body-size comparisons, and conventional mL/kg/min can still matter when total body mass is part of the task or outcome. ([Eriksson et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/); [Imboden et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31985577/); [Lolli et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28058696/); [Pedersen et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25082954/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7849897/))
