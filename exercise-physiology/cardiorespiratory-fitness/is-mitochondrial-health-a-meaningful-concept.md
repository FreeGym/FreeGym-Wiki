# Is “Mitochondrial Health” a Meaningful Concept?

“Mitochondrial health” is **most scientifically meaningful** when it is translated into a specific measurement in a specific tissue. In the exercise literature, that usually means skeletal-muscle measures such as mitochondrial volume density, respiratory capacity, phosphocreatine recovery, or structural features like cristae density. As a broad wellness phrase, though, it is weak: different assays capture different biological properties, and they do not collapse neatly into one master variable. ([Sharma et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40389059/); [Mancilla et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37340318/); [Nielsen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27696420/))

## What the term can and cannot mean

Recent methods work makes the core problem clear: mitochondrial assessment is heterogeneous across assays, tissues, and endpoints, and a combination of techniques is often needed to understand biological and clinical relevance. In a human skeletal-muscle validation study, citrate synthase activity and Complex V protein content tracked respiratory capacity better than many other measures, while mtDNA copy number was not significantly associated with ADP-stimulated respiration. The same study found no perfect concordance among markers. So “mitochondrial health” is **most scientifically useful** if the speaker names the marker. ([Sharma et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40389059/); [Mancilla et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37340318/))

## Why mitochondria still matter

That vagueness should not be confused with irrelevance. A 2025 systematic review/meta-regression found that exercise training substantially increases skeletal-muscle mitochondrial content, and a **small** 2025 endurance-training study in 14 healthy older and young adults found that gains in VO2max correlated with both maximal cardiac output and citrate synthase activity. That is the physiologically coherent middle ground: mitochondria matter for endurance, but they matter alongside oxygen delivery, vascular adaptation, and cardiac function rather than acting alone. The 2025 meta-regression reported no funding and no relevant conflicts. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Faricier et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40173327/))

There is also outcome-linked human evidence beyond **ex vivo** lab proxies. In the Baltimore Longitudinal Study of Aging, lower baseline muscle oxidative capacity predicted greater decline across multiple mobility measures over an average of 2.5 years in initially well-functioning older adults. The authors reported no conflict of interest. That supports translational relevance, even if it still does not justify treating “mitochondrial health” as a single all-purpose explanation. ([Tian et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35048491/))

## Why claims about metabolic health need restraint

The picture is more complicated in metabolic disease. A 2020 review concluded that mitochondrial alterations commonly accompany insulin resistance, but it remains unresolved whether they are a central cause or a consequence of insulin resistance. A newer 2025 meta-analysis in type 2 diabetes found that exercise improved oxidative capacity, antioxidant capacity, and MFN2, while overall effects on mitochondrial content were smaller and not statistically significant. The authors of the 2020 review reported no conflicts, and the 2025 meta-analysis reported no commercial or financial conflicts. So mitochondria clearly participate in metabolic health, but they are not yet a complete stand-alone explanation for it. ([Genders et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32971810/); [Zhu et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41195385/))

## Are mitochondrial gains unique to zone 2?

Current higher-level evidence does not support that claim. In the 2025 systematic review/meta-regression, mitochondrial content increased to a similar extent after traditional endurance training, high-intensity training, and sprint interval training once relevant covariates were considered. A separate 2025 meta-analysis of randomized trials **in healthy individuals** found that endurance exercise increased PGC-1α, though with substantial heterogeneity, but interval and continuous training did not differ significantly. A 2026 systematic review focused on moderate-intensity continuous training found that MICT can raise mitochondrial volume density and VO2max, with a **modest increase in citrate synthase and MFN2, no clear pooled change in TFAM, DRP1, or PGC-1α, and very low to low certainty of evidence**. The MICT review authors reported no competing interests. Zone 2 can work, but the evidence does not make it uniquely mitochondrial. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Abrego-Guandique et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40459444/); [Vabishchevich et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41481647/))

## Intervention studies point the same way

In a within-subject trial that matched HIIT and MICT for total work and session duration, interval training produced larger gains in citrate synthase activity and mitochondrial respiration over 2 weeks, yet single-leg VO2peak did not change. The authors declared no competing interests. Conversely, a 2024 concurrent resistance-plus-interval program increased PGC-1α and citrate synthase without improving aerobic capacity; those authors also reported no conflicts. These results argue against the idea that mitochondrial markers belong uniquely to zone 2. ([MacInnis et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407978/); [Sterczala et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38490811/))

## Even the zone 2 label is operationally messy

A 2025 laboratory study found substantial intra- and interindividual variability in common zone-2 markers, with coefficients of variation from 6% to 29%. VT1 and maximal fat oxidation aligned relatively well, but fixed percentages of HRmax and some lactate-style rules diverged widely from actual metabolic responses. The authors declared no conflicts of interest. So even before asking whether zone 2 is special, it is worth asking whether two people are even using the same physiological definition of zone 2. ([Meixner et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40225831/))

## The real endpoint problem

The strongest objection to loose mitochondrial language is not that mitochondria are unimportant. It is that cellular proxies are not the endpoint people actually care about. Mitochondrial markers can improve without parallel short-term changes in VO2peak or aerobic capacity, while clinically meaningful outcomes such as mobility decline do relate to mitochondrial function in some cohorts. That makes mitochondrial measures best viewed as mechanistic markers, not as automatic proof of better health or performance. ([Tian et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35048491/); [MacInnis et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407978/); [Sterczala et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38490811/))

That conclusion is strengthened by the fact that “mitochondrial quality” is multidimensional. A 2017 human study showed that cristae density, not just mitochondrial volume, was positively associated with oxygen uptake and mitochondrial content. So even when the concept is meaningful, it still is not one thing. Quantity, structure, respiration, coupling, and in vivo recovery kinetics are related but non-identical dimensions. ([Nielsen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27696420/); [Sharma et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40389059/))

## Quick reference

- Meaningful in research: yes, if it refers to a defined feature such as mitochondrial volume density, respiratory capacity, phosphocreatine recovery, or cristae structure in a stated tissue. ([Mancilla et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37340318/); [Nielsen et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27696420/))
    
- Weak as a blanket slogan: also yes, because different biomarkers reflect different aspects of biology and are not interchangeable. ([Sharma et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40389059/); [Mancilla et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37340318/))
    
- Unique to zone 2: no strong evidence. Continuous endurance, HIIT, sprint intervals, and some concurrent programs can all improve mitochondrial markers. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Abrego-Guandique et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40459444/); [MacInnis et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407978/); [Sterczala et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38490811/))
    
- Best endpoint hierarchy: clinical and performance outcomes first; mitochondrial biomarkers second, as mechanistic support. ([Tian et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35048491/); [MacInnis et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407978/); [Sterczala et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38490811/))
    

## Practical interpretation

A careful sentence is not “this improves mitochondrial health.” A better sentence is “this intervention increased skeletal-muscle citrate synthase activity,” or “this program improved phosphocreatine recovery and walking performance,” or “this training block increased mitochondrial content similarly to other endurance modalities.” That wording makes the tissue, assay, and real-world endpoint explicit. ([Mancilla et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37340318/); [Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Tian et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35048491/))

## Bottom line

“Mitochondrial health” is a meaningful concept only after it is unpacked. In research, it can name real and important biology. In consumer health language, it is often too vague to stand on its own. The current evidence supports mitochondria as one important determinant of endurance and aspects of metabolic and functional health, but not as a single master explanation, not as a unique justification for zone 2, and not as a substitute for clinical outcomes. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Genders et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32971810/); [Tian et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35048491/); [Meixner et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40225831/))
