# Exercise vs Medication

Fitness can lower some risk factors, but whether it can replace statins, antihypertensives, or GLP-1 drugs depends on the outcome being measured and the baseline risk of the person taking them. Direct head-to-head trials are rare, so much of the comparative literature relies on indirect comparison and network meta-analysis rather than true substitution trials. That matters, because "exercise works" is not the same claim as "exercise can replace a drug with outcome-trial evidence." ([PubMed - BP](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"), [Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02115-z "The Effect of Exercise Training on Blood Lipids: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2828487 "Aerobic Exercise and Weight Loss in Adults: A Systematic Review and Dose-Response Meta-Analysis"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [PubMed - SELECT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24473061/ "Comparative effectiveness of exercise and drug interventions on mortality outcomes: metaepidemiological study"))

## What substitution means

A true substitute would need to deliver a similar effect on the relevant endpoint in a comparable population. Lowering systolic blood pressure by several mmHg is not the same as lowering LDL cholesterol by about 1 mmol/L, and neither is the same as producing double-digit percentage body-weight loss or reducing cardiovascular events in people with established disease. The most useful comparisons are therefore condition-specific: exercise versus antihypertensives for blood pressure, exercise versus statins for LDL-related cardiovascular risk, and exercise versus GLP-1 drugs for obesity-related weight loss and downstream events. ([PubMed - BP](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [PubMed - STEP 1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33567185/ "Once-Weekly Semaglutide in Adults with Overweight or Obesity"), [PubMed - SELECT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"))

## Blood pressure is the strongest case for exercise as a partial substitute

In the best available network meta-analysis of 391 randomized trials, antihypertensive medications lowered systolic blood pressure more than structured exercise when all populations were pooled, with a mean difference of 3.96 mmHg in favor of medication. But in participants who were actually hypertensive, exercise reduced systolic blood pressure by 8.96 mmHg versus control, and there was no detectable difference between exercise and medication overall (difference 0.18 mmHg, 95% credible interval -1.35 to 1.68). Among the categories in this article, this is the clearest setting where exercise can approach first-line drug blood-pressure effects on an indirect comparison. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"))

The limitation is that this comparison is indirect. The same review found no randomized controlled trials that directly compared structured exercise against antihypertensive drugs, and the exercise literature was smaller and included fewer hypertensive participants than the medication literature. So the result is encouraging, but it is not proof that exercise and drugs are interchangeable in routine care. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"))

Medications also have much stronger hard-outcome evidence. In a large-scale individual-participant meta-analysis, every 5 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure from pharmacologic treatment reduced major cardiovascular events by about 10 percent. Exercise can lower blood pressure meaningfully, but the direct evidence base showing that exercise-alone substitution produces comparable event reduction in high-risk patients is much thinner than the drug outcome evidence. ([PubMed - BP lowering](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33933205/ "Pharmacological blood pressure lowering for primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease across different levels of blood pressure: an individual participant-level data meta-analysis"), [PubMed - BP exercise](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"))

Combination therapy looks better than either strategy framed in isolation. A 2021 systematic meta-review found strong evidence that exercise alone, medication alone, and the combination all reduce blood pressure; the combined effect was less than additive, but it still bolstered the antihypertensive effect of medication. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34192008/ "Do the combined blood pressure effects of exercise and antihypertensive medications add up to the sum of their parts? A systematic meta-review"))

## Statins and exercise are not interchangeable when LDL lowering is the goal

Exercise improves the lipid profile, but the average LDL effect is modest. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized exercise trials found LDL cholesterol fell by 7.22 mg/dL, triglycerides by 8.01 mg/dL, total cholesterol by 5.90 mg/dL, and HDL rose by 2.11 mg/dL. However, the paper's prediction intervals suggested these average lipid effects were not highly consistent across comparable studies. That review reported no financial support and no conflicts of interest. ([Springer Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02115-z "The Effect of Exercise Training on Blood Lipids: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis"))

By contrast, in the Cholesterol Treatment Trialists meta-analysis of 27 randomized statin trials, the mean LDL difference in statin-versus-control trials was 1.08 mmol/L, roughly 42 mg/dL. Each 1.0 mmol/L LDL reduction was associated with a 21 percent relative reduction in major vascular events, and among people with less than 10 percent 5-year risk the absolute reduction in major vascular events was about 11 per 1000 over 5 years. A separate 2022 systematic review of 21 large statin trials estimated absolute risk reductions of 0.8 percent for all-cause mortality, 1.3 percent for myocardial infarction, and 0.4 percent for stroke, while also noting substantial heterogeneity. ([PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [PubMed - Byrne et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35285850/ "Evaluating the Association Between Low-Density Lipoprotein Cholesterol Reduction and Relative and Absolute Effects of Statin Treatment: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis"))

This is why "exercise beats statins" is usually the wrong framing. An indirect metaepidemiological comparison found no statistically detectable difference between exercise and drug interventions for mortality in secondary prevention of coronary heart disease, but the exercise evidence across all included conditions came from only 57 trials and 14,716 participants, much smaller than the drug evidence, and it was not a statin-specific replacement trial. That is not strong enough evidence on its own to conclude that exercise can generally substitute for statins in people with established atherosclerotic risk. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24473061/ "Comparative effectiveness of exercise and drug interventions on mortality outcomes: metaepidemiological study"))

The best evidence on joint use points in the opposite direction. In a prospective cohort of 10,043 veterans with dyslipidaemia followed for a median of 10 years, mortality was 18.5 percent in statin users and 27.7 percent in nonusers, and higher fitness was associated with lower mortality in both groups. Among statin users, highly fit participants had a hazard ratio of 0.30 versus the least fit; among nonusers, the highest-fitness group had a hazard ratio of 0.53. The authors concluded that the combination of statin therapy and higher fitness was associated with lower mortality than either alone. This was observational, not randomized, but it reinforces the additive case. Funding was reported as none. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23199849/ "Interactive effects of fitness and statin treatment on mortality risk in veterans with dyslipidaemia: a cohort study"))

## GLP-1 drugs are much harder for exercise to substitute for when the target is large weight loss

Exercise alone does produce weight loss, but usually not at GLP-1 magnitude. A 2024 dose-response meta-analysis of 116 randomized trials in adults with overweight or obesity found that body weight, waist circumference, and body fat fell as aerobic exercise duration increased up to 300 minutes per week. At 300 minutes per week, body weight fell by about 4.19 kg and waist circumference by about 4.21 cm with moderate intensity or 5.34 cm with moderate-to-vigorous intensity; at least 150 minutes per week was associated with clinically important reductions in waist circumference and body fat. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2828487 "Aerobic Exercise and Weight Loss in Adults: A Systematic Review and Dose-Response Meta-Analysis"))

In STEP 1, semaglutide 2.4 mg once weekly plus lifestyle intervention reduced body weight by 14.9 percent at 68 weeks versus 2.4 percent with placebo. This trial was funded by Novo Nordisk, the manufacturer of semaglutide. The finding is clinically important, but it should be interpreted with that in mind. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33567185/ "Once-Weekly Semaglutide in Adults with Overweight or Obesity"))

The hard-outcome evidence also differs. In SELECT, a primary cardiovascular endpoint occurred in 6.5 percent of semaglutide-treated participants and 8.0 percent of placebo-treated participants, an absolute difference of 1.5 percentage points and a hazard ratio of 0.80. This trial was also funded by Novo Nordisk, so it should likewise be interpreted with awareness of sponsor involvement. Even with that caveat, this is randomized cardiovascular-outcomes evidence for semaglutide in people with overweight or obesity and established cardiovascular disease. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"))

There are also reasons not to reduce the comparison to weight on the scale alone. A 2024 systematic review concluded that semaglutide-associated weight loss appears to come primarily from fat mass, but notable lean-mass losses remain a concern in some trials. That is one reason the comparison should not be reduced to scale weight alone. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38629387/ "A systematic review of the effect of semaglutide on lean mass"))

## The combination case is strongest here too

A one-year randomized trial after initial diet-induced weight loss compared exercise, liraglutide, both together, and placebo. The combination group lost 9.5 kg more than placebo, versus 4.1 kg for exercise and 6.8 kg for liraglutide, and only the combination improved glycated hemoglobin, insulin sensitivity, and cardiorespiratory fitness. This trial was funded by the Novo Nordisk Foundation and others, so that funding context should be kept in mind when interpreting the result. Even so, it supports the broader point that exercise can add clinically meaningful benefits that GLP-1-class therapy alone does not fully replace. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33951361/ "Healthy Weight Loss Maintenance with Exercise, Liraglutide, or Both Combined"))

## Where lifestyle-first is most defensible

The case for emphasizing lifestyle first is strongest when baseline absolute risk is low and the main goal is risk-factor improvement rather than immediate event prevention. Blood pressure is the clearest example: in people with hypertension, structured exercise can produce drug-like systolic blood pressure reductions on indirect comparison. Statin decisions also become more preference-sensitive as baseline risk falls, because absolute benefit is smaller. In contrast, once baseline risk is high, or a person already has established cardiovascular disease, the argument for replacement gets weaker and the argument for combination gets stronger. That is an inference from the size and quality of the comparative evidence, not a substitute for individual medical judgment. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [PubMed - SELECT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"))

## Practical interpretation

- Claim 1, that fitness is more powerful than most medications for many outcomes, is too broad. It is most defensible for blood pressure in hypertensive populations, much less defensible for LDL lowering versus statins, and not supported if the comparison is large weight loss or semaglutide's cardiovascular-outcomes data. ([PubMed - BP](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2828487 "Aerobic Exercise and Weight Loss in Adults: A Systematic Review and Dose-Response Meta-Analysis"), [PubMed - STEP 1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33567185/ "Once-Weekly Semaglutide in Adults with Overweight or Obesity"), [PubMed - SELECT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"))
    
- Claim 2, that exercise and medication address different problems and that substitution is a higher-stakes decision in high-risk settings, is substantially correct. The drugs in these categories have direct randomized outcome evidence that exercise-as-replacement usually lacks. ([PubMed - BP lowering](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33933205/ "Pharmacological blood pressure lowering for primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease across different levels of blood pressure: an individual participant-level data meta-analysis"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"), [PubMed - SELECT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952131/ "Semaglutide and Cardiovascular Outcomes in Obesity without Diabetes"))
    
- Claim 3, that some low-risk people may reasonably emphasize lifestyle first while high-risk people are less suitable candidates for outright replacement, is the most evidence-consistent summary. The clearest support comes from the blood-pressure literature and from the smaller absolute benefits seen with statins at lower baseline risk. ([PubMed - BP](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30563873/ "How does exercise treatment compare with antihypertensive medications? A network meta-analysis of 391 randomised controlled trials assessing exercise and medication effects on systolic blood pressure"), [PubMed - CTT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22607822/ "The effects of lowering LDL cholesterol with statin therapy in people at low risk of vascular disease: meta-analysis of individual data from 27 randomised trials"))
    
- Claim 4, that the best strategy is often combination, is the strongest overall conclusion. It is supported by the blood-pressure meta-review, the statin-plus-fitness cohort, and the exercise-plus-liraglutide randomized trial. ([PubMed - meta-review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34192008/ "Do the combined blood pressure effects of exercise and antihypertensive medications add up to the sum of their parts? A systematic meta-review"), [PubMed - veterans cohort](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23199849/ "Interactive effects of fitness and statin treatment on mortality risk in veterans with dyslipidaemia: a cohort study"), [PubMed - liraglutide trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33951361/ "Healthy Weight Loss Maintenance with Exercise, Liraglutide, or Both Combined"))
