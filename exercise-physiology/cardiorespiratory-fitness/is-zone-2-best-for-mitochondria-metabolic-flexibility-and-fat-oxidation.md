# Is Zone 2 Best for "Mitochondria," Metabolic Flexibility, and Fat Oxidation?

Zone 2 can be a very good aerobic training tool, but the current literature does not show that it is uniquely effective for mitochondrial adaptations or uniquely superior for "fat burning." Moderate continuous exercise clearly improves mitochondrial markers and capillarization, and in adults with overweight or obesity both moderate-intensity continuous training and higher-intensity interval training can improve maximal fat oxidation. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2); [Vabishchevich et al., 2026](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0339902); [Yin et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X23000424)) In the newer human syntheses, no single "magic" zone emerges, and in the 2025 meta-regression training frequency and other training variables emerged as useful predictors of adaptation rather than any single zone. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2))

## What Zone 2 usually means

The term is not standardized. In a recent expert viewpoint, zone 2 was placed immediately below the first lactate or ventilatory threshold, and the authors explicitly noted that the expected central and peripheral adaptations are probably not unique to that intensity and may also be induced at slightly higher or lower intensities. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))

## Mitochondria and capillaries are not unique to Zone 2

A 2025 systematic review and meta-regression of human training studies found similar adjusted increases in mitochondrial content after continuous endurance training, high-intensity training, and sprint interval training: about 23% for endurance training, 27% for high-intensity training, and 27% for sprint interval training. Capillaries per fiber also increased similarly across intensity categories. Low- or moderate-intensity continuous training clearly works, but it did not emerge as uniquely mitochondrial in that synthesis. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2))

That same review found an important nuance: capillary density per mm² increased after endurance training and high-intensity training, with larger increases after endurance training than after high-intensity training or sprint interval training. So the evidence is not that all intensities are identical; it is that different aerobic intensities can produce overlapping mitochondrial and capillary adaptations, with some protocol-specific differences. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2))

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis focused specifically on moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT) found that MICT significantly increased mitochondrial volume density and VO₂max, with MFN2 expression also increasing and citrate synthase activity showing a borderline pooled effect. Evidence certainty was low, but the main point is straightforward: moderate continuous work is sufficient to improve several mitochondrial-related markers. ([Vabishchevich et al., 2026](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0339902))

Not all mitochondrial endpoints move identically. In a 2025 randomized human study in 20 healthy young men, both HIIT and MICT increased VO₂max, citrate synthase, complex I activity, mitochondrial content, and mitochondrial volume density, but HIIT showed a more pronounced signal for mitochondrial dynamics-related responses. That argues against "Zone 2 is special," but it also argues against "intensity never matters." ([Li et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2025.1554222/full))

## What matched-comparison trials suggest

Single-leg cycling studies help explain why claims of uniqueness are shaky. In one matched-total-work comparison, interval cycling produced superior mitochondrial adaptations relative to continuous cycling despite equal total work. In another study that matched absolute intensity and total work, short-term continuous training increased biomarkers of mitochondrial content whereas intermittent training did not. The practical takeaway is not that intervals always win or that continuous training always wins, but that mitochondrial responses are highly protocol-sensitive and depend on how "matched" training is defined. ([MacInnis et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5407978/); [Skelly et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36779702/))

## Fat oxidation during exercise is not unique to Zone 2

A 2023 meta-analysis in adults with overweight or obesity found that both HIIT and MICT increased maximal fat oxidation (MFO) versus no training, and direct HIIT-versus-MICT comparisons showed no meaningful difference in MFO change. Within the MICT studies, prescriptions around 65 to 70% of VO₂peak tended to perform well, but that does not make Zone 2 unique; it means moderate work is one effective way to improve fat oxidation. ([Yin et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X23000424))

## FATmax and "max fat burning" are not the same as better health

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis of FATmax training in people with overweight or obesity found reductions in body weight, BMI, body fat percentage, fat mass, waist circumference, fasting glucose, insulin, and HOMA-IR, along with improved cardiorespiratory fitness. That supports FATmax as a legitimate training prescription. But the same review rated certainty mostly very low to moderate and emphasized heterogeneity and the need for standardized protocols. In other words, FATmax can work, but the evidence does not show that chasing a lab-defined "maximum fat burning" point is a universally superior health strategy. ([Jiang et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41732883/))

There is also a conceptual problem with treating high exercise fat oxidation as a health endpoint by itself. In a 2021 study, exercise fat oxidation was positively associated with body fatness in men with obesity, directly challenging the idea that higher fat oxidation during exercise automatically reflects better metabolic health. ([Chávez-Guevara et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8297250/))

## Metabolic flexibility is a context-dependent marker

In this literature, "metabolic flexibility" usually refers to how readily substrate use shifts between fat and carbohydrate as conditions change. It is biologically plausible and sometimes clinically informative, but it is not a universal scoreboard for health. ([Prior et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23983100/); [Fernández-Verdejo et al., 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-79092-w))

In obese older adults, a lower ability to shift from fat toward carbohydrate oxidation during submaximal exercise was associated with impaired glucose tolerance. That suggests exercise metabolic inflexibility can matter in insulin-resistant populations. ([Prior et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23983100/))

But in a 2024 study of individuals without obesity, metabolic flexibility to lipid during exercise was not associated with metabolic health outcomes. So "more flexibility" on an exercise test does not consistently map onto better health across populations. ([Fernández-Verdejo et al., 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-79092-w))

## What likely matters most in practice

Taken together, the literature better supports the practical claims than the claim that Zone 2 is uniquely mitochondrial. Many intensities improve mitochondria. Zone 2 is not uniquely mitochondrial; a more defensible interpretation is that it is a practical, repeatable way to accumulate aerobic work. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))

That aligns with the 2025 meta-regression, which found larger mitochondrial and VO₂max gains with higher training frequencies and identified training variables as useful predictors of adaptation. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2))

That does not make Zone 2 overrated. It makes it useful. If someone can do more total aerobic work consistently at a moderate intensity than at harder intensities, that is a strong practical reason to emphasize it. But the evidence does not justify saying it is biologically special in a way other effective aerobic training is not. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303); [Yin et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X23000424))

## Quick reference points

- Zone 2 is usually placed just below the first lactate or ventilatory threshold, but the term is not standardized. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))
    
- Moderate continuous training improves mitochondrial volume density and VO₂max, with MFN2 also increasing and citrate synthase showing a borderline pooled effect. ([Vabishchevich et al., 2026](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0339902))
    
- Across large human datasets, mitochondrial content increased similarly after endurance training, high-intensity training, and sprint interval training after adjustment for key covariates. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2))
    
- Both HIIT and MICT improve maximal fat oxidation in adults with overweight or obesity, and direct comparisons do not show a clear difference. ([Yin et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X23000424))
    
- FATmax training can improve body composition and some cardiometabolic markers versus controls, but this is not proof that "max fat burning" is the best universal target. ([Jiang et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41732883/))
    
- Flexibility and fat-oxidation markers have mixed clinical meaning: they relate to glucose intolerance in some high-risk groups, but not to metabolic health outcomes in people without obesity. ([Prior et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23983100/); [Fernández-Verdejo et al., 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-79092-w); [Chávez-Guevara et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8297250/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Zone 2 is a good tool for building aerobic volume, not a uniquely mitochondrial zone. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))
    
- If the goal is better mitochondrial adaptation, capillarization, or fat oxidation, several aerobic prescriptions can work; total training done and how often it can be repeated likely matter a lot in practice, and training frequency was an important predictor in the 2025 meta-regression. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2); [Vabishchevich et al., 2026](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0339902); [Yin et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X23000424))
    
- If the goal is health, treating FATmax or a single "flexibility" number as the endpoint is too simplistic. ([Jiang et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41732883/); [Prior et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23983100/); [Fernández-Verdejo et al., 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-79092-w); [Chávez-Guevara et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8297250/))
