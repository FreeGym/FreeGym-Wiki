# VO₂ Max and Metabolic Health

Exercise improves glycemic control, but the tie to VO₂ max is real without being absolute. Higher cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF) is associated with lower future type 2 diabetes risk, exercise training improves clamp-measured insulin sensitivity, and combined training often produces the broadest improvement profile. But weight-controlled and mediation studies suggest that recent exercise, abdominal fat loss, and muscle-related adaptations explain a meaningful share of the benefit, so a rise in VO₂ max is better treated as one informative marker rather than the whole mechanism. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6560020/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12933-025-03048-1)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5147957/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4587640/))

## What VO₂ max does and does not capture

In this literature, VO₂ max or VO₂ peak is a common marker of cardiorespiratory fitness and aerobic capacity. It captures endurance-related adaptation well, but it does not summarize all metabolically relevant adaptations, because resistance training can improve glycemic control and strength even when VO₂ peak does not change much. In the recent meta-analysis of 100 randomized trials in type 2 diabetes, VO₂ peak improved after continuous aerobic training, combined training, and HIIT, but not after resistance training; despite that, resistance training still lowered HbA1c on average. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12933-025-03048-1))

## Diabetes risk and the case for fitness beyond body weight

A 2019 systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis of prospective cohorts found that each 1-MET higher cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with an 8% lower risk of incident type 2 diabetes in models adjusted for adiposity or body size. Muscular strength was also inversely associated with diabetes risk, with a 13% lower risk per standard deviation higher strength. That is strong associative evidence that fitness matters beyond body weight, even though observational studies cannot by themselves prove that raising VO₂ max is the causal pathway. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6560020/))

## Clamp studies: exercise can improve insulin sensitivity without major weight loss

Mechanistic trials support an exercise effect that is not reducible to fat loss alone. A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of 25 randomized trials using the hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp found that exercise training improved insulin-stimulated glucose disposal versus control (SMD 0.52). The effect was smaller but still present in trials without significant weight loss (SMD 0.32) and larger in trials with significant weight loss (SMD 0.89). The same review found no clear overall improvement in suppression of hepatic glucose production, suggesting that hepatic insulin sensitivity may depend more on weight loss than peripheral glucose disposal does. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/))

Older intervention studies point in the same direction. In previously sedentary adults, 6 months of walking training increased insulin sensitivity without changing BMI or waist circumference. So the claim that exercise helps glycemia only through fat loss is too strong. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12610001/))

## But VO₂ max is not the whole mechanism

The opposite overstatement is that the VO₂ max gain itself explains the metabolic improvement. In a carefully controlled energy-balance study in untrained women, 8 to 16 weeks of aerobic training did not significantly raise clamp-measured insulin sensitivity when testing was done in a no-exercise condition; insulin sensitivity rose only after a recent high-intensity interval session performed 22 hours before the clamp. That suggests some of the apparent “training effect” can reflect the acute after-effect of recent exercise and energy flux, not simply the chronic rise in VO₂ peak; it does not mean chronic training effects are absent. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30550514/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/))

A mediation analysis of a randomized trial in older adults came to a similar conclusion from a different angle. Exercise improved both CRF and insulin sensitivity, but change in abdominal adiposity, not change in CRF, mediated the insulin-sensitivity improvement. In practical terms, changes in VO₂ max can accompany improvement, but they are not a reliable stand-alone explanation for why insulin sensitivity improves. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5147957/))

## Resistance, aerobic, or combined training?

Resistance training is not metabolically minor. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials found that resistance training lowered HbA1c by 0.39%, and larger gains in muscular strength predicted larger HbA1c reductions. In a secondary analysis, resistance and aerobic training did not differ significantly for HbA1c. In type 2 diabetes with NAFLD, the RAED2 randomized trial found that aerobic and resistance training reduced hepatic fat and improved clamp-measured insulin sensitivity to a similar degree. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8915309/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23504926/))

But “equally effective” is not a universal summary. In the 2025 pairwise meta-analysis of 100 randomized trials, all exercise types lowered HbA1c, yet combined training produced the largest average reduction (−0.74%), followed by HIIT (−0.71%), continuous aerobic training (−0.62%), and resistance training (−0.36%). A separate 2025 network meta-analysis of 158 studies found a broadly similar pattern, but ranked HIIT highest: HIIT −0.61%, combined training −0.58%, aerobic training −0.58%, and resistance training −0.40%. Combined training also improved both VO₂ peak and muscle strength, whereas resistance training mainly improved strength and aerobic modalities mainly improved VO₂ peak. ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12933-025-03048-1)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39904457/))

For insulin-resistance endpoints, equivalence is also less consistent than for HbA1c. In abdominally obese older adults, aerobic and combined training improved clamp-based insulin resistance versus control, while resistance training did not, and combined training outperformed resistance training. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19171808/))

## Obesity, weight loss, and additive effects

In obesity, weight loss matters, but exercise adds more than calorie restriction alone. In older adults with obesity, a randomized trial found that weight loss plus exercise improved peripheral insulin sensitivity by 75% versus control, while weight loss alone improved it by 36% and did not significantly outperform control. The combined group also increased VO₂ peak and prevented losses of strength and lean mass. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34406407/))

That additive pattern also appears in matched-weight-loss work. In an RCT where participants lost a similar 6% to 8% of body weight through calorie restriction, exercise, or both, insulin sensitivity increased about twofold more in the calorie-restriction-plus-exercise group than in either calorie restriction alone or exercise alone. This argues against reducing the result to matched weight loss alone. Weight loss helps, but exercise contributed something extra. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25877812/))

## NAFLD trials: liver fat can improve even when weight change is small

NAFLD evidence also shows that exercise can improve liver-related outcomes even when weight change is small. A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis restricted to exercise interventions without significant weight loss or dietary changes found reduced intrahepatic lipid (SMD −0.76), ALT, AST, LDL, and triglycerides, with no significant change in fasting glucose, fasting insulin, or HbA1c. A 2023 meta-analysis of 11 randomized trials likewise found reductions in intrahepatic lipid and ALT without significant overall improvements in BMI or insulin resistance. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34579012/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36653265/))

But the “independent of weight loss” claim is not complete. In a randomized trial of 220 adults with NAFLD, both moderate and vigorous exercise reduced intrahepatic triglyceride content, yet adjustment for weight loss substantially attenuated those effects, and the authors concluded that the benefit appeared to be largely mediated by weight loss. So liver fat can fall with modest or no weight change, but larger improvements still often travel through fat loss. ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2530904))

Modality comparisons in NAFLD also look more balanced than a VO₂ max-only view would predict. In type 2 diabetes with NAFLD, aerobic and resistance training produced similar reductions in hepatic fat and similar improvements in insulin sensitivity. In NAFLD with dietary counseling in both groups, moderate-intensity aerobic and resistance training also produced similar reductions in liver fat and similar improvements in whole-body and muscle insulin sensitivity. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23504926/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33939383/))

## Practical interpretation

- Higher CRF clearly tracks lower diabetes risk, but the best evidence does not support “VO₂ max gain” as the single mechanism behind better metabolic health. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6560020/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5147957/))
    
- Exercise can improve insulin sensitivity even without major weight loss, but weight loss amplifies the effect, especially for hepatic insulin sensitivity and liver fat. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/)) ([JAMA Network](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2530904))
    
- Resistance training is a legitimate glucose-lowering modality, not an optional extra. But if the goal is the broadest metabolic package - glycemia, fitness, strength, body composition, and function - the best-supported default is a combined aerobic-plus-resistance approach. HIIT can also be highly effective for HbA1c when tolerated. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8915309/)) ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12933-025-03048-1)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39904457/))
