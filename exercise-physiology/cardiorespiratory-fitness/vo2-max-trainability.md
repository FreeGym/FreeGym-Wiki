# VO₂ Max Trainability: Mostly Genetics or Mostly Work?

VO₂ max trainability is not well described by either extreme claim. Baseline VO₂ max is strongly heritable ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26787216/)). Response to a fixed training program also shows a real familial/genetic signal in older twin/family studies ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6542620/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10484570/)). But newer responder-methodology papers show that many dramatic “high responder vs non-responder” stories are inflated by measurement error, day-to-day biological variation, and mismatched or under-dosed programming ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8606564/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39160296/); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5451738/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30673687/); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10199994/)). For performance, VO₂ max matters, but thresholds and economy often decide more once athletes already have high aerobic ceilings ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10647532/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17901124/)).

## What the question is and is not

There are really three different questions here. First, how much of a person’s **starting VO₂ max** is inherited? ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26787216/)) Second, how much of their **change in VO₂ max after a fixed training plan** is inherited? ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28097487/)) Third, how much does **VO₂ max itself** explain endurance performance compared with thresholds and economy? ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10647532/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17901124/)) These are related questions, but they are not interchangeable.

## Baseline VO₂ max is strongly heritable

Twin-sibling evidence suggests that baseline VO₂ max itself is strongly heritable. In a twin-sibling study plus meta-analysis of eight studies, heritability estimates were about 59% for absolute VO₂ max and 72% for body-mass-adjusted VO₂ max from childhood to early adulthood. Adult family data point in the same direction: the HERITAGE review reports a heritability estimate of 51% for baseline VO₂ max in adults after adjustment for major body-composition covariates. That means genetics clearly matter for where people start. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26787216/); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9012529/))

## Evidence that trainability also has a familial/genetic component under a fixed program

Classic twin work suggested that trainability itself is partly genotype-dependent. In a 20-week endurance-training study of 10 monozygotic twin pairs, maximal aerobic power rose by about 12% on average, but the gains ranged from roughly 0% to 41%, and co-twins tended to respond much more similarly than unrelated people; the intraclass correlation for the training-induced change in maximal aerobic power was 0.74. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6542620/))

The strongest longitudinal family evidence remains the HERITAGE Family Study. In 481 sedentary adults from 98 families who completed 20 weeks of standardized cycle training with repeated pre/post VO₂ max testing, the mean increase was about 400 mL/min, but responses ranged from little or no gain to more than 1.0 L/min. Between-family variance was 2.5 times within-family variance, and the maximal heritability estimate for the VO₂ max response was 47%. That is too large to dismiss as pure noise within that family-study design. However, because HERITAGE did not include a non-exercise comparator arm, it does not by itself isolate true individual-response variance from background within-person variability over the same time span. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10484570/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28097487/))

Later HERITAGE analyses also argue against the idea that response is explained mostly by obvious demographic factors. In that program, age, sex, ethnicity, and baseline VO₂ max together explained about 11% of response variance; achieved-versus-prescribed training differences explained about 6%; measurement error and day-to-day variation about 20%; and the model-based genetic/heritable component about 47% within the HERITAGE framework. In other words, there is a real familial/genetic signal, but the observed response is still a blend of biology, program execution, and noise. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9012529/))

At the gene-prediction level, however, the case is much weaker than the heritability headlines imply. A systematic review of 35 articles covering 15 cohorts identified 97 possible genes related to VO₂ max trainability, but only 13 variants had been reproduced by more than two authors, and the review emphasized small samples, limited replication, mostly Caucasian cohorts, and an immature evidence base. So the best current reading is that trainability is partly heritable, but not yet predictably “decodable” in a clinically useful way from a few named variants. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5688475/))

## Why the case for fixed “non-responders” is weaker than it looks

A major problem in this literature is that many studies call someone a “non-responder” without properly separating true person-specific response differences from measurement error and normal within-person variation. A 2021 systematic review found that only a small minority of exercise studies used statistical approaches capable of estimating true interindividual differences in trainability, and that zero-based thresholds inflated response rates. The authors argued that future work should account for error and a smallest worthwhile or clinically important change before classifying people. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8606564/))

That criticism became sharper in a 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis focused specifically on the **standard deviation of individual response** for VO₂ max, which is the statistic meant to isolate true person-specific response variability from random noise. Across 24 studies with non-exercise comparators, the authors concluded that most of the observed variation in change scores was due to measurement error, and that there was not strong evidence for a positive true individual-response variance after a single intervention. This does **not** mean training fails to raise VO₂ max on average in exercise-training studies. It means the evidence for large, stable, person-specific “this exact program works for her but not him” differences is weaker than older responder/non-responder narratives suggested. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39160296/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10484570/))

## Training dose, intensity, and individualization matter a lot

Some apparent non-response disappears when the training stimulus changes. In Montero and Lundby’s dose-escalation study, the prevalence of apparent cardiorespiratory-fitness non-response fell as weekly volume rose from one to five 60-minute sessions: 69%, 40%, 29%, 0%, and 0%, respectively. After a second 6-week block that added two more sessions per week for those initially classified as non-responders, non-response was eliminated. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5451738/))

Programming style matters too. In a 12-week randomized trial using verification testing to confirm true VO₂ max and a cohort-specific technical error threshold, individualized ventilatory-threshold-based prescription produced 100% responders versus 60% with standardized heart-rate-reserve prescription. That finding fits the broader idea that a meaningful part of “low response” is really “poorly targeted response.” ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30673687/))

Intensity also changes the picture. In a 2023 randomized trial, increasing training intensity while keeping total energy expenditure constant raised the VO₂ max responder rate from 53% to 87%, whereas simply continuing moderate training did not. In a separate 6-week work-matched trial, one-third of the moderate-intensity group were classified as non-responders, while no non-responders were seen in the HIIT group. These are not proof that everyone will respond to every high-intensity plan, but they do undermine the idea that one disappointing moderate program reveals a fixed biological ceiling. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10199994/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33811557/))

At the average group level, the newest broad synthesis I found points in the same direction. A 2026 meta-analysis of 115 randomized trials found that HIIT produced larger improvements than moderate-to-vigorous continuous training for relative and absolute VO₂ max, while thresholds, economy, and physical-performance outcomes were broadly similar on average. The comparison was moderated by age, sex, training status, interval type, and exercise mode, which is another sign that response depends heavily on what program is given to whom. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41804294/))

## Central versus peripheral adaptations

Mechanistically, short-to-moderate training blocks often raise VO₂ max mainly through **central** adaptations, especially maximal cardiac output. A 2015 systematic review/meta-analysis in healthy young adults found that endurance training increased VO₂ max and maximal cardiac output, but not maximal arteriovenous oxygen difference, and that the change in maximal cardiac output tracked the change in VO₂ max. A corresponding 2016 systematic review in middle-aged and older adults found a similar pattern across aging. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26553969/))

That said, the story is not “central only.” In Daussin et al.’s crossover trial, interval training increased both VO₂ max and maximal cardiac output, while both continuous and interval training improved oxygen extraction, suggesting that different programs can shift the balance of central and peripheral adaptation. After 4 weeks of sprint-interval training, Raleigh et al. found that some low/non-VO₂-max responders still showed increases in capillary density and oxidative enzyme markers, which suggests that peripheral adaptation can occur even when VO₂ max barely moves. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17661072/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29733694/))

More recent work keeps that mixed picture intact. A 2025 study in older and young adults linked VO₂ max gains to both cardiac changes and peripheral vascular/mitochondrial markers, while another 2025 randomized trial found the largest 6-week VO₂ max increases with HIIT and heavy-intensity training and attributed those gains predominantly to central adaptations. The best reading is that central delivery often dominates short-term VO₂ max gains, but peripheral adaptations still matter, especially for thresholds, economy, and sport-specific performance. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40173327/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40047443/))

## VO₂ max is important, but it is not the whole performance story

The claim that “VO₂ max is overrated” goes too far. Bassett and Howley argued that VO₂ max sets the upper ceiling for endurance performance because an athlete cannot sustain work above 100% of VO₂ max for long, but they also emphasized that running economy and fractional utilization of VO₂ max matter substantially. In their framing, speed at lactate threshold integrates all three and is the best physiological predictor of distance-running performance. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10647532/))

Joyner and Coyle made essentially the same point from the elite-performance side. They describe endurance performance as resting on three main factors: VO₂ max, lactate threshold, and efficiency/economy. Among athletes who already have similarly high VO₂ max values, differences in threshold and economy can explain why one competitor outperforms another. So VO₂ max is not overrated so much as **insufficient on its own**. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17901124/))

## Modality, age, sex, baseline fitness, and phenotype all modify response

Modality matters because VO₂ max is partly task-specific. Review data from triathletes show that runners usually record higher VO₂ max values on a treadmill, while cyclists can achieve cycle values similar to treadmill values; the same review also notes that muscles adapt specifically to the exercise task, so submaximal variables such as ventilatory threshold can improve without any change in VO₂ max. That means some “poor response” can simply reflect testing or training in the wrong mode. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19290675/))

Broad demographic variables do not seem to explain most of the variance in standardized VO₂ max response. HERITAGE found only limited influence of age, sex, ethnicity, and baseline VO₂ max on gains from a tightly controlled program. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9012529/)) A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis of HIIT studies found similar VO₂ max gains in men and women, with training status and intervention length explaining more heterogeneity than sex itself. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37676620/)) The newer 2026 meta-analysis likewise found that age, sex, training status, interval type, and exercise mode moderated the HIIT-versus-continuous comparison. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41804294/)) Some phenotype-level trial data go further: baseline fitness, arterial stiffness, and ventricular structure/function were associated with the size of the VO₂ max response in a 2021 work-matched trial. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33811557/))

## Quick reference values

- Baseline VO₂ max heritability in the twin-sibling meta-analysis: about 59% for absolute VO₂ max and 72% for body-mass-adjusted VO₂ max. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26787216/))
    
- HERITAGE maximal heritability estimate for the **training response** of VO₂ max: 47%. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10484570/))
    
- In HERITAGE, age, sex, ethnicity, and baseline VO₂ max explained about 11% of response variance. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9012529/))
    
- In the HERITAGE review’s variance summary, achieved-versus-prescribed training differences explained about 6% of response variance, while measurement error/day-to-day variation explained about 20%. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9012529/))
    
- In Weatherwax et al., individualized threshold-based prescription yielded 100% responders versus 60% with standardized heart-rate-reserve prescription. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30673687/))
    
- In Reuter et al., increasing intensity at constant energy expenditure raised the VO₂ max responder rate from 53% to 87%. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10199994/))
    
- In Renwick et al.’s 2024 meta-analysis, there was not strong evidence for a positive true individual-response variance for VO₂ max after a single intervention. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39160296/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the question is **why two people start at different VO₂ max levels**, genetics matter a lot. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26787216/))
    
- If the question is **whether training can raise VO₂ max**, the answer is also yes for most people, and the odds improve when dose, intensity, modality, and individualization are appropriate. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5451738/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30673687/); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10199994/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19290675/))
    
- If the question is **whether some people are permanently doomed to be VO₂ max non-responders**, the best current evidence says that conclusion is overstated. One disappointing intervention is not strong evidence of fixed non-trainability. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8606564/); [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39160296/))
    
- If the question is **what matters most for endurance performance**, VO₂ max is only part of the answer; threshold and economy often matter just as much, and sometimes more, once VO₂ max is already high. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10647532/))
