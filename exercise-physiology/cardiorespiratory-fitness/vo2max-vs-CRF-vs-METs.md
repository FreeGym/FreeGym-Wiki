# VO₂ Max vs CRF vs METs: Are We Even Talking About the Same Thing?

Cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF), VO₂max, and METs overlap, but they are not interchangeable terms. CRF is the underlying physiological construct. VO₂max or peak VO₂ measured during cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET) with gas exchange is its best laboratory measure. METs are a unit that can express either directly measured oxygen uptake or an estimate derived from workload, heart rate, speed/grade, or protocol time. In mortality research, these different measurements are often pooled under the same “fitness” label, which works reasonably well for population-level risk modeling but can hide meaningful differences in how fitness was actually assessed. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

## What these terms are and are not

The clearest recent framework comes from Singh et al. 2025. In that meta-analysis, **objectively measured CRF** meant measured VO₂max from maximal aerobic exercise testing using CPET. **Exercise-estimated CRF** meant estimated VO₂max from maximal or submaximal exercise tests using workload or heart-rate responses. **Non-exercise-estimated CRF** meant equation-based estimates derived from baseline variables such as age, sex, BMI, waist circumference, resting heart rate, smoking, and physical activity. That framework makes the hierarchy clearer: CRF is the broad construct, while VO₂max from CPET is one operationalization of it, and estimated METs are a more indirect one. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

A MET is not a separate physiological trait. It is a conventional unit. In this literature, 1 MET is typically treated as 3.5 mL O₂/kg/min. That convention is useful, but it is not literally true for everyone. A 2022 systematic review in adults aged 60 and older found that measured resting oxygen uptake was usually below 3.5 mL/kg/min, with an overall weighted average around 2.7 mL/kg/min. So even the MET itself contains an assumption that can matter in some populations. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34417980/))

Even “objective” lab fitness is not perfectly uniform across studies. CPET papers variously report peak VO₂, VO₂peak, or VO₂max, and a 2021 CPET-based meta-analysis in patients with cardiovascular disease grouped these under CPET-measured CRF. In healthy adults, a systematic review/meta-analysis of verification phases found that the mean absolute difference between the highest VO₂ reached in a standard CPET and in a verification bout was only 0.03 L/min, about 0.85%, which sits within common test variability. In other words, direct CPET is usually very close to a person’s highest attained value, but even there the exact label, confirmation criteria, and protocol still matter. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8724619/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/))

## Why the terms get mixed together

The mortality literature usually runs in METs. In Singh et al. 2025, the pooled relative risk per 1-MET higher CRF was 0.86 for all-cause mortality and 0.84 for cardiovascular mortality. The same paper found that exercise-estimated CRF was assessed in 6.5 times more cohorts and 234 times more participants than objectively measured CRF. So the field often uses a 1-MET gain in “fitness” as a common currency, even when the underlying measurement method is different. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

That is practical for epidemiology, but it can blur an important distinction. A treadmill-protocol MET estimate, a workload-derived estimated VO₂max, a CPET VO₂peak, and a directly measured VO₂max are related measures, not identical ones. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

## Is estimated treadmill fitness effectively a VO₂max proxy?

At the **group level**, often yes. In healthy adults, Evans et al. 2015 reviewed 19 studies and 43 submaximal exercise equations and found no significant difference between measured and predicted VO₂max for 28 equations, with correlations ranging from 0.57 to 0.92. In older adults, Smith et al. 2016 found nine studies and 13 equations, with correlations ranging from 0.4 to 0.9. A separate review of submaximal step tests found broad validity ranges as well, while noting that acceptable validity does not automatically mean strong reproducibility data for every protocol. That is the evidence base behind the claim that estimated CRF can be “good enough” for screening or large-scale risk stratification. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24721146/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26525524/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/))

At the **individual level**, no. Validation studies repeatedly show that a method can look acceptable on average while still producing substantial person-level error. In moderately trained young men, the ACSM submaximal treadmill running test overestimated measured VO₂max by about 3% on average and was not significantly different at the group mean, yet measured VO₂max was still underestimated in 30% of observations. In patients being evaluated for suspected myocardial ischemia, Bruce-estimated peak METs were higher than measured peak METs obtained with simultaneous gas analysis. In older adults, ACSM equations overestimated VO₂max whereas the Foster equation showed less bias. In athletes, the ACSM running equation used with Bruce testing overestimated directly measured VO₂max by 14.6%. In a 2023 study of healthy adults, ACSM-based estimates overestimated measured VO₂max by 7.3% to 8.1% in submaximal testing and by 20.9% when applied to maximal-test estimation. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22262016/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427601/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12544648/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3743617/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38133102/))

The best synthesis is that estimated treadmill METs are often adequate as a proxy for CRF in cohort-level risk models, but they are not interchangeable with gas-exchange VO₂max when the goal is precise individual measurement. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22262016/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427601/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38133102/))

## Why protocol and measurement method matter

Part of the disagreement is built into the equations themselves. The ACSM running equation was designed for steady-state exercise, yet it is often used in graded protocols such as Bruce, where speed and grade change and anaerobic contribution increases. That mismatch is a plausible reason protocol-based estimates often run high. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3743617/))

Direct CPET is not protocol-free either. A review of graded exercise test protocols concluded that ramp protocols show a higher correlation between VO₂ and workload than step protocols, which reduces error when predicting metabolic cost at individual workloads. The same review notes that test duration matters, with the familiar 8-12 minute target arising because VO₂max values tend to be lower when protocols are much shorter or longer than that range. In healthy volunteers with high exercise capacity, an individualized ramp protocol placed all participants in that target duration range, whereas only 14% landed there with Bruce; directly measured peak VO₂ was very similar between ramp and Bruce, though slightly lower with ramp. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5221270/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16617222/))

So the lab side is not perfectly monolithic either. But taken together, the evidence suggests that error is usually larger and less transparent when VO₂ is inferred from protocol time or workload than when it is measured directly from gas exchange. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427601/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38133102/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6960105/))

## Are mortality associations stronger when fitness is objectively measured?

The best current meta-analysis in general adult cohorts does **not** support that claim. Singh et al. 2025 found no statistically significant differences in the per-MET mortality association between objectively measured CRF and estimated CRF. For all-cause mortality, the pooled relative risk per 1-MET higher CRF was 0.90 for objectively measured CRF, 0.85 for maximal exercise-estimated CRF, and 0.94 for submaximal exercise-estimated CRF. For cardiovascular mortality, the corresponding values were 0.86, 0.86, and 0.91. That said, between-study heterogeneity was considerable (I² 97.66% for all-cause mortality and 92.50% for cardiovascular mortality), so similarity of pooled risk gradients should not be overread as proof of measurement interchangeability. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

That should not be misread as objective measurement being weak. Separate meta-analyses still show strong prognostic value. Laukkanen et al. 2022 pooled 37 cohort studies and found an all-cause mortality relative risk of 0.89 per 1-MET higher CRF, with strength of association not differing by CRF assessment method. In patients with established cardiovascular disease, Ezzatvar et al. 2021 found a hazard ratio of 0.81 for all-cause mortality per 1-MET higher CPET-measured CRF. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8724619/))

The real limitation is that the 2025 comparison is mostly **between cohorts**, not within the same participants. Singh et al. explicitly noted that no study both measured and estimated CRF within the same cohort in a way that allowed direct head-to-head prognostic comparison. So the current evidence supports similar **population-level risk gradients**, not true measurement interchangeability. Also, Singh’s meta-analysis was restricted to the **general adult population** and excluded participants with prevalent chronic conditions at baseline, so its comparison should not be generalized uncritically to all clinical populations. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

## Reliability and reproducibility

Well-conducted direct CPET is fairly reproducible. In healthy adults, the verification-phase meta-analysis found only about a 0.85% difference between standard CPET and verification values, within the commonly used 2-3% test-variability range. In young healthy men tested with total-capture indirect calorimetry, the mean within-subject coefficient of variation for VO₂max was 1.2%. In physically active women, CPET VO₂max showed an ICC of 0.984 and a coefficient of variation of 1.98%. In patient populations, repeatability is a bit lower but still generally good: Barron et al. reported peak VO₂ ICC 0.95, and Keteyian et al. reported a coefficient of variation of 5.9% in a multisite heart-failure trial. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30341979/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37754957/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24398370/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20522572/))

The reproducibility literature for estimated tests is thinner and more protocol-specific. In the systematic review of submaximal step tests, only two of 11 studies reported reliability measures. In cardiovascular rehabilitation, Reed et al. found that some submaximal methods may be acceptable for mean group change, but treadmill prediction showed proportional bias against CPET and the limits of agreement were wide enough to be problematic for individual-level change tracking. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6960105/))

## Limits and open debates

A great deal of the mortality literature is still expressed as estimated METs rather than gas-exchange VO₂max, because estimation is cheaper and easier to scale. The evidence supports that choice for population-level risk stratification, especially in general adult cohorts. But the same evidence does not erase the measurement problem. A treadmill protocol time, a workload-derived MET estimate, a CPET peak VO₂, and a verified VO₂max are related measures, not synonyms. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))

The most defensible synthesis is that the distinction is **not** merely academic, but its importance depends on the use case. It is small enough to fade in many large mortality meta-analyses and large enough to matter when the goal is accurate individual assessment, diagnosing exercise limitation, or tracking small changes over time. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6960105/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38133102/))

## Quick reference takeaways

- **CRF** is the construct; **VO₂max or peak VO₂ from CPET** is its best laboratory measure. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))
    
- **METs** are a unit, not a different physiological capacity. The usual 1-MET = 3.5 mL O₂/kg/min convention is useful but imperfect. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34417980/))
    
- **Estimated exercise CRF** is often good enough for large-scale risk stratification. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))
    
- **Estimated treadmill METs are not interchangeable with gas-exchange VO₂max at the individual level.** ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22262016/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21427601/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12544648/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3743617/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38133102/))
    
- **Current meta-analytic evidence in general adult cohorts does not show stronger mortality associations for objectively measured CRF than for exercise-estimated CRF.** ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))
    
- **True head-to-head prognostic comparisons within the same cohort are still missing.** ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))
    

## Practical interpretation

- For **epidemiology** and **population-scale risk stratification**, estimated exercise CRF expressed in METs is usually good enough, especially in general adult cohorts. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11809197/?term=%22J+Sport+Health+Sci%22%5Bjour%5D))
    
- For **precise individual assessment**, **clinical decision-making**, **mechanistic interpretation**, or **tracking modest changes over time**, CPET with gas exchange is preferable. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6960105/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30341979/), [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20522572/))
