# How Much VO₂ Max Is “Enough” for Healthspan?

VO₂ max is often asked as if it has one answer, but the literature is really addressing at least two different questions: what is enough to stay independent in daily life, and what level is associated with lower long-term disease risk. The best available evidence suggests that the familiar ~15-18 mL/kg/min figures are rough, cohort-specific low-end markers of independent living in older adults, not universal thresholds. Strictly, several of the key studies cited below used peak VO₂/VO₂peak rather than a directly confirmed VO₂max, which is one reason age- and sex-specific reference values and task-specific reserve are often more informative than one absolute cutoff. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/); [Paap & Takken, 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25418758/); [Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/); [Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Santana et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39920345/))

## Where the classic 15-18 mL/kg/min idea comes from

One commonly cited source is a study of 298 independently living adults aged 55-86. Paterson et al. reported that the minimum aerobic power “compatible with an independent life” at age 85 was approximately 18 mL/kg/min in men and 15 mL/kg/min in women in that cohort. Those figures are often repeated, but in the paper they are cohort-specific estimates rather than a universal frailty threshold. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/))

But the threshold story is not as clean as it is often presented. Morey et al. directly tested whether a threshold exists between peak VO₂ and self-reported physical functioning in adults aged 65-90. Peak VO₂ was the strongest predictor of physical function, but there was **not** a threshold effect. Their decision-tree analysis still identified 18.3 mL/kg/min as the best empirical cut point separating lower from higher function. In other words, 18-ish was a useful classifier in that cohort, not proof of a universal biological cliff. ([Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/))

Frailty trials landed in a similar range. In a randomized trial of exercise training in frail community-dwelling older adults, Binder et al. used VO₂peak between 10 and 18 mL/kg/min as one of the defining criteria for mild-to-moderate physical frailty, and exercise improved both VO₂peak and functional outcomes. That does not establish a universal cutoff either, but it shows that the 10-18 range is clinically relevant in frailty research. ([Binder et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12473001/))

## Why there is no single universal threshold

The case against one universal VO₂ number is strong. A systematic review of CPET reference values concluded that there is no single ideal set of reference values, because populations, equipment, and methods are too diverse to pool into one equation. That makes any one-size-fits-all threshold suspect from the start. ([Paap & Takken, 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25418758/))

Age and sex change the meaning of the number dramatically. In the FRIEND registry, the 50th percentile treadmill VO₂max fell from 48.0 to 24.4 mL/kg/min in men and from 37.6 to 18.3 in women between ages 20-29 and 70-79. So the same absolute value can be close to average in one group and clearly low in another. ([Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/))

Body composition matters too. A 2025 FRIEND-derived paper found that older reference equations were miscalibrated in obese individuals and that calibration improved when lean body mass and body fat were incorporated. That is another reason absolute mL/kg/min values can mislead when treated as universal targets. ([Santana et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39920345/))

## Daily living depends on reserve, not just a raw peak value

For everyday function, reserve matters at least as much as raw capacity. In adults aged 70-92, Arnett et al. measured oxygen uptake during a battery of daily tasks and found that low-, moderate-, and high-workload tasks used about 32%, 43%, and 50% of VO₂peak, respectively. They concluded that light housework and carrying groceries require roughly 40%-50% of peak oxygen consumption. ([Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/))

Standard activity tables can also misestimate how hard daily life is for older adults. Knaggs et al. found that measured MET values differed from normative values in 14 of 17 daily tasks in adults aged 70-90. In those with mobility impairment, the speed-adjusted metabolic cost of tasks was 16% to 27% higher. So two people with the same measured VO₂peak may not experience the same task difficulty if one has higher task costs, slower gait, or mobility limitation. ([Knaggs et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22091979/))

This helps explain why “enough” depends on what you want to do. A VO₂peak that covers basic ADLs may still leave little reserve for repeated stairs, carrying loads, or other more demanding tasks. That last point is an inference from the task-cost literature: the lower the reserve, the smaller the buffer when task demands rise. ([Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Knaggs et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22091979/))

## VO₂peak does relate to gait speed, stairs, and functional performance

In women aged 75 and older, Binder et al. found that VO₂peak was independently associated with total physical performance score, gait speed, chair-rise time, and stair-climb time. So the link between aerobic capacity and function is not just theoretical; it shows up in the tasks that often matter most for staying independent. ([Binder et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10462167/))

That said, these relationships are associations, not proof that one VO₂ number determines independence by itself. The more defensible reading is that low aerobic capacity narrows functional reserve, and when reserve gets small enough, ordinary tasks start consuming a large share of the person’s maximum. ([Binder et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10462167/); [Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/))

## Disease-risk targets sit higher than bare-independence thresholds

If the question shifts from bare independence to broader healthspan, the bar moves up. In observational meta-analyses of healthy men and women, Kodama et al. found that each 1-MET higher fitness level was associated with relative risks of 0.87 for all-cause mortality and 0.85 for CHD/CVD events. A newer 2025 meta-analysis of 42 studies and 3.8 million observations reported pooled relative risks of 0.86 for all-cause mortality and 0.84 for CVD mortality per 1-MET higher CRF. ([Kodama et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))

A 2024 overview of meta-analyses representing more than 20.9 million observations reached the same general conclusion and reported an 18% lower risk of incident heart failure per 1-MET higher CRF. The exact percentage varies by outcome and analysis, but the broad pattern is consistent: higher cardiorespiratory fitness is associated with lower risk across a wide range of outcomes. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))

In one contemporary Norwegian cohort of adults aged 70-77 with 5-year follow-up, mortality risk was lower in those classified as moderately fit than in those classified as unfit, with no additional risk reduction among those classified as more fit. The authors’ mortality-linked cut points were about 26.5 mL/kg/min for men and 22.2 for women, roughly 85% of the age- and sex-specific average in that cohort. Those numbers were derived from mortality associations in a specific cohort, not from ADL testing, but they do show that mortality-associated “enough” in that cohort sat well above the classic 15-18 mL/kg/min independence markers reported in older functional-aging studies. ([Tari et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39638222/); [Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/))

## So what is “enough”?

Taken together, the literature supports a layered answer rather than a magic number. Around 15-18 mL/kg/min looks like a rough lower boundary where independent living starts to become precarious in many older adults, based largely on cohort-specific functional-aging studies. But broader healthspan is not the same as basic independence. Once the goal includes lower cardiometabolic risk and more reserve above minimum daily demands, higher values look preferable. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/); [Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Kodama et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/); [Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/); [Tari et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39638222/))

A percentile-based framing is usually more useful than a universal absolute number. The same VO₂peak can be near average for an older woman, distinctly below average for an older man, and very low in midlife. So, as a practical inference, “stay out of the bottom end of the age- and sex-specific distribution, and keep enough reserve for the tasks you actually care about” is a better rule than “everyone needs X mL/kg/min.” ([Paap & Takken, 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25418758/); [Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/); [Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Santana et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39920345/))

## Quick reference values

- Classic “independent living at age 85” marker: about **18 mL/kg/min in men** and **15 mL/kg/min in women**. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/))
    
- Best empirical functional cut point in one older-adult cohort: **18.3 mL/kg/min**, but **no true threshold effect** was found. ([Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/))
    
- Frailty-trial inclusion range: **VO₂peak 10-18 mL/kg/min**. ([Binder et al., 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12473001/))
    
- Daily tasks in older adults often use about **40%-50% of VO₂peak**. ([Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/))
    
- FRIEND 50th percentile treadmill VO₂max at ages 70-79: **24.4 mL/kg/min in men** and **18.3 mL/kg/min in women**. ([Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/))
    
- Across meta-analyses, each **1-MET** higher CRF is associated with roughly **13%-16% lower all-cause/CVD mortality risk**, depending on the analysis and outcome. ([Kodama et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))
    
- In adults aged 70-77 in one Norwegian cohort, mortality-linked cut points were about **26.5 mL/kg/min in men** and **22.2 mL/kg/min in women**. ([Tari et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39638222/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If your measured VO₂peak is around **15-18 mL/kg/min**, that is better viewed as a **rough lower boundary for independence** than as a **broad healthspan target**. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/); [Kodama et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/); [Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))
    
- If basic ADLs are the main goal, ask whether they can be done with reserve. If repeated stairs, carrying loads, or other more demanding tasks matter, you probably want **clearly more than the minimum**. ([Arnett et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18287178/); [Knaggs et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22091979/))
    
- For interpretation, an **age/sex reference value or percent-predicted VO₂peak** is usually more meaningful than raw mL/kg/min alone. ([Paap & Takken, 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25418758/); [Kaminsky et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4919021/); [Santana et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39920345/))
    
- If CPET is unavailable, a **usual-paced 400 m walk** can help estimate fitness in ambulatory older adults, though it may underestimate higher-fit individuals; in SOMMA, each **0.05 m/s** faster walking speed was associated with **0.40 mL/kg/min** higher VO₂peak, and the study’s multivariable estimation equation correlated with measured VO₂peak at **r = 0.71**. ([Garcia et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38149438/))
    
- “Enough for independence” and “enough for broader healthspan” are **not the same number**. Associations with lower mortality risk generally extend well above the classic frailty/independence range. ([Paterson et al., 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10613433/); [Morey et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9710861/); [Kodama et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19454641/); [Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/); [Tari et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39638222/))
