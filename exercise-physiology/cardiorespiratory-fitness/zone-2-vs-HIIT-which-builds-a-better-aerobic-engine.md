# Zone 2 vs HIIT: Which Builds a Better Aerobic Engine?

The evidence does not support treating this as a clean either/or choice. If the outcome is maximal aerobic power, usually measured as VO₂max, HIIT generally has the edge. If the outcome includes submaximal steady-state performance, low-intensity work matters more. In trained endurance athletes, the best-supported answer is usually not all Zone 2 or all HIIT, but a distribution with a lot of easy work and a smaller amount of hard work. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Matomäki et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0307275); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3))

## What “Zone 2” and HIIT are and are not

“Zone 2” is not a perfectly standardized term. In a recent expert viewpoint, sport scientists and coaches converged on a practical definition of Zone 2 as work performed immediately below the first lactate or ventilatory threshold. The same paper also argued that many of the expected adaptations from this intensity are probably not unique to that narrow band and could also be induced slightly above or below it. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))

That definitional issue matters because most direct comparative trials do not clearly pit HIIT against a uniformly lactate-anchored definition of Zone 2. Instead, they usually compare HIIT with broader categories such as moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT), endurance training, or low-intensity training. So the evidence base is strongest for HIIT versus steady continuous training more generally, and only indirectly about “Zone 2” in the stricter coaching sense. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))

## What head-to-head trials and meta-analyses show about VO₂max

A 2015 systematic review and meta-analysis of controlled trials in healthy young to middle-aged adults found that both endurance training and HIIT improved VO₂max, but the gains were greater with HIIT. The estimated advantage of HIIT over endurance training was about 1.2 mL/kg/min, and the advantage tended to be larger in people with lower baseline fitness and in longer interventions. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0))

A 2019 meta-analysis sharpened that picture. HIIT was clearly effective for improving VO₂max across populations, but its superiority over MICT depended on the protocol. Compared with MICT, benefits were seen mainly when HIIT used longer work intervals of at least 2 minutes, session volumes of at least 15 minutes of interval work, and programs lasting roughly 4 to 12 weeks. Shorter and lower-volume HIIT was still effective and time-efficient, but not automatically superior to continuous training in every head-to-head comparison. ([Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013))

A good reminder comes from a 5-week randomized trial in obese young women: a 20-minute HIIT program and a 40-minute continuous program both improved VO₂peak by about 9 to 10 percent, with no significant between-group difference in the VO₂peak change. HIIT was rated as more enjoyable during the intervention and took less time, but it was not a clean physiological knockout. ([Kong et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0158589))

So the first popular claim, that HIIT produces larger VO₂max gains per minute than Zone 2, is mostly right in practical terms, but it is stronger as a real-world pattern than as a mathematically exact universal law. The overall literature favors HIIT for time-efficient VO₂max improvement, yet not every HIIT protocol beats every continuous protocol. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013); [Kong et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0158589))

## What low-intensity work seems to add

Where low-intensity training makes its best case is not “HIIT can never do this,” but specificity. In a 2024 randomized trial in recreationally active but untrained adults, a very-high-volume low-intensity program averaged about 6.7 hours per week at 63 percent of maximal heart rate, while the HIT program averaged about 1.6 hours per week. The low-intensity arm improved a pooled low-intensity performance category, while the HIT arm improved a pooled high-intensity performance category. In other words, low-intensity volume helped the kinds of traits that matter for steady work, while intensity helped the kinds of traits that matter for harder efforts. ([Matomäki et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0307275)) That trial also received equipment and salary support from Firstbeat Analytics; the authors reported no competing interests, but the findings still deserve that context. ([Matomäki et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0307275))

That is a more defensible version of the “base” argument. Low-intensity work appears to matter for submaximal adaptations, but the stronger claim that a narrow Zone 2 band has uniquely irreplaceable physiology behind it is not well supported. The recent expert viewpoint on Zone 2 explicitly noted that the expected adaptations are likely not unique to that intensity alone. ([Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))

## Trained versus untrained athletes

For untrained or less-fit adults, the broad takeaway is that both approaches work, with HIIT usually producing the larger VO₂max response and steady training often giving useful submaximal adaptations. The size of HIIT’s advantage tends to be bigger when baseline fitness is lower, but trials in less-trained populations still show that continuous training can meaningfully raise fitness, especially when done consistently. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Kong et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0158589); [Matomäki et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0307275))

In trained endurance athletes, the question shifts from “HIIT or not?” to “how should intensity be distributed?” A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis found a small overall advantage of polarized training for VO₂peak versus other training-intensity distributions, with the advantage showing up mainly in interventions shorter than 12 weeks and in highly trained athletes. The same review found similar effects for time trial, time to exhaustion, and second-threshold surrogates. The randomized trials in that review were judged to have some concern for risk of bias under RoB-2, but the review also rated the certainty of evidence as high, so the result is best read as supportive in that context rather than as a universal rule. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z))

A 2025 individual-participant-data network meta-analysis then made the picture more specific: there was no overall difference between polarized and pyramidal training for VO₂max or time-trial performance, but competitive athletes appeared more likely to benefit from polarized training, whereas recreational athletes appeared more likely to benefit from a pyramidal distribution. ([Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3))

So the third popular claim, that the optimal approach is polarized, is best supported in trained endurance athletes, not as a universal rule for everyone trying to get fitter. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3))

## Adherence, enjoyment, and long-term follow-up

Psychology does not rescue an easy answer either. A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis found small advantages for HIIT over MICT on post-exercise enjoyment scales, while affect during exercise was more mixed. The authors concluded that HIIT could be psychologically viable, but also noted that longer-term studies were needed before translating enjoyment findings into confidence about adherence. ([Oliveira et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0197124))

That caution looks justified. In a 2023 randomized within-subject study in insufficiently active healthy adults, training sequence influenced in-task affective valence, but there were no significant overall effects of training type or sequence on enjoyment, affective attitude, or intrinsic motivation. In plain terms: context and sequencing mattered, and neither modality owned motivation. ([Dierkes et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2023.84))

The best direct adherence synthesis came in a 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis. Supervised compliance was high and not significantly different between HIIT and MICT, while unsupervised adherence was moderate in both groups and also not significantly different. The supervised-compliance evidence was of moderate quality, whereas the unsupervised-adherence evidence was very low quality and heterogeneous, which makes strong claims risky. A small 2015 pilot trial in adults with prediabetes did find higher short-term logbook adherence after a supervised HIIT introduction than after MICT, but both groups improved fitness similarly, and the follow-up lasted only one month. ([Santos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-023-01535-w); [Jung et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1155/2015/191595))

For longer-term follow-up, one useful example is the FITR Heart Study. In a randomized cardiac-rehabilitation trial, HIIT improved VO₂peak more than MICT after 4 weeks, but the between-group difference had faded by 12 months, when both groups showed similar improvement from baseline. Adherence over the home-based follow-up was also similar. That is useful evidence for feasibility and longer-term drift, but it comes from a supervised clinical population with coronary artery disease, not from healthy endurance athletes. ([Taylor et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2020.3511))

## Limits and open debates

The cited higher-level evidence speaks much more directly to VO₂max and endurance-performance outcomes than to injury. That is an inference from the scope of the sources cited here: the main comparative reviews focus on aerobic adaptation, training distribution, enjoyment, and adherence, while the longer-follow-up safety data cited here come largely from supervised cardiac rehabilitation rather than healthy athletic populations. That does not mean injury risk is equal; it means these sources do not yet answer that part cleanly. ([Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013); [Santos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-023-01535-w); [Taylor et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2020.3511))

A 2025 narrative review went further and argued that current evidence does not support Zone 2 as the optimal intensity for improving mitochondrial or fatty-acid oxidative capacity in the general population, especially when training time is limited. Because this was a narrative review rather than a systematic review or meta-analysis, it should be weighted below the syntheses above. Still, it is a useful corrective to the strongest online claims about Zone 2. ([Storoschuk et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02261-y))

## Quick reference values

- Healthy adults, HIIT versus endurance training: about **+1.2 mL/kg/min** greater VO₂max improvement with HIIT in the 2015 meta-analysis. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0))
    
- HIIT versus MICT: superiority showed up mainly with **intervals ≥2 minutes**, **session volume ≥15 minutes**, and **programs lasting 4 to 12 weeks** in the 2019 meta-analysis. ([Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013))
    
- Polarized versus other intensity distributions in endurance athletes: **VO₂peak SMD 0.24 overall**, with a larger effect in **highly trained athletes (SMD 0.46)** in the 2024 meta-analysis. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z))
    
- Polarized versus pyramidal in endurance athletes: **no overall difference** in VO₂max or time-trial performance in the 2025 individual-participant-data network meta-analysis, but competitive athletes appeared to respond better to polarized training and recreational athletes to pyramidal training. ([Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3))
    
- Compliance and adherence: supervised compliance averaged **89.4% for HIIT** and **92.5% for MICT**; unsupervised adherence averaged **63.0% for HIIT** and **68.2% for MICT**, with no significant difference in the meta-analysis. ([Santos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-023-01535-w))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If the main goal is to raise **VO₂max as efficiently as possible**, HIIT usually has the better evidence. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013))
    
- If the goal also includes **steady-state performance and submaximal efficiency**, low-intensity work deserves a major role, but the claim that its adaptations are uniquely irreplaceable is overstated. ([Matomäki et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0307275); [Sitko et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2024-0303))
    
- For **trained endurance athletes**, the best-supported default is usually **mostly easy training plus some hard training**, not pure Zone 2 and not pure HIIT. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02034-z); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3))
    
- For **newer exercisers or people with lower fitness**, either approach can work, and the best program is often the one they can repeat consistently; adherence is not clearly better with HIIT over the long run. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0365-0); [Santos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-023-01535-w))
    
- The fourth popular claim is therefore the safest overall summary: the “best” depends on **baseline fitness, event demands, recovery tolerance, and adherence**, while the sources cited here address injury risk less directly than the physiology outcomes. ([Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02149-3); [Wen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2019.01.013); [Santos et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-023-01535-w); [Taylor et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2020.3511))
