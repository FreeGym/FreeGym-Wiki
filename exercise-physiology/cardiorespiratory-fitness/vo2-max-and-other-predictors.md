# VO₂ Max vs Other Predictors: Resting HR, HRV, Steps, Grip Strength

Many metrics compete as the “best health marker,” but they are not measuring the same thing. In outcome research, the strongest evidence base usually involves cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF), often expressed in METs or estimated rather than measured by direct gas-exchange VO₂ max. A 2025 meta-analysis found similar dose-response associations for objectively measured and estimated CRF for mortality prediction, so the practical CRF literature and the VO₂ max literature overlap substantially for risk prediction, even though estimated CRF should not be treated as physiologically identical to directly measured VO₂ max. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))

The strongest evidence is still concentrated on all-cause mortality and cardiovascular outcomes, not a standardized “healthspan” endpoint. That matters, because a marker can be excellent at predicting death or cardiovascular events without being the single best summary of disability, cognition, frailty, or quality of life. It also matters because effect sizes across these literatures are not perfectly comparable: they are often reported on different exposure scales, in different populations, and with different adjustment sets. ([Al Rifai et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31221461/); [Kim et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29594847/); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5227916/); [Hamaya et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38767892/); [Studenski et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))

## Why CRF/VO₂ max usually comes out on top

An overview of 26 systematic reviews and meta-analyses, representing more than 20.9 million observations from 199 unique cohort studies, found that high versus low CRF was associated with a very large mortality gradient for all-cause mortality (HR 0.47), and that each 1-MET higher CRF was associated with an 11 to 17 percent lower all-cause mortality risk. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))

A newer 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 42 studies representing 35 cohorts and 3.8 million observations found pooled relative risks per 1-MET higher CRF of 0.86 for all-cause mortality and 0.84 for cardiovascular mortality. The same paper found no statistically significant difference between objectively measured CRF and estimated CRF for mortality prediction, which is important because estimated CRF is much easier to use clinically. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))

CRF also improves multivariable risk prediction, not just crude association, although the incremental gains range from modest to meaningful depending on outcome. In the FIT Project, adding treadmill METs to the ASCVD risk score improved the c-statistic from 0.778 to 0.798 for all-cause mortality and from 0.726 to 0.733 for myocardial infarction. In the HUNT study, estimated CRF alone had c-statistics of 0.848 in men and 0.878 in women for cardiovascular mortality, and adding traditional clinical variables increased those figures only slightly. ([Al Rifai et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31221461/); [Nauman et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27866655/))

The claim that VO₂ max matters only in the low-fitness range is too strong. In a large treadmill cohort using exercise-test estimated CRF, CRF was inversely associated with long-term mortality with no observed upper limit of benefit, and extremely high aerobic fitness was associated with the greatest survival, including in older patients and those with hypertension. ([Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/))

## Why simple measures still perform surprisingly well

Daily step counts are not a trivial proxy. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 57 studies from 35 cohorts found that, compared with 2000 steps/day, 7000 steps/day was associated with a 47 percent lower risk of all-cause mortality, a 25 percent lower risk of cardiovascular disease incidence, and a 47 percent lower risk of cardiovascular mortality. The same review also found associations with dementia, depressive symptoms, falls, and physical function, although it noted limited age-specific analysis, residual confounding, and varying certainty across outcomes as important caveats. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40713949/))

In older women, step counts and time spent in moderate-to-vigorous activity had very similar predictive performance. In [Hamaya et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38767892/), the AUC for all-cause mortality was 0.55 for both step counts and MVPA time, and the hazard ratio per standard deviation was 0.74 for steps versus 0.82 for MVPA. That suggests a simple ambulatory measure can perform about as well as more guideline-style activity metrics in some settings, although absolute discrimination was modest in that analysis. ([Hamaya et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38767892/))

Gait speed is especially impressive in older adults. A meta-analysis of 48 cohorts found that each 0.1 m/s slower gait speed was associated with a 12 percent higher mortality risk and an 8 percent higher cardiovascular risk. In the pooled analysis by [Studenski et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/), predicted survival based on age, sex, and gait speed was as accurate as survival predicted from a much larger clinical model that also included chronic conditions, smoking history, blood pressure, body mass index, and hospitalization. ([Veronese et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30056008/); [Studenski et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/))

Grip strength also predicts risk consistently. An umbrella review found highly suggestive evidence that higher grip strength was associated with lower all-cause mortality (relative risk 0.72) and lower cardiovascular death risk (relative risk 0.84). A 2017 meta-analysis found that the lowest versus highest grip-strength category was associated with an HR of 1.41 for all-cause mortality and 1.63 for cardiovascular disease. ([Soysal et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32565244/); [Wu et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28549705/))

That said, “simple measures predict as well or better” is only partly supported. In a national population study, handgrip strength improved all-cause mortality prediction beyond the Framingham Risk Score, but walking speed alone did not; combinations of handgrip with walking speed or peak flow performed better than single physical markers. So simple measures can rival or materially improve existing models, but their relative ranking depends heavily on age, outcome, and comparator. ([Lee et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5227916/))

## Where resting heart rate and HRV fit

Resting heart rate is a real predictor, not just noise. A meta-analysis of 46 prospective studies found that each 10 bpm higher resting heart rate was associated with a 9 percent higher all-cause mortality risk and an 8 percent higher cardiovascular mortality risk. Compared with the lowest category, resting heart rate above 80 bpm was associated with relative risks of 1.45 for all-cause mortality and 1.33 for cardiovascular mortality. ([Zhang et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26598376/))

Resting heart rate also does not appear to be just a surrogate for poor fitness. In the Copenhagen Male Study, the association between higher resting heart rate and mortality persisted after adjustment for physical fitness, leisure-time physical activity, and standard cardiovascular risk factors. Men with resting heart rate above 90 bpm had an HR of 3.06 versus those at or below 50 bpm, and each 10 bpm increase was associated with a 16 percent higher mortality risk. ([Jensen et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23595657/))

HRV is also prognostic. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis including 32 studies, two individual-participant datasets, and 38,008 participants from healthy and patient populations found that lower HRV predicted higher mortality across ages, sexes, continents, populations, and recording lengths. In sub-analyses, the lowest quartile of 5-minute RMSSD versus the other quartiles yielded a combined HR of 1.56 for mortality. ([Jarczok et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243195/))

The limitation is comparative, not associative. The higher-level RHR and HRV literature is strong on hazard ratios, but there is much less clean head-to-head work showing c-statistic gains or reclassification against CRF, gait speed, steps, or grip strength. So RHR and HRV look useful as adjunct markers, but their place in a strict “best overall predictor” ranking is less settled. ([Zhang et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26598376/); [Jarczok et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243195/); [Al Rifai et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31221461/); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5227916/))

## Additive versus redundant value

The cleanest evidence for additive value comes from studies that include more than one physical domain. In UK Biobank, both CRF and grip strength predicted mortality independently after adjustment for each other. Compared with the lowest CRF group, the highest CRF group had an HR of 0.65 for all-cause mortality after adjustment for grip strength. Compared with the lowest grip-strength group, the highest grip-strength group had an HR of 0.79 after adjustment for CRF. The combination of highest CRF and highest grip strength had an HR of 0.53 for all-cause mortality and 0.31 for cardiovascular mortality versus the lowest-lowest group. ([Kim et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29594847/))

Body composition adds context, but it does not erase the value of fitness. A 2025 meta-analysis found that overweight-fit and obese-fit groups were not at statistically different mortality risk from normal-weight fit groups, whereas normal-weight unfit, overweight unfit, and obese unfit groups had roughly 2- to 3-fold higher mortality risks. That pattern argues against body size as a sufficient substitute for fitness. ([Weeldreyer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39537313/))

In older adults, function and strength often seem to matter more than muscle quantity alone. In the Health ABC cohort, low muscle mass did not explain the association between strength and mortality, and grip strength gave risk estimates similar to quadriceps strength. In a review focused on adults aged 80 and older, physical performance strongly predicted mortality, hospitalization, and disability, while muscle strength strongly predicted mortality and hospitalization, independently of muscle mass, inflammatory markers, and comorbidity. ([Newman et al., 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16456196/); [Legrand et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24802886/))

Association size and discrimination gain are not the same thing. Even when hazard ratios are strong, c-statistic improvements can be modest once age, sex, and standard risk factors are already in the model. That is one reason the strongest practical conclusion is not “pick one winner,” but “combine domains that capture different physiology.” ([Al Rifai et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31221461/); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5227916/))

## Older adults and age-stratified evidence

The strongest direct age-specific evidence for simple measures is in older adults. Gait speed strongly predicts survival in people 65 and older, and physical performance and muscle strength predict mortality, hospitalization, and disability in adults 80 and older even after accounting for muscle mass and comorbidity. That helps explain why gait speed and grip strength often look especially impressive in geriatric cohorts. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/); [Legrand et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24802886/))

At the same time, CRF does not stop mattering later in life. The treadmill data showing no observed upper limit of benefit also reported survival advantages in older patients. So the evidence supports a both-and view: CRF remains important across the range, while strength and mobility become especially informative as aging, frailty risk, and disability risk rise. ([Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/))

The gap is that age-stratified head-to-head comparisons are still limited. The 2025 steps meta-analysis explicitly noted a lack of age-specific analysis, and much of the gait-speed literature is older-adult heavy by design. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40713949/); [Veronese et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30056008/))

## Limits and open debates

The claim that CRF/VO₂ max dominates because it integrates multiple organ systems is the closest to being correct if the question is, “What is the best single overall predictor of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality risk?” The mortality gradient is large, the dose-response is consistent, and CRF adds information beyond conventional risk scores. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/); [Al Rifai et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31221461/))

The claim that simple measures predict outcomes as well or better is also partly true, but mostly in narrower contexts. Gait speed is exceptionally informative in older adults, grip strength can improve discrimination beyond standard risk scores, and daily steps are a surprisingly strong marker for several outcomes. But there is not good evidence that these simple markers universally outperform CRF across adult populations. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/); [Soysal et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32565244/); [Ding et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40713949/))

The claim that VO₂ max matters mostly in the low-fitness range is not well supported by the current evidence. The best large-cohort data show a graded relationship with no observed upper limit of benefit, not a pattern in which CRF stops mattering once someone escapes the lowest category. ([Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/); [Lang et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38599681/))

The claim that a combined score of fitness, strength, and body composition best predicts healthspan is the most defensible nuanced conclusion, but it is still ahead of the direct comparative evidence. Existing data clearly support additive value for fitness plus strength, and they show that body composition should be interpreted alongside fitness rather than instead of it. What remains thinner is formal head-to-head testing of multicomponent “healthspan” scores across diverse ages with discrimination and reclassification statistics. ([Kim et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29594847/); [Weeldreyer et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39537313/); [Lee et al., 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5227916/))

## Practical interpretation

- If one marker has to stand alone, CRF/VO₂ max is the best-supported choice overall for all-cause and cardiovascular mortality risk prediction. When lab VO₂ max testing is impractical, estimated CRF is a credible substitute for population-level risk prediction, though it should not be treated as physiologically identical to directly measured VO₂ max. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))
    
- If the goal is low-cost, scalable monitoring, daily steps are unusually strong and clinically useful. ([Ding et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40713949/))
    
- If the population is older adults, gait speed and grip strength deserve much more respect than they usually get. ([Studenski et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21205966/); [Legrand et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24802886/))
    
- Resting heart rate and HRV are meaningful adjuncts, but the comparative prediction literature around them is not as mature. ([Zhang et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26598376/); [Jarczok et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243195/))
    
- The best overall frame is not fitness versus strength versus body composition. It is fitness plus strength/function, with body composition used as context rather than as a replacement for performance measures. ([Kim et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29594847/))
