# Central vs Peripheral Limits: What Actually Determines VO₂ Max?

VO₂ max is a whole-system outcome. It is the highest rate at which oxygen can be delivered from the air to the working muscles and used during maximal exercise. The classic debate asks whether the main ceiling is set by central oxygen delivery (cardiac output, blood volume, arterial oxygen content) or by peripheral oxygen extraction and utilization in muscle (capillaries, diffusion, mitochondria). The best human evidence supports a central-dominant view for whole-body VO₂ max in most healthy adults, especially for short-term training gains, but it does not support a heart-only model. Peripheral traits still matter, and they appear to matter more at the margin as training status rises and in some hypoxic settings. ([Montero et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000000640); [Montero & Díaz-Cañestro, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1177/2047487315617118); [Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486); [Wagner, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10974-022-09636-y)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/"))

## What the Fick equation is and is not

At maximal exercise, the Fick equation describes VO₂ max as maximal cardiac output multiplied by arterial–venous oxygen difference. Cardiac output is how much blood the heart can pump per minute. Arterial–venous oxygen difference is the gap between oxygen content in arterial blood and venous blood, so it reflects how much oxygen the tissues removed. This partition is useful, but it does not by itself prove that one side is the single limiter. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486); [Wagner, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10974-022-09636-y)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))

Whole-body arterial–venous oxygen difference is influenced by arterial oxygen content, regional blood-flow distribution, diffusion from capillary to mitochondria, and mitochondrial oxygen use. In integrated models of oxygen transport, lungs, heart, blood, circulation, muscle diffusion, and mitochondrial respiration act as an in-series system, so changing one component changes the others. That is why the question “what is _the_ limiter?” is often less useful than “which variables matter most in this person, under these conditions?” ([Wagner, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10974-022-09636-y)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2939967/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2939967/"))

A second caveat is measurement. In many training studies, arterial–venous oxygen difference is calculated indirectly from VO₂ and cardiac output rather than measured directly with arterial and venous catheters. In that setting, a small or absent change in whole-body arterial–venous oxygen difference does not prove that the muscle periphery did not adapt, because changes in arterial oxygen content, blood-flow distribution, and error in cardiac-output measurement can mask real changes in extraction. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))

## Why central delivery usually dominates the whole-body number

In the highest-level synthesis for healthy young adults, a systematic review/meta-analysis of 13 endurance-training studies found that 5 to 13 weeks of training increased VO₂ max and maximal cardiac output, but not maximal arterial–venous oxygen difference, in previously untrained or moderately trained adults. The change in maximal cardiac output tracked the change in VO₂ max, while the change in arterial–venous oxygen difference did not. On average, short-term gains in whole-body VO₂ max were therefore explained more by improved oxygen delivery than by improved extraction. ([Montero et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000000640)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/"))

A similar pattern appears with healthy ageing. In a systematic review of 16 studies in mostly untrained adults aged 42 to 71, endurance training increased VO₂ max and maximal cardiac output. Maximal oxygen extraction rose clearly only in interventions lasting more than 12 weeks, and meta-regression again linked the gain in VO₂ max to the gain in maximal cardiac output more than to the gain in extraction. That argues for central dominance in many whole-body training responses, while still leaving room for peripheral contributions over longer time scales. ([Montero & Díaz-Cañestro, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1177/2047487315617118)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26553969/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26553969/"))

Several mechanistic intervention studies point the same way, though phlebotomy findings are not fully uniform. In a muscle-biopsy and phlebotomy study of 16 untrained young men, 6 weeks of endurance training increased peak oxygen uptake, peak cardiac output, blood volumes, capillary-to-fibre ratio, and mitochondrial volume density. But when the training-induced rise in red cell volume was removed by phlebotomy, peak oxygen uptake and peak cardiac output returned to pretraining levels even though the measured muscle structural adaptations remained. The authors concluded that the improvement was primarily explained by higher peak cardiac output and blood oxygen-carrying capacity, not by the measured capillary and mitochondrial changes. ([Montero et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP270250)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4606528/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4606528/"))

A similar result was reported after sprint-interval training. In nine healthy adults, 6 weeks of sprint-interval training increased VO₂ max by 11%, blood volume by 5.4%, and maximal cardiac output by 8.8%, while systemic oxygen extraction increased by 4.0%. When blood volume was brought back toward baseline by phlebotomy, VO₂ max and maximal cardiac output fell back toward pretraining values. That supports a primary role for the hypervolemic and cardiac-output response, with a smaller peripheral contribution. ([Mandić et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP283807)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37071120/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37071120/"))

However, not all phlebotomy studies fully reverse the training response. After 10 weeks of endurance training in 12 untrained adults, removal of the training-induced rise in haemoglobin mass and blood volume did _not_ abolish the increase in peak oxygen uptake. The improvement was attributed mainly to higher peak cardiac output, with a smaller contribution from wider arterial–venous oxygen difference. That still supports central predominance, but it weakens any stronger claim that blood-volume expansion alone usually explains the training response. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-020-04336-2)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7181565/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7181565/"))

## Why peripheral muscle factors still matter

If central delivery were the whole story, training should not consistently remodel muscle. But it does. A 2025 systematic review/meta-regression found that endurance training, high-intensity training, and sprint-interval training all increased mitochondrial content by roughly 23 to 27% and capillaries per fibre by roughly 10 to 15% after adjustment for relevant covariates. Lower initial fitness predicted larger relative gains, and improvements in capillarization and mitochondrial content were moderately correlated with changes in VO₂ max. Peripheral adaptation is therefore real and relevant, even if it is not always the main explanation for the whole-body VO₂ max gain in a given intervention. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11787188/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11787188/"))

The strongest athlete-side argument is not that oxygen extraction replaces delivery as the only limiter, but that very high VO₂ max is associated with exceptionally high extraction on top of high delivery. In a pooled analysis of invasive studies in healthy young men, systemic oxygen extraction fraction rose with VO₂ max up to about 90%, and leg extraction fraction approached roughly 90 to 95%. Muscle oxygen diffusing capacity also increased with VO₂ max. The authors concluded that oxygen delivery is the main limiting factor overall, but that enhanced oxygen extraction contributes to the remarkably high VO₂ max seen in endurance-trained individuals. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))

Training-modality studies also show that peripheral remodeling is not just background noise. In a randomized cross-over trial in sedentary adults, both continuous and interval training improved VO₂ max, but only interval training significantly increased maximal cardiac output and skeletal-muscle mitochondrial oxidative capacity, while capillary density increased after both programs and rose more after continuous training. Different training stimuli can therefore shift central and peripheral adaptations in different proportions while still improving VO₂ max. ([Daussin et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpregu.00875.2007)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18417645/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18417645/"))

## How the balance shifts with training status

The relative contribution of central and peripheral factors can change over time within the same training block. In a 12-week endurance-training study comparing older and young men, VO₂ max rose within 3 weeks in both groups. In older men, about 69% of the pre-to-post increase was attributed to greater maximal cardiac output and about 31% to wider arterial–venous oxygen difference. In young men, the split was 56% versus 44%, but the early gains in the first 3 weeks relied mainly on wider arterial–venous oxygen difference, whereas later gains were explained by greater maximal cardiac output. This suggests that “the limiter” can shift with training stage and age. ([Murias et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.01152.2009)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20056848/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20056848/"))

A newer local-muscle study makes the same point more directly. In five initially sedentary young men performing single-leg knee-extensor training, higher inspired oxygen did not increase pretraining muscle VO₂ max, suggesting that before training the active muscle was not oxygen-transport-limited in normoxia. After 8 weeks of training, muscle VO₂ max became higher in hyperoxia than in normoxia or hypoxia. The authors interpreted this as a shift from limitation by mitochondrial capacity to use oxygen toward limitation by oxygen transport to the mitochondria. This was a small, highly specific local-muscle study rather than a whole-body treadmill or cycle VO₂ max test, but it strongly supports the idea that training status changes where the bottleneck sits. ([Broxterman et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP285650)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38048175/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38048175/"))

## What happens at altitude

Altitude shifts the problem upstream because lower inspired oxygen pressure lowers arterial oxygen content unless acclimatization fully compensates. But the available invasive studies do not show a simple switch from “central” at sea level to “peripheral” at altitude. In one study at 5,260 m, acclimatization restored arterial oxygen content to sea-level values, yet VO₂ max remained markedly reduced because maximal cardiac output and leg blood flow stayed about 15% lower, and a smaller fraction of cardiac output reached the legs. Leg oxygen extraction remained at sea-level values. ([Calbet et al., 2003](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpregu.00156.2002)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12388462/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12388462/"))

A related study at 4,100 m found that lowlanders maintained about 90% fractional muscle oxygen extraction from sea level through 2 and 8 weeks of acclimatization. Muscle oxygen conductance fell after 8 weeks, but when the authors accounted for the reduction in maximal leg blood flow, the main explanation for the persistently lower leg VO₂ max was still the loss of maximal leg blood flow rather than a large new failure of extraction. ([Lundby et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1113/jphysiol.2006.106765)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16581864/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16581864/"))

That said, altitude is not purely a blood-flow story. In a smaller study after 7 to 8 days at 4,559 m, absolute arterial–venous oxygen extraction at peak exercise fell, while the oxygen extraction ratio estimated from central venous measurements did not change. The authors interpreted this pattern as suggesting that oxygen extraction may have become constrained in that context. So the most defensible summary is that altitude mainly lowers VO₂ max through reduced oxygen supply and reduced maximal blood flow, while diffusion or extraction limits can also contribute depending on altitude, acclimatization time, and measurement method. ([Martin et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1093/bja/aeu404)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25501722/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25501722/"))

## Why the single-limiter model is too simple

The integrated-system conclusion comes mainly from mechanistic modeling and physiological synthesis rather than from randomized training trials, because it is a systems question about how the oxygen transport chain is coupled. In these analyses, ventilation, lung diffusion, blood oxygen content, cardiac output, regional muscle blood flow, muscle diffusion, and mitochondrial respiration all interact. There is no single determinant that is always _the_ limiter. ([Wagner, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10974-022-09636-y)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2939967/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2939967/"))

That framing fits the human training and altitude data better than a strict central-versus-peripheral binary. Whole-body VO₂ max usually depends most on delivery. Peripheral adaptations are still required for high extraction, strong local oxygen conductance, and the ability to turn delivered oxygen into aerobic work. As fitness rises, the system can become more delivery-limited precisely because the muscle has improved. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486); [Broxterman et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP285650); [Mølmen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))

## Practical interpretation

- For most whole-body VO₂ max tests at sea level, start with central delivery. Cardiac output, blood volume, and arterial oxygen content usually explain more of the ceiling than muscle utilization alone, especially in less-trained people and during the first weeks of training. ([Montero et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000000640); [Montero & Díaz-Cañestro, 2016](https://doi.org/10.1177/2047487315617118); [Mandić et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP283807)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25680086/"))
    
- A stable whole-body arterial–venous oxygen difference does not prove that the muscle periphery did not adapt. Capillaries, mitochondrial content, oxygen diffusion, and flow distribution can improve even when the Fick partition appears unchanged. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486); [Mølmen et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02120-2)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))
    
- In trained athletes, peripheral properties matter more at the margin. The current evidence does not show that they usually replace cardiac output as the main limiter of whole-body VO₂ max, but it does show that very high VO₂ max is associated with both exceptional delivery and very high extraction. ([Skattebo et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.13486)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7540168/"))
    
- At altitude, reduced oxygen supply and reduced maximal blood flow usually dominate the fall in VO₂ max, but peripheral extraction or diffusion can also contribute depending on context. ([Calbet et al., 2003](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpregu.00156.2002); [Lundby et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1113/jphysiol.2006.106765); [Martin et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1093/bja/aeu404)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12388462/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12388462/"))
