# Heat Training: Does It Improve VO₂ Max and Performance?

Heat acclimation is best understood as specific preparation for exercising in the heat, not as a universal “fitness hack.” The strongest evidence says it lowers physiological strain and improves endurance performance in hot conditions. Evidence for meaningful gains in VO₂ max or performance in cool conditions is smaller and more inconsistent overall, and in some matched-control trials it does not exceed what good ordinary endurance training already provides. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/), [2021 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/), [2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

## What heat acclimation is and is not

Consensus guidance describes heat acclimation/acclimatisation as repeated exercise-heat exposure used to reduce physiological strain and improve exercise capacity in hot environments. The practical frame is usually about 1 to 2 weeks of repeated heat sessions. Early adaptations can appear within a few days, but the main physiological adaptations are not usually complete until about a week, and around 2 weeks is preferred when possible. Athletes are also advised to begin sessions euhydrated and to limit dehydration during training and competition. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

It is not presented in the consensus literature as a replacement for ordinary endurance training. The consensus literature frames it mainly as preparation for hot competition, and it specifically notes that measures in temperate environments cannot be used as a substitute for a test in hot ambient temperatures when the goal is hot-weather performance. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

## VO₂ max: strongest effects in hot testing

A 2021 meta-analysis that pooled 28 peer-reviewed articles found that heat acclimation improved VO₂ max more in hot testing than in thermoneutral testing. In control-group comparisons, the effect was small in thermoneutral conditions (Hedges’ g = 0.30) and larger in hot conditions (g = 0.75), with longer heat exposure and higher ambient training temperature associated with larger hot-condition effects. This is the strongest summary in favor of heat training improving aerobic capacity, but the gains are clearly more robust when the post-test itself is hot. ([2021 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/))

The stricter RCT-only meta-analysis points in a more cautious direction. In 11 randomized controlled trials with 215 participants, heat acclimation improved time-trial performance, but it did not significantly improve VO₂ max relative to non-heat controls (mean difference 2.51 mL/kg/min, 95% CI −1.36 to 6.37; p = 0.20). In the same review, there was also no significant between-group effect on plasma volume. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/))

Recent controlled studies fit that pattern. In a 2024 crossover study in trained cyclists and triathletes, heat acclimation increased VO₂ peak by 6% in hot testing but not in cool or hypoxic testing. ([2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0)) In contrast, a 2019 randomized trial of 5.5 weeks of heat training in trained cyclists found that cool-condition VO₂ max remained unchanged, and cool-condition time-trial gains were no better than matched cool-condition training. ([2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)) That said, a landmark 2010 controlled study reported about 5% higher VO₂ max and 6% better time-trial performance in cool conditions after 10 days of heat acclimation, which is one reason the cool-condition transfer question remains unsettled rather than resolved. ([2010 controlled study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2963322/)) Taken together, the best reading is that heat training can improve aerobic capacity measured in the heat, but general transfer to cool-condition VO₂ max is inconsistent and often modest on average. ([2021 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/), [2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/), [2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0))

## Performance: benefits are clearer than VO₂ max

Performance is where the evidence is strongest. The RCT-only meta-analysis found a significant benefit for time-trial performance (SMD 0.50, 95% CI 0.03 to 0.97). That does not mean every athlete gets a large gain, but it does mean the average direction across controlled trials favors heat acclimation for endurance performance, especially when the task itself is performed in the heat. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/))

Transfer still matters. In the 2024 crossover study, mean 20-minute time-trial power improved by about 8% in hot conditions and about 4% in cool conditions after heat acclimation, while hypoxic performance did not improve significantly. ([2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0)) But the longer 2019 randomized trial found that cool-condition time-trial performance improved similarly in the heat and control groups, which suggests that some apparent transfer to cool environments can reflect normal training adaptation rather than a unique effect of heat exposure itself. ([2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)). ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0))

That is why the fairest summary is not “heat training boosts fitness across the board.” It is closer to this: heat acclimation reliably helps you perform better in the heat, while the incremental value for cool-condition performance is context-specific and not always larger than what you would get from just continuing to train well. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/), [2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

## Why plasma volume matters but does not settle the question

Plasma volume expansion is one of the classic mechanisms used to explain heat-training benefits, because a larger plasma volume can support stroke volume and cardiovascular stability during exercise. But the evidence does not support treating plasma volume as a simple on/off explanation for better performance. The consensus statement notes that the role of plasma volume expansion remains debated and may be more useful as an indicator of adaptation than as the direct mechanism improving exercise capacity in the heat. It also states that measures in a temperate environment cannot be used as a substitute for a test in hot ambient temperatures. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

The RCT-only meta-analysis found no significant overall between-group increase in plasma volume after heat acclimation. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/)) And in the 2024 crossover study, plasma volume was about 4.2% higher late in the heat-acclimation block, but only about 1% above baseline at post-testing, even though hot-condition VO₂ peak and time-trial performance improved. ([2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0)) That combination argues against a simple claim that plasma volume expansion by itself explains the full performance effect. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0))

A cautious mechanistic interpretation is that plasma volume probably contributes to the heat-specific performance benefit, but it overlaps with other adaptations such as lower skin and core temperature responses, altered sweating, and reduced cardiovascular strain. In other words, plasma volume is part of the story, not the whole story. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/), [2024 crossover study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42978-024-00300-0)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

## Heat adaptations fade if you mistime them

A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis on decay and re-induction estimated that end-exercise heart-rate adaptations fall by about 2.3% per day of decay and core-temperature adaptations by about 2.6% per day, with a practical, approximate rule of thumb that roughly 1 day of heat acclimation is lost for every 2 days without heat exposure. Re-acclimation is faster than first-time acclimation, but timing still matters. ([2018 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/))

That matters in practice because a heat block done too early before a race may lose much of its value before the event, whereas a well-timed block closer to a hot competition is more likely to preserve the adaptations that actually matter on race day. ([2018 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/))

## Females: fewer studies, but a similar performance-in-the-heat signal

The older RCT meta-analysis was based on a literature that was overwhelmingly male (91% men). ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/)) A newer 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis focused on females found reductions in resting and exercise core temperature, skin temperature, and heart rate, plus increased sweat rate and improved performance test outcomes in the heat. Unlike the classic narrative, that female-specific meta-analysis did not find a significant plasma-volume change. ([2023 female-specific meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10289939/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10289939/))

So the female-specific evidence does support meaningful heat adaptation for performance in hot conditions, but it also weakens the idea that plasma volume expansion is always the central explanation. The more defensible conclusion is that the heat-performance effect appears to generalize to females, while some mechanisms may not mirror the male-heavy literature exactly. ([2023 female-specific meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10289939/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10289939/))

## Safety and whether it is worth it

Heat acclimation is not risk-free, which is why the consensus guidance emphasizes progressive exposure, adequate hydration, and monitoring rather than treating heat training as a casual add-on. The same consensus paper recommends it specifically for athletes planning to compete in hot ambient conditions, not as a blanket recommendation for everyone. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

Real-world heat-illness incidence is highly setting-specific, but mass-participation road-race data show that meaningful event risk can exist when conditions are hot. In the Falmouth Road Race cohort from 2012 to 2019, the mean incidence was 4.83 total heat illnesses and 2.07 exertional heat-stroke cases per 1000 finishers, and incidence rose with wet-bulb globe temperature. These are race data, not acclimation-trial incidence data, but they are a useful reminder that heat stress is a real safety issue, not just a discomfort issue. ([2024 Falmouth Road Race analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10976335/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10976335/))

For non-elite athletes, the “worth it” question depends on the goal. As an inference from the evidence above, a heat block makes the most sense when the target event will actually be hot and the protocol can be done progressively and safely. If the goal is simply to raise cool-condition VO₂ max or get a generic training boost, the incremental value over normal endurance training looks small and inconsistent. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/), [2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))

## Quick reference points

- Best-supported claim: heat acclimation improves endurance performance in hot conditions. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))
    
- VO₂ max improvements are more consistent when measured in the heat than in cool conditions. ([2021 meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8222027/))
    
- Plasma volume expansion is a common adaptation, but it is not a complete or universally reliable explanation for better performance. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))
    
- Heat adaptations decay fairly quickly, so timing matters. ([2018 systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29129022/))
    
- For athletes not preparing for hot competition, the extra benefit over ordinary endurance training is usually modest or uncertain. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Heat acclimation is a specific tool for hot-weather performance, not a universal upgrade to aerobic fitness. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))
    
- If the event is hot, a well-timed 1- to 2-week heat block is evidence-based and often worthwhile. ([consensus guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4602249/))
    
- If the event is cool, claims about better VO₂ max or clearly superior performance should be framed cautiously, because the incremental value beyond good training is small and inconsistent. ([2019 RCT meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/), [2019 randomized trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6843002/), [2010 controlled study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2963322/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6543994/))
