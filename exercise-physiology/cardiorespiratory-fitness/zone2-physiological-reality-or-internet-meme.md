# Zone 2: Physiological Reality or Internet Meme?

“Zone 2” is partly a real physiological idea and partly a terminology problem. The stronger claim - that there is an individualized low-intensity domain around or below the first lactate or ventilatory threshold (LT1/VT1) that can improve aerobic fitness, and that low-to-moderate continuous endurance training can improve some mitochondrial markers - is supported. The weaker claim - that there is a uniquely magical fat-burning or mitochondrial zone that produces adaptations unavailable at nearby intensities - is not well supported by the higher-level literature. (Nuuttila et al., 2026; Mølmen et al., 2025; Vabishchevich et al., 2026). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/), [Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/), [Vabishchevich et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12758752/))

## What “Zone 2” is and is not

In five-zone coaching models, “Zone 2” commonly refers to low-intensity work. In the three-zone model used in much of the endurance literature, however, Zone 1 is below LT1/VT1 and Zone 2 is the intensity domain between the first and second thresholds. Part of the terminology problem is therefore semantic before it is physiological. (Meixner et al., 2025; Oliveira et al., 2024). ([Meixner et al., 2025 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986187/), [Oliveira et al., 2024 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11329428/))

A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis found a significant association between FATmax and the aerobic threshold, and a 2025 laboratory study found that VT1 and FATmax can overlap in some individuals but are not interchangeable markers, with FATmax often lower than VT1. But both lines of evidence also highlight substantial methodological and interindividual variability, which means fixed percentages of HRmax or VO2max are poor substitutes for individualized threshold-based prescription. (Peric et al., 2022; Meixner et al., 2025). ([Peric et al., 2022 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9180269/), [Meixner et al., 2025 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986187/))

## Why LT1/VT1 matters

A 2026 systematic review/meta-analysis of randomized trials in healthy working-age adults defined low-intensity training as work below LT1/VT1 or equivalent low-intensity criteria. It found moderate-to-large improvements in VO2max, maximal aerobic power, and VT1 versus controls, along with smaller but significant improvements in blood lipids and blood pressure. The same review found no definitive minimum effective intensity inside the low-intensity domain for positive fitness or health effects, although the upper end of that domain may improve VO2max more than the lower end. (Nuuttila et al., 2026). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/))

That is a reasonable description of physiological reality: LT1/VT1 marks a meaningful transition, and training below it works. It is not, by itself, good evidence for a razor-thin magic zone, because the same review notes that the specific effects of intensity within the low-intensity domain have received less attention and that benefits were seen across a fairly broad low-intensity band. (Nuuttila et al., 2026). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/))

## Mitochondria: real adaptation, not unique magic

The mitochondrial story is real. A 2026 systematic review/meta-analysis of moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT) studies with muscle biopsies found significant increases in mitochondrial volume density and VO2max, with smaller increases in citrate synthase activity and MFN2, although the certainty of evidence was very low to low. (Vabishchevich et al., 2026). ([Vabishchevich et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12758752/))

But those adaptations are not unique to Zone 2. A 2025 systematic review/meta-regression found that traditional endurance training, high-intensity interval training, and sprint interval training all increased mitochondrial content on average after adjustment, while higher training frequency and, in some training categories, longer interventions were associated with larger mitochondrial gains. Per unit time, SIT was more efficient for mitochondrial-content gains than HIT, and HIT was more efficient than traditional endurance training. (Mølmen et al., 2025). ([Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/))

## Volume matters, but so does intensity

If volume alone explained the mitochondrial effect, work-matched interval and continuous training should produce similar responses. In a short-term within-subject trial in young active men, they did not: interval training produced greater increases in citrate synthase maximal activity and mitochondrial respiration than continuous training matched for total work and session duration. That does not mean easy training is useless; it means that, in this study, matching total work and session duration did not eliminate interval-versus-continuous differences in these mitochondrial measures. (MacInnis et al., 2017). ([MacInnis et al., 2017 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27396440/))

The same broader conclusion shows up in athlete-program data. A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis found that polarized training improved VO2peak more than other intensity distributions, but not time-trial performance, time to exhaustion, or velocity/power at LT2/VT2, and its VO2peak advantage was mostly seen in shorter interventions. Because polarized training typically combines a lot of training below LT1/VT1 with a smaller amount of high-intensity work, these findings are consistent with programs that combine a lot of easy volume with some higher-intensity work. (Oliveira et al., 2024). ([Oliveira et al., 2024 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11329428/))

## Fat oxidation and metabolic flexibility

Fat oxidation is relevant to this discussion, but it should not be confused with the final goal of training. A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis found that FATmax and the aerobic threshold are meaningfully associated. At the same time, association is not the same as close individual agreement, and the review reported substantial heterogeneity and recommended strict standardization. (Peric et al., 2022). ([Peric et al., 2022 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9180269/))

A metabolic-flexibility study in obese men showed that 10 days of aerobic training at 70% VO2peak increased fatty-acid oxidation and citrate synthase activity, but did not produce an additional fatty-acid-oxidation rise when a high-fat diet was added after training. In that strict experimental sense, exercise improved fat-oxidation capacity, but did not show a further fatty-acid-oxidation increase with the added high-fat-diet challenge. (Battaglia et al., 2012). ([Battaglia et al., 2012 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3532462/))

Fat oxidation also is not exclusive to continuous low-intensity work. A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis in adults who were not trained athletes found that HIIT and SIT improved fat oxidation during exercise and were slightly more effective than MICT on that endpoint, with larger effects in longer interventions and in people with overweight or obesity. So maximizing in-session fat burn is not required for improving fat oxidation. (Atakan et al., 2022). ([Atakan et al., 2022 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35859145/))

## Limits and open debates

The best case for Zone 2 is that training below the first threshold is a real, repeatable intensity domain that can improve aerobic fitness. The best case against the meme version is that mitochondrial remodeling, fat-oxidation improvements, and aerobic gains are also produced by higher-intensity training and by mixed intensity distributions. (Nuuttila et al., 2026; Mølmen et al., 2025; MacInnis et al., 2017; Atakan et al., 2022). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/), [Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/), [MacInnis et al., 2017 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27396440/), [Atakan et al., 2022 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35859145/))

Another limit is measurement. Papers variously define the target using LT1, VT1, AerT, FATmax, fixed HRmax percentages, or generic “moderate” intensity. That makes direct comparison messy and limits precise cross-study comparison. (Meixner et al., 2025; Peric et al., 2022; Nuuttila et al., 2026). ([Meixner et al., 2025 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986187/), [Peric et al., 2022 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9180269/), [Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/))

## Quick reference conclusions

- LT1/VT1 is a real physiological anchor for low-intensity training, but “Zone 2” means different things in different zoning systems. (Meixner et al., 2025; Oliveira et al., 2024). ([Meixner et al., 2025 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986187/), [Oliveira et al., 2024 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11329428/))
    
- Low-to-moderate continuous training can improve VO2max, VT1, blood lipids, and blood pressure, and moderate-intensity continuous training can improve mitochondrial volume density and some mitochondrial markers. (Vabishchevich et al., 2026; Nuuttila et al., 2026). ([Vabishchevich et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12758752/), [Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/))
    
- Those benefits are not unique to Zone 2. Higher-intensity training can match or exceed some mitochondrial and fat-oxidation outcomes, especially per unit time. (Mølmen et al., 2025; MacInnis et al., 2017; Atakan et al., 2022). ([Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/), [MacInnis et al., 2017 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27396440/), [Atakan et al., 2022 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35859145/))
    
- For health, low-intensity training can be effective whether or not it is labeled “Zone 2.” Current evidence does not show one universally optimal fat-burning intensity inside the whole low-intensity domain for every outcome. (Nuuttila et al., 2026; Peric et al., 2022). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/), [Peric et al., 2022 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9180269/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- If you want physiological precision, anchor easy aerobic work to LT1/VT1 or a validated threshold proxy, not a universal percentage. (Meixner et al., 2025; Peric et al., 2022). ([Meixner et al., 2025 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986187/), [Peric et al., 2022 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9180269/))
    
- If you want health, low-to-moderate intensity is effective even if you never call it Zone 2. (Nuuttila et al., 2026). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/))
    
- If you want performance, a program made entirely of “Zone 2” is probably narrower than the evidence cited here. Easy volume matters, but the cited literature also supports a role for higher-intensity work. (Oliveira et al., 2024; MacInnis et al., 2017; Mølmen et al., 2025). ([Oliveira et al., 2024 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11329428/), [MacInnis et al., 2017 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27396440/), [Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/))
    
- The most defensible summary is: physiological reality, yes; uniquely magical internet myth, no. (Nuuttila et al., 2026; Mølmen et al., 2025; Atakan et al., 2022). ([Nuuttila et al., 2026 at PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12810095/), [Mølmen et al., 2025 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/), [Atakan et al., 2022 at PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35859145/))
