# Zone 2 and Glycemic Control: Is It Special for Metabolic Health?

Zone 2-like training clearly helps glycemic control, but the current evidence does not support a stronger claim that it is uniquely special for metabolic health. In prediabetes and type 2 diabetes, several exercise modes improve glucose outcomes, and the "best" modality changes depending on whether the outcome is HbA1c, fasting glucose, postprandial glucose, continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) metrics, or clamp-based insulin sensitivity. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415); [Zhang et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2025.1560676/full))

## What "Zone 2" means in this literature

Most diabetes trials do not prescribe exercise using coaching-zone language. They usually classify aerobic training as moderate or vigorous intensity using percentages of heart-rate reserve, maximal heart rate, maximal oxygen uptake, or oxygen uptake reserve. In practice, what most people call Zone 2 maps most closely onto moderate-intensity continuous aerobic training, and in prediabetes trials that often meant prescriptions around 60% to 70% of HRmax, although the included studies were heterogeneous and also used HRR- and VO2max-based prescriptions. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full))

That distinction matters because the literature is really about moderate continuous aerobic training, not about a standardized, universally defined "Zone 2" intervention. So any claim that Zone 2 is metabolically unique is already starting from a category mismatch between coaching language and trial design. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full))

## Meta-analyses in type 2 diabetes and prediabetes

In type 2 diabetes, a 2018 network meta-analysis found that compared with no exercise, combined aerobic plus resistance training reduced HbA1c by about 0.53 percentage points, while supervised aerobic and supervised resistance each reduced HbA1c by about 0.30 points. In that analysis, combined training ranked best for HbA1c, while supervised aerobic ranked best for fasting blood glucose. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3))

A newer 2025 network meta-analysis of 158 randomized trials with 17,059 participants updated that picture. All major exercise modes improved HbA1c versus control: HIIT by about 0.61 points, combined training by 0.58, aerobic training by 0.58, resistance training by 0.40, and even physical activity advice by 0.35. HIIT ranked highest, but combined and aerobic training were close behind. ([Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415))

In prediabetes, a 2024 network meta-analysis found that moderate-intensity aerobic training, low-to-moderate-load resistance training, vigorous aerobic training, and combined moderate aerobic plus low-to-moderate resistance training all improved glycemic control versus no exercise. In that analysis, low-to-moderate resistance training ranked best for fasting glucose, combined moderate aerobic plus resistance ranked best for HbA1c, and vigorous aerobic training was strongest for 2-hour post-meal glucose. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full))

A 2025 prediabetes network meta-analysis refined this further: in its main results, aerobic plus resistance training produced the largest fasting-glucose decrease, while aerobic exercise alone produced the largest reductions in 2-hour post-meal glucose and HbA1c. That is another reason not to overstate a single metabolically privileged zone. ([Zhang et al., 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2025.1560676/full))

## Head-to-head trials: aerobic, resistance, and combined

Direct randomized trials point in the same direction. In the [DARE trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17876019/), aerobic training alone and resistance training alone both improved glycemic control, but the largest improvements occurred with combined aerobic plus resistance training. A later large trial, [HART-D](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/186960), also found that combined aerobic and resistance training improved HbA1c versus control for the primary endpoint, whereas aerobic-only and resistance-only training did not achieve that endpoint against control. ([Sigal et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17876019/); [Church et al., 2010](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/186960))

Resistance training should not be treated as second-class therapy. In the [RAED2 trial](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/35/4/676/38408/Metabolic-Effects-of-Aerobic-Training-and), resistance training improved metabolic features, insulin sensitivity, and abdominal fat similarly to aerobic training. In the hepatic-fat follow-up from the same program, resistance and aerobic training produced comparable increases in clamp-derived insulin sensitivity, with similar reductions in HbA1c and liver fat. ([Bacchi et al., 2012](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/35/4/676/38408/Metabolic-Effects-of-Aerobic-Training-and); [Bacchi et al., 2013](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/hep.26393))

So claim 3 is partly right: resistance training is often as good as aerobic training for insulin sensitivity and some glycemic endpoints, especially fasting glucose in some analyses and body-composition-related changes. But it is not clearly better overall, particularly for postprandial or CGM-based glucose stability, where aerobic and interval-based work often looks stronger. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full); [Wang and Tang, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1496271/full))

## Interval versus continuous training

This is where the "Zone 2 plus bursts" idea gets some real support. In a 4-month randomized trial that energy-matched interval walking against continuous walking, interval walking improved glycemic control, insulin sensitivity index, peripheral glucose disposal, and disposition index, while continuous walking did not. The authors concluded that alternating intensity, not just volume and mean intensity, was a key determinant of improvement. ([Karstoft et al., 2014a](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00125-014-3334-5))

The acute data point the same way. In a crossover study of one interval-walking session versus oxygen-consumption- and time-matched continuous walking, interval walking lowered same-day CGM mean glucose more than continuous walking. In a 2-week crossover trial, short-term interval walking improved CGM-derived glycemic variability and maximal glucose, whereas continuous walking did not. ([Karstoft et al., 2014b](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/99/9/3334/2538522); [Karstoft et al., 2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00125-016-4170-6))

But the interval advantage is not universal. In a 1-year randomized trial comparing HIIT plus resistance training with moderate continuous training plus resistance training, neither arm improved glycemic control after adjustment, and some body-composition and cardiorespiratory-fitness outcomes favored the moderate-continuous arm. In a 2024 randomized trial, low-volume combined HIIT and a much higher-volume combined moderate program improved glycemic control similarly over 8 weeks. ([Magalhães et al., 2019](https://dom-pubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/dom.13551); [Gajanand et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11459303/))

So claim 4 is only partly right. Short high-intensity bursts can outperform matched continuous training in some tightly controlled studies, but the broader literature does not prove that "Zone 2 plus bursts" is a universally superior formula. The most reproducible winner is still multimodal training, especially aerobic plus resistance. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415))

## CGM and clamp studies

CGM outcomes are especially useful here because they capture day-to-day glucose stability better than HbA1c alone. A 2020 meta-analysis found that exercise reduced mean 24-hour glucose in short-term studies, reduced time spent in hyperglycemia, and improved indices of glycemic variability, but did not consistently reduce fasting glucose. That is an important nuance: exercise often helps daily glucose profiles more clearly than fasting labs. ([Munan et al., 2020](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2020.00495/full))

A 2024 meta-analysis focused specifically on aerobic exercise and CGM found that aerobic training improved 24-hour mean glucose overall. Moderate-intensity and high-intensity aerobic exercise both worked, and benefits were seen with sessions lasting 20 to 60 minutes. In subgroup analyses, effects were clearer in BMI groups above 29 kg/m². That supports the claim that aerobic exercise helps overweight people with impaired metabolism, but it does not show that moderate continuous work is uniquely privileged over other aerobic options. ([Wang and Tang, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1496271/full))

Clamp studies weaken the case for Zone 2 being uniquely special. In a 2-week randomized study of sprint interval training versus moderate-intensity continuous training in people with type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, whole-body insulin-stimulated glucose uptake increased by about 25% in both groups. There was a small local advantage for sprint training in one thigh muscle, but the main result was that both interval and moderate continuous training rapidly improved insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. ([Sjöros et al., 2018](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sms.12875))

## Limits and open debates

The biggest limitation is that the literature is not truly "Zone 2" literature. It is literature on moderate aerobic, vigorous aerobic, resistance, combined training, and interval protocols. That means strong claims for Zone 2 as a distinct metabolic tool usually outrun the way the trials were actually designed. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full); [Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3))

Another limitation is that rankings depend on outcome choice and intervention grouping. Older type 2 diabetes network meta-analyses that emphasized supervised aerobic, supervised resistance, and combined training tended to favor combined training for HbA1c. Newer analyses that split HIIT into its own category often rank HIIT first for HbA1c, with combined and aerobic training close behind. That is not a contradiction so much as a reminder that "best" depends on what exactly is being compared. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415))

## Quick reference findings

- **Claim 1: "Zone 2 is particularly effective."** Supported in the sense that moderate continuous aerobic exercise clearly improves glycemia, including HbA1c, fasting glucose, 2-hour glucose, and CGM outcomes. Not supported in the stronger sense that it is uniquely superior. ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full); [Wang and Tang, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1496271/full))

- **Claim 2: "Any consistent aerobic exercise helps; the exact zone matters less."** This is closest to the overall literature. Moderate- and high-intensity aerobic exercise both improve CGM outcomes, and aerobic training in general reliably improves HbA1c. ([Wang and Tang, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1496271/full); [Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415))

- **Claim 3: "Resistance training is as good or better."** Partly supported. Resistance training often matches aerobic training for insulin sensitivity and some glycemic outcomes, and in prediabetes it may be especially good for fasting glucose, but it is not clearly better overall. ([Bacchi et al., 2012](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/35/4/676/38408/Metabolic-Effects-of-Aerobic-Training-and); [Bacchi et al., 2013](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/hep.26393); [Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2024.1308959/full))

- **Claim 4: "Combining Zone 2 with short high-intensity bursts is superior."** Sometimes, in direct matched trials, yes. Universally, no. The strongest consistent evidence still favors aerobic plus resistance as the most dependable combined approach. ([Karstoft et al., 2014a](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00125-014-3334-5); [Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Gajanand et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11459303/))


## Practical interpretation

The most defensible conclusion is that Zone 2-like moderate continuous aerobic exercise is a good option, not a magical one. It works. It is especially plausible as a sustainable base for sedentary or overweight adults. But the current evidence does not justify treating it as metabolically special relative to all other consistent exercise. If the goal is the broadest glycemic benefit, the best-supported program is usually one that includes aerobic work and resistance training, with intervals used as an optional enhancement rather than as proof that Zone 2 needs to be "upgraded" to matter. ([Pan et al., 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3); [Garcia et al., 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168822725000415); [Wang and Tang, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1496271/full); [Bacchi et al., 2012](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/35/4/676/38408/Metabolic-Effects-of-Aerobic-Training-and))
