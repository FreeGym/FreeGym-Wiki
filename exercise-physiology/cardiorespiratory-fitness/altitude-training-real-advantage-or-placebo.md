# Altitude Training: Real Advantage or Placebo?

Altitude training can cause real physiological changes, especially in erythropoietic signaling and total hemoglobin mass, but the average payoff for sea-level performance is smaller and less reliable than altitude culture often implies. The best reading of the controlled literature is not that altitude is “just placebo,” and not that it predictably works for everyone, but that adequate hypoxic dose can improve oxygen-carrying capacity while competition outcomes remain modest, delayed, and highly individual. ([Gore et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/))

Newer studies through 2025 do not clearly overturn that older pattern. Recent work still shows that blood-related adaptations can occur without a matching sea-level performance improvement, especially when protocol, timing, or athlete-specific context is not favorable, while recent meta-analyses remain mixed rather than uniformly positive or uniformly negative. ([Mujika et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11456557/), [Kuorelahti et al. 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12423153/), [Chen et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10559955/), [Deng et al. 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-1729/15/2/305))

## What altitude training is and is not

“Altitude training” is not one intervention. It includes classic live-high-train-high camps, live-high-train-low (LHTL), normobaric versus hypobaric exposure, and shorter intermittent hypoxic exposure (IHE). Mixing those together creates confusion, because they differ in total hypoxic dose, training quality, and the likelihood of producing a measurable hematological response. ([Garvican-Lewis et al. 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4424472/), [Saugy et al. 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0114418), [Gore et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/))

LHTL was developed partly to solve a real tradeoff: hypoxic exposure can drive adaptation, but hypoxic training can reduce the absolute workload an athlete can sustain. Reviews of the protocol literature note that the model was developed specifically to avoid that loss of training intensity, while the best-known positive LHTL studies keep key high-intensity work at lower altitude. That is a narrower claim than saying any altitude camp, tent, or hypoxic gadget will improve performance. ([Seitz et al. 2020](https://www.germanjournalsportsmedicine.com/fileadmin/content/archiv2020/Heft_2/DtschZSportmed_Review_Seitz_Effects_on_Endurance_of_Live_Low-Train_High_2020-02.pdf), [Stray-Gundersen et al. 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11509506/))

## How the main hematological mechanism works

The classic argument for altitude training is hematological. Reduced oxygen availability increases erythropoietic drive, which can eventually increase total hemoglobin mass (Hbmass or tHbmass), a practical proxy for oxygen-carrying capacity. In elite runners studied by [Stray-Gundersen and colleagues](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11509506/), serum erythropoietin rose sharply after ascent, hemoglobin concentration increased, and sea-level performance improved after 27 days of a HiHiLo / LHTL-style camp. Across 17 studies that used optimized carbon monoxide rebreathing, [Gore and colleagues](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/) estimated that Hbmass rises by about 1.1% per 100 hours of adequate exposure, with post-altitude Hbmass remaining about 3.3% above baseline for up to 20 days.

That measurement detail matters. Many modern altitude studies use optimized carbon monoxide rebreathing because the analytical error is about 2%, which is small enough to detect meaningful Hbmass changes but large enough that tiny “improvements” near that range should be interpreted cautiously. ([Gore et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/))

## What performance studies actually show

The most cited positive example remains the [Stray-Gundersen 2001](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/10.1152/jappl.2001.91.3.1113) elite-runner study. After 27 days of living at 2500 m and doing key high-intensity training at 1250 m, the athletes improved their sea-level 3000 m time-trial performance by 1.1% and increased VO2max by about 3%. That is a real effect, but it is also a small one in absolute terms. ([Stray-Gundersen et al. 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11509506/))

Other controlled studies show a more mixed translation from blood changes to performance. In elite triathletes, 17 days of [Humberstone-Gough et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3772580/) increased Hbmass by 3.2% versus placebo, but there were no clear changes in VO2max, velocity at VO2max, or time to exhaustion; the main signals were in running economy and lactate-speed measures. In another LHTL comparison, [Saugy et al. 2014](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0114418) found similar immediate gains in Hbmass and VO2max in normobaric and hypobaric camps, but the 3 km performance benefit appeared only 21 days later in the hypobaric group, which also accumulated a larger hypoxic dose.

Recent work still shows the same disconnect. In elite swimmers, a 22-day camp at 2320 m increased tHbmass by 5.6%, yet performances were slower than previous personal bests both immediately after the camp and 2 weeks later. In female endurance athletes, 21 days of normobaric LHTL increased Hbmass by 3.3% but did not improve performance parameters. The broad pattern is that hematological adaptation is often easier to demonstrate than a clear sea-level competition gain. ([Mujika et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11456557/), [Kuorelahti et al. 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12423153/))

## Comparisons between protocols

Adequate hypoxic dose matters more than the label on the method. In the [Gore meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/), a 2-week classic camp of about 336 hours was estimated to increase Hbmass by roughly 3.4% on average, and about 97.5% of adequately prepared athletes were predicted to gain at least 1% after around 300 hours of exposure. The same analysis concluded that classic altitude and LHTL can be similarly effective for Hbmass when exposure is matched.

Short exposures are much less convincing. In the elite-triathlete trial, the IHE group accumulated only about 10.2 hours of hypoxia over 17 days and showed no useful performance signal, whereas the LHTL group accumulated about 240 hours and did increase Hbmass. That does not prove every full LHTL camp works, but it does show that brief passive hypoxic exposure is not equivalent to a real altitude block. ([Humberstone-Gough et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3772580/))

There is also evidence that the threshold is not absolute. In one controlled runner study, 2 to 3 weeks of classic altitude at 1800 m increased Hbmass by about 3.0% to 3.1% compared with control, suggesting that lower natural altitude can still work when exposure is continuous and long enough. ([Garvican-Lewis et al. 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4424472/))

## “Responders,” “non-responders,” and personalization

The literature supports variability, but not a simple permanent responder-versus-non-responder identity. In the [Nummela et al. 2021](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sms.13804) study of 59 elite endurance athletes across 82 camps, tHbmass increased in 56% of camps overall and in 65% of camps above 2000 m. Among the 15 athletes who completed at least two camps, only 27% were always positive responders, 13% were always negative, and 60% showed both positive and negative responses across different camps. That is strong evidence against treating responsiveness as a fixed trait.

Among the variables that look most useful for personalization are hypoxic dose, camp altitude, inflammatory status, and the athlete’s own baseline hematological state. In the same Nummela dataset, altitude was the strongest predictor, and camps at 2000 to 2500 m with ferritin above 30 µg/L and hs-CRP below 3 mg/L produced a positive response in 69% of camps. In a larger elite-athlete dataset, each additional 100 km·h of hypoxic dose was associated with about 0.4% more Hbmass gain, while each 1 g/kg higher pre-altitude Hbmass predicted about 1% less gain; in that study, pre-altitude ferritin and iron supplementation did not show a significant interaction effect on Hbmass response. ([Nummela et al. 2021](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sms.13804), [Koivisto-Mørk et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33908091/))

Repeated-camp data point in the same direction. [Skattebo and Hallén](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35959514/) found that Hbmass rose by 3.4% on average across repeated altitude sojourns, but the within-athlete response was highly inconsistent, and larger gains tended to occur when the athlete arrived with a lower-than-usual pre-altitude Hbmass relative to their own typical baseline. That argues for monitoring the individual athlete’s state before camp rather than assuming a stable altitude phenotype.

## Placebo, selection bias, and measurement traps

The placebo argument is partly right and partly wrong. In the [Saunders race-walker study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21266736/), all groups improved treadmill performance during a 3-week camp, but only the LHTL group showed clear increases in Hbmass and VO2peak relative to the nocebo group. The performance gain itself was not greater than the general camp effect. That means expectancy, camp structure, tapering, and ordinary training effects can clearly mimic “altitude success” on performance tests, even when a real blood adaptation is present.

But the same study also shows why “altitude is just placebo” is too strong. Placebo can affect effort-dependent performance tests; it does not plausibly raise total hemoglobin mass. [Gore’s meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/) made that point explicitly: post-altitude performance is susceptible to placebo effects, whereas Hbmass is not meaningfully explained by belief alone.

A second trap is selection and design bias. In camp studies without a parallel sea-level control group, training load, recovery, tapering, nutrition, illness, and competition timing can change at the same time. That makes it easy to over-attribute success to altitude itself. The swimmer data are a good example: the authors explicitly noted that their design could not cleanly separate altitude effects from training-program effects. That caution is worth carrying into similar camp-based studies. ([Mujika et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11456557/), [Chen et al. 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10559955/))

## Practical interpretation

- Altitude training is not placebo in the hematological sense. Adequate exposure can genuinely increase Hbmass. ([Gore et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/))
    
- The performance benefit is real in some studies, but usually small and far less consistent than the blood response. ([Stray-Gundersen et al. 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11509506/), [Mujika et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11456557/), [Kuorelahti et al. 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12423153/))
    
- “Responder” and “non-responder” are better treated as temporary states than permanent labels. Dose, altitude, current hematological status, iron sufficiency, inflammation, illness, and training context all matter. ([Nummela et al. 2021](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sms.13804), [Koivisto-Mørk et al. 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33908091/), [Skattebo and Hallén 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35959514/))
    
- The fairest bottom line is that altitude is neither magic nor fake. It is a real but noisy intervention whose value depends on using enough hypoxic dose, protecting training quality, and measuring the individual athlete rather than trusting camp folklore. ([Seitz et al. 2020](https://www.germanjournalsportsmedicine.com/fileadmin/content/archiv2020/Heft_2/DtschZSportmed_Review_Seitz_Effects_on_Endurance_of_Live_Low-Train_High_2020-02.pdf), [Gore et al. 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3903147/), [Nummela et al. 2021](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sms.13804))
