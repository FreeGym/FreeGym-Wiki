# How Should You Find Your Zone 2 in Practice?

The cleanest physiological target for practical Zone 2, in common fitness usage, is work in the moderate-intensity domain, generally below the first lactate threshold (LT1) or first ventilatory threshold (VT1). Reviews of exercise-intensity prescription argue that threshold-based methods have the strongest construct validity for separating intensity domains, whereas fixed percentages of maximal heart rate or maximal oxygen uptake are much cruder anchors, and recent empirical work also shows meaningful error in fixed-anchor LT1 estimates ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8); [Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05748-8)).

## Why LT1/VT1 is the cleanest anchor

That does not make lab testing the only useful option, but it does make it the most precise one. In well-trained cyclists, ventilatory thresholds and blood-lactate thresholds showed good test-retest reliability, with coefficients of variation around 1.6 to 3.5% for ventilatory thresholds and 3.4 to 3.7% for lactate thresholds, and the lactate and ventilatory methods aligned reasonably well ([Pallarés et al., 2016](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0163389)).

So claim 1 - that lab thresholds are the **only** reliable way to identify Zone 2 - is too strong. The better statement is that lab LT1/VT1 is the best reference method when precision matters ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8)).

## How well field methods perform

A review of subjective methods concluded that the talk test (TT) and rating of perceived exertion (RPE) have construct validity for the ventilatory threshold and respiratory compensation point. That is the strongest case for field methods: not that they locate LT1/VT1 with laboratory precision in every person, but that they track related physiological transitions well enough to be useful in real training ([Bok et al., 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01690-3)).

A randomized training study also found that TT-guided prescriptions produced improvements comparable to traditional %HRR guidance over 10 weeks. That does not prove the TT pinpoints LT1, but it does support its practical usefulness ([Porcari et al., 2018](https://ojs.srce.hr/kinesiology/article/view/6321)).

The main caveat is that exact TT stage mapping depends on protocol and population. In a small cycling study, LT1 heart rate and power were lower than the last-positive TT stage, while the equivocal stage matched LT2 rather than LT1. In practice, that makes the TT better for bracketing intensity than for pinpointing LT1 with lab-like precision ([De Lucca et al., 2021](https://ojs.srce.hr/kinesiology/article/view/9097)).

RPE works the same way: useful, but not magic. A large 2024 cross-sectional study placed moderate exercise broadly around RPE 12 to 14 on the Borg 6 to 20 scale, and found that age, sex, ergometer, exercise duration, and fitness all influenced RPE, especially at lower intensities. So RPE is practical, but not a fixed universal number ([Grummt et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00729-1)).

## Fixed anchors, formulas, and why they miss

In 165 recreational runners, fixed anchors used to estimate LT1 produced mean absolute errors of about 0.6 to 0.8 km/h and 4.9 to 7.4 bpm. Speed-derived estimates were best, RPE-derived estimates were worst, and the 95% confidence interval for RPE at LT1 ran from 9.4 to 15.7, which is far too wide to treat one RPE value as precise ([Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05748-8)).

Threshold position also changes with the person. In a large treadmill dataset, the percentage of VO2max, HRmax, and maximal speed at which VT1 and VT2 occurred rose with training status, meaning the same fixed percentage can place different people in different physiological domains. That is exactly why beginners and trained athletes can both follow the same formula and still land in different places ([Benítez-Muñoz et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05622-z)).

By extension, fixed formulas such as MAF (180-age) are best understood as heuristics rather than direct measurements of LT1/VT1. The evidence cited here supports that general point for fixed anchors, although it does not specifically test the MAF formula itself ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8); [Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05748-8)).

## Why heart rate helps but should not stand alone

Heart rate remains useful, but it should not stand alone. A review on heart-rate monitoring noted small day-to-day variability and a steady rise in HR during exercise, while hydration and heat literature shows that prolonged exercise, fluid loss, and hyperthermia contribute to heart-rate drift. More importantly for Zone 2 practice, prolonged-cycling data show that HR at the moderate-heavy transition can rise from 142 ± 9 to 151 ± 12 bpm while power at that transition falls, so a single lab-derived HR number is not stable enough to serve as the whole prescription ([Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12762827/); [Mora-Rodriguez & Hamouti, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23075561/); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).

So claim 3 is only partly right. HR-based prescriptions are not useless; they are just too state-dependent to be used alone. Taken together, these data support using HR as a guardrail alongside other markers rather than as a standalone detector of "true Zone 2" ([Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12762827/); [Mora-Rodriguez & Hamouti, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23075561/); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).

## What decoupling can and cannot tell you

Decoupling or HR drift is better thought of as a validator than a primary detector. In trained cyclists, time to task failure correlated with the time until VT1 power had fallen by 5%, but the timing of that drop varied enormously across individuals, from 21 to 279 minutes. That means durability matters, but it also means individuals drift at very different rates ([Gallo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05440-3)).

A follow-up study showed that HR decoupling related to changes in the moderate-heavy transition during prolonged cycling and may be more useful than breathing-frequency decoupling, yet the same paper explicitly noted that a stable baseline HR at VT1 cannot be used to judge real-time proximity to VT1 during prolonged exercise. In other words, decoupling tells you whether a chosen intensity stayed durable in the real world; it does not replace threshold determination itself ([Rothschild et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05815-0)).

So claim 4 is also too strong. Decoupling is useful, but current evidence supports it more as a durability check than as the best primary way to locate Zone 2 ([Gallo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05440-3); [Rothschild et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05815-0)).

## Nasal breathing

Nasal breathing is not a strong standalone Zone 2 detector. In a crossover study of 60 minutes of low-intensity cycling, nasal-only breathing did not change heart-rate- or power-based intensity distribution and did not prevent people from drifting above the intended intensity ([Rappelt et al., 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2023.1134778/full)).

## What the competing claims get right and wrong

- **Lab thresholds are the only reliable way:** too strong. Lab LT1/VT1 is the cleanest and most precise anchor, but not the only useful one ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8)).
    
- **Field methods are accurate enough for most people:** mostly right, if "accurate enough" means good practical approximation rather than exact threshold measurement ([Bok et al., 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01690-3)).
    
- **HR-based prescriptions are unreliable:** partly right. HR is valuable, but too context-sensitive to stand alone ([Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12762827/); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).
    
- **Decoupling is the best real-world validator:** too strong. It is a useful validator of durability after you pick an intensity, not the best first-pass locator of Zone 2 ([Gallo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05440-3); [Rothschild et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05815-0)).
    

## Quick reference values

- Best anchor when precision matters: LT1/VT1 from blood-lactate testing or CPET ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8)).
    
- Best field estimate for most people: combine talk test, moderate RPE, and steady pace or power rather than relying on one marker alone. This is a practical synthesis of the evidence here rather than a directly validated single formula ([Bok et al., 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01690-3); [Porcari et al., 2018](https://ojs.srce.hr/kinesiology/article/view/6321); [De Lucca et al., 2021](https://ojs.srce.hr/kinesiology/article/view/9097); [Grummt et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00729-1)).
    
- Broad practical RPE for moderate work often lands around 12 to 14/20, but fitness, modality, duration, age, and sex shift that range ([Grummt et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00729-1)).
    
- Fixed-anchor estimates can miss LT1 by roughly 5 to 7 bpm on average in recreational runners, and RPE alone is noisier ([Nuuttila et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05748-8)).
    
- HR is best used as a contextual check, not the sole determinant ([Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12762827/); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).
    
- Decoupling is most useful as a durability validator during steady sessions, not as a replacement for threshold finding ([Gallo et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05440-3); [Rothschild et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05815-0)).
    

## Practical interpretation

- If precision matters, use lactate testing or CPET to find LT1/VT1 ([Jamnick et al., 2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01322-8)).
    
- If you do not have lab access, a practical starting point is the highest pace or power at which speech is still comfortable and effort still feels moderate, then let HR sit beside that as a check rather than the driver ([Porcari et al., 2018](https://ojs.srce.hr/kinesiology/article/view/6321); [Grummt et al., 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00729-1); [Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12762827/); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).
    
- Recheck the same effort under similar conditions. If HR rises while pace or power fades across a steady session, you are probably above a durable Zone 2 for that day ([Rothschild et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-025-05815-0); [Stevenson et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36127418/)).
    
- Be especially skeptical of formulas and fixed %max rules if you are very new, very trained, or working in hot conditions, because threshold position and HR behavior vary meaningfully across people and states ([Benítez-Muñoz et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05622-z); [Mora-Rodriguez & Hamouti, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23075561/)).
