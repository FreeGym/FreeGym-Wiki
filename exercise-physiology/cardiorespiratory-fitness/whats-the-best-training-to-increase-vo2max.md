# What’s the Best Training to Increase VO₂ Max?

VO₂max usually improves with almost any appropriately dosed aerobic training. If the question is **which method raises VO₂max most efficiently per unit time**, the evidence leans toward **higher-intensity interval work**. If the question is **how to organize training inside a serious endurance program**, the evidence is generally more supportive of **mostly easy volume plus some hard work** than of making threshold/tempo the center of the week. The literature does **not** support one universal winner across all populations, because training status, baseline fitness, interval design, session frequency, and intervention length all modify the result. ([Crowley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/); [Milanović et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/); [Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/ "The Effect of Exercise Training Intensity on VO2max in Healthy Adults: An Overview of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses - PubMed"))

## What the question is and is not

This is a question about **maximizing VO₂max**, not necessarily about maximizing race performance, durability, threshold speed, or enjoyment. Those outcomes overlap, but they are not identical. In athlete-focused reviews of training-intensity distribution, polarized training sometimes shows a small VO₂peak advantage without a clear universal advantage for time trial, time to exhaustion, or LT2/VT2-type outcomes. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/ "Comparison of Polarized Versus Other Types of Endurance Training Intensity Distribution on Athletes' Endurance Performance: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

## HIIT versus moderate continuous training

Higher-level evidence in healthy adults shows that **both** moderate continuous training and higher-intensity training increase VO₂max. The disagreement is about **how much more** higher intensity adds. In an overview of systematic reviews/meta-analyses, all included reviews found VO₂max improvements across intensities, while direct HIT-versus-LIT comparisons were usually either **small to moderate in favor of HIT** or too heterogeneous to support a clean universal conclusion. ([Crowley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/ "The Effect of Exercise Training Intensity on VO2max in Healthy Adults: An Overview of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses - PubMed"))

A 2015 meta-analysis in healthy young to middle-aged adults found that endurance training improved VO₂max by about **4.9 mL/kg/min** versus control, while HIT improved it by about **5.5 mL/kg/min** versus control. The extra benefit of HIT tended to be larger in **longer interventions** and in people with **lower baseline fitness**. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/ "Effectiveness of High-Intensity Interval Training (HIT) and Continuous Endurance Training for VO2max Improvements: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Controlled Trials - PubMed"))

A separate meta-regression in young healthy adults found **no clear linear intensity-response relationship** once training intensity, session dose, total training volume, and baseline VO₂max were modeled together. But it also found that similar VO₂max gains could be achieved with **lower training doses at higher intensities**, which is one reason HIIT often looks more time-efficient even when absolute superiority is modest. ([Scribbans et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27182424/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27182424/ "The Effect of Training Intensity on VO2max in Young Healthy Adults: A Meta-Regression and Meta-Analysis - PubMed"))

## What happens when total work is controlled

The cleanest way to compare methods is to match total work or energy expenditure. In those trials, **harder training usually wins for VO₂max**.

Helgerud et al. randomized moderately trained men to long slow distance, threshold, 15/15 intervals, or 4 × 4-minute intervals. All four protocols were matched for total oxygen consumption and training frequency, yet the interval groups increased VO₂max more than both threshold and long slow distance. ([Helgerud et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/ "Aerobic high-intensity intervals improve VO2max more than moderate training - PubMed"))

Gormley et al. likewise controlled exercise volume/energy expenditure across moderate-, vigorous-, and near-maximal-intensity training. VO₂max increased in all exercise groups, but the increase was **largest with near-maximal intensity**, intermediate with vigorous training, and smallest with moderate training. ([Gormley et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18580415/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18580415/ "Effect of intensity of aerobic training on VO2max - PubMed"))

More recently, Inglis et al. compared moderate, lower-heavy, upper-heavy, HIIT, and sprint-interval training using domain-based prescription; all groups except SIT were work-matched. HIIT produced the **largest** VO₂max increase, upper-heavy continuous training was **close behind**, and moderate continuous training was not different from control. That is strong evidence that plain moderate work is usually the weakest choice if VO₂max is the only target, while threshold-adjacent continuous work can still be respectable. ([Inglis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/ "Heavy-, Severe-, and Extreme-, but Not Moderate-Intensity Exercise Increase V̇o 2max and Thresholds after 6 wk of Training - PubMed"))

## Threshold/tempo training

Threshold or tempo training **can** improve VO₂max, and it may be an attractive compromise for people who dislike very hard intervals. But if the sole outcome is **VO₂max**, threshold-heavy training is **not** the best-supported single winner.

In Helgerud’s work-matched RCT, threshold training underperformed interval training for VO₂max. In Stöggl and Sperlich’s 9-week randomized trial in well-trained endurance athletes, the threshold-focused group improved less than polarized training, and the polarized group showed the largest VO₂peak increase. ([Helgerud et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/); [Stöggl & Sperlich, 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24550842/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/ "Aerobic high-intensity intervals improve VO2max more than moderate training - PubMed"))

The strongest recent argument for threshold-adjacent continuous work as a compromise comes from Inglis et al., where **upper-heavy continuous training** nearly matched HIIT over 6 weeks. But even there, HIIT still produced the largest mean increase. So the fairest reading is that threshold/tempo-type work is a **useful middle ground**, not the best-supported top choice for maximizing VO₂max itself. ([Inglis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/ "Heavy-, Severe-, and Extreme-, but Not Moderate-Intensity Exercise Increase V̇o 2max and Thresholds after 6 wk of Training - PubMed"))

## Polarized training: lots of easy work plus some hard work

For endurance athletes, the question changes. The aim is not only to raise VO₂max in isolation, but to do so inside a larger program that also supports recovery, volume, and sport-specific performance.

An older meta-analysis of randomized trials found a **moderate performance effect** favoring polarized over threshold training, but only **four** studies qualified for the review and just **three** for the meta-analysis, with modest methodological quality. ([Rosenblat et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29863593/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29863593/ "Polarized vs. Threshold Training Intensity Distribution on Endurance Sport Performance: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials - PubMed"))

A newer 2024 meta-analysis found a **small VO₂peak advantage** for polarized training overall, especially in interventions **shorter than 12 weeks** and in **highly trained athletes**, but not a clear superiority for other common endurance-performance surrogates. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/ "Comparison of Polarized Versus Other Types of Endurance Training Intensity Distribution on Athletes' Endurance Performance: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

However, the newest athlete-level synthesis is more cautious. A 2025 individual-participant-data network meta-analysis found **no overall difference** between polarized and pyramidal training for VO₂max or time-trial performance, while suggesting that **competitive athletes may benefit more from polarized** and **recreational athletes more from pyramidal**. In trained cyclists, a 2025 multilevel meta-analysis similarly found **no significant modality difference** in VO₂max, with **longer intervention duration** mattering more than whether the program was labeled polarized or not. ([Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/); [Cove et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39788807/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/ "Which Training Intensity Distribution Intervention will Produce the Greatest Improvements in Maximal Oxygen Uptake and Time-Trial Performance in Endurance Athletes? A Systematic Review and Network Meta-analysis of Individual Participant Data - PubMed"))

So the best evidence does **not** say “polarized always wins.” It says that for trained endurance athletes, a **low-intensity-dominant plan with some hard training** is well supported, while a **threshold-heavy** distribution is less consistently favored. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/); [Cove et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39788807/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/ "Comparison of Polarized Versus Other Types of Endurance Training Intensity Distribution on Athletes' Endurance Performance: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

## Dose matters as much as the label

The literature does not give one magic **minutes-per-week** threshold that reliably maximizes VO₂max across everyone. What shows up more consistently are **interval duration**, **accumulated hard-work minutes per session**, **weekly frequency**, **program length**, and **total training load**.

A meta-analysis of HIIT protocols found that even **short, low-volume, short-term** HIIT can improve VO₂max, but larger effects—especially versus MICT—are more consistently seen with **intervals ≥2 minutes**, **session work volume ≥15 minutes**, and **programs lasting at least 4 to 12 weeks**. ([Wen et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30733142/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30733142/ "Effects of different protocols of high intensity interval training for VO2max improvements in adults: A meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials - PubMed"))

Seiler’s RCT in trained cyclists also showed that **4 × 8-minute intervals** produced larger VO₂peak gains than 4 × 4 or 4 × 16, suggesting that the best VO₂max stimulus is not simply “harder,” but often **enough accumulated time at a very high aerobic demand**. ([Seiler et al., 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21812820/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21812820/ "Adaptations to aerobic interval training: interactive effects of exercise intensity and total work duration - PubMed"))

Mølmen’s meta-regression, which also assessed VO₂max as a secondary outcome, found that VO₂max increased similarly with endurance training, HIT, and SIT after adjustment, though HIT tended to be greater and SIT was most time-efficient per hour. It also found larger VO₂max gains with **higher training frequency** and with **lower initial fitness**, and concluded that **training load (volume × intensity)** is a suitable predictor of VO₂max change, suggesting that the program label alone is often not enough. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/ "Effects of Exercise Training on Mitochondrial and Capillary Growth in Human Skeletal Muscle: A Systematic Review and Meta-Regression - PubMed"))

## Baseline fitness and adherence

Baseline fitness is one of the most consistent moderators in this literature. Lower-fit participants generally improve more, and some reviews suggest the clearest edge for higher-intensity training appears in **older and/or less fit** populations. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/); [Crowley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/); [Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/ "Effectiveness of High-Intensity Interval Training (HIT) and Continuous Endurance Training for VO2max Improvements: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Controlled Trials - PubMed"))

In more highly trained athletes, the margins get smaller and the surrounding program matters more. HIIT can still outperform conventional training in elite cohorts, but training-intensity distribution effects depend on athlete level, intervention length, and how the intensity is organized. ([Ma et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37346345/); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37346345/ "VO2max (VO2peak) in elite athletes under high-intensity interval training: A meta-analysis - PubMed"))

The claim that “response depends more on adherence than program type” is only **partly** supported. Adherence obviously matters in practice, but comparative evidence does **not** clearly show that HIIT has better or worse adherence than MICT. A 2023 meta-analysis in **insufficiently active adults and adults with medical conditions** found no significant difference in supervised compliance or unsupervised adherence between HIIT and MICT, though adherence evidence was noisier and lower quality than compliance evidence. So current evidence supports **baseline fitness as a clear moderator** more strongly than it supports a universal adherence advantage for any one program type. ([Santos et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37990239/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37990239/ "Rates of compliance and adherence to high-intensity interval training: a systematic review and Meta-analyses - PubMed"))

## Quick take on the competing claims

- **“HIIT is superior to moderate continuous training for increasing VO₂max.”**  
    Mostly **yes, but only modestly and not always**. In healthy adults, HIIT/HIT usually has a small edge or achieves similar gains in less time; in work-matched RCTs, intervals usually outperform moderate or threshold continuous work. ([Crowley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/); [Milanović et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/); [Helgerud et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/); [Gormley et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18580415/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/ "The Effect of Exercise Training Intensity on VO2max in Healthy Adults: An Overview of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses - PubMed"))
    
- **“The best approach is polarized: lots of easy volume + some very hard work.”**  
    **Often reasonable for athletes, but not a universal winner.** Polarized training is better supported than threshold-heavy training, yet newer athlete-level meta-analyses do not show that it beats every alternative in every setting. ([Rosenblat et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29863593/); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/); [Cove et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39788807/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29863593/ "Polarized vs. Threshold Training Intensity Distribution on Endurance Sport Performance: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials - PubMed"))
    
- **“Threshold/tempo training is the most time-efficient compromise.”**  
    **Mixed.** Threshold/tempo can work well and may come close to HIIT, but it is not the best-supported single winner for VO₂max. ([Helgerud et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/); [Inglis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/ "Aerobic high-intensity intervals improve VO2max more than moderate training - PubMed"))
    
- **“Training response depends more on baseline fitness and adherence than program type.”**  
    **Baseline fitness: yes, strongly. Adherence: less clearly.** Lower starting fitness reliably predicts larger gains; adherence matters, but current comparative meta-analysis does not show a clear HIIT-versus-MICT adherence difference. ([Mølmen et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/); [Santos et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37990239/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39390310/ "Effects of Exercise Training on Mitochondrial and Capillary Growth in Human Skeletal Muscle: A Systematic Review and Meta-Regression - PubMed"))
    

## Practical interpretation

- For **untrained or recreational adults who want the biggest VO₂max gain per unit time**, HIIT or other vigorous-to-severe interval work has the strongest evidence. ([Milanović et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/); [Scribbans et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27182424/); [Wen et al., 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30733142/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243014/ "Effectiveness of High-Intensity Interval Training (HIT) and Continuous Endurance Training for VO2max Improvements: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Controlled Trials - PubMed"))
    
- For **endurance athletes training many hours per week**, a **low-intensity-dominant week plus some very hard work** is better supported than making threshold the main course. ([Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/); [Cove et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39788807/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/ "Comparison of Polarized Versus Other Types of Endurance Training Intensity Distribution on Athletes' Endurance Performance: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis - PubMed"))
    
- **“Zone 2” by itself is not the fastest route to a higher VO₂max**; its strength is as the easy aerobic volume that lets you accumulate training and recover well enough to do the harder sessions that push VO₂max more directly. ([Crowley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/); [Oliveira et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38717713/); [Rosenblat et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39888556/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38655159/ "The Effect of Exercise Training Intensity on VO2max in Healthy Adults: An Overview of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses - PubMed"))
    
- Threshold/tempo is still useful, especially if it is what you will actually do consistently, but it is better viewed as a **good compromise** than as **the best VO₂max builder**. ([Inglis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/); [Helgerud et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17414804/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38376995/ "Heavy-, Severe-, and Extreme-, but Not Moderate-Intensity Exercise Increase V̇o 2max and Thresholds after 6 wk of Training - PubMed"))
