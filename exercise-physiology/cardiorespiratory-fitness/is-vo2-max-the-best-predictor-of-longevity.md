# Is VO₂ Max the Best Predictor of Longevity?

VO₂ max, or more broadly cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF), is one of the strongest **observational** predictors of all-cause mortality in the medical literature. But the phrase “the best” or “the single greatest predictor of longevity” is stronger than the evidence supports. The newest syntheses still show a large, graded association between higher CRF and lower mortality, but most of that evidence comes from cohort studies, not randomized mortality trials, and a 2025 Mendelian-randomization study did **not** support a simple direct causal effect of genetically predicted **estimated** VO₂ max on **longevity measured as parental survival**. The overall certainty for mortality outcomes in the 2024 overview ranged from very low to moderate. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/); [Laukkanen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/); [Han et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/); [Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))

## What the literature actually measures

In practice, the literature behind this claim is broader than “lab-measured VO₂ max.” Many mortality studies use peak treadmill METs, exercise-estimated CRF, or submaximal-test estimates rather than direct respiratory-gas VO₂ max. A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis of 42 studies found very similar dose-response associations for objectively measured and estimated CRF, which is clinically useful, but it also means that strong claims about **VO₂ max specifically** often rest on a broader CRF evidence base. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/); [Laukkanen et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32370851/))

That distinction matters because “best predictor of longevity” sounds like a claim about a single physiological variable measured precisely in the lab. The underlying evidence is more mixed: some studies are on direct VO₂ max, many are on exercise-test performance or estimated CRF, and those are related but not identical constructs. ([Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))

## Why CRF is often called one of the strongest predictors

A 2024 overview identified 26 systematic reviews with meta-analysis representing more than 20.9 million observations from 199 unique cohort studies, and the mortality signal was consistently strong. Across **meta-analyses summarized in the 2024 overview** in general adult populations, the highest versus lowest CRF categories were associated with roughly **41% to 53% lower** all-cause mortality, while each **1-MET higher** CRF was associated with about **11% to 14% lower** all-cause mortality. Those are large effect sizes for an observational risk marker. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/); [Laukkanen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/); [Han et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/))

This is the main reason advocates speak so strongly about CRF. In a large clinical treadmill-testing cohort of 122,007 adults, the mortality gradient associated with **low treadmill-estimated fitness (peak estimated METs)** was comparable to or greater than that seen for coronary artery disease, smoking, or diabetes. That finding is real, but it comes from a referred testing population rather than a clean general-population sample, so it is better read as evidence that CRF can be an unusually informative marker, not as proof of a universal ranking that applies in every setting. ([Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6324439/))

## What “best predictor” gets wrong

The problem is not that CRF is weak. The problem is that the slogan is too absolute. Most of the evidence is observational; many studies measure CRF indirectly; and some of the most dramatic “it beats traditional risk factors” comparisons come from clinical referral cohorts where treadmill performance is especially informative. That is enough to justify saying CRF is **among** the strongest predictors of mortality. It is not enough to justify saying it is a stand-alone master variable that makes age, smoking, blood pressure, lipids, or disease burden secondary. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/); [Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6324439/))

A good example comes from UK Biobank. Higher CRF was linearly associated with lower mortality, and adding CRF to a 5-year mortality model did improve reclassification. But the change in the C-index was very small: **+0.0012 (P=.49)**. That is what a useful complementary marker often looks like. It adds information beyond conventional risk factors, but it does not replace them. ([Laukkanen et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32370851/))

## Does higher VO₂ max cause longer life?

The evidence closer to causality is mixed. On the pro-causality side, the association persists over very long follow-up and survives attempts to reduce reverse-causation bias. In a 46-year cohort of 5,107 healthy middle-aged men, higher age-adjusted estimated VO₂ max was associated with longer mean life expectancy, and the results were essentially unchanged after excluding men who died in the first 10 years. Serial-fitness cohorts point the same way: in 93,060 adults who completed two treadmill tests, changes of at least 1.0 MET were associated with proportional changes in mortality risk, and in a smaller population-based study using direct VO₂ max, each **1 mL/kg/min** higher long-term change in VO₂ max was associated with about **9% lower** all-cause mortality after adjustment for baseline fitness, socioeconomic status, smoking, lipids, BMI, physical activity, diabetes, and ischemic heart disease. ([Clausen et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30139444/); [Kokkinos et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36948729/); [Laukkanen et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27444976/))

On the anti-overclaim side, the most explicitly causal design in this literature is less supportive. In the 2025 bidirectional Mendelian-randomization study, the genetic instruments for VO₂ max were derived from **estimated** VO₂ max from a submaximal cycle ramp test, **and longevity was assessed as parental survival**; within that design, genetically predicted VO₂ max was **not associated with longevity**. The authors argued that VO₂ max may be more consequence than cause of favorable body composition and physical activity. That does not prove VO₂ max is irrelevant, but it does argue against the simple claim that VO₂ max itself is the single direct driver of lifespan. ([Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))

The most defensible inference from those two bodies of evidence is that CRF is clearly a **marker** and may also lie on part of the pathway linking physical activity, body composition, cardiovascular reserve, and metabolic health to outcomes. Some of those pathways are plausibly causal, but the current evidence does not support treating VO₂ max as a fully isolated causal determinant of longevity. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/); [Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))

## What intervention trials show

Intervention data are much thinner than the cohort data, especially for all-cause mortality in generally healthy adults. In coronary heart disease, exercise-based cardiac rehabilitation reduces cardiovascular mortality, hospitalizations, and myocardial infarction, but not clearly **overall mortality** in contemporary meta-analysis. In chronic heart failure, a 2024 meta-analysis of 61 RCTs found **no mortality benefit**, although exercise improved quality of life and reduced hospitalization. ([Dibben et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/); [Al Hennawi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39074671/))

The [Generation 100 trial](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7539760/) is especially relevant because it was a long-term randomized trial in community-dwelling older adults. Over five years, the combined supervised exercise groups did **not** reduce all-cause mortality versus guideline-based activity advice, even though high-intensity training showed a favorable signal against moderate-intensity training. The trial authors also noted that no other randomized trial in a general population existed for direct comparison, and they explicitly stated that high-quality randomized evidence linking current physical-activity advice to longevity is lacking. That is a major reason the causal claim still needs restraint. ([Stensvold et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7539760/))

Even within trials, “fitness improvement predicts better outcomes” is not the same as “fitness improvement causes longer life.” In [HF-ACTION](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19351941/), the protocol-specified primary analysis was not significant for all-cause death or hospitalization, and all-cause mortality was also not significantly reduced. Prespecified adjusted analyses showed modest benefit, and a secondary analysis found that every **6% increase in peak VO₂** was associated with lower subsequent event rates, including a **7% lower all-cause mortality**. Useful as that is, it still falls short of proving that peak VO₂ itself is the sole causal lever. ([O’Connor et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19351941/); [Swank et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22773109/))

## Mechanistic plausibility is real, but it is not proof

Mechanistic support is substantial. Meta-analyses of randomized exercise trials show improved insulin action, better endothelial function, lower inflammatory markers, and broader improvements in cardiometabolic biomarkers alongside improved CRF. That makes it biologically plausible that the physiology associated with higher CRF contributes to better long-term outcomes. ([Rebello et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10148910/); [Tao et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9950521/); [Wang et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10499556/); [Lin et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/))

But those trials test **exercise programs**, not VO₂ max as an isolated variable. So the mechanistic literature strongly supports exercise and fitness-related physiology, while remaining weaker as proof that VO₂ max itself is the single best causal predictor of lifespan. ([Lin et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4608087/); [Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))

## Quick reference values

- Highest versus lowest CRF in recent meta-analyses: about **45% lower** all-cause mortality overall, with estimates ranging roughly from **RR 0.47 to 0.55**. ([Han et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/); [Laukkanen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/); [Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/))
    
- Per **1-MET higher** CRF: about **11% to 14% lower** all-cause mortality in recent pooled analyses. ([Laukkanen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/); [Han et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/); [Singh et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39271056/))
    
- UK Biobank: adding CRF to established risk factors improved reclassification, but the **C-index change was only +0.0012 (P=.49)**. ([Laukkanen et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32370851/))
    
- Serial-fitness cohort: in **93,060** adults, a CRF change of at least **1.0 MET** tracked inversely with mortality risk. ([Kokkinos et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36948729/))
    
- Mendelian randomization: genetically predicted **estimated** VO₂ max was **not associated with longevity (measured as parental survival)** in the 2025 2-sample MR study. ([Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))
    

## Practical interpretation

- Saying CRF is **among the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality** is well supported. Saying it is **the single greatest predictor of longevity** is too strong for the current evidence. ([Lang et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11103301/); [Laukkanen et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35562197/); [Han et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35022163/))
    
- CRF clearly matters clinically, and low CRF should not be dismissed as just noise. But it should be treated as a powerful **complementary** risk marker rather than a replacement for conventional risk factors and disease burden. ([Mandsager et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6324439/); [Laukkanen et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32370851/))
    
- Improving CRF is very likely beneficial, but the strongest evidence supports exercise and broader fitness physiology more than a simple “raise VO₂ max and you will necessarily extend lifespan” model. ([Dibben et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36746187/); [Al Hennawi et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39074671/); [Stensvold et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7539760/); [Kjaergaard et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12012764/))
