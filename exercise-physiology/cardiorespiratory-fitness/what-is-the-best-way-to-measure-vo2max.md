# What Is the Best Way to Measure VO₂ Max?

VO₂ max can be measured directly only with gas exchange during maximal exercise, but that does not mean every question requires a fully corroborated “true max” test. The best method depends on whether you need a direct physiological measurement, a repeatable estimate for training, or a lower-burden clinical or field alternative. ([Poole & Jones, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/); [Mayorga-Vega et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/); [Bennett et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/); [Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/"))

## What a VO₂ max test is and is not

Direct gas exchange during a maximal cardiopulmonary exercise test (CPET) is the reference-standard way to _measure_ oxygen uptake itself, because oxygen uptake is recorded breath by breath during exercise rather than inferred from performance, heart rate, or a proprietary algorithm. Field tests, submaximal tests, and wearables can be useful, but they estimate aerobic capacity rather than directly measuring VO₂. ([Poole & Jones, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/); [Mayorga-Vega et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/); [Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/"))

The terminology matters. _VO₂peak_ is the highest oxygen uptake observed in a given test. _VO₂max_ is a stronger claim: that the test elicited the highest physiologically attainable oxygen uptake. Day and colleagues showed that a visible plateau is not obligatory during an incremental ramp test, and that ramp-test VO₂peak often does not differ from constant-load maximum values, but they also noted that without additional testing one cannot be certain. ([Day et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/ "The maximally attainable VO2 during exercise in humans: the peak vs. maximum issue - PubMed"))

## Why CPET with gas exchange is the gold standard

If the goal is an actual measured VO₂ value, CPET with gas exchange is the best method. It directly captures pulmonary gas exchange during large-muscle exercise and remains the standard against which field tests, submaximal predictions, and consumer wearables are validated. ([Poole & Jones, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/); [Mayorga-Vega et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26336340/); [Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/"))

That said, “CPET” and a corroborated VO₂max claim are not automatically the same thing. A maximal test with gas exchange can still end at a defensible VO₂peak rather than a fully corroborated VO₂max if the evidence for maximality is incomplete. ([Day et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/ "The maximally attainable VO2 during exercise in humans: the peak vs. maximum issue - PubMed"))

## How VO₂ max is usually verified

Historically, VO₂ max was identified by a plateau in oxygen uptake despite increasing workload. In practice, many people do not show an obvious plateau on modern ramp tests, which is one reason secondary criteria such as respiratory exchange ratio, age-predicted maximal heart rate, or postexercise lactate became common. ([Day et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/); [Poole et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17968581/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/ "The maximally attainable VO2 during exercise in humans: the peak vs. maximum issue - PubMed"))

The problem is that these secondary criteria are weak as validators. In Poole’s ramp-test study, commonly used RER thresholds could markedly undermeasure VO₂max, while age-predicted heart-rate and lactate criteria rejected some subjects who had already demonstrated a plateau. Midgley and colleagues likewise found that traditional secondary criteria were often satisfied well before exhaustion. ([Poole et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17968581/); [Midgley et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19370041/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17968581/ "Validity of criteria for establishing maximal O2 uptake during ramp exercise tests - PubMed"))

The best-studied modern add-on is a verification phase: a short constant-load bout to exhaustion after the incremental test. In a systematic review and meta-analysis of apparently healthy adults, Costa and colleagues found that verification bouts produced very similar peak VO₂ values to the preceding CPET, with a mean absolute difference of about 0.03 L/min, or 0.85%, and no clear effect of sex, fitness, exercise modality, CPET protocol, or verification-phase design. The same review also concluded that, because concordance was so high, verification is not necessary in every testing circumstance in apparently healthy adults. ([Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7888616/"))

## VO₂ peak versus VO₂ max

This is where the argument usually gets overstated. It is too strong to say that any unverified incremental test is meaningless, because incremental VO₂peak often approximates the maximum value well. But it is also too strong to say that “true max” never matters, because the exact ceiling can matter in physiology research, in method-development studies, and whenever a small numerical difference is the main outcome. ([Day et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/); [Poole & Jones, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/ "The maximally attainable VO2 during exercise in humans: the peak vs. maximum issue - PubMed"))

The practical compromise is straightforward: if maximality is well supported by protocol quality and corroboration, reporting VO₂max is reasonable; if not, report VO₂peak. That is not hair-splitting. It is simply more precise labeling. ([Day et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12857763/ "The maximally attainable VO2 during exercise in humans: the peak vs. maximum issue - PubMed"))

## Ramp versus step protocols

There is no single ramp-versus-step winner that dominates in the available studies, and the evidence is population-specific. In severe COPD, Benzo and colleagues compared three ramp slopes with a step protocol and found differences in test duration and peak workload, but not in VO₂peak, heart rate, ventilation, perceived exertion, or symptoms. ([Benzo et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/"))

Similarly, in recreationally trained men performing cycle ergometry, Roffey and colleagues found no difference in VO₂max across 1-minute stages, 3-minute stages, and a hybrid protocol, even though some other physiological variables differed. ([Roffey et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17786685/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17786685/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17786685/"))

So the better question is usually not “ramp or step?” but “does the protocol fit the person and the purpose?” The available evidence from small, population-specific studies suggests that reasonable protocols can produce similar maximal oxygen uptake values, while changing duration, workload, and some secondary variables. ([Benzo et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/); [Roffey et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17786685/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17925427/"))

## Test–retest reliability

Well-run laboratory testing is highly repeatable. In moderately trained men, Succi and colleagues reported excellent reliability for treadmill graded exercise testing, with ICC 0.988, SEM 1.0 mL/kg/min, and coefficient of variation 2.03%. In that study, the standalone graded test actually produced higher values than the submaximal verification bout, leading the authors to conclude that the verification bout may not be required in that population. ([Succi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/"))

Reproducibility is also strong in older adults. In a prospective multicenter cohort, Wolf and colleagues reported that treadmill CPET in adults aged 70 and older had a VO₂peak intraclass correlation coefficient of 0.97. ([Wolf et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/"))

Taken together, the reliability data support a practical point: when a lab needs a stable serial measure, a standardized CPET or VO₂peak protocol may already be very useful even if every test is not followed by a separate verification bout. That is an inference from the reproducibility data, not a claim that terminology no longer matters. ([Succi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/); [Wolf et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36243831/"))

## Field tests: when they are useful and for whom

The 20-meter shuttle run, or beep test, has decent criterion validity, but its accuracy depends on population. In a meta-analysis of 57 studies, Mayorga-Vega and colleagues found moderate-to-high validity overall, with stronger validity in adults than in children. In children, adding variables such as age, sex, and body mass improves estimation. The same paper emphasized that shuttle performance is an estimate, not a direct measure. ([Mayorga-Vega et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26336340/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4541117/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4541117/"))

Time- and distance-based tests are similar. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 123 studies found that the 1.5-mile test and the 12-minute walk/run test were among the better field alternatives when laboratory testing was not feasible, but the overall validity of walk/run tests still ranged only from low to moderate. In other words, these are useful estimates, not interchangeable with direct gas-exchange testing. ([Mayorga-Vega et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/"))

The Cooper 12-minute run is also reasonably repeatable in young healthy adults, but that does not eliminate individual bias. Penry and colleagues found high overall reliability, yet Bland–Altman analysis showed that the 12-minute run tended to underestimate lower-fit individuals and overestimate higher-fit individuals. ([Penry et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20647946/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20647946/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20647946/"))

Submaximal step tests are often a good compromise when the goal is practicality rather than a direct maximal measurement. A systematic review concluded that step tests are safe, simple, and ecologically valid in healthy adults, and that the Chester Step Test appears particularly suitable for tracking changes because of its high test–retest reliability. ([Bennett et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/"))

The 6-minute walk test can also be useful, but it should be understood for what it is. In healthy adults on a 15-meter track, Mänttäri and colleagues found it could predict VO₂max with about 1 MET of accuracy. That is good enough for broad fitness estimation, but it is still not a direct VO₂max measurement. ([Mänttäri et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29851229/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29851229/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29851229/"))

Submaximal cycle tests vary in quality as well. Jamnick and colleagues compared the YMCA test with a custom submaximal test against a verified “true” VO₂max protocol. The custom test did not differ significantly from verified VO₂max on average, whereas the YMCA protocol underestimated it and showed poorer agreement. That is a good reminder that “submaximal test” is not one thing; some prediction models are better than others. ([Jamnick et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26339726/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26339726/ "Comparison of the YMCA and a Custom Submaximal Exercise Test for Determining VO2max - PubMed"))

## Wearables

Wearables are improving, but they are still best treated as estimated fitness metrics rather than true VO₂max measurements. In a systematic review and meta-analysis, exercise-based wearable algorithms performed better than resting-based algorithms, but the individual-level limits of agreement remained wide enough that the authors judged them inadequate for many sport or clinical decisions. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/"))

A newer independent qualitative systematic review reached a similar practical conclusion. Mežan found that consumer wearables often gave valid or acceptable VO₂max estimates in healthy untrained adults, recreational athletes, and some team-sport athletes, but usefulness in elite endurance sport remained questionable. Most included studies involved Garmin devices paired with chest-strap heart-rate sensors and Firstbeat algorithms. ([Mežan, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41477023/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41477023/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41477023/"))

One important conflict-of-interest issue applies here. The 2022 INTERLIVE wearable meta-analysis was partly funded by Huawei Technologies Finland. That does not invalidate the paper, but the findings should be interpreted with that in mind, especially since wearables are a commercial product category. It is reassuring that the later independent review points in a similar direction rather than a radically different one. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/); [Mežan, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41477023/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/"))

## Safety of maximal testing

Maximal CPET is not risk-free, but the available data suggest it is generally safe when done with appropriate screening, supervision, and stopping rules. In a large cohort of patients with high-risk cardiovascular disease, Skalski and colleagues reported 8 adverse events and no fatalities, for an adverse-event rate of 0.16%. ([Skalski et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23091065/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23091065/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23091065/"))

In older adults, Wolf and colleagues found CPET was safe and well tolerated: 95.8% completed the VO₂peak phase, only two adverse events occurred, and reproducibility was high. ([Wolf et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/"))

Submaximal tests still have an important role. They reduce burden and are often adequate when the practical goal is broad fitness stratification, rehabilitation monitoring, or exercise prescription rather than a direct measurement of physiological ceiling. The key point is that “safer” and “more accurate” are different questions. ([Bennett et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/); [Wolf et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38598351/); [Skalski et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23091065/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/"))

## Practical interpretation

- If you need a _measured_ VO₂ value, the best method is a maximal CPET with direct gas exchange, using sensible maximality checks and, when the exact ceiling matters, a verification approach or equivalent corroboration. ([Poole & Jones, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/); [Costa et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33596256/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28153947/"))
    
- If you need a practical estimate for training in a healthy population, a validated field test or submaximal test is often good enough, provided it has been validated in people like the person being tested and is repeated consistently. ([Mayorga-Vega et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26336340/); [Mayorga-Vega et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26987118/); [Bennett et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/); [Jamnick et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26339726/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4541117/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4541117/"))
    
- If maximal exertion is not feasible or not necessary, submaximal step or walk tests are often adequate, but they should be described honestly as estimates of aerobic fitness rather than direct VO₂max measurements. ([Bennett et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/); [Mänttäri et al., 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29851229/)) ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/ "https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26670455/"))
    
- If all you have is a wearable, treat it as a rough trend indicator, not a laboratory-equivalent number. ([Molina-Garcia et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35072942/); [Mežan, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41477023/)) ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/"))
