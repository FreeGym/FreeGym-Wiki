# Overtraining vs “Just Not Recovered Yet”

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) is a real construct, and the research literature defines it narrowly. In research, OTS is a long-term performance decrement following a persistent mismatch between total stress and recovery, and because there is no gold-standard diagnostic test, it remains a diagnosis of exclusion ([Carrard et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/ "Diagnosing Overtraining Syndrome: A Scoping Review - PubMed"))

That matters because a bad week, a plateau, or a low HRV score is not the same thing as OTS ([Weakley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/); [Roete et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/); [Bosquet et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18308872/); [Bellenger et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26888648/)). A systematic review that required objective pre-onset performance data, suppressed performance for more than 4 weeks, and psychological symptoms found no studies that met those criteria ([Weakley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/)). Taken together, the evidence argues for ruling out shorter-lived overload, inadequate recovery, low energy availability, and nontraining stress before concluding OTS ([Weakley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/); [Tian et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/); [Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/); [Hamlin et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6361803/); [Kuikman et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35819582/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/ "Overtraining Syndrome Symptoms and Diagnosis in Athletes: Where Is the Research? A Systematic Review - PubMed"))

## What overtraining syndrome is and is not

In one 8-year cohort of 114 elite female Chinese wrestlers, investigators recorded 13 instances of functional overreaching, 23 of nonfunctional overreaching, and 2 of OTS, using coach-assessed decrement with operational cutoffs based on recovery duration. That single-sport cohort does not establish universal prevalence, but within that cohort temporary overload states were recorded more often than clear OTS ([Tian et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/"))

The difficulty is that the field still lacks a stable diagnostic framework. [Carrard et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/) identified 39 eligible publications, corresponding to 30 independent studies, and a wide range of proposed tools, including hormonal measures, questionnaires, exercise tests, HRV, EEG, immune markers, and composite scores such as the EROS tools. Their review also emphasized that these composite scores still need validation in larger samples and in female athletes ([Carrard et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/ "Diagnosing Overtraining Syndrome: A Scoping Review - PubMed"))

## Why the diagnosis is hard

A recent diagnostic review in this area is notable partly for what it could not find. [Weakley et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/) required objective pre-onset performance data, performance suppression lasting more than 4 weeks, and psychological symptoms, and found no studies that met those criteria. That means much of the OTS literature does not include cleanly documented onset that satisfies those criteria, which makes confident diagnosis difficult and makes casual overuse of the term especially risky ([Weakley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/); [Carrard et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/ "Overtraining Syndrome Symptoms and Diagnosis in Athletes: Where Is the Research? A Systematic Review - PubMed"))

## Endocrine markers are often overread

Basal endocrine markers have not performed well as diagnostic markers. In a systematic review of 38 studies, basal hormone levels were usually normal in OTS, functional overreaching, and nonfunctional overreaching, whereas exercise-based stimulation tests more often showed blunted ACTH and GH responses. The authors concluded that basal hormone levels were poor predictors, while stimulation responses were more promising ([Cadegiani and Kater, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/ "Hormonal aspects of overtraining syndrome: a systematic review - PubMed"))

The longitudinal evidence points the same way. In the wrestler cohort, creatine kinase, hemoglobin, testosterone, and cortisol all showed poor diagnostic sensitivity, with no clear mean differences between overreached and non-overreached periods. The authors explicitly concluded that single blood variables should not be used as markers of overreaching in that setting ([Tian et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/ "https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/"))

## HRV and resting heart rate are useful context, not stand-alone diagnostics

The evidence does not support the strong claim that HRV and resting heart rate can reliably detect or distinguish overreaching on their own. In a 2021 systematic review on functional overreaching in endurance athletes, changes in power output, heart rate, heart-rate recovery, rating of perceived exertion, and DALDA/POMS scores could reflect functional overreaching, whereas HRV parameters generally could not ([Roete et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/ "A Systematic Review on Markers of Functional Overreaching in Endurance Athletes - PubMed"))

Broader reviews are similarly mixed. [Bosquet et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18308872/) found that short-term overload increased resting heart rate and LF/HF ratio, but longer interventions changed submaximal and maximal heart rate without altering resting values. [Bellenger et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26888648/) later found that post-exercise HRV and heart-rate recovery increased both with positive adaptation and with overreaching, which means those changes are not uniquely warning signs of maladaptation ([Bosquet et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18308872/); [Bellenger et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26888648/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18308872/ "Is heart rate a convenient tool to monitor over-reaching? A systematic review of the literature - PubMed"))

More recent soccer-specific evidence is suggestive, not definitive. [Lipka et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40405528/) included 19 studies and found many associations between HRV indices and OTS-related markers, but the average methodological quality was only fair and the authors emphasized the need for standardization and better control of confounding factors. In daily swimmer monitoring, HRV moved with perceived sleep, fatigue, stress, and mood, which is useful, but it also means HRV reflects both training and nontraining stress rather than training load alone ([Lipka et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40405528/); [Flatt et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40405528/ "Heart rate variability and overtraining in soccer players: A systematic review - PubMed"))

## Subjective measures often outperform common biometrics for readiness

Subjective readiness measures have stronger review-level support than many commonly used objective measures. In a systematic review of 56 studies that included concurrent subjective and objective measures, subjective and objective markers generally did not correlate; subjective measures tracked acute and chronic training loads with superior sensitivity and consistency, and when measures differed within studies, 85% of those differences favored the subjective measures. Mood disturbance, perceived stress, recovery, and stress symptoms were especially responsive ([Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/ "Monitoring the athlete training response: subjective self-reported measures trump commonly used objective measures: a systematic review - PMC"))

Even here, specificity is imperfect. [Roete et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/) found that both DALDA and POMS could reflect functional overreaching, but POMS did not clearly distinguish functional overreaching from acute fatigue. So the best use of subjective measures is not to label an athlete as “overtrained” from one questionnaire, but to track repeated within-athlete changes in fatigue, soreness, sleep, mood, stress, and readiness alongside performance and training load ([Roete et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/); [Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/ "A Systematic Review on Markers of Functional Overreaching in Endurance Athletes - PubMed"))

## Life stress and low energy availability complicate the picture

The monitoring literature repeatedly points beyond training alone. [Saw et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/) identified non-training stress as one of the more useful self-report domains, and [Flatt et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/) showed that HRV tracked perceived sleep, stress, fatigue, and mood as well as training-related strain. In 182 elite university athletes followed over 4 years, decreased mood and sleep duration and increased academic stress predicted injury; examination periods coincided with the highest stress and increased likelihood of illness, and sudden preseason workload increases were associated with increased injury and illness incidence. That is a reminder to assess total load, not just training load ([Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/); [Flatt et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/); [Hamlin et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6361803/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/ "Monitoring the athlete training response: subjective self-reported measures trump commonly used objective measures: a systematic review - PMC"))

Low energy availability overlaps with underperformance, but the overlap is incomplete. In a systematic review and meta-analysis of overload studies, half of the underperforming athlete groups had at least 2 low-energy-availability markers after training, but other groups showed low-energy-availability markers without underperformance, and many showed neither. Fat mass, resting metabolic rate, and leptin tended to fall in underperforming athletes, but cortisol, insulin, and testosterone were unchanged on average. That supports screening for low energy availability, while also rejecting the idea that every case of underperformance is explained by low energy availability alone ([Kuikman et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35819582/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35819582/ "Markers of Low Energy Availability in Overreached Athletes: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis - PubMed"))

## Practical interpretation

- A bad week, a plateau, or a single lab panel does not diagnose OTS. OTS is a diagnosis of exclusion, and prospective evidence meeting strict diagnostic criteria is surprisingly thin. ([Carrard et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/); [Weakley et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35320774/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/ "Diagnosing Overtraining Syndrome: A Scoping Review - PubMed"))
    
- Give more weight to performance trend, symptom duration, mood, fatigue, soreness, sleep, stress, and recent training load than to a single HRV or hormone reading. ([Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/); [Cadegiani and Kater, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/ "Monitoring the athlete training response: subjective self-reported measures trump commonly used objective measures: a systematic review - PMC"))
    
- Use HRV and resting heart rate as supportive context, not as a verdict. Serial within-athlete data are more useful than isolated values, and changes can reflect positive adaptation, nontraining stress, or overload. ([Roete et al., 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/); [Bellenger et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26888648/); [Flatt et al., 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/); [Lipka et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40405528/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34108275/ "A Systematic Review on Markers of Functional Overreaching in Endurance Athletes - PubMed"))
    
- Do not overinterpret basal testosterone, cortisol, creatine kinase, or similar single markers. ([Cadegiani and Kater, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/); [Tian et al., 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/)). ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/ "Hormonal aspects of overtraining syndrome: a systematic review - PubMed"))
    
- Before calling it overtraining, screen for abrupt load spikes, poor sleep, illness, academic or life stress, and low energy availability. ([Hamlin et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6361803/); [Kuikman et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35819582/); [Saw et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/)). ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6361803/ "Monitoring Training Loads and Perceived Stress in Young Elite University Athletes - PMC"))
    

## Conflict of interest notes on the cited evidence

- Among the cited studies with accessible disclosure statements, [Carrard et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34496702/), [Cadegiani and Kater](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28785411/), [Saw et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/), [Lipka et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40405528/), and [Flatt et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/) reported no conflicts of interest. [Saw et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4789708/) reported support from Deakin University and the Australian Institute of Sport, and [Flatt et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6162498/) reported no funding.
    
- [Tian et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4495439/) reported public funding from the China Institute of Sport Science and the General Administration of Sports of China. [Hamlin et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6361803/) reported that one author was employed by Sports Doctors, and the dataset was not publicly available because of commercial sensitivity related to the Metrifit software platform. Their findings are still useful, but that context should be kept in mind.
